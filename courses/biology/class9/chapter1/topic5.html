Biological Method

How do biologists actually discover new knowledge? They follow a careful, logical process called the biological method — a specific version of the scientific method applied to living systems.

5.1 What is the Biological Method?

The biological method (also called the scientific method in biology) is a systematic, logical approach to solving biological problems and answering questions about living organisms. It ensures that biological knowledge is based on evidence, not guesswork or tradition.

It consists of the following steps, always performed in order:

Step 1: Observation

Step 2: Identification of the Problem

↓

5.2 Step-by-Step Explanation

Step 1: Observation

Observation means carefully noticing something and recording it. It is the starting point of all biological inquiry. Observations can be:

  • Direct observations: Using your own five senses (sight, hearing, smell, taste, touch)
  • Indirect observations: Using instruments — microscope, thermometer, pH meter, balance, telescope
  • Qualitative observations: Descriptive, non-numerical (e.g., "the leaf is yellow")
  • Quantitative observations: Numerical, measurable (e.g., "the plant grew 5 cm in one week")

Example: A biologist observes that plants kept near a window grow taller and healthier than plants kept in a dark room.

Important

Observations must be accurate, objective, and repeatable. Personal bias must be avoided. An observation is a fact — not an interpretation.

Step 2: Identification of the Problem

After observing, the biologist identifies a specific problem or question to investigate. The question should be clear, specific, and answerable through experimentation.

Example question: "Does the amount of light affect the rate of growth in plants?"

Step 3: Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a proposed, testable explanation for the observation — an educated guess based on prior knowledge and reasoning. It must be:

  • Testable — you must be able to design an experiment to test it
  • Falsifiable — it must be possible to prove it wrong
  • Specific — not vague; it should clearly state the expected relationship
  • Usually written as: "If [cause], then [effect]"

Example hypothesis: "If plants receive more light, then they will grow taller, because light is needed for photosynthesis which provides energy for growth."

Hypothesis vs Guess

A hypothesis is NOT a random guess. It is an educated prediction based on existing knowledge. A good hypothesis comes from understanding what is already known about the topic.

Step 4: Deductions

Deductions are logical predictions that follow from the hypothesis. They take the form: "If the hypothesis is true, then we should observe..."

Example deduction: "If light promotes plant growth, then plants given 12 hours of light per day should be taller than plants given only 2 hours of light per day after 4 weeks."

Deductions help design the experiment — they tell you exactly what to measure and compare.

Step 5: Experiment

An experiment is a controlled procedure to test the hypothesis. Every good experiment has:

Independent Variable
The factor that is deliberately changed by the experimenter. In our example: the amount of light (e.g., 2 hr, 6 hr, 12 hr per day)
Dependent Variable
The factor that is measured to see how it responds to the change. In our example: the height of the plant (in cm)
Controlled Variables
All other factors that are kept the same in all groups, so they cannot affect the results. Example: same soil, same amount of water, same pot size, same temperature, same plant species
Control Group
A group that receives no treatment (or standard conditions). It serves as a baseline for comparison. Example: plants kept in complete darkness
Experimental Group
The group(s) that receive the treatment being tested. Example: plants given 2, 6, or 12 hours of light
Rule of One Variable

In a controlled experiment, only ONE variable is changed at a time (the independent variable). All other variables must remain constant. If multiple variables change, we cannot know which one caused the result.

Step 6: Results and Analysis

During and after the experiment, data (results) are collected and recorded. Data is organised in tables and displayed in graphs for easy analysis.

  • Data tables — rows and columns listing measurements
  • Bar graphs — compare values across categories
  • Line graphs — show trends over time
  • Pie charts — show proportions

Analysis involves looking for patterns, trends, and relationships in the data. Do the results support the hypothesis?

Step 7: Conclusion

The conclusion is a statement that directly answers the original question based on the data collected. It states whether the hypothesis was:

  • Supported (accepted) — the results match the prediction
  • Not supported (rejected) — the results do not match the prediction

A rejected hypothesis is not a failure — it is valuable information that eliminates one possible explanation and guides future investigation.

Example conclusion: "The results support the hypothesis. Plants given 12 hours of light per day grew significantly taller (avg. 18 cm) than plants given 2 hours (avg. 5 cm), confirming that light promotes plant growth."

5.3 Hypothesis → Theory → Law

When a hypothesis is tested many times by many different scientists and the results always support it, it gains a higher status:

Hypothesis
A testable, educated explanation for a single observation. Has limited evidence. Can be accepted or rejected.
Theory
A well-tested, well-supported explanation for a broad range of observations. Has been tested by many scientists with consistent results. Example: Cell Theory, Theory of Evolution, Germ Theory of Disease.
Law
A statement that describes a natural phenomenon that always occurs under certain conditions. Laws describe WHAT happens but not WHY. Example: Mendel's Laws of Inheritance.
Common Misconception

A scientific theory is NOT a guess. In everyday language, "theory" means an uncertain idea. In science, a THEORY is the highest form of explanation — it has been tested repeatedly by independent scientists and is strongly supported by evidence.

5.4 Types of Reasoning in Biology

Inductive Reasoning

Drawing a general conclusion from many specific observations. You observe many individual cases and then form a general rule.

Example: You observe 1000 different types of cells under a microscope and all of them have a membrane. You conclude: "All cells have a membrane." (This is inductive.)

Deductive Reasoning

Starting with a general rule and applying it to predict a specific outcome. If the general rule is true, then the specific case must also be true.

Example: General rule: "All cells have a membrane." Specific case: "A red blood cell is a cell. Therefore, a red blood cell has a membrane." (This is deductive.)

5.5 Biological Tools and Techniques

Biologists use a range of tools and techniques to make observations and conduct experiments:

Microscopy
Using microscopes to observe organisms, cells, and structures too small to see with the naked eye. Light microscopes magnify up to 1,500×; electron microscopes can magnify up to 1,000,000×.
Dissection
Carefully cutting open organisms (or organs) to examine internal structures. Used in anatomy studies.
Staining
Applying dyes to specimens to make specific structures visible under the microscope. Example: iodine stains starch blue-black; methylene blue stains nuclei.
Culturing
Growing bacteria, fungi, or cells in controlled laboratory conditions on nutrient media (agar plates or broth).
Chromatography
Separating mixtures of molecules (e.g., plant pigments) based on how they travel through a medium.
Centrifugation
Spinning samples at high speed to separate components by density (e.g., separating cell organelles).
