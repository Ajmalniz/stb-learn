Chapter 1 · Topic 5

Unicellular, Colonial & Multicellular Life

Some living things are one cell only; some live in groups; some have many cells like you, a frog, or sarsoon (mustard). Your textbook uses Amoeba, Volvox, mustard plant, and frog — we explain them in simple words.

5.1 Unicellular Organisation

Unicellular = made of one cell. The single cell does all life activities: digestion, respiration, excretion, movement, etc.

Examples: Bacteria, Amoeba, Paramecium, Euglena.

Amoeba — real-life picture in words

  • Found in mud of shallow ponds, pools, stagnant water (you may see such water near villages or after rain).
  • Size about 0.25 mm — needs a microscope.
  • Irregular shape; changes shape as it moves.
  • Cell membrane — controls what enters/leaves; protects cytoplasm.
  • Ectoplasm — outer clear part (gel); endoplasm — inner part (sol).
  • Inside: nucleus, food vacuoles, mitochondria, etc.
  • Moves using pseudopodia (“false feet”) — pushes cytoplasm outward.
Simple idea

Amoeba is like a one-room house where eating, breathing, and moving all happen in the same room.

5.2 Colonial Organisation

Many unicellular organisms live together in a colony, but:

  • There is no division of labour (no special jobs for different cells).
  • Each cell lives its own life.
  • They do not depend on each other to survive.
  • They do not form one true multicellular body.

Volvox — example from textbook

  • Green alga in freshwater (ponds, ditches).
  • Forms a round colony of up to thousands of cells.
  • Each cell has two flagella; they beat together to roll the colony in water.
  • Has chlorophyll — makes its own food by photosynthesis (like plants).
  • Important in aquatic ecosystems; not harmful to humans (does not make poison).

Compare: Volvox = many students in a circle holding hands and rolling together — but each student is still a full student, not part of one body.

5.3 Multicellular Organisation

Multicellular = body made of many cells. Textbook examples: frog and mustard plant (sarsoon).

Mustard plant — Brassica campestris (sarsoon)

  • Common in Pakistan; grown in winter.
  • Height about 1 to 1.5 metre.
  • Leaves used as vegetable; seeds for oil.
  • Vegetative parts: root, stem, leaves.
  • Reproductive parts: flowers (yellowish) → fruit → seeds.

Real life: Sarsoon ka saag and sarsoon ka tel both come from this plant.

Frog — Rana tigrina (common spotted frog)

  • Found near ditches, pools, ponds, slow streams in our region.
  • Lives in water and on land (amphibian).
  • Body: head + trunk (no neck).
  • Made of organ systems (digestive, circulatory, respiratory, etc.).
  • Organs made of tissues (epithelial, muscular, nervous, glandular, …).
  • Eats small insects.

5.4 Lab Activity — Frog Dissection (Summary)

In school you may study a preserved frog. Main idea: open the belly carefully and see which organ belongs to which system.

Materials: preserved frog, dissecting tray, dissection box, pins.

Steps (simple): Place frog on back; pin limbs; cut abdomen from cloaca to mouth; open skin on limbs; observe organs inside.

Organ system Main organs
DigestiveMouth, stomach, intestines, liver, pancreas, cloaca
CirculatoryHeart, blood vessels
RespiratoryLungs, nostrils
ExcretoryKidneys, ureter, urinary bladder
ReproductiveTestes / ovaries and related parts
NervousBrain, spinal cord, nerves

Draw a labelled diagram of the dissected frog in your notebook — this is often asked in practical exams.

5.5 Chapter Summary (Quick Revision)

  • Biology = study of living beings; life shown by processes like digestion, movement, reproduction, etc.
  • Three divisions: Zoology, Botany, Microbiology; many branches (Morphology, Anatomy, Genetics, …).
  • Biology links with physics, chemistry, maths; careers in medicine, agriculture, fisheries, biotechnology.
  • Quran teaches about life and water; Muslim scientists like Jabir Bin Hayan, Asmai, Bu Ali Sina contributed greatly.
  • Levels: atom → molecule → cell → … → biosphere.
  • Amoeba = unicellular; Volvox = colonial; mustard & frog = multicellular.
