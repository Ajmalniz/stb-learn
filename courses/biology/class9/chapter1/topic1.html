What is Biology?

Biology is one of the oldest and most important sciences. Before we study living things, we must understand what biology actually means, where it came from, and what makes something "alive" in the first place.

1.1 Definition of Biology

The word Biology is made up of two Greek words:

Bios
A Greek word meaning Life
Logos
A Greek word meaning Study or Knowledge

So, Biology = the study of life. It is the branch of science that deals with the study of living organisms — their structure, function, growth, origin, evolution, and distribution.

Definition

Biology is the natural science that studies life and living organisms, including their physical structure, chemical processes, molecular interactions, physiological mechanisms, development, and evolution.

1.2 Brief History of Biology

Humans have been curious about living things since ancient times. Here is a brief timeline of how biology developed:

Aristotle (384–322 BC)
Known as the Father of Biology. He was the first to classify animals and study them systematically. He wrote "Historia Animalium" (History of Animals), describing over 500 species.
Theophrastus (371–287 BC)
Student of Aristotle. Known as the Father of Botany. He classified and described plants in detail.
Ibn Sina / Avicenna (980–1037 AD)
Muslim scientist who wrote "Canon of Medicine" — a masterpiece covering anatomy, physiology, and disease. His work was used in European universities for over 600 years.
Robert Hooke (1665)
Discovered the cell using a microscope while examining cork. He called them "cells" because they reminded him of monks' rooms (cellulae in Latin).
Carolus Linnaeus (1707–1778)
Father of Modern Taxonomy. Developed the binomial nomenclature system for naming organisms (Genus + species).
Charles Darwin (1809–1882)
Proposed the Theory of Evolution by Natural Selection in his book "On the Origin of Species" (1859).
Gregor Mendel (1822–1884)
Known as the Father of Genetics. Discovered the laws of inheritance through experiments on pea plants.
Watson & Crick (1953)
Discovered the double helix structure of DNA — one of the greatest discoveries in the history of biology.
Did You Know?

The term "Biology" was first used in 1799 by German physician Karl Friedrich Burdach, and independently by Gottfried Reinhold Treviranus and Jean-Baptiste Lamarck in 1802.

1.3 Characteristics of Living Things

To study biology, we must first know what separates living things from non-living things. All living organisms share the following 7 characteristics:

1. Nutrition

All living things need food (nutrients) to get energy, grow, and repair themselves. There are two types:

  • Autotrophs (self-feeders): Make their own food using sunlight (e.g., plants through photosynthesis)
  • Heterotrophs: Cannot make their own food — must eat other organisms (e.g., animals, fungi)

2. Respiration

Living things break down food to release energy. This process is called cellular respiration. It occurs in every living cell.

  • Aerobic respiration: Uses oxygen → produces more energy
  • Anaerobic respiration: Without oxygen → produces less energy (e.g., in yeast, muscle cells during exercise)

3. Movement

All living things can move — either the whole organism or parts of it. Plants move toward light (phototropism) and water (hydrotropism). Animals move from place to place.

4. Growth

Living things grow by increasing in cell number (cell division) or cell size. Growth in living organisms is from within (intrinsic), unlike non-living things that may grow by adding material from outside.

5. Reproduction

All living things can produce offspring (copies of themselves) to continue the species. Without reproduction, a species would become extinct.

  • Asexual reproduction: Involves one parent (e.g., binary fission in bacteria, budding in yeast)
  • Sexual reproduction: Involves two parents and the fusion of gametes

6. Excretion

Living things produce waste products from their chemical reactions. These must be removed to prevent poisoning the organism. In humans, urea is excreted in urine; CO₂ is excreted through breathing.

7. Sensitivity (Response to Stimuli)

All living things can detect and respond to changes in their environment (called stimuli). A stimulus is any change that triggers a response.

  • Plants grow toward light (respond to light stimulus)
  • You pull your hand away from a hot object (respond to heat stimulus)
  • A flower closes at night (responds to darkness)
Remember — MRSGREN

An easy way to remember the 7 characteristics of life: Movement · Respiration · Sensitivity · Growth · Reproduction · Excretion · Nutrition

1.4 Why Study Biology?

Biology is not just a school subject — it is the foundation of almost everything that affects human life. Here is why it matters:

Medicine and Health

  • Understanding disease-causing organisms (bacteria, viruses) allows us to develop vaccines and antibiotics
  • Biology helps doctors understand how the human body works so they can treat illness
  • Cancer research, heart disease, diabetes — all depend on biological knowledge

Agriculture and Food

  • Biology helps develop disease-resistant and high-yield crops
  • Understanding plant physiology improves irrigation and fertiliser use
  • Animal husbandry (farming of animals) is based on biological knowledge

Environment and Conservation

  • Understanding ecosystems helps us protect endangered species
  • Biology reveals the effects of pollution on living organisms
  • Conservation biology works to preserve biodiversity

Biotechnology

  • Genetic engineering produces medicines like insulin
  • Stem cell research offers hope for treating previously incurable diseases
  • Forensic biology helps solve crimes through DNA fingerprinting
Did You Know?

Insulin — used daily by millions of diabetic patients worldwide — was first produced using genetically engineered bacteria in 1982. Before that, it had to be extracted from pig and cattle pancreases.

1.5 Unifying Themes of Biology

Despite covering millions of different species and systems, all of biology is connected by a few fundamental themes:

Cell Theory
All living things are made of cells. The cell is the basic unit of life. All cells come from pre-existing cells.
Evolution
All life on Earth shares a common ancestry and has changed over billions of years through natural selection.
Genetics
DNA carries the genetic instructions for all living organisms. Traits are passed from parents to offspring.
Homeostasis
Living things maintain a stable internal environment (e.g., constant body temperature, blood pH) despite changes in the external environment.
Energy
All living things need and use energy. Energy flows through ecosystems from the sun → plants → animals.
Interdependence
All living things depend on each other and on their non-living environment. No organism lives in complete isolation.
