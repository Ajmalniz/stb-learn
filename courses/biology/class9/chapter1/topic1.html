What is Biology?

Biology is the science of living things — from a tiny germ in water to a huge whale in the sea. In this topic we learn what biology means, and how we can tell if something is alive.

1.1 Definition of Biology

Biology is a branch of natural science. It deals with the study of living beings (organisms).

Living things are not all the same. They differ in shape, size, and composition. A cat, a mango tree, and bacteria in yogurt are all living — but they look very different. Biology helps us understand them.

The word biology comes from Greek:

Bios
Means life (live)
Logos
Means thought, reasoning, or study

So biology = study of life.

Definition (for exams)

Biology is a branch of natural sciences which deals with the study of living beings. It gives us knowledge about organisms that differ from each other in shape, size, composition, etc.

Real-life example

When a doctor checks why you have fever, or a farmer chooses better wheat seeds, they are using biological knowledge — even if they do not call it “biology.”

1.2 What is Life?

Scientists still cannot give one perfect definition of life. But we can recognise living things because they show certain life processes (functions).

If something does these activities, we treat it as alive. A stone does not eat or grow; a goat does — so the goat is living.

According to your STB textbook, living organisms show these processes:

  • Digestion — breaking down food and using it
  • Movement — changing position or moving parts of the body
  • Excretion — removing waste from the body
  • Respiration — releasing energy from food (often using oxygen)
  • Growth — increase in size or number of parts
  • Irritability — responding to changes around (stimuli)
  • Metabolism — all chemical reactions inside the body
  • Development — changes as the organism becomes more complex
  • Reproduction — producing new individuals of the same kind

Simple examples from daily life

Digestion
You eat roti and dal; your stomach breaks it into simpler substances your body can use.
Movement
You walk to school; a cat runs; roots of a plant grow downward into soil.
Excretion
You pass urine and sweat; plants release extra water through leaves.
Respiration
You breathe in oxygen; cells use it to get energy from food so you can study and play.
Growth
A baby grows taller; a chick becomes a hen; a small plant becomes a big tree.
Irritability
You pull your hand from a hot pan; a sunflower turns toward sunlight.
Metabolism
All together: digestion, respiration, and building new proteins inside cells — like a factory always working inside you.
Development
A caterpillar becomes a butterfly; a seed becomes a flowering plant.
Reproduction
A hen lays eggs; wheat plants produce seeds; humans have children.
Remember

Non-living things (chair, rock, water bottle) do not carry out all these life processes together. That is the easiest way to separate living from non-living at Class 9 level.

1.3 Why Should We Study Biology?

Biology is not only for exams. It is useful in real jobs and daily life in Pakistan:

  • Health: doctors treat diseases; vaccines protect children from polio and measles.
  • Agriculture: better crops, fruits, and dairy for our country’s farmers.
  • Environment: clean air, forests, and safe water for future generations.
  • Industry: medicines, fish farms, and biotechnology (e.g. insulin from bacteria).
