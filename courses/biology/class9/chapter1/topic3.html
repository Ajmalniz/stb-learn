Biology and Other Sciences

Biology does not exist in isolation. It connects deeply with physics, chemistry, mathematics, and social sciences. These connections have given rise to exciting new fields that solve real-world problems.

3.1 Biology is a Multidisciplinary Science

Modern biology is interdisciplinary — it overlaps and combines with many other branches of science. This has led to the development of new fields that use tools and ideas from multiple disciplines to answer complex biological questions.

Consider a simple question: "How does a cell produce energy?" Answering it requires understanding chemical reactions (chemistry), molecular structures (physics), enzyme interactions (biochemistry), and data analysis (mathematics). No single science is enough on its own.

Key Idea

Biology is called the "central science" because it sits at the intersection of chemistry, physics, mathematics, and the social sciences. Progress in biology depends on progress in all these fields.

3.2 Biology and Chemistry → Biochemistry

Biochemistry is the study of the chemical processes and substances that occur within living organisms. It is perhaps the most important link between biology and chemistry.

What Biochemistry Studies:

  • The structure and function of biological molecules — proteins, carbohydrates, lipids (fats), and nucleic acids (DNA, RNA)
  • How enzymes catalyse (speed up) chemical reactions in the body
  • How cells produce energy through respiration (cellular metabolism)
  • How DNA stores and transfers genetic information
  • How hormones act as chemical messengers in the body

Real-World Applications:

  • Developing new medicines by understanding how drugs interact with biological molecules
  • Understanding diseases like cancer, diabetes, and Alzheimer's at the molecular level
  • Food science — improving nutrition and food preservation
Did You Know?

Hemoglobin — the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen — was one of the first proteins whose 3D structure was determined. This required both biology AND advanced chemistry techniques.

3.3 Biology and Physics → Biophysics

Biophysics applies the principles and methods of physics to understand biological systems.

What Biophysics Studies:

  • How muscles generate mechanical force and how bones support weight (biomechanics)
  • How electrical signals travel along nerve cells (bioelectricity)
  • How the heart generates electrical impulses detected by an ECG machine
  • How light is absorbed by chlorophyll during photosynthesis
  • The physical forces involved in hearing (sound waves) and vision (light waves)
  • How X-rays and MRI machines create images of the inside of the body

Real-World Applications:

  • Design of medical equipment — MRI scanners, X-ray machines, ultrasound
  • Development of prosthetic limbs and artificial organs
  • Understanding how viruses inject their DNA into host cells

3.4 Biology and Mathematics → Biomathematics / Bioinformatics

Biomathematics uses mathematical models to understand biological processes. Bioinformatics combines biology with computer science and statistics to analyse large biological datasets (especially genetic data).

What They Study:

  • Mathematical models of population growth (how species populations change over time)
  • Analysing the human genome — the full set of human DNA containing ~3 billion base pairs
  • Predicting how diseases spread through populations (epidemiology)
  • Comparing DNA sequences between species to understand evolution
  • Drug discovery — using computers to predict which molecules might be effective medicines
Did You Know?

The human genome project — completed in 2003 — sequenced all 3 billion base pairs of human DNA. This was only possible using advanced mathematics and computing. It would take one person typing non-stop for about 9.5 years to type out all that DNA code!

3.5 Biology and Geography → Biogeography

Biogeography studies the past and present geographical distribution of living organisms — where they live, why they live there, and how they got there.

Key Questions:

  • Why are kangaroos only found in Australia?
  • How did species spread across continents after glaciers melted?
  • Why do islands often have unique species found nowhere else?
  • How does climate determine which plants and animals can survive in an area?

Biogeography combines biology with geology, climatology, and geography to answer these questions and understand biodiversity patterns on Earth.

3.6 Biology and Social Sciences → Social Biology

Social Biology studies the biological basis of social behaviour — why organisms live in groups, how they communicate, cooperate, and compete.

  • Why do bees live in colonies with a queen?
  • How do wolves organise into packs with an alpha leader?
  • What biological factors influence human behaviour, emotions, and social bonding?

This field bridges biology, psychology, and sociology.

3.7 Summary: Biology's Connections

Biology + Chemistry
→ Biochemistry: studies chemical reactions in living organisms
Biology + Physics
→ Biophysics: applies physical principles to biological systems
Biology + Mathematics
→ Biomathematics / Bioinformatics: models and analyses biological data
Biology + Geography
→ Biogeography: studies the geographical distribution of species
Biology + Social Sciences
→ Social Biology: studies the biological basis of social behaviour
Biology + Computer Science
→ Bioinformatics: uses computers to analyse genomic and biological data
Biology + Medicine
→ Biomedicine / Pharmacology: applies biology to treat human diseases
