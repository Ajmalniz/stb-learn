Section B — Short Answer Questions

Q1. Define biology and explain its significance in everyday life.

Definition: Biology is the natural science that studies life and living organisms. The word comes from two Greek words: bios (life) and logos (study). It investigates the structure, function, growth, origin, evolution, and distribution of living things.

Significance in everyday life:

  • Medicine: Understanding biology allows doctors to treat diseases. Vaccines, antibiotics, and cancer treatments all arise from biological research.
  • Food and Agriculture: Biology enables the development of high-yield, disease-resistant crops and improved animal husbandry practices.
  • Environment: Biological knowledge helps us understand and protect ecosystems, conserve endangered species, and address pollution.
  • Biotechnology: Biology enables the production of life-saving medicines like insulin through genetic engineering.
Q2. What are the characteristics of living organisms? Explain any three.

The seven characteristics of living organisms are remembered by MRSGREN: Movement, Respiration, Sensitivity, Growth, Reproduction, Excretion, Nutrition.

Three characteristics explained:

  • Nutrition: All living things require food (nutrients) to obtain energy, grow, and repair themselves. Autotrophs (like plants) make their own food through photosynthesis; heterotrophs (like animals) eat other organisms.
  • Respiration: Living organisms break down food molecules to release energy through cellular respiration. Aerobic respiration uses oxygen; anaerobic respiration occurs without oxygen (e.g., in yeast).
  • Reproduction: All living things produce offspring to continue their species. It may be asexual (one parent, e.g., binary fission in bacteria) or sexual (two parents, fusion of gametes).
Q3. Write a note on the branches of biology based on the type of organism studied.

Because life is so vast and diverse, biology is divided into specialised branches. The following branches are defined by the type of organism they study:

  • Zoology: Study of animals — their structure, behaviour, physiology, and distribution.
  • Botany: Study of plants — their structure, growth, reproduction, and uses.
  • Microbiology: Study of microorganisms (bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa) too small to see with the naked eye.
  • Virology: Study of viruses and the diseases they cause.
  • Mycology: Study of fungi — mushrooms, moulds, and yeasts.
  • Entomology: Study of insects — the most numerous group of animals.
  • Ornithology: Study of birds — their anatomy, behaviour, and migration.
  • Ichthyology: Study of fish.
Q4. How is biology related to chemistry and physics? Give examples of each combination.

Biology + Chemistry → Biochemistry:

Biochemistry studies the chemical processes within living organisms. It investigates biological molecules (proteins, carbohydrates, lipids, nucleic acids), how enzymes catalyse reactions, how cells produce energy (metabolism), and how DNA carries genetic information. Example: Understanding how haemoglobin carries oxygen in blood requires both biological and chemical knowledge.

Biology + Physics → Biophysics:

Biophysics applies the principles of physics to biological systems. It studies how muscles generate mechanical force, how electrical signals travel along nerve cells, how light is absorbed during photosynthesis, and how medical imaging (MRI, X-ray, ultrasound) works. Example: An ECG machine measures the electrical signals of the heart — this is biophysics in action.

Q5. Describe the levels of biological organisation from the atom to the organism.

Life is organised in a hierarchy. The levels from simplest to most complex are:

  • Atom: The simplest level. The atoms most important to life are C, H, O, N, P, S (CHONPS).
  • Molecule: Two or more atoms bonded together. Examples: water (H₂O), glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆), DNA.
  • Organelle: Specialised structures inside cells, each performing a specific job. Examples: mitochondria (energy production), nucleus (DNA storage), ribosome (protein synthesis).
  • Cell: The basic unit of life — the smallest living unit. Can be unicellular (bacteria, amoeba) or part of a multicellular organism.
  • Tissue: A group of similar cells working together. Examples: muscle tissue, nervous tissue.
  • Organ: Two or more tissue types working together. Examples: heart, stomach, leaf.
  • Organ System: Group of organs performing a major function. Examples: circulatory system, digestive system.
  • Organism: A complete, independent living thing (e.g., a human, a frog, a tree).
Q6. What is a hypothesis? What are the characteristics of a good hypothesis?

A hypothesis is a proposed, testable explanation for an observation — an educated guess based on prior knowledge and reasoning. It is typically written in the form: "If [cause], then [effect]."

Characteristics of a good hypothesis:

  • Testable: You must be able to design an experiment to test it.
  • Falsifiable: It must be possible to prove it wrong. A statement that cannot be disproved is not scientific.
  • Specific: It should clearly state the expected relationship between variables — not vague.
  • Based on prior knowledge: A hypothesis is NOT a random guess. It is an educated prediction based on existing knowledge.

Example: "If plants receive more light, then they will grow taller, because light is needed for photosynthesis which provides energy for growth."

Q8. What are controlled experiments? Why is it important to change only one variable at a time?

A controlled experiment is a scientific procedure designed to test a hypothesis by deliberately changing one factor (the independent variable) while keeping all other factors constant (controlled variables) and measuring the result (dependent variable).

Every controlled experiment has:

  • Experimental group: The group that receives the treatment being tested.
  • Control group: The group that receives no treatment — serves as a baseline for comparison.

Why only one variable must change at a time (Rule of One Variable):

If multiple variables change at the same time, we cannot determine which variable caused the observed result. For example, if you change both the amount of light AND the amount of water given to plants, and the plants grow faster, you cannot tell whether light, water, or both caused the growth. By changing only one variable, we can establish a clear cause-and-effect relationship.

Section C — Long Answer Questions

Q1. Write a detailed note on the biological method (scientific method in biology). Explain all its steps with a suitable example.

The Biological Method

The biological method is a systematic, logical approach used by biologists to solve problems and discover knowledge about living organisms. It ensures that conclusions are based on evidence rather than guesswork. It consists of 8 steps, always performed in order.

Example used throughout: A biologist notices that plants near a window grow taller than plants in a dark room.

Step 1: Observation

Observation means carefully noticing and recording something. It is the starting point of all scientific inquiry.

  • Direct: Using the five senses
  • Indirect: Using instruments (microscope, thermometer)
  • Qualitative: Descriptive ("the leaf is yellow")
  • Quantitative: Numerical ("the plant grew 5 cm")

Example: Biologist observes that plants near a window grow taller and healthier than those in a dark room.

Step 2: Identification of the Problem

After observing, the biologist identifies a specific, answerable question.

Example: "Does the amount of light affect the rate of plant growth?"

Step 3: Hypothesis

A hypothesis is a proposed, testable explanation — an educated guess based on prior knowledge. It is written as "If [cause], then [effect]."

Example: "If plants receive more light, then they will grow taller, because light is needed for photosynthesis, which provides energy for growth."

Step 4: Deductions

Deductions are logical predictions that follow from the hypothesis: "If the hypothesis is true, then we should observe..."

Example: "Plants given 12 hours of light per day should be taller than plants given only 2 hours of light per day after 4 weeks."

Step 5: Experiment

A controlled procedure to test the hypothesis. Key elements:

  • Independent variable: Amount of light (2 hr, 6 hr, 12 hr per day)
  • Dependent variable: Height of plant (measured in cm)
  • Controlled variables: Same soil, water, pot size, temperature, plant species
  • Control group: Plants kept in complete darkness
  • Experimental groups: Plants given 2, 6, or 12 hours of light

Step 6: Results and Analysis

Data is collected and recorded in tables, then displayed in graphs (bar graphs, line graphs) for easy analysis. Patterns and trends are identified.

Step 7: Conclusion

The conclusion states whether the hypothesis was supported or rejected based on the data.

Example: "The results support the hypothesis. Plants given 12 hours of light grew an average of 18 cm, while plants given 2 hours grew only 5 cm. Light promotes plant growth."

Step 8: Theory and Law

If the hypothesis is supported by many independent experiments over time, it may be elevated to a theory (broad explanation) and eventually a scientific law (describes what always happens under given conditions).

Key Point

A rejected hypothesis is not a failure. It eliminates one possible explanation and guides future investigation.

Q2. Describe the levels of biological organisation from atom to biosphere. Explain the concept of emergent properties with examples.

Levels of Biological Organisation

Life is organised in a precise, hierarchical manner. There are 12 levels, from the simplest (atom) to the most complex (biosphere).

1. Atom: The simplest level. The most important atoms for life are Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen, Nitrogen, Phosphorus, and Sulfur — remembered as CHONPS. Carbon forms the backbone of all organic molecules; oxygen and hydrogen make water; nitrogen is in DNA and proteins.

2. Molecule: Two or more atoms bonded together. Biological molecules include water (H₂O), glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆), DNA, and proteins.

3. Organelle: Specialised structures within cells. The nucleus controls cell activities; mitochondria produce energy (ATP); chloroplasts (in plants) carry out photosynthesis; ribosomes make proteins.

4. Cell: The basic unit of life. The smallest unit considered to be alive. Can be unicellular (bacteria, amoeba) or part of a multicellular organism (humans have ~37 trillion cells).

5. Tissue: A group of similar cells performing a specific function. Examples: muscle tissue (movement), nervous tissue (signal transmission), epithelial tissue (covering surfaces), connective tissue (support).

6. Organ: Two or more tissue types working together. Examples: the heart (cardiac + connective + nervous tissue → pumps blood), stomach (muscle + epithelial + nervous tissue → digests food).

7. Organ System: A group of organs performing a major body function. Examples: the circulatory system (heart + blood vessels + blood → transports materials); the digestive system (mouth, stomach, intestines → digests food).

8. Organism: A complete, independent living thing (a human, a frog, a tree).

9. Population: All individuals of the same species living in the same area at the same time (e.g., all lions in the Serengeti).

10. Community: All populations of different species living in the same area and interacting (e.g., all plants, animals, fungi, and bacteria in a forest).

11. Ecosystem: A community plus its abiotic (non-living) environment — soil, water, air, sunlight, temperature (e.g., a coral reef ecosystem).

12. Biosphere: The highest level — all ecosystems on Earth combined. The thin layer of the planet where life exists, from the deep ocean floor to high in the atmosphere.

Emergent Properties

At each level, new properties appear that did not exist at the level below. These are called emergent properties — the whole is greater than the sum of its parts.

  • A single water molecule (H₂O) is not wet. Billions of water molecules together are wet. Wetness is an emergent property.
  • A single neuron cannot think. Billions of neurons in the brain produce consciousness. Consciousness is an emergent property.
  • A single heart cell can beat rhythmically. But the whole heart can pump blood throughout the body. Pumping is an emergent property.

This concept explains why biologists must study life at all levels of organisation, not just one.

Q3. Write a detailed note on the contributions of major scientists to the development of biology.

Contributions of Scientists to Biology

1. Aristotle (384–322 BC) — Father of Biology:
Aristotle was the first scientist to study and classify animals systematically. He wrote Historia Animalium (History of Animals) in which he described and classified over 500 species. He also distinguished between plants and animals and studied marine life. His work laid the foundation of zoology, botany, and comparative anatomy.

2. Theophrastus (371–287 BC) — Father of Botany:
A student of Aristotle, Theophrastus wrote detailed works on the structure, growth, and classification of plants. He described over 500 plant species in his books Historia Plantarum (History of Plants) and De Causis Plantarum (The Causes of Plants).

3. Ibn Sina / Avicenna (980–1037 AD):
A Muslim scientist, physician, and philosopher, Ibn Sina wrote the Canon of Medicine (Al-Qanun fi al-Tibb) — a comprehensive encyclopedia covering anatomy, physiology, and disease. His classification of diseases and approach to medicine were used in European universities for over 600 years.

4. Robert Hooke (1665):
Robert Hooke discovered the cell in 1665 while examining thin slices of cork under a primitive microscope. He named them "cells" because they reminded him of monks' rooms (called cellulae in Latin). This discovery was crucial because it eventually led to the development of Cell Theory.

5. Carolus Linnaeus (1707–1778) — Father of Modern Taxonomy:
Linnaeus developed the binomial nomenclature system — the two-name system for naming organisms using genus and species (e.g., Homo sapiens). He classified thousands of plants and animals in his work Systema Naturae, providing a universal language for biology.

6. Charles Darwin (1809–1882):
Darwin proposed the Theory of Evolution by Natural Selection in his landmark book On the Origin of Species (1859). He showed that all species descended from common ancestors and evolved over millions of years through natural selection — those organisms best adapted to their environment survive and reproduce.

7. Gregor Mendel (1822–1884) — Father of Genetics:
Mendel discovered the laws of inheritance through 8 years of experiments on pea plants in his monastery garden. He identified how traits (like seed colour and shape) are passed from parents to offspring through what we now call genes. His work was neglected until 1900, when it was rediscovered and formed the foundation of the science of genetics.

8. Watson and Crick (1953):
James Watson and Francis Crick, using X-ray diffraction data produced by Rosalind Franklin, discovered the double helix structure of DNA in 1953. This was one of the greatest scientific discoveries of the 20th century. Understanding DNA's structure revealed how genetic information is stored, copied, and transmitted — forming the basis of modern molecular biology and genetic engineering.

Q4. Explain the relationship between biology and other sciences. How have these connections led to new fields of study?

Biology and Other Sciences

Biology is an interdisciplinary science — it overlaps and combines with many other branches of science to answer complex biological questions. This interconnection has given rise to entirely new scientific fields.

1. Biology + Chemistry → Biochemistry:
Biochemistry studies the chemical processes and substances within living organisms. It investigates the structure and function of biological molecules (proteins, carbohydrates, lipids, nucleic acids), how enzymes catalyse reactions, cellular metabolism, and how DNA carries genetic information. Applications include drug development, understanding diseases at the molecular level, and food science.

2. Biology + Physics → Biophysics:
Biophysics applies the principles and methods of physics to understand biological systems. It studies how muscles generate mechanical force (biomechanics), how electrical signals travel along nerve cells (bioelectricity), how X-rays and MRI machines image the body, and how light is absorbed during photosynthesis. Applications include medical imaging technology and the design of prosthetic limbs.

3. Biology + Mathematics/Computer Science → Biomathematics and Bioinformatics:
Biomathematics uses mathematical models to understand biological processes such as population growth and disease spread (epidemiology). Bioinformatics uses computers and statistics to analyse large biological datasets — especially genomic data. The Human Genome Project (completed 2003), which sequenced all 3 billion base pairs of human DNA, was only possible using advanced computing and mathematics.

4. Biology + Geography → Biogeography:
Biogeography studies the geographical distribution of organisms — where species live, why they live there, and how they got there. It combines biology with geology and climatology to explain patterns of biodiversity. Example: Why kangaroos are only found in Australia, or why island species are often unique.

5. Biology + Social Sciences → Social Biology:
Social Biology studies the biological basis of social behaviour — why organisms live in groups, how they communicate, cooperate, and compete. It bridges biology, psychology, and sociology. Examples: why bees live in colonies, how wolves organise into packs, and what biological factors influence human emotions and social bonding.

Conclusion: No single science is sufficient to answer the complex questions of modern biology. The interdisciplinary nature of biology is one of its greatest strengths, enabling breakthroughs in medicine, agriculture, environmental science, and technology.
