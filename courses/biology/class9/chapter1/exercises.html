Complete solved exercises from the Sindh Textbook Board Chapter 1 — Introduction to Biology. All six sections covered for exam preparation.

Section 1 — Encircle the Correct Answer (MCQs)

(i) A localized group of organisms that belong to the same species is called a:
A. Biosphere
B. Community
C. Ecosystem
D. Population
A population is all organisms of the same species living in a specific area. A community has different species; a biosphere is all life on Earth.
(ii) Increased quantity and quality of fish production:
A. Fisheries
B. Farming
C. Animal husbandry
D. Forestry
Fisheries is the branch concerned with improving the quantity and quality of fish production through scientific methods.
(iii) Study of remote past organic life, with the help of fossils:
A. Entomology
B. Paleontology
C. Taxonomy
D. Histology
Paleontology studies past life using fossils. Entomology = insects; Taxonomy = classification; Histology = tissues.
(iv) Laws and techniques of physics are applied to explain the living processes of life:
A. Biometry
B. Biostatistics
C. Biophysics
D. Bio-economics
Biophysics applies the laws and techniques of physics to understand biological processes such as nerve impulse transmission and muscle contraction.
(v) Choose the incorrect statement:
A. Six elements such as C, H, O, N, S and P are called basic elements of life.
B. Foundation of life is based on chemicals.
C. Members of different species form a population.
D. Part of Earth where life exists is called biosphere.
Statement (C) is incorrect. A population consists of members of the same species, not different species. Members of different species form a community.
(vi) Science of diagnosis and treatment of diseases:
A. Agriculture
B. Medicine
C. Surgery
D. Both B and C
Medicine deals with diagnosis and non-surgical treatment; Surgery treats diseases through operations. Together, both cover diagnosis and treatment.
(vii) Similar cells combine together to form:
A. Organs
B. System
C. Tissue
D. Body
Similar cells → Tissue → Organ → Organ System → Organism. This is the cellular level of organisation.
(viii) Scientific name of frog is:
A. Palaeon
B. Rana tigrina
C. Periplaneta
D. Pheretima
Rana tigrina is the spotted frog. Periplaneta = cockroach; Pheretima = earthworm.
(ix) Select the correct sequence of biological organisation:
A. Atom → Cell → Tissue → Molecule → Organ
B. Atom → Tissue → Cell → Molecule → Organ
C. Atom → Molecule → Cell → Tissue → Organ
D. Atom → Cell → Molecule → Tissue → Organ
Correct sequence: Atom → Molecule → Cell → Tissue → Organ → Organ System → Organism. Atoms first join to make molecules, then cells, then tissues, then organs.
(x) Volvox is a polyphyletic genus of:
A. Green algae
B. Red algae
C. Brown algae
D. None of these
Volvox belongs to green algae (Chlorophyta). It is an example of colonial organisation — many green algal cells living together in a hollow sphere.

Section 2 — Fill in the Blanks

(i) Techniques for manipulation of gene to achieve desirable characters are called ____________.

Genetic Engineering. It involves adding, removing, or modifying genes in an organism's DNA to produce useful traits (e.g. disease-resistant crops).

(ii) Distribution of different living organisms in different regions of the world ____________.

Biogeography. It studies why and how organisms are distributed across different geographical regions of the Earth.

(iii) Part of agriculture for the development of new varieties of plant, and their fruit is ____________.

Horticulture. It is the branch of agriculture that deals with growing fruits, vegetables, flowers, and developing improved plant varieties.

(iv) Bio elements considered as vital for life are __________ in number.

16. There are 16 bio-elements; of these, six are considered basic/essential: Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O), Nitrogen (N), Sulphur (S), and Phosphorus (P).

(v) Members of different species living in specific habitat are called __________.

Community. A community is all the different species (populations) living together in the same habitat or area.

(vi) The Muslim scientist who identified many diseases like tuberculosis, meningitis and other such inflammations was _______.

Bu Ali Sina (Avicenna) — 980–1037 AD. He was the greatest Muslim scientist in the field of medicine and authored Al-Qanoon and Al-Shifa.

(vii) Part of Earth where life exists is called ______.

Biosphere. The biosphere includes all regions of Earth's surface, water, and atmosphere where living organisms are found.

(viii) Foundation of life is based on ________.

Chemicals (Protoplasm). All life processes are chemical in nature. Protoplasm — the living substance inside cells — is the chemical basis of life.

(ix) Fish is one of the best sources of _________.

Protein. Fish is a rich source of high-quality proteins, essential fatty acids, and minerals, making it a valuable food for human health.

(x) Radio labelling and carbon dating also show some uses of radioactive isotopes in determining the ________ of fossils.

Age. Radioactive carbon-14 dating is used in paleontology to determine how old fossils and ancient remains are.

Section 3 — Define the Following Terms

(i) Anatomy

Anatomy is the branch of biology that deals with the study of the internal structure of organisms by means of cutting and dissection. For example, studying the internal organs of a frog after dissecting it.

(ii) Histology

Histology is the study of the microscopic structure of tissues. It requires a microscope to examine thin slices of tissue. For example, studying the structure of blood or muscle tissue under a microscope.

(iii) Immunology

Immunology is the branch that deals with the study of the immune system — how the body defends itself against diseases, germs, and foreign substances. It also covers vaccines and allergies.

(iv) Pharmacology

Pharmacology is the study of drugs — their sources, compositions, effects on the body, and uses in treating diseases. It connects biology, chemistry, and medicine.

(v) Entomology

Entomology is the branch of zoology that deals with the study of insects. It includes their structure, behaviour, life cycles, and role in the ecosystem (e.g. bees, mosquitoes, locusts).

(vi) Biometry

Biometry (or Biostatistics) is the application of mathematical and statistical methods to biological data. It helps biologists analyse experimental results and draw conclusions.

(vii) Biogeography

Biogeography is the study of the geographical distribution of organisms across the world — where different species live, why they are found there, and how they spread.

(viii) Surgery

Surgery is the branch of medicine concerned with treating diseases, injuries, or deformities through operations. A surgeon operates on a patient's body to remove, repair, or replace tissues and organs.

(ix) Animal Husbandry

Animal Husbandry is the branch of agriculture that deals with the scientific breeding, feeding, and caring of farm animals (cattle, goats, sheep, poultry) to increase the production of meat, milk, eggs, and wool.

(x) Bioelements

Bioelements are the chemical elements that make up living organisms. There are 16 bioelements in total. The six most important (basic) ones are: C, H, O, N, S, P (Carbon, Hydrogen, Oxygen, Nitrogen, Sulphur, Phosphorus).

Section 5 — Short Answers

(i) Why is the subject Biology named a multidimensional subject?

Biology is called a multidimensional subject because it covers many different aspects and branches of life:

  • It studies organisms at different levels — from atoms and molecules to ecosystems.
  • It has many branches (Zoology, Botany, Microbiology, Genetics, Physiology, etc.).
  • It overlaps with other sciences — Chemistry (Biochemistry), Physics (Biophysics), Mathematics (Biometry), Social sciences (Medicine, Agriculture).
  • It applies to fields like health, food, environment, industry, and technology.

Because it covers so many dimensions of life and connects to so many other fields, Biology is rightly called multidimensional.

(ii) How does the farming profession help mankind?

Farming (Agriculture) helps mankind in many ways:

  • Food supply: It provides grains, vegetables, and fruits that feed billions of people.
  • Animal products: Animal husbandry provides milk, meat, eggs, and wool.
  • Raw materials: Crops like cotton provide raw material for industries.
  • Employment: It provides livelihoods to a large part of the population.
  • Economy: Agricultural exports earn foreign exchange for the country.
  • Fisheries and forestry: Provide fish (protein) and timber/fuel.
(iii) Why is species called the smallest taxonomic level?

A species is the smallest (most basic) unit in taxonomy because:

  • Members of a species look alike, have the same body structure and behaviour.
  • They can interbreed naturally and produce fertile offspring.
  • They cannot successfully interbreed with members of a different species.

All higher taxonomic levels (genus, family, order, class, phylum, kingdom) are made up of groups of species. Since species cannot be divided into any smaller biological unit that still reproduces naturally, it is the smallest taxonomic level.

(iv) How is population different from community?
  • Population: All members of the same species living in a specific area at the same time. Example: all crows in a park.
  • Community: All members of different species living in the same habitat. Example: crows + sparrows + parrots + lizards in the same park.

In short: Population = one species; Community = many species in the same place.

(v) How are new varieties of plants produced?

New varieties of plants are produced through Horticulture and Genetic Engineering using these methods:

  • Selective breeding (hybridisation): Cross-pollinating two varieties with desirable traits to produce a better hybrid variety.
  • Genetic engineering: Inserting or modifying genes to give the plant resistance to disease, drought, or pests.
  • Tissue culture: Growing new plants from cells in a lab to produce disease-free, uniform plants quickly.
  • Mutation breeding: Using radiation or chemicals to cause mutations and selecting useful ones.

Section 6 — Detailed Answers

(i) Describe the role of Muslim scientists in the field of biology.

Muslim scientists made great contributions to biology especially during the golden age of Islam (8th–11th century AD):

1. Jabir Bin Hayan (722–817 AD):

  • Born in Iran; known in the West as "Geber."
  • Worked mainly in chemistry but also contributed to biology.
  • Wrote books on plants (Al-Nabatiat) and animals (Al-Haywan).
  • Used experimental methods in his work.

2. Abdul Malik Asmai (741–828 AD):

  • A great Arab zoologist and linguist.
  • Wrote separate books on horse, camel, sheep, and wild animals.
  • Also wrote Khalqul Insan — a detailed study of the human body.
  • His work laid the foundation for zoology in the Arab world.

3. Bu Ali Sina / Avicenna (980–1037 AD):

  • Born in Bukhara (Central Asia); greatest Muslim physician and scientist.
  • Identified and described diseases such as tuberculosis, meningitis, and other inflammations.
  • Also worked in mathematics, astronomy, physics, and paleontology.
  • His two famous works: Al-Qanoon fit-Tib (Canon of Medicine) and Al-Shifa (The Book of Healing).
  • Al-Qanoon was used as a medical textbook in European universities for centuries.

These scientists used observation, experimentation, and writing to advance biological knowledge at a time when Europe was in the Dark Ages. Their work influenced both Eastern and Western science.

(ii) Describe the relationships of biology to other sciences.

Biology is related to many other sciences. This shows that it is a multidimensional subject:

1. Biology and Chemistry → Biochemistry:
Chemistry helps explain the chemical reactions in living organisms. Biochemistry studies molecules like proteins, carbohydrates, lipids, and DNA. Example: How glucose is broken down in cells to release energy.

2. Biology and Physics → Biophysics:
Biophysics applies the laws of physics to living processes. Example: How muscles contract, how nerve impulses travel, how X-rays are used in medicine.

3. Biology and Mathematics → Biometry (Biostatistics):
Mathematical methods are used to analyse biological data, conduct experiments, and calculate probabilities. Example: Calculating growth rates of a population.

4. Biology and Social Sciences → Agriculture, Medicine, Pharmacy:
Biology is the basis of agriculture (growing crops), medicine (treating patients), and pharmacology (making drugs). These fields directly serve human society.

5. Biology and Environmental Sciences:
Ecology, which is a part of biology, is deeply connected to environmental science. Understanding ecosystems helps in solving problems like pollution, deforestation, and climate change.

6. Biology and Technology → Biotechnology, Genetic Engineering:
Modern technology combined with biology gives rise to biotechnology, which is used to develop vaccines, improve crops, and treat genetic diseases.

Conclusion: Biology does not exist in isolation. Its connection to other sciences makes it one of the most important and versatile subjects in modern education and research.

(iii) Describe the levels of organisation.

The living world is organised from the simplest level to the most complex. These are called levels of organisation:

1. Atomic Level:
The most basic level. All matter is made of atoms. In biology, there are 16 bio-elements (such as C, H, O, N, S, P) that make up living organisms.

2. Molecular Level:
Atoms combine to form molecules. Small molecules (micro-molecules) include glucose and amino acids. Large molecules (macro-molecules) include proteins, carbohydrates, fats, and nucleic acids.

3. Cellular Level:
Molecules form protoplasm (the living material). Protoplasm is enclosed in a cell — the basic unit of life. Similar cells form a tissue → Tissues form an organ → Organs form an organ system → Organ systems form a multicellular organism.

4. Taxonomic Level:
A species is a group of similar organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring. It is the smallest taxonomic unit.

5. Population:
All members of the same species in a specific area form a population. Example: all frogs in a pond.

6. Community:
Different species (populations) living together in the same habitat form a community. Example: frogs + fish + water plants in a pond.

7. Ecosystem:
A community together with its non-living (abiotic) environment (water, soil, air, sunlight) forms an ecosystem. Example: a pond ecosystem or a forest ecosystem.

8. Biosphere:
The highest level — the part of Earth where life exists. It includes all ecosystems on land, in water, and in the air.

Summary: Atom → Molecule → Cell → Tissue → Organ → Organ System → Organism → Population → Community → Ecosystem → Biosphere.
