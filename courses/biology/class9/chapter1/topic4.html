Chapter 1 · Topic 4

Levels of Biological Organisation

Life is organised in levels — from the smallest atom to the entire biosphere. Each level is made up of units from the level below it, and together they form the next level above. Understanding these levels is essential for all of biology.

4.1 The Concept of Organisation

One of the most remarkable features of living things is their highly organised structure. Unlike non-living matter, life is not random — it is arranged in a precise, hierarchical (step-by-step) manner from the simplest chemical level all the way up to the entire living world.

This organisation allows life to function efficiently. Each level has its own unique properties that the level below it does not have — this is called emergent properties. For example, a single heart cell can beat rhythmically, but it cannot pump blood around the body — you need the whole heart (organ level) for that.

4.3 Detailed Explanation of Each Level

Level 1 — Atom

The atom is the simplest level of organisation. Atoms are the smallest units of matter that retain chemical properties. The atoms most important to life are: Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H), Oxygen (O), Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), and Sulfur (S) — remembered as CHONPS.

  • Carbon is the backbone of all organic molecules in living things
  • Oxygen and hydrogen make up water, which is essential for life
  • Nitrogen is found in amino acids (proteins) and DNA

Level 2 — Molecule

When two or more atoms bond together, they form a molecule. Biological molecules (biomolecules) are the chemical building blocks of life:

  • Water (H₂O) — essential for all life processes
  • Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) — the main energy source for cells
  • DNA — stores genetic information
  • Proteins — carry out most functions in the cell (enzymes, structural proteins, antibodies)

Level 3 — Organelle

Organelles are specialised structures inside a cell, each performing a specific job. "Organelle" means "little organ". Examples:

  • Nucleus — controls cell activities, contains DNA
  • Mitochondria — produces energy (ATP) through respiration
  • Chloroplast — site of photosynthesis (in plant cells)
  • Ribosome — makes proteins

Level 4 — Cell

The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of all living things. It is the smallest unit that is considered to be alive. Every living organism is made of one or more cells.

  • Unicellular organisms: made of one cell only (e.g., bacteria, amoeba, paramecium)
  • Multicellular organisms: made of many cells (e.g., humans have ~37 trillion cells)
Cell Theory

1. All living things are made of cells.   2. The cell is the basic unit of life.   3. All cells come from pre-existing cells.

Level 5 — Tissue

A tissue is a group of similar cells working together to perform a specific function. In multicellular organisms, cells are organised into tissues.

  • Muscle tissue — enables movement (made of muscle cells)
  • Nervous tissue — transmits nerve signals (made of neurons)
  • Epithelial tissue — covers surfaces, lines organs (e.g., skin cells)
  • Connective tissue — supports and connects other tissues (blood, bone, cartilage)

Level 6 — Organ

An organ is made of two or more different types of tissues working together to perform a specific function.

  • Heart — made of cardiac muscle tissue, connective tissue, and nervous tissue → pumps blood
  • Stomach — made of muscle tissue, epithelial tissue, nervous tissue → digests food
  • Leaf (plant) — made of photosynthetic tissue, vascular tissue → makes food

Level 7 — Organ System

An organ system is a group of organs that work together to carry out a major function. The human body has 11 major organ systems:

Digestive System
Organs: mouth, oesophagus, stomach, intestines, liver · Function: digests food and absorbs nutrients
Circulatory System
Organs: heart, blood vessels, blood · Function: transports materials around the body
Respiratory System
Organs: lungs, trachea, bronchi · Function: gas exchange (O₂ in, CO₂ out)
Nervous System
Organs: brain, spinal cord, nerves · Function: controls and coordinates body activities
Skeletal System
Organs: bones, cartilage · Function: support, protection, movement
Excretory System
Organs: kidneys, skin, lungs · Function: removes metabolic waste
Reproductive System
Organs: testes/ovaries, uterus · Function: produces offspring

Level 8 — Organism

An organism is a complete, independent living thing. All its organ systems work together to keep it alive. Examples: a single human, a single oak tree, a single bacterium, a single frog.

Level 9 — Population

A population is a group of individuals of the same species living in the same area at the same time and able to interbreed.

  • All the lions living in the Gir Forest, India = a population
  • All humans currently living on Earth = the human population (≈8 billion)
  • All the mango trees in a particular orchard = a population

Level 10 — Community (Biotic Community)

A community is all the populations of different species living in the same area and interacting with each other.

  • All the plants, animals, fungi, and bacteria living in a pond = a pond community
  • In a forest community: trees, birds, insects, fungi, deer, wolves — all interact

Level 11 — Ecosystem

An ecosystem is a community of organisms plus their non-living (abiotic) environment — soil, water, air, sunlight, temperature. All components interact with each other.

  • A forest ecosystem: trees, animals, soil, rainfall, sunlight, decomposers
  • A marine ecosystem: fish, coral, plankton, seawater, currents, sunlight
  • A desert ecosystem: cacti, snakes, sand, scarce rainfall, intense sunlight

Level 12 — Biosphere

The biosphere is the highest level of organisation — it includes all ecosystems on Earth combined. It is the thin layer of Earth where life exists — from the deep ocean floor to high in the atmosphere.

  • The biosphere extends about 10 km upward into the atmosphere
  • And about 8 km downward into the ocean
  • It is a closed system — the same matter is recycled endlessly through all living things

4.4 Emergent Properties

At each level, new properties appear that did not exist at the lower level. These are called emergent properties.

  • A single water molecule (H₂O) is not wet. But a collection of many water molecules is wet. Wetness is an emergent property.
  • A single neuron (nerve cell) cannot think. But billions of neurons in the brain can. Consciousness is an emergent property.
  • A single heart cell can beat. But a whole heart can pump blood throughout the body. Pumping is an emergent property.
Key Point

The whole is greater than the sum of its parts. Each level of biological organisation has properties that its component parts do not have. This is one of the most fascinating aspects of biology.
