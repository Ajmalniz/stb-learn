Divisions and Branches of Biology

Life is huge — no one person can study everything. So biology is split into divisions (big groups) and branches (special subjects). Learn the names your textbook uses — they often come in exams.

2.1 Three Major Divisions of Biology

These are the main divisions based on what kind of living thing we study:

(i) Zoology
Word: Greek Zoon = animals, Logos = study.
Meaning: Study of animals.
Example: How a fish breathes in water, how a mosquito spreads disease, how a cow digests grass.
(ii) Botany
Word: Greek Butane = plants, Logos = study.
Meaning: Study of plants.
Example: How wheat grows, why leaves are green, how roses reproduce.
(iii) Microbiology
Meaning: Study of microscopic organisms — so small we need a microscope to see them (e.g. many bacteria).
Example: Bacteria in spoiled milk, germs that cause infection, yeast in bread.

2.2 Important Branches of Biology

Modern biology also has special branches. Each branch focuses on one part of life — shape, function, genes, environment, etc.

Below are the branches from your STB book, in simple words with a real-life example each.

(i) Morphology
Study of external form and structure of organisms.
Example: Shape of leaves on a neem tree, wings of a butterfly.
(ii) Anatomy
Study of internal parts by opening the body (dissection).
Example: Heart and lungs inside a frog in the lab.
(iii) Cell Biology
Study of the cell and its organelles (tiny parts inside the cell).
Example: Nucleus and mitochondria seen under a microscope.
(iv) Histology
Study of tissues (groups of similar cells) in plants and animals.
Example: Muscle tissue that helps you move your arm.
(v) Physiology
Study of functions of living organisms — how parts work.
Example: How your kidneys filter blood and make urine.
(vi) Taxonomy
Rules for naming, grouping, and classifying organisms.
Example: Scientific name of frog: Rana tigrina; mustard: Brassica campestris.
(vii) Genetics
Study of heredity — how characters pass from parents to children.
Example: Eye colour, blood group, height running in a family.
(viii) Developmental Biology
Study of formation and development of embryo.
Example: How a fertilised egg becomes a baby chick inside the egg.
(ix) Environmental Biology
Study of relationship between living things and non-living factors (air, water, soil) and their effects on each other.
Example: Fish dying when factory waste pollutes a river.
(x) Paleontology
Study of ancient life using fossils.
Example: Bones of dinosaurs found in rocks.
(xi) Biotechnology
Use of techniques to change genes to get useful characters.
Example: Bacteria made to produce human insulin for diabetic patients.
(xii) Socio-biology
Study of social behaviour of organisms — how they interact.
Example: Ants working together in a colony; bees in a hive.
(xiii) Parasitology
Study of parasites — organisms that live on or inside another and harm it.
Example: Tapeworm in intestine; lice in hair.
(xiv) Pharmacology
Study of action of drugs on the body.
Example: How paracetamol reduces fever; how antibiotics kill bacteria.
(xv) Molecular Biology
Study of organic molecules that make cells and organelles.
Example: DNA, proteins, and fats inside a cell.
Exam tip

Division = Zoology, Botany, Microbiology (3 only).
Branch = Morphology, Anatomy, Genetics, etc. (many special subjects).
