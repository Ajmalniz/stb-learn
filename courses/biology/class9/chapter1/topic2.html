Branches of Biology

Because life is so vast and complex, biologists specialise in different areas. Each branch focuses on a specific aspect of living things — from the tiniest virus to the entire biosphere.

2.1 Why Are There So Many Branches?

Living organisms range from microscopic bacteria to 100-metre-tall trees and 30-metre-long whales. No single scientist could study all of this. So biology is divided into specialised branches, each dealing with a specific group of organisms or a specific aspect of life.

Currently, biologists have identified about 8.7 million species on Earth. The branches of biology help us organise and study this enormous diversity.

2.2 Main Branches — Based on Type of Organism

These branches are defined by what type of living thing they study:

Zoology
The study of animals — their structure, behaviour, physiology, classification, and distribution. Example: studying the migration patterns of birds.
Botany
The study of plants — their structure, growth, reproduction, metabolism, diseases, and uses. Example: studying how a plant responds to drought.
Microbiology
The study of microorganisms — organisms too small to see with the naked eye, including bacteria, viruses, fungi, algae, and protozoa. Example: studying E. coli bacteria.
Virology
The study of viruses — their structure, classification, and the diseases they cause. Example: studying the COVID-19 virus.
Bacteriology
The study of bacteria — their biology, classification, and role in disease and the environment.
Mycology
The study of fungi — including mushrooms, moulds, and yeasts. Fungi are important decomposers and have many uses in food and medicine (e.g., penicillin from Penicillium mould).
Phycology (Algology)
The study of algae — simple photosynthetic organisms found in water. Important as the base of aquatic food chains.
Parasitology
The study of parasites — organisms that live on or inside another organism (the host) and cause harm. Example: studying the malaria parasite (Plasmodium).
Entomology
The study of insects. Insects are the most numerous group of animals. Important for pest control, pollination research, and forensic investigations.
Ichthyology
The study of fish — their diversity, behaviour, ecology, and use to humans.
Ornithology
The study of birds — their anatomy, physiology, behaviour, migration, and ecology.
Herpetology
The study of reptiles and amphibians — such as snakes, lizards, frogs, and turtles.
Mammalogy
The study of mammals — warm-blooded vertebrates that feed their young on milk.

2.3 Branches Based on Structural Level or Function

These branches study how living things are built or how they work:

Cell Biology (Cytology)
The study of the cell — its structure, function, and life processes. Deals with organelles, cell division, and cell metabolism.
Histology
The study of tissues — groups of similar cells. Uses microscopes to examine thin slices of tissue.
Anatomy
The study of the structure of organisms — their organs, organ systems, and overall body plan. Usually involves dissection.
Physiology
The study of how living things and their parts function — how they work. Example: how the heart pumps blood, how kidneys filter urine.
Morphology
The study of the external form and structure of organisms — their shape, size, colour, and physical characteristics.
Embryology
The study of the development of organisms from fertilised egg (zygote) to birth/hatching. Studies how a single cell becomes a complex organism.
Genetics
The study of heredity and variation — how traits are passed from parents to offspring through genes and chromosomes.
Molecular Biology
Studies biological processes at the molecular level — focusing on DNA, RNA, and proteins and how they control life processes.

2.4 Branches Based on Environment and Ecology

Ecology
The study of the relationships between organisms and their environment — both living (biotic) and non-living (abiotic) factors.
Environmental Biology
Studies the impact of human activities on the environment — pollution, climate change, deforestation, and species loss.
Marine Biology
The study of organisms living in sea water — from microscopic plankton to the blue whale.
Freshwater Biology (Limnology)
The study of organisms in fresh water — rivers, lakes, ponds, and streams.
Biogeography
Studies the geographical distribution of organisms — why certain species live in certain places and how they got there.

2.5 Applied Branches of Biology

These branches use biological knowledge to solve practical problems:

Biotechnology
Uses living organisms or their products for practical applications — medicines (insulin), genetically modified crops, biofuels, and industrial enzymes.
Pharmacology
The study of drugs and medicines — how they affect the body, their dosages, and side effects.
Immunology
The study of the immune system — how the body defends itself against disease, and how vaccines work.
Neurobiology
The study of the nervous system — the brain, spinal cord, and nerves, and how they control behaviour.
Taxonomy (Systematics)
The branch that deals with the identification, naming, and classification of organisms. Provides the scientific names used by biologists worldwide.
Palaeontology
The study of fossils — preserved remains or traces of organisms that lived millions of years ago. Helps us understand the history of life on Earth.
Social Biology
Studies the social behaviour of organisms — how they interact in groups, form societies, and cooperate or compete.
Human Biology
Specifically focused on the human body — its structure, function, development, health, and disease.
