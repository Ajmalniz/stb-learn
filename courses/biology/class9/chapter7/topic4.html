Chapter 7 · Topic 4

Respiration & Anaerobic Respiration

Plants make food in photosynthesis — but all living cells (plants and animals) need to break that food down to release energy. This is respiration. We'll cover what respiration is, why it's different from breathing, and the type that happens without oxygen.

4.1 What is Respiration?

Respiration
The breakdown of food molecules to release energy inside cells.

The main food broken down is sugar — glucose. Cells use oxygen to oxidise the glucose, producing CO₂ and water as waste, and energy in the form of ATP.

The overall equation:

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂  →  6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP
Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon dioxide + Water + Energy

4.2 Respiration vs Breathing — Not the Same Thing

People often mix up these words, but they mean different things:

Feature Breathing (Ventilation) Respiration
What it isMovement of air in and out of the organismA chemical reaction inside cells to release energy
Where it happensLungs (or gills, etc.)Inside cells (cytosol + mitochondria)
PurposeSupply O₂ and remove CO₂Release energy stored in food

So breathing, gaseous exchange and respiration are different but linked — together they make energy production possible at the cellular level.

4.3 Two Types of Respiration

Living organisms have evolved two ways to release energy from food:

  • Anaerobic respiration (fermentation) — without O₂
  • Aerobic respiration — with O₂ (covered in Topic 5)

4.4 Anaerobic Respiration (Fermentation)

Anaerobic Respiration
The primitive type of respiration that takes place without oxygen. Also called fermentation.

Key facts:

  • It evolved on early Earth when there was no oxygen.
  • Glucose is not broken down completely, so much less energy is released.
  • Yields only 5–10% of the energy compared to aerobic respiration.
  • But it lets organisms survive when O₂ is unavailable.
  • Found in some bacteria, fungi, endoparasites, and sometimes animals.

There are two types of fermentation:

4.5 (i) Alcoholic Fermentation

Happens in many bacteria and fungi (especially yeast) when oxygen is not available.

C₆H₁₂O₆  →  2 C₂H₅OH + 2 CO₂ + Some ATP
Glucose → Ethanol + Carbon dioxide + Some energy

The products are ethyl alcohol (ethanol) and CO₂.

4.6 (ii) Acidic Fermentation (Lactic Acid Fermentation)

In animals (including humans), when aerobic respiration cannot supply enough energy — e.g. during a hard sprint — muscles switch to anaerobic respiration.

C₆H₁₂O₆  →  2 C₃H₆O₃ + Some energy
Glucose → Lactic acid + Some energy
  • The product is lactic acid.
  • Energy released is limited but enough to power muscles during a short sprint.
  • The lactic acid builds up in the muscles and blood stream, causing the painful feeling we call muscle fatigue.
Real-life example

When an athlete sprints at full speed for the first few seconds, oxygen cannot reach the muscles fast enough. They switch to lactic-acid fermentation — that burning feeling in your legs after a hard sprint is the lactic acid!

4.7 Importance of Anaerobic Respiration

Anaerobic respiration is an emergency power supply. It also has many commercial uses:

Use How it works
Survival without O₂Lets bacteria, fungi and some parasites live in oxygen-free environments
Short bursts of energyPowers sprinting muscles when aerobic respiration cannot keep up
VinegarOrganic acids (vinegars) made commercially by acidic fermentation
Alcoholic drinksBeer, wine and other beverages — made by alcoholic fermentation of yeast
Baking industryCO₂ from yeast gives cakes, bread and pizza their fluffy soft texture; starch breaks down into simple sugars
