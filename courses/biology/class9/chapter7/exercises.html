Section 1 — Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

Encircle the correct answer. Correct option is marked in green.

(i) In an oxidation process 14135 kJ of energy is released. How many moles of glucose are consumed?
A. 1
B. 3
C. 5
D. 10
Each mole of glucose releases 2827 kJ when fully oxidised. So: 14135 ÷ 2827 = 5 moles of glucose.
(ii) The stage of aerobic respiration that takes place at the cristae of mitochondria is:
A. Electron transport chain
B. Glycolysis
C. Krebs cycle
D. C₃ cycle
The electron transport chain happens on the cristae (inner membrane) of mitochondria. Glycolysis is in the cytosol; Krebs cycle is in the matrix; the C₃ cycle is part of photosynthesis (stroma).
(iii) In cellular respiration 180 ATP molecules are produced. How many moles of glucose are consumed?
A. 2
B. 5
C. 8
D. 10
Each mole of glucose yields 36 ATP in aerobic respiration. So: 180 ÷ 36 = 5 moles of glucose.
(iv) Loss of electron and proton is called:
A. Oxidation reaction
B. Reduction reaction
C. Redox reaction
D. I, II and III
Oxidation is the loss of electrons (e⁻) and protons (H⁺). Reduction is the opposite — gain of e⁻ and H⁺. Remember OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.
(v) Each mole of ATP stores energy:
A. 7.3 kcal/mole
B. 7.3 kJ/mole
C. 17.3 kcal/mole
D. 17.3 kJ/mole
Each mole of ATP stores about 7.3 kcal/mole of free energy in its high-energy phosphate bond. This is the energy released when ATP breaks down to ADP + P.
(vi) The fundamental molecule produced during photosynthesis is:
A. Glucose
B. Amino acid
C. Fatty acid
D. Nucleotide
The fundamental product of photosynthesis is the simple sugar glucose. Plants then use glucose to build amino acids, fats, and nucleotides.
(vii) Light-dependent reaction takes place in:
A. Stroma
B. Thylakoid
C. Cristae
D. Cisternae
The light-dependent reaction occurs on the thylakoid membrane of the chloroplast — that's where the photosystems and chlorophyll sit. The dark reaction (Calvin cycle) happens in the stroma.
(viii) The reaction in which solar energy is transferred to glucose from ATP and NADPH₂, takes place in stroma. It is called:
A. Light reaction
B. Dark reaction
C. Light reaction and dark reaction
D. Light-dependent reaction
The dark reaction (Calvin cycle) takes place in the stroma. It uses the ATP and NADPH₂ made in the light reaction to convert CO₂ into glucose. It does not need light itself.
(ix) Splitting of water in the presence of light is called:
A. Hydrolysis
B. Glycolysis
C. Photolysis
D. None of these
Photolysis = "photo" (light) + "lysis" (splitting). It is the light-driven splitting of water during the light reaction, releasing O₂, H⁺ and electrons.
(x) Splitting of glucose (glycolysis) releases a small amount of energy, which is enough to generate:
A. 2 ATP
B. 5 ATP
C. 18 ATP
D. 36 ATP
Glycolysis produces only 2 ATP per glucose. The bulk of ATP (~34) comes later from the electron transport chain. Total per glucose ≈ 36 ATP.

Section 2 — Fill in the Blanks

(i) The only source of energy on Earth is __________.

The Sun. All forms of energy on Earth — light, heat, food, fossil fuels, even hydroelectric power — can be traced back to solar energy.

(ii) Conversion of free energy into different forms by living organisms is called __________.

Bioenergetics. It is the branch of biology that studies energy transformations and transmissions in living organisms.

(iii) In living organisms, energy is stored in a special molecule called __________.

ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate). It is the universal energy currency of cells. Each mole stores about 7.3 kcal of free energy.

(iv) Plants utilise simple inorganic molecules (CO₂ and H₂O) to prepare __________.

Glucose (food). In photosynthesis, CO₂ + H₂O are combined using light energy and chlorophyll to make glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) and release oxygen.

(v) Feeding sequences and relationships are called __________.

Food chains. They show how energy flows from producers (plants) to herbivores to carnivores.

(vi) Photosynthesis is the only process which produces free O₂ by splitting __________.

Water (H₂O). During photolysis, water is split into O₂, H⁺ and electrons. The O₂ is released into the atmosphere — and is the oxygen we breathe.

(vii) Chloroplast is a double-membrane bounded organelle with a semifluid protein-containing medium called __________.

Stroma. The stroma is the gel-like substance inside the chloroplast. It contains the enzymes for the dark reaction (Calvin cycle) and surrounds the thylakoid membranes.

(viii) In chloroplast different pigments absorb light of different __________.

Wavelengths. Chlorophyll absorbs mainly violet, blue and red light and reflects green. Other pigments absorb other wavelengths, capturing a wider range of sunlight.

(ix) The breakdown of food molecules to release energy is called __________.

Respiration. It is the chemical reaction inside cells that releases energy from food and stores it in ATP.

(x) Each mole of glucose produces a maximum energy of __________.

2827 kJ/mole (or 36 ATP molecules) when oxidised completely through aerobic respiration.

Section 3 — Define the Following Terms

(i) Bioenergetics

Bioenergetics is the study of the conversion of free energy into different forms by living organisms. It deals with the energy involved in the formation and breakdown of chemical bonds in biological molecules.

(ii) Energy

Energy is the capacity to do work. According to the law of conservation of energy, energy can neither be created nor destroyed — it can only be transformed from one form to another (light, heat, chemical, mechanical, electrical, etc.).

(iii) Oxidation reaction

An oxidation reaction is a chemical reaction in which a molecule loses electrons (e⁻) and protons (H⁺). In biological systems, the lost electrons carry energy and pass it on to another molecule, which gets reduced.

(iv) Food chain

A food chain is the feeding sequence and relationship between living organisms in an ecosystem. It usually starts with a producer (plant), then a herbivore, then carnivores — showing how energy flows from one trophic level to the next.

(v) Granum

A granum (plural: grana) is a stack of disc-shaped thylakoids inside a chloroplast. The chlorophyll molecules sit on the thylakoid membrane of the grana, where they capture light during the light reaction of photosynthesis.

(vi) Photolysis

Photolysis is the splitting of water by light energy during the light reaction of photosynthesis. It produces oxygen (released into the atmosphere), hydrogen ions (H⁺) and electrons (e⁻), which are used inside the chloroplast.

(vii) Fermentation

Fermentation is another name for anaerobic respiration — the breakdown of glucose in the absence of oxygen. It releases only a small amount of energy and produces either ethanol + CO₂ (in yeast) or lactic acid (in animal muscle).

(viii) Stroma

Stroma is the semi-fluid, protein-containing medium inside the chloroplast, surrounding the thylakoid membranes. It contains the enzymes that carry out the dark reaction (Calvin cycle) of photosynthesis.

(ix) Aerobic respiration

Aerobic respiration is the type of respiration that takes place in the presence of oxygen. It breaks down glucose completely into CO₂ and H₂O, releasing the maximum amount of energy — about 36 ATP per glucose. It happens in three steps: glycolysis, Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain.

(x) Pyruvic acid

Pyruvic acid is a 3-carbon organic acid produced when a 6-carbon glucose molecule is broken down during glycolysis. One glucose yields two molecules of pyruvic acid. In aerobic respiration, pyruvic acid enters the Krebs cycle in the mitochondria.

Section 5 — Short Answer Questions

(i) How does CO₂ maintain the temperature of the Earth?

CO₂ helps maintain Earth's temperature because:

  • CO₂ has the property of absorbing heat from the sun (it is a greenhouse gas).
  • At normal levels, it traps just enough heat to keep the planet warm and habitable.
  • If CO₂ increases in the atmosphere (from deforestation, burning fossil fuels), it traps more heat → global warming.
  • Photosynthesis by plants removes CO₂ from the air, keeping concentration in balance.
  • So plants indirectly help keep Earth's temperature stable by controlling CO₂ levels.
(ii) Why is the second phase of photosynthesis called the dark reaction?

The second phase is called the dark reaction because:

  • It does not directly need light energy from the sun to take place.
  • It uses the chemical energy stored in ATP and NADPH₂ (made during the light reaction).
  • As long as ATP and NADPH₂ are available, this phase can continue — even in darkness.
  • However, in practice it also runs during the day, alongside the light reaction.
  • It is also called the Calvin cycle (or C₃ cycle) and it fixes atmospheric CO₂ into glucose.
(iii) How is respiration different from breathing?
Feature Breathing (Ventilation) Respiration
What it isMovement of air in and out of the organismChemical reaction inside cells to release energy

The two are linked — breathing supplies the O₂ that respiration needs, and removes the CO₂ that respiration produces.

(iv) Why is acidic fermentation harmful?

Acidic (lactic-acid) fermentation can be harmful because:

  • It produces lactic acid as a by-product.
  • When muscles cannot get enough O₂ during heavy exercise, they switch to lactic-acid fermentation.
  • The lactic acid builds up in the muscles and bloodstream.
  • This causes the painful, burning feeling known as muscle fatigue or muscle cramps.
  • If allowed to build up too much, it can damage muscle tissues and slow down performance.
  • It also produces only a small amount of energy (5–10% of aerobic respiration), so it cannot sustain the body for long.
(v) How does glucose form secondary products in plants?

The glucose made in photosynthesis is the raw material for almost everything in a plant:

  • Starch & polysaccharides — extra glucose is joined together and stored as starch in seeds, fruits and tubers.
  • Sucrose — used to transport sugar through the plant (from leaves to other parts via phloem).
  • Cellulose — glucose is joined into long chains to build cell walls.
  • Fats — glucose is converted into fatty acids and glycerol → fats for storage in seeds.
  • Amino acids & proteins — combined with nitrates from the soil to make amino acids; then linked into proteins.
  • Nucleic acids (DNA, RNA) — combined with nitrates and phosphates to form nucleotides → DNA / RNA.

So glucose is the central building block — plants use it to make every other biomolecule they need.

Section 6 — Long Answer Questions

(i) What is the energy currency of the cell? Describe the chemical process of energy transmission.

The energy currency of the cell is ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate). Just like money is used to pay for any kind of work in the economy, ATP is used to pay for any kind of work in the cell — muscle contraction, transport, building molecules, nerve impulses, etc.

Structure of ATP:

  • Adenine (a base) + pentose sugar = adenosine.
  • Adenosine joined to three phosphate groups by high-energy bonds.

Each mole of ATP releases 7.3 kcal of free energy when it breaks down to ADP + P.

Chemical process of energy transmission

Energy in living systems is transferred through the gain or loss of electrons during chemical bond formation and breaking. The two opposite processes involved are:

  • Oxidation — loss of electrons (e⁻) and protons (H⁺). The electrons carry energy away from the molecule. The molecule that loses them is the reducing agent.
  • Reduction — gain of e⁻ and H⁺. The molecule that gains them is the oxidising agent and stores the energy that came with the electrons.

Oxidation and reduction always happen together as a redox reaction:

Reduced A + Oxidised B → Oxidised A + Reduced B

Role of ATP in energy transmission:

  • When an oxidation reaction releases energy, that energy is used to attach a phosphate group to ADP: ADP + P + energy → ATP.
  • The energy is now stored in the high-energy phosphate bond.
  • When a cell needs to do work, ATP breaks down: ATP → ADP + P + 7.3 kcal/mole.
  • The freed energy powers the cellular work.

So ATP is the middleman — it takes energy from food (oxidation) and delivers it to wherever the cell needs work done.

(ii) Describe the phases of photosynthesis with a suitable diagram.

Photosynthesis happens in two phases inside the chloroplast:

1. Light-Dependent Reaction (Light Reaction)

  • Site: Thylakoid membrane.
  • Needs light: Yes.
  • Steps:
    • Chlorophyll absorbs light (violet, blue and red).
    • Energy boosts an electron from chlorophyll a in the reaction centre.
    • The electron passes along the electron transport system of Photosystems I and II.
    • Photolysis splits water: H₂O → O₂ + H⁺ + e⁻. O₂ is released.
    • Energy released as the electron travels is used to form ATP (phosphorylation) and to reduce NADP into NADPH₂.
  • Outputs: ATP, NADPH₂, O₂.

2. Light-Independent Reaction (Dark Reaction / Calvin Cycle / C₃ Cycle)

  • Site: Stroma.
  • Needs light: No — uses ATP and NADPH₂ from the light reaction.
  • What it needs: CO₂, a CO₂-capturing pentose sugar, enzymes, energy from ATP and NADPH₂.
  • What it does: Captures atmospheric CO₂ and combines it step by step (cyclic process) to form glucose.
  • Outputs: Glucose, ADP, NADP (the latter two go back to the light reaction).

Flow diagram (text version):

Sunlight + Chlorophyll
↓
Light Reaction (thylakoid)
H₂O → O₂ + H⁺ + e⁻  |  ADP → ATP  |  NADP → NADPH₂
↓

CO₂ + ATP + NADPH₂ → Glucose
↓
Glucose + Oxygen released
(iii) Describe aerobic respiration in a living system.

Aerobic respiration is the type of cellular respiration that happens in the presence of oxygen. It breaks down glucose completely into CO₂ and H₂O, releasing the maximum amount of energy (≈ 36 ATP per glucose, or 2827 kJ/mole).

Overall equation:

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + 36 ATP

Aerobic respiration occurs in three connected steps:

1. Glycolysis

  • Site: Cytosol (cytoplasm of the cell).
  • Glucose (6-carbon) is split into two molecules of pyruvic acid (3-carbon).
  • Does not need oxygen (so it happens in both aerobic and anaerobic respiration).
  • Produces 2 ATP per glucose.

2. Krebs / Citric Acid Cycle

  • Site: Matrix of mitochondria.
  • Pyruvic acid enters the mitochondria, where O₂ is available.
  • Pyruvic acid is broken down completely into CO₂ in a cyclic series of reactions.
  • Some ATP is produced directly.
  • Co-enzymes NAD and FAD are reduced into NADH₂ and FADH₂ (energy carriers).

3. Electron Transport Chain (ETC)

  • Site: Cristae (inner membrane) of mitochondria.
  • NADH₂ and FADH₂ release their electrons.
  • Electrons pass down a chain of carriers, releasing energy at each step.
  • This energy is used to make a large number of ATP — about 34 ATP.
  • Electrons finally combine with H⁺ and O₂ to form H₂O.

Total energy yield:

  • Glycolysis: 2 ATP
  • Krebs cycle: some ATP + many NADH₂ & FADH₂
  • ETC: ≈ 34 ATP
  • Grand total: ≈ 36 ATP per glucose

This is why aerobic respiration is the preferred energy source for most multicellular organisms — it gives them far more usable energy than anaerobic respiration (which yields only ~2 ATP).
