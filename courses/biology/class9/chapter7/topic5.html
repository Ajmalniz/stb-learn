Aerobic Respiration — Mechanism

Aerobic respiration is the type of respiration that uses oxygen to break down glucose completely — releasing the maximum amount of energy (36 ATP per glucose). It happens in three carefully connected steps: glycolysis → Krebs cycle → electron transport chain.

5.1 What is Aerobic Respiration?

Aerobic Respiration
The type of respiration that requires free oxygen to break down food completely, releasing the maximum amount of energy.
  • Used by the majority of organisms.
  • Energy yield: 2827 kJ/mole of glucose ≈ 36 ATP per glucose molecule.
  • End products: CO₂ + H₂O.
C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂  →  6CO₂ + 6H₂O + 36 ATP

5.2 Three Steps of Aerobic Respiration

Aerobic respiration happens in three steps, each in a different place in the cell:

1. Glycolysis   (in cytosol)
↓
2. Krebs / Citric Acid Cycle   (in matrix of mitochondria)
↓
3. Electron Transport Chain   (on cristae of mitochondria)

5.3 Step 1 — Glycolysis

Glycolysis = "Glyco" (sugar) + "lysis" (breakdown).

Glycolysis
The first step of respiration, where one molecule of glucose (6-carbon) is broken down into two molecules of pyruvic acid (3-carbon).
  • Site: Cytosol (cytoplasm).
  • Does not require oxygen.
  • Happens in both aerobic and anaerobic respiration.
  • Releases enough energy to produce 2 ATP molecules.
  • It is a complex sequence of many small reactions.
Glucose  →  2 Pyruvic Acid + 2 ATP

5.4 Step 2 — Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle)

  • Site: Matrix of mitochondria.
  • Requires oxygen.
  • The pyruvic acid (from glycolysis) enters the mitochondria.
  • It is broken down completely into CO₂ and H₂O in a cyclic series of reactions.
  • Some ATP is produced.
  • Co-enzymes NAD and FAD are reduced to NADH₂ and FADH₂ (energy carriers — like batteries getting charged).
Why "Citric Acid Cycle"?

The first compound formed when pyruvic acid combines with the cycle is citric acid — the same acid that gives lemons their sour taste.

5.5 Step 3 — Electron Transport Chain (ETC)

  • Site: Cristae (inner membrane) of mitochondria.
  • The reduced co-enzymes NADH₂ and FADH₂ from the Krebs cycle release their electrons.
  • Electrons travel down a chain of carriers, releasing energy at each step.
  • This energy is used to make a large number of ATP molecules.
  • At the end, electrons combine with oxygen + H⁺ → H₂O.
  • The ETC produces about 34 ATP from each glucose.

5.7 Uses of Respiratory Energy in the Body

Many activities in the body need energy — all of it ultimately comes from respiration:

  • Synthesis of molecules — building large molecules (like proteins, DNA) from small ones needs energy.
  • Cell division — making DNA and proteins, and moving chromosomes during mitosis/meiosis.
  • Growth without cell division — cells enlarging in size still need to make molecules.
  • Active transport — moving ions/molecules from low to high concentration requires ATP (e.g. Na⁺/K⁺ pump).
  • Muscle contraction — chemical energy converted into kinetic energy of movement.
  • Nerve impulse transmission — nerve signals depend on active transport of ions.
  • Maintenance of body temperature — in mammals and birds, respiration releases heat that keeps body temperature constant.
