Chapter 7 · Topic 3

Light & Dark Reactions + Limiting Factors

Photosynthesis happens in two phases — a light-dependent reaction at the thylakoid membrane and a light-independent (dark) reaction in the stroma. This topic explains both phases plus the factors that limit the rate of photosynthesis.

3.1 Two Phases of Photosynthesis — At a Glance

Feature Light Reaction Dark Reaction (Calvin Cycle)
Needs light?YesNo (uses products of light reaction)
SiteThylakoid membraneStroma
Pathway typeNon-cyclicCyclic (Calvin–Benson cycle / C₃ cycle)
InputsLight, H₂O, ADP, NADPCO₂, ATP, NADPH₂
OutputsATP, NADPH₂, O₂Glucose, ADP, NADP

3.2 Light Reaction (Light-Dependent Reaction)

This phase happens at the thylakoid membrane. Light energy is captured and converted into chemical energy stored in two energy-rich compounds.

Step 1 — Light Absorption

  • Pigments absorb light of different wavelengths.
  • Chlorophyll is the main light-capturing molecule — it absorbs violet, blue and red light and reflects green.
  • The pigments and electron carriers are organised into clusters called photosystems.
  • Each thylakoid contains thousands of copies of two types: Photosystem I and Photosystem II.
  • Each photosystem has a light-harvesting complex and an electron transport system.

Step 2 — Photolysis (Splitting of Water)

Photolysis
The splitting of water in the presence of light into oxygen, electrons (e⁻) and hydrogen ions (H⁺).
  • O₂ is released into the environment — this is the oxygen we breathe!
  • H⁺ and e⁻ are used inside the chloroplast.

Step 3 — Energy is Stored in ATP and NADPH₂

When the chlorophyll in the reaction centre receives light energy:

  • An electron from chlorophyll a leaves and jumps onto the electron transport system.
  • As the electron moves from one carrier to the next, it releases energy.
  • This energy is used to form two energy-rich compounds:
Compound How it forms
ATPADP combines with phosphate using energy released from photons (this is called phosphorylation): Light energy + ADP + P → ATP
NADPH₂NADP accepts H⁺ (from photolysis) and e⁻: NADP + 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → NADPH₂

Both ATP and NADPH₂ now carry the energy needed for the next phase.

3.3 Dark Reaction (Light-Independent Reaction)

The dark reaction also takes place during the day, alongside the light reaction. But it does not need light energy — it uses the ATP and NADPH₂ made by the light reaction.

  • Site: Stroma
  • Pattern: Cyclic — also called the Calvin–Benson Cycle or C₃ Cycle (because the first compound formed has three carbons).
  • Main job: Use ATP and NADPH₂ to power the synthesis of glucose from CO₂.
  • This step also fixes atmospheric CO₂ into organic molecules.
  • The reaction can keep going without light as long as ATP and NADPH₂ are available.

What the C₃ Cycle Needs

  1. CO₂ — usually from the air; some comes from respiration.
  2. CO₂-capturing sugar — a pentose (5-carbon) sugar.
  3. Enzymes to catalyse all the reactions.
  4. Energy from ATP and NADPH₂ (from light reaction).

3.4 Limiting Factors of Photosynthesis

Limiting Factor
A factor that controls the rate of a biochemical reaction — if you change it, the rate changes; if you remove the limitation, the rate stops increasing.

The three main limiting factors of photosynthesis are:

  • Light intensity
  • CO₂ concentration
  • Temperature

How They Take Turns Being the Limit

If you look at a graph of rate vs light intensity, you see three regions:

Condition Limiting factor
At low light intensity (point Z on the graph)Light intensity
Bright light + moderate temperatureCO₂ concentration — adding more CO₂ raises the rate
High light + high CO₂Temperature — but if it goes too high, enzymes denature
Key idea

Only one factor limits the rate at a time — the one that is in shortest supply. Increasing the other factors won't help until you fix the limiting one.

3.5 Activity — Effect of Light Intensity on Photosynthesis

Apparatus: beaker of water, boiling tube, stand & clamp, paper clip, Hydrilla plant, ruler, stopwatch, thermometer, lamp.

Procedure (summary):

  1. Place a fresh piece of Hydrilla upside down in a boiling tube of water, clamped upright in a beaker of water.
  2. Use a lamp as the light source. Use the surrounding water to keep the temperature constant.
  3. Bubbles of O₂ appear from the cut end of the plant. Count bubbles per minute.
  4. Move the lamp closer or further; count bubbles at each distance.
  5. Take 3 readings at each distance for accuracy.

Conclusion: the rate of bubbling decreases as the lamp is moved away — so the rate of photosynthesis falls as light intensity falls. This proves light intensity is a limiting factor at low values.
