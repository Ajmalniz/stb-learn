Chapter 7 · Topic 2

Photosynthesis — Introduction & Chloroplast

Plants are nature's solar panels. They use sunlight, water and CO₂ to make their own food — and along the way, release the oxygen we breathe. This topic introduces photosynthesis, its equation, the role of chlorophyll, and the chloroplast where it all happens.

2.1 What is Photosynthesis?

Photosynthesis
The fundamental process in which chlorophyll-containing organisms use light energy to convert simple inorganic molecules (CO₂ and H₂O) into glucose and oxygen.

The word breaks down as photo (light) + synthesis (to prepare).

Photosynthesis is carried out by:

  • Plants
  • Algae
  • Some protozoans
  • Some bacteria

2.2 The Overall Equation

6CO₂ + 6H₂O  →  C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
(in the presence of chlorophyll & light)

In words: Carbon dioxide + Water → Glucose + Oxygen.

  • Reactants: CO₂ + H₂O
  • Products: Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) + O₂
  • Requirements: Light energy + Chlorophyll

2.3 Role of Chlorophyll

Chlorophyll is the green pigment found in the chloroplasts of plant cells. It is NOT a reactant — it works as the light absorber.

  • Chlorophyll captures a specific part of visible light.
  • It absorbs violet, blue and red light strongly.
  • It reflects green light — which is why plants look green.
  • The captured light energy is used to drive the reaction — converting light energy into chemical energy.
Key idea

Photosynthesis is the only process in nature that converts light energy → chemical energy on a large scale.

2.4 What Happens to the Glucose?

The fundamental product of photosynthesis is glucose (simple sugar). Plants use it for many things:

Use of glucose Product / outcome
EnergyUsed in respiration to produce ATP
StorageConverted into starch & other polysaccharides; stored in seeds, fruits, tubers
SugarsSucrose for transport in plants
Fats & proteinsWith nitrates (for amino acids → proteins) and other elements (for fats)
Nucleic acidsCombined with nitrates and phosphates to form DNA / RNA

2.5 Why Photosynthesis Matters for Everyone

1. Food chains. All food chains depend on plants:

  • Herbivores directly eat plants.
  • Carnivores eat herbivores.
  • So all animals are indirectly dependent on photosynthesis for food.
Food chain
The feeding sequence and relationships between living organisms — usually starting with a plant (producer) and moving through consumers.

2. Oxygen. Photosynthesis is the only process that releases free O₂ by splitting water. All aerobic organisms depend on this oxygen for respiration.

3. Maintaining atmospheric balance. Plants fix CO₂ and release O₂. This:

  • Keeps the CO₂ concentration in check.
  • Indirectly helps control global temperature — because CO₂ traps heat (greenhouse effect). High CO₂ → global warming.

2.6 The Chloroplast — Where It All Happens

Photosynthesis takes place inside a special organelle in green plant cells called the chloroplast.

Structure of the chloroplast:

  • Double membrane — an outer and an inner membrane.
  • Stroma — a semi-fluid, protein-rich medium inside.
  • Thylakoid membrane — a network of flat membrane sacs embedded in the stroma.
  • Grana (singular: granum) — stacks of thylakoid discs.
  • Chlorophyll — the green pigment, located on the thylakoid membrane.

Two Sites = Two Reactions

Photosynthesis is not one single reaction — it is a long sequence catalysed by many enzymes. It happens in two sets of reactions at two different sites:

Where What happens
Thylakoid membrane Light-dependent reaction — light energy is captured and converted into chemical energy stored in ATP & NADPH₂. Coupled with photolysis (splitting of water).
Stroma Light-independent reaction (dark reaction) — the captured energy in ATP and NADPH₂ is used to make glucose by fixing atmospheric CO₂ (Calvin cycle).

We will go through both reactions in detail in the next topic.
