Section 1 — Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

Encircle the correct answer. Correct option is marked in green.

(i) Which processes involve mitosis?
A. Growth, reduction division and asexual reproduction.
B. Growth, repair and asexual reproduction.
C. Growth, repair and semiconservative replication.
D. Growth, reduction division and repair.
Mitosis is involved in growth, repair (healing wounds, replacing somatic cells) and asexual reproduction. "Reduction division" is the job of meiosis, not mitosis.
(ii) What occurs in metaphase of mitosis?
A. Chromosomes line up on the equator of the cell.
B. Chromatids reach the poles of the spindle.
C. Chromatids separate and move to opposite poles.
D. Chromosomes start to coil up and become visible.
"Meta" means middle. In metaphase, each chromosome arranges itself on the equator (middle) of the spindle and attaches to a spindle fibre by its centromere. The other options describe telophase, anaphase, and prophase respectively.
(iii) Select the mismatched:
A. Anaphase → Chromatids move
B. Prophase → Centriole move
C. Telophase → Nuclear membrane disappears
D. Metaphase → Chromosomes line up
In telophase the nuclear membrane reforms — it does not disappear. It is in prophase that the nuclear membrane disappears. So option C is mismatched.
(iv) Which process occurs during prophase of mitosis in an animal cell?
A. Division of centromeres
B. Formation of chromosomes
C. Replication of DNA
D. Separation of centrioles
In prophase of an animal cell, the centrosome divides into two centrioles, and the two centrioles separate and move toward opposite poles to form the spindle. DNA replication happened earlier in S-phase; the chromatin only becomes visible (it is not "formed"); centromeres divide in anaphase.
(v) Sequence of physiological changes by which cell commits suicide:
A. Apoptosis
B. Necrosis
C. Autophagy
D. Both A and C
Apoptosis (also called autophagy or self-destruction) is the programmed sequence of physiological changes by which a cell commits suicide. Both names refer to the same process.
(vi) Choose the incorrect statement regarding meiosis.
A. Maintains chromosome number from generation to generation.
B. Reduces the diploid number of chromosomes to half.
C. Takes place in germ cells to produce gametes.
D. Production of new somatic cells from germ cells.
Meiosis produces gametes (sperm and eggs) — not somatic (body) cells. New somatic cells are produced by mitosis. The other statements are correct.
(vii) A type of cell division in which spore mother cell produces spores:
A. Amitosis
B. Mitosis
C. Meiosis
D. Both A and C
In plants, spore mother cells (S.M.C.) undergo meiosis to produce spores — just like germ cells in animals undergo meiosis to produce gametes.
(viii) Stage of mitosis in which chromatids reach the poles and their movement ceases:
A. Prophase
B. Metaphase
C. Anaphase
D. Telophase
In telophase, the chromatids (now called chromosomes) reach the poles, stop moving, and a nuclear membrane forms around each set. Anaphase is when they are still moving.
(ix) Stage of meiosis in which centromeres shorten and the paired chromatids are pulled away from one another:
A. Anaphase II
B. Metaphase II
C. Telophase II
D. Prophase II
In Anaphase II the spindle fibres attached to the centromeres shorten and the chromatids of each chromosome are pulled apart toward opposite poles — just like anaphase in mitosis.
(x) Phenomenon in which pair of homologous chromosomes fails to separate is called:
A. Non-disjunction
B. Terminalization
C. Synapsis
D. Linkage
Non-disjunction is the failure of homologous chromosomes (or sister chromatids) to separate during meiosis. It produces gametes with abnormal chromosome numbers and can cause disorders like Down syndrome.

Section 2 — Fill in the Blanks

(i) Chromosomes are thread-like structures that appear at the time of __________.

Cell division. Outside of cell division, the genetic material exists as a loose chromatin network. It is only during cell division that it condenses into visible chromosomes.

(ii) Sequence of changes which occurs between one cell division and the next is called __________.

Cell cycle. The cell cycle has two main phases — interphase (G₁, S, G₂) and M phase (mitosis + cytokinesis).

(iii) The two chromatids are attached to each other at __________.

Centromere. The centromere is the point where the two sister chromatids of a chromosome are joined together — it is also where the spindle fibre attaches during cell division.

(iv) Chromosomes in the cell which are similar to each other in shape and size are called __________.

Homologous chromosomes. One member of each homologous pair comes from the mother and one from the father. They pair up during prophase I of meiosis (synapsis).

(v) Chromosomes with one arm very small and the other very long are called __________.

Acrocentric (also called sub-telocentric). The centromere is sub-terminal, producing one very short arm and one very long arm.

(vi) Chromosomes consisting of two genetically identical copies of thread are called __________.

Sister chromatids. After DNA replication in S phase, each chromosome is made of two identical copies (chromatids) joined at the centromere.

(vii) Period of extensive metabolic activity, in which cell grows and enzymes are synthesised, is __________.

G₁ (Gap 1) phase. During G₁, the cell grows, makes enzymes, and accumulates DNA base units in preparation for the S phase.

(viii) In animals, meiosis produces __________.

Gametes (sperms and eggs). In animals, meiosis happens in germ cells to produce haploid gametes. In plants, it happens in spore mother cells to produce spores.

(ix) During metaphase, homologous chromosomes arrange themselves on the __________.

Equatorial plane (equator of the spindle). This is the middle line of the cell. In metaphase I of meiosis, bivalents (paired homologous chromosomes) line up here.

(x) Cell death which is caused by external factors is called __________.

Necrosis. Necrosis is accidental cell death caused by external factors like infection, toxin or tumour. The other type — programmed cell death — is called apoptosis.

Section 3 — Define the Following Terms

(i) Pachytene

Pachytene is the third sub-stage of prophase I of meiosis. The synaptic forces weaken; homologous chromosomes uncoil and separate slightly but stay in contact at points called chiasmata. Each chromosome splits longitudinally (except at the centromere), so each bivalent now contains four chromatids — a bivalent tetrad.

(ii) Cytokinesis

Cytokinesis is the division of the cytoplasm that follows karyokinesis (nuclear division). In animal cells it happens by a constriction (cleavage furrow); in plant cells it happens by formation of a cell plate. The result is two daughter cells.

(iii) Bivalent

A bivalent is a pair of homologous chromosomes joined together by synapsis during prophase I of meiosis. Each bivalent contains four chromatids (a tetrad) once the chromosomes have split lengthwise.

(iv) Chiasmata

Chiasmata (singular: chiasma) are the points of contact between homologous chromosomes during pachytene of prophase I, where the chromatids cross over each other. Crossing over (exchange of segments) takes place here.

(v) Chromatids

Chromatids are the two identical halves of a chromosome after DNA replication, joined together at the centromere. They are also called sister chromatids. During anaphase, the centromere splits and each chromatid becomes an independent chromosome.

(vi) Diakinesis

Diakinesis is the fifth (last) sub-stage of prophase I of meiosis. During this stage the nucleolus and nuclear membrane disappear, the mitotic apparatus (spindle) is completed, and the chiasmata move toward the ends of the chromosomes like a zipper (terminalization).

(vii) Terminalization

Terminalization is the movement of chiasmata from the centromere towards the ends of chromosomes, like a zipper closing. It occurs during diakinesis of prophase I.

(viii) Necrosis

Necrosis is a type of cell death caused by external factors such as infection, toxin, tumour or injury. It is accidental and uncontrolled, often causing damage and inflammation in surrounding tissues.

(ix) Crossing over

Crossing over is the exchange of segments of chromatids between homologous chromosomes at chiasmata during diplotene of prophase I. It is the main source of genetic variation and the raw material of evolution.

(x) Centromere

The centromere is the point on a chromosome where the two sister chromatids are joined. It is also the site where the spindle fibre attaches during cell division. Its position decides the chromosome type (metacentric, sub-metacentric, acrocentric or telocentric).

Section 5 — Short Answer Questions

(i) Why is Meiosis-I called reduction division?

Meiosis-I is called the reduction division because:

  • At the start of meiosis-I, the cell is diploid (2n) — it has two sets of chromosomes.
  • During Anaphase I, the homologous chromosomes separate — one of each pair moves to each pole.
  • This means each daughter cell receives only half the number of chromosomes — they become haploid (n).
  • So meiosis-I reduces the chromosome number from diploid to haploid — this is the actual reduction step.
  • Meiosis-II does not reduce the number further; it just divides the haploid cells like a mitosis.
(ii) Why is mitosis necessary for growth?

Mitosis is necessary for growth because:

  • An organism grows when the number of cells in its body increases.
  • Mitosis produces exact copies of cells with the same chromosome number — keeping the genetic information stable.
  • Without mitosis, you could not develop from a single fertilised egg into an organism made of trillions of cells.
  • Mitosis also produces new somatic cells (skin, blood, muscle, bone), which are needed continuously throughout life.
  • Healing of wounds and repair of wear and tear in tissues also use mitosis.
(iii) How does the number of chromosomes remain constant from generation to generation?

The chromosome number stays constant due to the combination of meiosis and fertilisation:

  • Body cells of a species are diploid (2n) — they have two sets of chromosomes.
  • During meiosis, the chromosome number is reduced to half (n) in the gametes.
  • During fertilisation, a male gamete (n) and a female gamete (n) fuse to form a zygote (n + n = 2n).
  • This restores the diploid number in the offspring.
  • So each new generation has the same chromosome number as the parents. Without meiosis the number would double in each generation.
(iv) Why is interphase called the phase of high metabolic activity?

Interphase is called the phase of high metabolic activity because:

  • The cell grows in size during G₁ and G₂.
  • Specific enzymes are synthesised for DNA replication and cell division.
  • DNA base units (nucleotides) are accumulated.
  • DNA replication takes place in the S phase — a massive use of energy.
  • Cell organelles replicate in number during G₂ so each daughter cell will receive enough.
  • All these activities need a high rate of metabolism, lots of energy (ATP), and lots of raw materials.
(v) Why is interphase between Meiosis-I and Meiosis-II short?

The interphase between meiosis-I and meiosis-II is short because:

  • In normal interphase, the main activity is DNA replication in the S phase — this takes time.
  • But between meiosis-I and meiosis-II, DNA replication does NOT occur.
  • This is because each chromosome already has two chromatids from the previous S phase (before meiosis-I).
  • So the cell can go straight into meiosis-II without spending time copying DNA again.
  • Some times this interphase may even be skipped entirely.

Section 6 — Long Answer Questions

(i) Describe various stages of mitosis with suitable diagrams.

Mitosis is the type of cell division in which a parent cell divides into two genetically identical daughter cells with the same chromosome number as the parent. It has two main parts: karyokinesis (nuclear division) and cytokinesis (cytoplasmic division). Karyokinesis is divided into four phases: Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase.

1. Prophase

  • Chromatin condenses into visible thread-like chromosomes.
  • Each chromosome has two chromatids joined at the centromere.
  • The nuclear membrane disappears.
  • The centrosome divides into two centrioles (in animal cells), which move to opposite poles and form the spindle fibres.

2. Metaphase

  • Each chromosome arranges itself on the equator of the spindle.
  • Each chromosome attaches to a separate spindle fibre by its centromere.

3. Anaphase

  • The spindle fibres contract and the centromere of each chromosome divides.
  • The two chromatids of each chromosome separate and move toward opposite poles — one set to each pole.

4. Telophase

  • The chromatids (now chromosomes) reach the poles and their movement stops.
  • Each pole receives the same number of chromosomes as the parent cell.
  • The nuclear membrane reforms around each set, giving two daughter nuclei.

5. Cytokinesis (cytoplasmic division)

  • In animal cells → a constriction (cleavage furrow) deepens until the cell splits in two.
  • In plant cells → a cell plate forms in the middle and develops outward.

Simple flow diagram (text version):

Parent cell (interphase)
↓
Prophase — chromosomes visible
↓
Metaphase — chromosomes at equator
↓
Anaphase — chromatids move to poles
↓
Telophase — two nuclei reform
↓
Cytokinesis — TWO identical daughter cells
(ii) Describe stages of Meiosis-I with suitable diagrams.

Meiosis-I is the first division of meiosis and is called the reduction division because it reduces the chromosome number from diploid (2n) to haploid (n). It has four phases.

1. Prophase I — the longest phase of meiosis. Divided into five sub-stages:

  • Leptotene — chromatin network breaks into long, thin beaded threads.
  • Zygotene — homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis); each pair is called a bivalent.
  • Pachytene — chromosomes split lengthwise (except at the centromere); each bivalent now has four chromatids (a tetrad); homologous chromosomes stay in contact at points called chiasmata.
  • Diplotene — homologous chromosomes exchange segments at chiasmata — this is crossing over, the main source of genetic variation.
  • Diakinesis — nucleolus and nuclear membrane disappear; spindle is completed; chiasmata move toward chromosome ends (terminalization).

2. Metaphase I

  • The bivalents (paired homologous chromosomes) line up at the equatorial plane.
  • The centromere of each chromosome attaches to a spindle fibre.

3. Anaphase I

  • One chromosome from each bivalent moves toward each pole (not chromatids — whole chromosomes).
  • This is the actual reduction step — half the chromosomes go to each pole.
  • Because of crossing over, the two chromatids of a chromosome are no longer genetically identical.

4. Telophase I

  • Nuclear membrane reforms around the chromosomes at each pole.
  • Chromosomes uncoil; nucleolus reappears.
  • Two daughter nuclei are formed.
  • Cytokinesis may or may not occur immediately.

Simple flow diagram (text version):

Diploid parent cell (2n)
↓
Prophase I — 5 sub-stages (synapsis, crossing over)
↓
Metaphase I — bivalents at equator
↓
Anaphase I — homologous chromosomes separate
↓
Telophase I — two nuclei form
↓
TWO haploid daughter cells (n)

These two haploid cells will go through meiosis-II to produce a total of four haploid daughter cells.
