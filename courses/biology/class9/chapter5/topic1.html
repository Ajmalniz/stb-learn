Chapter 5 · Topic 1

Chromosomes

Chromosomes are the thread-like structures inside our cells that carry all our genetic information. This topic explains what they look like, what they are made of, and the four types you'll meet in MCQs.

1.1 What is a Chromosome?

Chromosome
A thread-like structure that appears during cell division, found in a specific number in eukaryotic cells, made of chromatin material. Each chromosome carries the hereditary units called genes.

Chromosomes are made of DNA + basic proteins called histones. They appear rod-shaped during cell division.

Who named them?

The term "chromosome" was given by the German embryologist Walther Flemming in 1882. While studying rapidly dividing cells of salamander larvae stained with Perkin's aniline, he noticed these structures took the stain much more darkly than the rest of the cell. "Chromo" = colour, "soma" = body — but it's actually a misnomer, because chromosomes are themselves colourless.

1.2 How Chromosomes Are Formed

Inside the nucleus, the long DNA molecule is wrapped around histone proteins to form bead-like structures called nucleosomes. These nucleosomes are packaged into chromatin fibres.

  • Chromatin allows the very long DNA molecules to fit inside the tiny nucleus.
  • During cell division, chromatin condenses further to form microscopically visible chromosomes.
  • The structure of chromosomes varies through the cell cycle (loose chromatin in interphase, condensed chromosomes during division).

At the beginning of cell division, each chromosome consists of two genetically identical copies joined together — these copies are called chromatids (or sister chromatids).

1.3 Parts of a Chromosome

Every chromosome has two main parts: arms and a centromere. The full structure includes:

Part Role
TelomereThe tip / end of each arm — protects the chromosome end
P armThe shorter arm (above centromere)
CentromereThe point where the two sister chromatids are joined; also where the spindle fibre attaches during division
Q armThe longer arm (below centromere)
Secondary constrictionA second pinching point — site where the nucleolus forms
Sister chromatidsTwo identical copies of the chromosome joined at the centromere

1.4 Types of Chromosomes (by Centromere Position)

Chromosomes are classified into four types depending on where the centromere is located:

Type Centromere position Arms
MetacentricIn the middleTwo equal arms
Sub-metacentricSlightly off the middleTwo unequal arms
Acrocentric (sub-telocentric)Near one end — sub-terminalOne arm very short, the other very long (rod-like)
TelocentricAt the very end (terminal)Effectively only one arm
Easy memory hook

Think of the centromere walking from the middle to the end: Meta (middle) → Sub-meta (slightly off) → Acro (near end) → Telo (at the end).

1.5 Why Are Chromosomes Important?

  • They carry the genes — the units of heredity.
  • During the cell cycle they are replicated and passed to daughter cells, so the next generation has the same genetic information.
  • Sometimes cell division also causes genetic diversity (especially during meiosis, covered in Topic 5).
  • Every species has its own specific number of chromosomes (humans = 46; pea = 14; fruit fly = 8).
