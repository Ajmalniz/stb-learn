Chapter 5 · Topic 3

Mitosis

Mitosis is the kind of cell division that keeps the chromosome number the same. One parent cell → two identical daughter cells. It is how your body grows, repairs wounds, and replaces old cells.

3.1 What is Mitosis?

Mitosis
A type of cell division in which a parent cell divides into two daughter cells in a way that the number of chromosomes in the daughter cells remains the same as in the parent cell.

Mitosis is a continuous process, but for study we split it into two stages:

  • Karyokinesis — division of the nucleus.
  • Cytokinesis — division of the cytoplasm.

Karyokinesis is further divided into four phases:

Prophase → Metaphase → Anaphase → Telophase

Easy memory hook

"PMAT" — Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase.

3.2 Phase 1 — Prophase

  • Chromatin material condenses and becomes visible as thick, coiled, thread-like chromosomes.
  • Each chromosome is already double at this stage — it consists of two chromatids joined at the centromere.
  • The nuclear membrane gradually disappears.
  • The centrosome divides into two centrioles (in animal cells only — centrioles are absent in plant cells).
  • The centrioles move to opposite poles of the cell and form the spindle fibres.

3.3 Phase 2 — Metaphase

  • Each chromosome arranges itself on the equator of the spindle (the middle line of the cell).
  • Each chromosome attaches to a separate spindle fibre by its centromere.
Memory hook

Meta = Middle. Chromosomes line up at the middle of the cell.

3.4 Phase 3 — Anaphase

  • The spindle fibres contract.
  • The centromere of each chromosome divides.
  • The two chromatids of each chromosome separate from each other.
  • Each chromatid (now an independent chromosome) moves toward the opposite pole — one set going up, the other going down.
Memory hook

Ana = Apart. Chromatids move apart to opposite poles.

3.5 Phase 4 — Telophase

  • The chromatids (now called chromosomes) reach the poles and their movement stops.
  • Each pole receives the same number of chromosomes as the parent cell.
  • The nuclear membrane reforms around each set of chromosomes.
  • Two daughter nuclei are formed in the cell.

3.6 Cytokinesis — Division of the Cytoplasm

After karyokinesis, the cytoplasm of the cell also divides, giving two daughter cells.

Cell type How cytokinesis happens
Animal cellA constriction (cleavage furrow) forms in the middle. It becomes deeper until the cytoplasm splits into two equal halves.
Plant cellA cell plate forms in the middle and grows outward to divide the cell into two.

The end result: two daughter cells that are exact copies of the parent cell.

3.7 Significance of Mitosis

Mitosis is essential for the life of multicellular organisms:

  • Growth — multicellular bodies grow from a single fertilised egg into trillions of cells, all by mitosis.
  • Development — exact copies of cells are produced for organ formation.
  • Repair — healing of wounds and replacement of worn-out tissue depend on mitosis.
  • Replacement — new somatic cells (like blood cells, skin cells) are constantly made by mitosis.
  • Asexual reproduction — almost all kinds of asexual reproduction and vegetative propagation happen by mitosis.
Exam tip

If you see "growth, repair, asexual reproduction" together in an MCQ, the answer is almost always mitosis.
