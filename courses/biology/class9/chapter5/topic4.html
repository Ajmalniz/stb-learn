Chapter 5 · Topic 4

Apoptosis & Necrosis — Two Ways Cells Die

Cells don't only divide — sometimes they need to die. In a multicellular body, cell death is just as important as cell birth. There are two ways a cell can die: apoptosis (planned and useful) and necrosis (accidental and harmful).

4.1 Is Cell Death Beneficial?

It sounds strange, but yes — programmed cell death is essential for normal development. Examples:

  • The tail of a developing human embryo disappears because the cells in it are killed off.
  • The webbing between developing fingers and toes is removed by programmed death so the digits become free.
  • Old or damaged cells are removed before they cause problems (e.g. cancer).

Each cell in our body depends on extracellular signals — even the timing of its death is programmed.

4.2 Apoptosis — Programmed Self-Destruction

Apoptosis (Autophagy / Self-destruction)
A programmed sequence of physiological changes in a cell by which the cell commits suicide. It is a controlled, orderly process.

Apoptosis is needed for proper control of multicellular development — to delete entire structures (like the embryonic tail) or tissues that are no longer required (like webbing between fingers).

4.3 Necrosis — Accidental Cell Death

Necrosis
Cell death caused by external factors such as infection, toxin or tumour. It is accidental, uncontrolled cell death.

Unlike apoptosis, necrosis is not part of normal development. It happens when a cell is damaged from outside and can no longer carry out its functions.

4.4 Apoptosis vs Necrosis — Comparison

Feature Apoptosis Necrosis
TypeProgrammed cell deathAccidental cell death
CauseInternal signals — cell decides to dieExternal factors — infection, toxin, tumour, injury
ControlOrderly, controlledUncontrolled
Effect on neighboursNo damage; no inflammationInflammation; neighbouring cells may also be damaged
Useful?Yes — essential for development & healthNo — always harmful

Memory hook
