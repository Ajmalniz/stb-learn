Meiosis — Reduction Division

Meiosis is the special cell division that makes gametes (sperms, eggs, spores). One parent cell → four daughter cells, each with half the number of chromosomes. Without meiosis, every new generation would have double the chromosomes of the last.

5.1 What is Meiosis?

Meiosis
A type of cell division in which a single cell divides into four daughter cells, and the number of chromosomes becomes half in each daughter cell.

Where it happens:

  • In animals → in germ cells (to produce gametes: sperms and eggs).
  • In plants → in spore mother cells (S.M.C.) (to produce spores).

Meiosis is a series of two divisions:

  • Meiosis I — the reduction division (chromosome number is halved).
  • Meiosis II — essentially a mitotic division of the haploid cells.

Result: 4 haploid daughter cells from 1 diploid parent cell.

5.2 Meiosis I — The Reduction Division

Meiosis I has four main phases — Prophase I, Metaphase I, Anaphase I, Telophase I. Prophase I is the most complex and is split into five sub-stages.

5.3 Prophase I — Five Sub-Stages

Prophase I is the longest phase of meiosis. Its sub-stages are:

Leptotene → Zygotene → Pachytene → Diplotene → Diakinesis

Sub-stage What happens
1. Leptotene The chromatin network breaks into specific numbers of long, thin, beaded threads called leptotene. Each thread has a similar partner (homologous structure) in the cell.
2. Zygotene Homologous chromosomes (one from mother, one from father) are attracted to each other and pair up lengthwise. This pairing is called synapsis. The paired chromosomes are called a bivalent.
3. Pachytene Synaptic forces weaken. Chromosomes uncoil and separate slightly but remain in contact at one or more points called chiasmata. Each chromosome splits longitudinally (except at the centromere) — so each bivalent now has four chromatids → it is a bivalent tetrad.
4. Diplotene Homologous chromosomes exchange parts of their chromatids at chiasmata. This is called crossing over — the cause of genetic variation.
5. Diakinesis Nucleolus and nuclear membrane disappear. The spindle (mitotic apparatus) is completed. Chiasmata move from the centromere toward the ends of chromosomes like a zipper — this is called terminalization.
Synapsis
The pairing of homologous chromosomes during zygotene of prophase I.
Bivalent
A pair of homologous chromosomes joined by synapsis.
Chiasmata (singular: chiasma)
The points of contact between paired homologous chromosomes where crossing over occurs.
Crossing Over
The exchange of segments of chromatids between homologous chromosomes at chiasmata. The main source of genetic variation.
Terminalization
The movement of chiasmata from the centromere towards the ends of the chromosomes during diakinesis.

5.4 Metaphase I, Anaphase I, Telophase I

Metaphase I

  • The bivalents line up at the equatorial plane (middle of the cell).
  • The centromere of each chromosome attaches to a spindle fibre.

Anaphase I

  • One chromosome from each bivalent (not chromatid!) moves toward each pole.
  • This is where the actual reduction happens — half the number of chromosomes goes to each pole.
  • Because of crossing over, the two chromatids of a chromosome are no longer genetically identical.

Telophase I

  • Nuclear membrane forms around the chromosomes at each pole.
  • Chromosomes uncoil; nucleolus reappears.
  • Two daughter nuclei are formed.
  • Cytokinesis may or may not occur here.

Short Interphase

A short interphase follows telophase I (if it occurs). It is similar to the regular interphase, except DNA replication does NOT occur — because each chromosome already has two chromatids.

5.5 Meiosis II — Like a Mini-Mitosis

The second meiotic division divides each of the two haploid cells from meiosis I into two daughter haploid cells. It is essentially a mitotic division of haploid cells and has four phases:

Phase What happens
Prophase IISpindle fibres form. Nuclear membrane and nucleolus disappear.
Metaphase IIChromosomes attach to spindle fibres by their centromeres and arrange at the equatorial plane.
Anaphase IISpindle fibres shorten; chromatids are pulled apart. A complete set of chromosomes reaches each pole.
Telophase IISpindle disappears; chromosomes uncoil and become long. A nuclear envelope forms around each group.

After karyokinesis, cytokinesis occurs in each haploid meiotic cell — producing a total of four haploid cells.

5.6 Significance of Meiosis

1. Constant chromosome number from generation to generation

  • Meiosis reduces the diploid (2n) number to haploid (n) in the gametes.
  • When two gametes fuse during fertilisation, the diploid number is restored.
  • This keeps the species' chromosome number constant across generations.

2. Genetic variation among species

  • Through crossing over, meiosis provides an opportunity for the exchange of genes between homologous chromosomes.
  • This causes variations among individuals — the raw material of evolution.
What if meiosis didn't happen?

Without meiosis, every fertilisation would double the chromosome number. After a few generations, this would cause abnormal growth, changes in species characteristics, and may prove fatal.

5.7 Meiotic Errors — Non-disjunction

Non-disjunction
The failure of a pair of homologous chromosomes to separate from one another during meiosis I.

Effects:

  • Produces gametes with an abnormal number of chromosomes — either too many or too few.
  • If such an abnormal gamete fuses with a normal one, the resulting zygote will also have an abnormal number of chromosomes.
  • This can lead to genetic disorders (e.g. an extra chromosome 21 causes Down syndrome).
