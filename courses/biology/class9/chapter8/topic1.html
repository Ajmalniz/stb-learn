Chapter 8 · Topic 1

Introduction to Nutrition

Every living thing needs food to grow, repair itself, and get energy. But not all organisms get food the same way. In this topic, we learn what nutrition is, the two main modes of getting food, and how even tiny single-celled organisms like Amoeba eat.

1.1 What is Nutrition?

Nutrition
The process by which organisms obtain and use the nutrients required for maintaining life. It includes taking in food, digesting it, and using it for energy, growth, and repair.
Nutrients
Essential substances that the body needs in order to grow and stay healthy — such as carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins, minerals, water, and fibre.

There are two main ways organisms get their food:

  • Autotrophic nutrition — make their own food
  • Heterotrophic nutrition — depend on other organisms for food

1.2 Autotrophic Nutrition

Autotrophic Nutrition
A mode of nutrition in which an organism makes its own food from simple inorganic materials (CO₂, water, minerals) using energy (from sunlight or chemicals). The word comes from Greek: autos = self, trophe = nutrition.

Who are autotrophs?

  • Green plants, algae, and some bacteria (they contain chlorophyll)
  • Some bacteria that use chemical energy instead of sunlight (called chemoautotrophs — chemosynthesis)

How do plants make food? — Photosynthesis

Green plants use sunlight, chlorophyll, water, and CO₂ to make glucose (sugar) and release oxygen. The equation is:

6CO₂ + 12H₂O  →  C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6H₂O + 6O₂
(with sunlight + chlorophyll)
Key point

Autotrophic nutrition produces food — it is the foundation of all food chains. Without autotrophs (plants), no heterotrophs (animals) could survive.

1.3 Heterotrophic Nutrition

Heterotrophic Nutrition
A mode of nutrition in which an organism cannot make its own food and depends on other organisms for it. The word comes from Greek: heteros = other, trophe = nutrition.

Heterotrophs take in complex organic food and break it down using enzymes. All animals, non-green plants, and fungi are heterotrophs. They are also called consumers.

Depending on how they live and how they get food, heterotrophs are divided into three types:

Type How they get food Examples
Parasitic Live on or inside a host organism and steal food from it. The host gets no benefit. Cuscuta (akash-bel), hookworms, tapeworms, leeches
Saprotrophic Feed on dead and decaying organic matter. They secrete enzymes outside their body to break down the food, then absorb it. Moulds, mushrooms, yeasts, many bacteria
Holozoic Ingest (swallow) complex food whole, digest it inside the body using internal enzymes, absorb digested nutrients, and egest (expel) waste. Amoeba, Paramecium, humans, most animals
Did you know?

Herbivores eat plants; carnivores eat animals. Both are heterotrophs. Both depend on autotrophs (plants) at the base of the food chain.

1.4 Nutrition in Unicellular Organisms

Single-celled organisms like Amoeba and Paramecium also need to eat. They use holozoic nutrition, but in fascinating ways.

Amoeba

Amoeba is a shapeless organism that moves and eats using pseudopodia (false feet — projections of cytoplasm).

  1. Amoeba identifies food nearby.
  2. It extends pseudopodia around the food particle.
  3. The pseudopodia enclose the food, forming a food vacuole (a small bag inside the cell).
  4. Lysosomes (organelles) release digestive enzymes into the food vacuole, breaking down the food.
  5. Digested food is distributed into the cytoplasm (absorbed).
  6. Undigested waste is egested (expelled) through the cell membrane.

Paramecium

Paramecium is a unicellular organism with a fixed shape. It has a special food-gathering structure.

  1. Cilia (tiny hair-like structures) cover the entire surface of Paramecium.
  2. The lashing movement of cilia creates a current that sweeps food toward a special opening called the cytostome (cell mouth).
  3. Food enters through the cytostome → food vacuole forms.
  4. Digestion and egestion follow the same pattern as Amoeba.
Remember

Unlike complex animals that have a whole digestive system, unicellular organisms like Amoeba and Paramecium do their entire digestion inside a single cell.
