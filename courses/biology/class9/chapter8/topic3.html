Components of Human Food

Our food is made up of several different types of substances, each doing a unique job in the body. There are 7 major classes of nutrients — and understanding each one helps us understand why a healthy, varied diet matters.

Overview — The 7 Classes of Nutrients

Nutrient Main function
CarbohydratesPrimary source of energy
ProteinsGrowth and repair of cells
FatsEnergy storage and insulation
VitaminsRegulate metabolic reactions, prevent disease
MineralsBuild bones, make blood, regulate enzymes
WaterTransport, chemical reactions, temperature control
Dietary FibrePrevents constipation, lowers cholesterol

3.1 Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the body's primary and quickest source of energy. Glucose — a simple carbohydrate — is the main fuel for brain and body cells.

Carbohydrates come in two forms:

  • Simple carbohydrates (e.g. glucose, sucrose) — digest quickly and give a fast energy boost. Found in fruits, sugars, white rice, white flour.
  • Complex carbohydrates (e.g. starch, cellulose) — digest slowly and provide sustained energy. Found in green vegetables, potatoes, whole grains, beans, lentils.
Unit of energy

The energy obtained from carbohydrates, proteins, and fats is measured in units called calories. Carbohydrates and proteins give about 4 kcal per gram; fats give about 9 kcal per gram.

3.2 Proteins

Proteins are large, complex molecules made up of smaller units called amino acids.

  • There are 20 different amino acids in total.
  • The body can make 11 of them from other molecules.
  • The remaining 9 are called essential amino acids — they must come from food because the body cannot synthesise them.

Functions of proteins:

  • Growth and repair of cells and tissues
  • Making enzymes, hormones, and antibodies
  • Can be used for energy if carbohydrate stores are depleted

Sources: meat, fish, eggs, dairy, beans, lentils, nuts.

Why are proteins a "slow" energy source?

Proteins are complex and take longer to break down than carbohydrates. So they provide energy more slowly and for a longer time — great for keeping you full.

3.3 Fats

Fats are complex molecules made of fatty acids and glycerol. The body needs fats for:

  • Growth and energy (most energy-efficient food — 9 kcal/gram)
  • Synthesising hormones and other vital substances
  • Insulating the body against cold
  • Protecting organs (cushioning)

The body stores excess fat in two places:

  • Omental fat — in the abdomen (belly)
  • Subcutaneous fat — under the skin

Too much fat stored in blood vessels and organs can block blood flow and cause serious disease.

Sources of saturated fats: fatty cuts of beef and lamb, poultry skin, tropical oils (coconut, palm, cocoa butter), full-fat dairy (butter, cheese, ice cream).

3.4 Vitamins

Vitamin
An organic molecule that an organism needs in small quantities for the proper functioning of its metabolism. Vitamins cannot be made by the body in sufficient amounts, so they must come from food.

Vitamins are divided into two types based on how they dissolve:

Type Which vitamins Key property
Fat-soluble A, D, E, K Dissolve in fats; stored in body fat (excess is not easily lost)
Water-soluble B, C Dissolve in water; easily destroyed by cooking or heat

Vitamin deficiency diseases:

Vitamin Deficiency Disease
Vitamin ANight blindness, eye infections, rough skin, respiratory infections
Vitamin BBeriberi (weakness, nerve damage)
Vitamin CScurvy (bleeding gums, poor wound healing)
Vitamin DRickets (soft, weak bones) and osteomalacia
Vitamin KBleeding disorder (blood cannot clot properly)

3.5 Minerals

Minerals are naturally occurring solid inorganic substances. The body needs them for vital functions. Two of the most important are calcium and iron.

Calcium (Ca)

  • Needed for strong bones and teeth
  • Helps with blood clotting
  • Prevents hypertension (high blood pressure) and kidney stones

Sources: milk, cheese, dairy foods, green leafy vegetables, soya beans, nuts, bread, fish.

⚠ Calcium Deficiency Symptoms:
  • Fainting, heart failure
  • Numbness and tingling around the mouth, fingers, and toes
  • Difficulty swallowing, voice changes (larynx spasm)
  • Chest pain, wheezing
  • Muscle cramps (especially back and legs), which can progress to muscle spasm (tetany)

Iron (Fe)

  • A key component of haemoglobin in red blood cells — carries oxygen around the body
  • Also in myoglobin in muscle cells — stores oxygen in muscles

Sources: beans, lentils, tofu, dark green leafy vegetables (spinach), meat.

⚠ Iron Deficiency Symptoms:
  • Extreme fatigue, weakness
  • Pale skin
  • Chest pain, fast heartbeat, shortness of breath
  • Brittle nails
  • Headache, dizziness
  • Inflammation or soreness of the tongue
  • Poor appetite (especially in infants)

3.6 Water

Water makes up about 70% of the human body and is essential for life. It:

  • Is the medium for all enzymatic and chemical reactions in the body
  • Moves nutrients, hormones, antibodies, and oxygen through the blood and lymphatic system
  • Maintains body temperature through evaporation (sweating)
  • Severe dehydration causes cardiovascular problems

3.7 Dietary Fibre

Dietary fibre is the edible part of plants (such as cellulose) that cannot be digested in the small intestine.

  • Prevents constipation by adding bulk to faeces and speeding up movement through the bowel
  • Soluble fibre (dissolves in water) helps lower blood cholesterol and blood sugar levels

Sources: whole grains, fruits, vegetables, legumes, nuts.
