Human Digestive System

The food you eat is made of large, complex molecules that your cells cannot directly use. The digestive system breaks these molecules down into small, simple molecules that can be absorbed into the blood and used by every cell in the body.

5.1 What is Digestion?

Digestion
The process in which large, non-diffusible molecules of food are converted into smaller, diffusible molecules that can cross cell membranes and be absorbed into the blood.

The digestive system is made up of the alimentary canal (a long tube from mouth to anus, about 9 metres long) plus accessory organs like the liver and pancreas.

Steps of digestion:

Step What happens
IngestionTaking food into the mouth
PropulsionMoving food along the canal by peristalsis (wave-like muscle contractions)
Mechanical digestionPhysical grinding and breaking of food (chewing, churning)
Chemical digestionEnzymes break carbohydrates, proteins, and fats into simple molecules
AbsorptionTransfer of digested nutrients from the gut into the blood
EgestionRemoval of undigested waste from the body (via the anus)
Peristalsis

Peristalsis is the wave of rhythmic muscular contractions and relaxations that push food along the alimentary canal. You don't consciously control it — it happens automatically.

5.2 The Oral Cavity (Mouth)

Digestion begins in the mouth — even before we swallow. The oral cavity performs several functions:

Food selection
When food enters the mouth, we taste and feel it. Hard objects, dirt, or bad-tasting food is rejected. Smell and vision also help us select food.
Grinding (Mastication)
Teeth grind food into small pieces. This is necessary because the oesophagus can only pass small pieces, and enzymes cannot act on large chunks.
Lubrication
Three pairs of salivary glands release saliva. Saliva adds water and mucus to the food. It also contains salivary amylase — an enzyme that begins breaking down starch into simpler sugars.
Bolus formation
The tongue rolls the food into a small, slippery, spherical mass called a bolus, ready for swallowing.

5.3 Pharynx & Oesophagus

After swallowing, the bolus passes into the pharynx (the throat — about 5 inches long).

  • The epiglottis (a flap of tissue) reflexively closes over the windpipe (trachea) when we swallow, preventing food from entering the lungs.
  • From the pharynx, food travels into the oesophagus — a muscular tube in the chest.
  • Peristalsis in the oesophagus pushes the bolus downward to the stomach.
  • A muscular ring (the cardiac sphincter) at the bottom of the oesophagus lets food into the stomach, then closes to prevent food from coming back up.

5.4 The Stomach

The stomach is a J-shaped, thick-walled, expandable bag located in the upper left abdomen. It has three regions:

  • Cardiac region — just after the oesophagus
  • Fundus — the largest part
  • Pyloric region — opens into the small intestine

What happens in the stomach:

  • Stomach walls contract and relax (churning), mixing food with gastric juice
  • Gastric juice (from glands in the stomach wall) contains:
    • Mucus — protects the stomach wall from acid
    • Hydrochloric acid (HCl) — creates the acidic environment needed for digestion; also kills microorganisms in food
    • Pepsinogen (inactive enzyme) — HCl converts it into active pepsin, which begins digesting proteins into shorter chains (polypeptides)
  • The thick, liquid mixture formed in the stomach is called chyme
  • The pyloric sphincter controls the release of chyme into the small intestine
Why doesn't the stomach digest itself?

The stomach lining produces mucus that coats the inner surface and forms a protective barrier against the acidic gastric juice.

5.5 The Small Intestine

The small intestine is where most digestion and almost all absorption takes place. It has three parts:

  • Duodenum (~25 cm, C-shaped) — receives chyme from the stomach; most chemical digestion happens here
  • Jejunum — the coiled middle section
  • Ileum — the final section, leads into the large intestine

Digestive juices entering the duodenum:

  • Bile (from the liver, stored in the gallbladder, enters via the bile duct): bile salts emulsify fats — they break large fat globules into tiny droplets, giving enzymes a larger surface area to work on
  • Pancreatic juice (from the pancreas): contains trypsinogen (digests proteins), pancreatic lipase (digests fats), and amylase (digests starch)
  • Intestinal juices: enzymes from the intestine wall break down all four groups of food molecules

Structure of Villi — Absorption

The inner wall of the small intestine is covered with millions of tiny, finger-like projections called villi (singular: villus). Each villus:

  • Is made of only one cell layer thick — allowing nutrients to pass through easily
  • Has a network of blood capillaries — absorb simple sugars and amino acids → carried to the liver via the hepatic portal vein
  • Has a lacteal (lymph vessel) — absorbs glycerol, fatty acids, and fat-soluble vitamins
  • Together the villi hugely increase the surface area for absorption
Complex molecule Broken down into Absorbed via
Starch (carbohydrate)GlucoseBlood capillaries
ProteinsAmino acidsBlood capillaries
FatsFatty acids + GlycerolLacteals (lymph)

5.6 The Large Intestine

By the time food reaches the large intestine, most nutrients have been absorbed. The large intestine's main job is to remove water from undigested material and form solid waste.

The large intestine has three parts:

  • Caecum — the pouch at the beginning; the appendix (finger-like, no clear function) hangs from it
  • Colon (ascending, transverse, descending) — absorbs remaining fluids and salts; the descending colon stores faeces (undigested material + bacteria + water + bile pigments)
  • Rectum — stores faeces until they are expelled via the anus (egestion)

5.7 The Liver

The liver is the largest internal organ and plays a vital role in digestion and metabolism:

  • Produces bile (stored in the gallbladder; released into the duodenum via the bile duct)
  • Neutralises stomach acid
  • Receives absorbed nutrients from the small intestine via the hepatic portal vein and processes them (stores glucose as glycogen, converts amino acids, detoxifies substances)

5.8 Disorders of the Gut

Diarrhea
Frequent, watery, loose bowel movements caused by fast peristalsis — food moves so quickly through the colon that water cannot be absorbed. Causes: bacteria, viruses, lack of clean water. Treatment: ORS (oral rehydration salts — water + salts + nutrients).
Constipation
Hard faeces that are difficult to eliminate. Caused by excessive water absorption from the colon, low fibre intake, dehydration, or certain medications (containing iron, calcium, or aluminium). Treatment: dietary changes, exercise, laxatives.
Peptic Ulcer
A sore (wound) in the lining of the digestive tract. Types: gastric ulcer (stomach), duodenal ulcer, esophageal ulcer. Caused by: aspirin/anti-inflammatory drugs, smoking, coffee, colas, spicy food. Symptoms: abdominal pain, nausea, loss of appetite. Treatment: alkaline medicines, avoiding spicy food.
