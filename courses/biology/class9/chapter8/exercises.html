Section 1 — Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

Encircle the correct answer. Correct option is marked in green.

(i) Select the mismatched pair:
A. Protein → Amino acid
B. Carbohydrate → Glucose
C. Fats → Starch
D. Nucleic acid → Nucleotide
C is mismatched. Fats are broken down into fatty acids and glycerol — NOT starch. Starch is a carbohydrate. All other pairs are correctly matched: protein → amino acids; carbohydrate → glucose; nucleic acid → nucleotides.
(ii) Deficiency of Vitamin K causes:
A. Rickets
B. Anemia
C. Scurvy
D. Beriberi
Note: None of the options listed are actually caused by Vitamin K deficiency. According to the STB textbook, Vitamin K deficiency causes a bleeding disorder (the blood cannot clot properly). Rickets = Vitamin D, Anemia = Iron, Scurvy = Vitamin C, Beriberi = Vitamin B. This question appears to have an error in its options — the correct disease (bleeding disorder) is not listed. In an exam, eliminate all options you know are wrong and flag this as a defective question.
(iii) The period of rapid growth and development is called:
A. Childhood
B. Adulthood
C. Adolescence
D. Both A and B
Adolescence is the period of rapid growth and development when puberty occurs. During this stage, the demand for energy and most nutrients is relatively high.
(iv) Deficiency of dietary protein causes an osmotic imbalance in the gastrointestinal system causing:
A. Marasmus
B. Edema
C. Diarrhea
D. Kwashiorkor
Kwashiorkor is caused specifically by protein deficiency. The lack of protein disturbs the osmotic balance in the gut, causing swelling (edema) of the abdomen. Marasmus is energy deficiency; edema is a symptom, not the disease.
(v) An animal that migrates great distances would obtain the greatest benefit from storing its energy as:
A. Fats
B. Carbohydrates
C. Protein
D. Minerals
Fats are the most energy-efficient storage form — 1 gram of fat provides about 9 kcal, compared to only 4 kcal per gram for carbohydrates or protein. For a long migration, fats give the most energy per kilogram of body weight carried.
(vi) Which of the following vitamins is correctly associated with its use?
A. Vitamin K → Production of white blood cells
B. Vitamin C → Curing rickets
C. Vitamin E → Protection of skin from cancer
D. Vitamin A → Incorporated into the visual pigment of the eye
Vitamin A is correctly matched. It is a component of rhodopsin, the visual pigment in the retina of the eye. Deficiency of Vitamin A causes night blindness. Vitamin K is for blood clotting, Vitamin C prevents scurvy, Vitamin D (not C) cures rickets.
(vii) Which of the following statements describes pepsin?
A. It is manufactured by the pancreas
B. It helps stabilize fat-water emulsions
C. It splits maltose into monosaccharides
D. It begins the hydrolysis of proteins in the stomach
Pepsin is the active enzyme in the stomach that begins digesting proteins into polypeptides and shorter peptide chains. It is made in the stomach (not the pancreas), and it is inactive as pepsinogen until HCl activates it. Bile stabilizes fat emulsions; other enzymes split maltose.
(viii) Which of the following is true of bile salts?
A. They are enzymes
B. They are manufactured by the pancreas
C. They emulsify fats in the duodenum
D. They increase the efficiency of pepsin action
Bile salts emulsify fats — they break large fat globules into tiny droplets, greatly increasing the surface area for lipase enzymes to work on. Bile is made by the liver (not the pancreas) and is NOT an enzyme.
(ix) In the human digestive system, the trachea and oesophagus both connect to the:
A. Large intestine
B. Stomach
C. Pharynx
D. Rectum
Both the trachea (windpipe, goes to the lungs) and the oesophagus (food pipe, goes to the stomach) open into the pharynx (throat). The epiglottis flap covers the trachea opening during swallowing to prevent food from entering the lungs.
(x) All of the following are sources of calcium EXCEPT:
A. Red meat
B. Green leafy vegetables
C. Broccoli
D. Nuts
Red meat is NOT a good source of calcium. Calcium-rich foods include green leafy vegetables, broccoli, nuts, dairy (milk, cheese), soya beans, bread, and fish. Red meat is rich in protein and iron, not calcium.

Section 2 — Fill in the Blanks

(i) Sea food is an excellent source of protein because it's usually low in ________.

Fat. Seafood (fish, shellfish) provides high-quality protein with relatively little saturated fat, making it a healthy protein source.

(ii) Iron deficiency is associated with decreased cognitive abilities and resistance to ________.

Disease. Iron deficiency (and the anemia it causes) weakens the immune system, reducing the body's ability to fight infections and disease.

(iii) Wave of rhythmic movements of muscle contraction and relaxation called ________.

Peristalsis. Peristalsis is the automatic wave-like movement along the alimentary canal that pushes food from the oesophagus all the way to the rectum.

(iv) Liquid secreted by the pancreas, which contains a variety of ________.

Enzymes. Pancreatic juice contains several digestive enzymes: trypsinogen (digests proteins), pancreatic lipase (digests fats), and amylase (digests starch).

(v) Living organisms which derive their food from dead and decaying organic materials called ________.

Saprotrophes (also written saprotrophs). They release external enzymes that break down dead organic matter and then absorb the digested products. Examples: fungi (moulds, mushrooms, yeast) and many bacteria.

(vi) Nutrients that are needed in the highest concentration called ________.

Macronutrients. Macronutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus, calcium, magnesium, etc.) are required by plants in larger quantities than micronutrients (zinc, copper, iron, etc.).

(vii) Fertilizers are substances containing chemical elements such as manure or mixture of ________.

Nitrates. Fertilizers supply key nutrients — especially nitrogen (in the form of nitrates), phosphorus, and potassium — to supplement soil nutrients and improve plant growth.

(viii) High solubility of fertilizers also degrades ecosystems through ________.

Eutrophication. When fertilizers wash into water bodies, they cause an excess of nutrients (especially nitrogen and phosphorus), triggering explosive algae growth. This depletes oxygen in the water and kills aquatic life.

(ix) Carbohydrates which are digested quickly are called ________.

Simple carbohydrates. Simple carbohydrates (like glucose and sucrose) have a simple molecular structure and are quickly broken down and absorbed, giving a rapid energy boost. Examples: table sugar, white rice, fruits.

(x) Each gram of fat supplies the body with about ________.

9 kcal (kilocalories). Fats supply about 9 kcal per gram — more than double the energy from carbohydrates or proteins (which each give about 4 kcal per gram). This is why fats are called the most energy-efficient form of food.

Section 3 — Define the Following Terms

(i) Vitamins

Vitamins are organic molecules that an organism needs in small quantities for the proper functioning of its metabolism. They cannot be synthesised by the body in sufficient amounts, so they must come from food. They are essential for maintaining normal health and development. Vitamins are of two types: fat-soluble (A, D, E, K) and water-soluble (B, C).

(ii) Malnutrition

Malnutrition is a condition that occurs when the body does not get enough nutrients, or gets them in the wrong proportions. It results from a poor diet, lack of food, or imbalanced food intake. Consuming less than 2,100 calories per day leads to under-nourishment. It includes both under-nutrition (too little food) and over-nutrition (too much food).

(iii) Goiter

Goiter is a condition in which the thyroid gland becomes enlarged, causing visible swelling in the neck. It is caused by an insufficient amount of iodine in the diet. The thyroid gland uses iodine to produce hormones that control the body's normal functioning and growth.

(iv) Anemia

Anemia literally means "a lack of blood." It is a condition where the number of red blood cells drops below normal levels. It is most commonly caused by iron deficiency — without enough iron, the body cannot make sufficient haemoglobin, so not enough functioning RBCs are formed. The person becomes weak and suffers from an oxygen shortage throughout the body.

(v) Constipation

Constipation is a condition where a person experiences hard faeces that are difficult to eliminate. It is caused by excessive absorption of water in the colon (making faeces too hard), insufficient dietary fibre, dehydration, or certain medications. Treatment includes dietary changes, increased exercise, and laxatives.

(vi) Obesity

Obesity is a medical condition in which excess body fat has accumulated to the extent that it negatively affects health. It is most commonly caused by excessive food intake (especially high-fat and high-carbohydrate foods), lack of physical activity, and genetic factors. It is sometimes called the "mother disease" because it leads to heart disease, hypertension, and diabetes.

(vii) Starvation

Starvation is a severe deficiency in caloric energy intake — it is the most extreme form of malnutrition. Prolonged starvation causes the body to consume its own muscles and organs for energy, leading to permanent organ damage and eventually death.

(viii) Ingestion

Ingestion is the intake of food into the body through the mouth. It is the first step of holozoic nutrition and digestion. In complex animals it involves eating; in unicellular organisms like Amoeba it involves engulfing food with pseudopodia.

(ix) Chyme

Chyme is the thick, paste-like, acidic liquid formed when food is mixed with gastric juice in the stomach. After the stomach churns and chemically digests the food, the resulting semi-liquid mixture (chyme) is gradually released through the pyloric sphincter into the small intestine for further digestion.

(x) Ulcer

An ulcer (specifically, a peptic ulcer) is a sore or open wound in the lining of the digestive tract. Types include gastric ulcer (stomach), duodenal ulcer, and esophageal ulcer. It is caused by the breakdown of the protective mucus layer by acidic gastric juice, often worsened by aspirin, smoking, coffee, or spicy food.

Section 5 — Short Answer Questions

(i) Why are fertilizers necessary for plants?

Fertilizers are necessary for plants because:

  • Plants need a steady supply of mineral elements (especially nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium) from the soil.
  • Over time, continuous cropping removes these minerals from the soil faster than they can naturally replenish.
  • Fertilizers supplement soil nutrients, ensuring plants have enough minerals for healthy growth, development, and reproduction.
  • They lead to increased yields, faster growth, stronger resistance to pests, and more attractive produce.
(ii) How are stomach linings protected from the acidic environment?

The stomach produces highly acidic gastric juice (HCl) which could damage the stomach lining itself. Protection is provided by mucus:

  • Glands in the stomach wall produce a thick layer of mucus.
  • This mucus coats the inner lining of the stomach, forming a physical barrier between the acidic gastric juice and the stomach cells.
  • Mucus is alkaline and viscous, so it neutralises the acid on contact with the lining.
  • If the mucus layer is damaged (e.g. by aspirin, bacteria like H. pylori, or excessive acid), the stomach wall can develop an ulcer.
(iii) Why is nitrogen essential for plants?

Nitrogen is essential for plants because it is a core component of several vital biological molecules:

  • Amino acids — which are the building blocks of proteins
  • Chlorophyll — the green pigment needed for photosynthesis
  • Nucleic acids (DNA and RNA) — needed for cell division and genetic information
  • Enzymes — which control all metabolic reactions in the plant

Nitrogen is needed in the largest quantities of all mineral elements. Nitrogen-deficient plants show stunted growth, reduced yields, and pale green leaves.

(iv) Why are fats called the most efficient form of food?

Fats are called the most efficient form of food because:

  • Each gram of fat provides approximately 9 kcal of energy — more than double the 4 kcal/gram provided by carbohydrates or proteins.
  • This means the body can store a large amount of energy in a relatively small mass of fat.
  • Fats are the slowest but most energy-rich source — they release energy over a long period.
  • The body deposits excess fat in the abdomen (omental fat) and under the skin (subcutaneous fat) as a long-term energy reserve.
(v) Why are grinding and lubrication necessary for swallowing?

Grinding (mastication) is necessary because:

  • The oesophagus is a narrow tube — it can only pass small pieces of food.
  • Digestive enzymes cannot efficiently act on large, whole pieces of food — grinding increases the surface area for enzymes to work on.

Lubrication (by saliva) is necessary because:

  • Saliva mixes with the ground food and makes it slippery and moist, forming a bolus.
  • A dry lump of food would be difficult or impossible to swallow and could scratch the oesophagus.
  • The mucus in saliva helps the bolus slide smoothly through the pharynx and down the oesophagus.

Section 6 — Long Answer Questions

(i) Describe the environmental hazards related to chemical fertilizers.

While chemical fertilizers increase crop yields, their excessive and improper use causes several serious environmental hazards:

1. Reduced soil nutrient-holding capacity:
Massive application of inorganic fertilizers damages the natural structure of soil, reducing its ability to hold and slowly release nutrients. The soil becomes dependent on synthetic inputs.

2. Eutrophication:
Chemical fertilizers have high solubility — they dissolve easily and wash into rivers, lakes, and groundwater. This excess of nitrogen and phosphorus in water bodies causes eutrophication: explosive growth of algae (algal blooms), depletion of dissolved oxygen, and death of fish and other aquatic organisms.

3. Greenhouse gas emission:
Storage and application of nitrogen-containing fertilizers results in the release of nitrous oxide (N₂O), a greenhouse gas far more potent than CO₂. This contributes to global warming and climate change.

4. Soil acidity:
Ammonia gas (NH₃) is emitted from applied inorganic fertilizers. This extra ammonia increases soil acidity, harming organisms that prefer neutral or alkaline soil conditions.

5. Pest problems:
Excess nitrogen stimulates rapid vegetative growth, which provides more food for pests. This increases pest reproduction rates, requiring even more pesticides.

6. Nutrient imbalance pollution:
Applying more fertilizer than crops need creates an excess of nutrients in the soil. This excess pollutes both soil and groundwater. A careful balance between soil nutrient content and crop requirements must be maintained — any excess causes some form of pollution.

(ii) Describe the function of the stomach and intestine with a suitable diagram of the human digestive system.

The Stomach:

The stomach is a J-shaped, muscular, expandable organ located in the upper left abdomen. It has three regions: cardiac, fundus, and pyloric.

  • Mechanical function: The stomach walls contract and relax (churning), physically breaking down food and mixing it with gastric juice.
  • Chemical function: Gastric glands secrete gastric juice containing: mucus (protects lining), HCl (creates acidic environment, kills microbes, activates pepsin), and pepsinogen → activated by HCl to form pepsin (digests proteins into polypeptides).
  • The stomach produces chyme — a semi-liquid mixture of food and gastric juice.
  • The pyloric sphincter controls the release of chyme into the small intestine.

The Small Intestine:

The small intestine (duodenum → jejunum → ileum) is where most chemical digestion and all absorption takes place.

  • Duodenum receives bile from the liver (via gallbladder and bile duct) and pancreatic juice from the pancreas.
  • Bile salts emulsify fats into tiny droplets, increasing surface area for lipase.
  • Pancreatic juice digests proteins (trypsinogen), fats (lipase), and starch (amylase).
  • Intestinal enzymes complete digestion of all four food groups.
  • The inner wall has villi — millions of tiny finger-like projections that hugely increase the surface area for absorption. Blood capillaries in villi absorb sugars and amino acids (→ hepatic portal vein → liver). Lacteals absorb fatty acids and glycerol (→ lymph system).

The Large Intestine:

  • Absorbs water and mineral salts from undigested material.
  • Forms solid faeces (undigested matter + bacteria + bile pigments + water).
  • Rectum stores faeces until expelled via the anus (egestion).
Mouth → Pharynx → Oesophagus → Stomach → Small Intestine (duodenum/jejunum/ileum) → Large Intestine → Rectum → Anus
(iii) What are vitamins? Describe the types of vitamins.

Vitamins are organic molecules that an organism needs in small quantities for the proper functioning of metabolism. They cannot be synthesised by the body in sufficient amounts and must come from food. They are essential for maintaining normal health, development, and preventing disease.

Vitamins are classified into two types based on their solubility:

Type 1: Fat-soluble Vitamins (A, D, E, K)

  • Dissolve in organic solvents (fats and oils)
  • Stored in body fat — not easily excreted; toxic if taken in excess
  • Less destroyed by cooking than water-soluble vitamins
  • Key vitamins:
    • Vitamin A — visual pigment; deficiency → night blindness, eye infections
    • Vitamin D — bone and teeth health; deficiency → rickets, osteomalacia
    • Vitamin K — blood clotting; deficiency → bleeding disorder
    • Vitamin E — antioxidant

Type 2: Water-soluble Vitamins (B and C)

  • Dissolve in water (H₂O)
  • Not stored in the body — excess is excreted in urine
  • Destroyed more readily by cooking or heating
  • Key vitamins:
    • Vitamin B (group) — nerve function, energy metabolism; deficiency → beriberi
    • Vitamin C — collagen synthesis, immune function; deficiency → scurvy (bleeding gums, poor wound healing)
(iv) Describe mineral deficiency diseases in humans.

Mineral deficiency diseases result from an insufficient intake of specific minerals. Important ones include:

1. Goiter (Iodine deficiency):

  • The thyroid gland needs iodine to produce hormones that control growth and metabolic rate.
  • Without enough iodine, the thyroid gland enlarges as it tries to compensate, causing visible swelling in the neck.
  • Prevention: use iodised salt.

2. Anemia (Iron deficiency):

  • Iron is needed to make haemoglobin (the oxygen-carrying pigment in RBCs).
  • Without enough iron, haemoglobin cannot be formed → fewer functional RBCs → inadequate oxygen delivery to body cells.
  • Symptoms: extreme fatigue, pale skin, weakness, shortness of breath, headache, brittle nails.
  • The most common mineral deficiency disease globally.

3. Calcium deficiency:

  • Calcium is essential for strong bones and teeth, muscle function, and blood clotting.
  • Deficiency leads to: muscle cramps (tetany), brittle bones, fainting, heart problems.
  • In children, severe deficiency leads to rickets (similar to vitamin D deficiency).

4. Over-intake of minerals:

  • Too much of certain nutrients (excess Vitamin D → calcium deposition in tissues; excess fat and carbohydrates → obesity and cardiovascular disease) can be as harmful as deficiency.
(v) Describe the effects of malnutrition on humans.

Malnutrition affects humans both physically and mentally, causing a wide range of serious health consequences:

1. Starvation:
The most extreme effect. When the body receives far too few calories, it first uses up glycogen stores, then breaks down fat, and finally uses muscle and organ tissue for energy. Prolonged starvation causes permanent organ damage and, if untreated, death.

2. Heart disease:
An unbalanced diet high in fats raises blood cholesterol levels. Cholesterol gets deposited on the inner walls of blood vessels (atherosclerosis), narrowing them and obstructing blood flow. This can lead to heart attacks and strokes.

3. Constipation:
A diet lacking in dietary fibre and irregular meal patterns leads to constipation — hard, difficult-to-pass faeces. This causes discomfort and, long-term, can damage the bowel.

4. Obesity:
Consuming too many calories (especially from fats and carbohydrates) combined with low physical activity leads to excessive fat accumulation. Obesity is called the "mother disease" because it significantly increases the risk of Type 2 diabetes, hypertension, heart disease, and joint problems.

5. Protein Energy Malnutrition (PEM):
In children: Kwashiorkor (protein deficiency → edema, moon face) and Marasmus (energy deficiency → extreme emaciation). These are the most severe forms of malnutrition and a leading cause of child mortality in developing countries.

6. Weakened immune system:
Malnutrition compromises the immune system, reducing the body's ability to fight infections. The skin, stomach acid, and blood antibodies all function less effectively when the body is malnourished.
