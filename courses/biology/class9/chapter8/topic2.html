Chapter 8 · Topic 2

Mineral Nutrition in Plants

Plants make food from sunlight, but they also need minerals from the soil to stay healthy — just like we need vitamins and minerals in our diet. In this topic, we learn which minerals plants need, what happens if they run short, and how fertilizers help (and hurt) the environment.

2.1 What is Mineral Nutrition?

Mineral Nutrition (in plants)
The process involving the absorption, distribution, and utilisation of mineral substances by plants for their growth and development.

Plants need many elements to survive. These are divided into two broad groups:

Group Where obtained Examples
Framework elements From air and water Carbon (C), Hydrogen (H)
Mineral elements From soil in ionic forms See table below

Mineral elements are split into macronutrients and micronutrients:

Type Needed in Examples
Macronutrients Large quantities Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), Calcium (Ca), Magnesium (Mg)
Micronutrients Small quantities Zinc (Zn), Copper (Cu), Manganese (Mn), Iron (Fe), Boron (B), Molybdenum (Mo), Chlorine (Cl), Nickel (Ni)
Important note

Macronutrients are NOT "more important" than micronutrients — the names just mean one is needed in larger amounts. Both are essential for healthy plant growth.

2.2 Role of Nitrogen (N)

Nitrogen is one of the most important elements for plants. It is needed:

  • To make amino acids — the building blocks of proteins
  • To make chlorophyll — the green pigment for photosynthesis
  • To make nucleic acids (DNA and RNA)
  • To make enzymes that control all chemical reactions in the plant

Of all the elements plants absorb from the soil, nitrogen is needed in the largest amounts.

⚠ Nitrogen Deficiency Symptoms:
  • Stunted growth — the plant is much smaller than normal
  • Reduced yields — fewer fruits, seeds, or leaves produced
  • Pale green foliage — leaves lose their dark green colour and become yellowish-green

2.3 Role of Magnesium (Mg)

Magnesium is important for plants because:

  • It is a component of chlorophyll — every chlorophyll molecule contains one magnesium atom at its centre. Without Mg, plants cannot make chlorophyll and cannot photosynthesise properly.
  • Many enzymes in plant cells require magnesium as a cofactor to work correctly.
⚠ Magnesium Deficiency Symptoms:
  • Most common on sandy soils (which leach minerals easily), especially after heavy rainfall
  • Main symptom: interveinal chlorosis — the veins of the leaf stay dark green, but the areas between the veins turn yellow
  • The bottom leaves are always affected first (because the plant moves Mg to new growth first)
Easy memory trick

Mg is in chloroMgyll. That's how you remember that magnesium is a part of chlorophyll. Lose Mg → lose green colour → yellow leaves.

2.4 Fertilizers

Fertilizers
Substances containing chemical elements (such as manure or mixtures of nitrates) that improve the growth of plants by providing nutrients to the soil.

Fertilizers help by:

  • Supplying the three most important plant nutrients: Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), and Potassium (K)
  • Producing more fruit, faster growth, more attractive flowers
  • Helping plants develop tolerance against pests (weeds, insects, diseases)

Fertilizers can be organic (manure, compost — complex, slow to break down) or inorganic/chemical (factory-made, immediately available to plants).

2.5 Environmental Hazards of Chemical Fertilizers

While fertilizers increase crop yields, using too many chemical fertilizers causes serious environmental problems:

1. Reduces soil nutrient-holding capacity

Massive use of inorganic fertilizers damages the soil structure, reducing its ability to hold and release nutrients naturally.

2. Eutrophication

Fertilizers dissolve easily and wash into rivers and lakes. This causes eutrophication — an excess of nitrogen and phosphorus in water, leading to explosive algae growth that kills fish and other aquatic life by removing oxygen from the water.

3. Greenhouse gas emission

Storage and use of nitrogen fertilizers releases nitrous oxide (N₂O) — a potent greenhouse gas that contributes to global warming.

4. Soil acidity

Ammonia gas (NH₃) released from inorganic fertilizers increases the acidity of soil, harming plants that prefer neutral or alkaline conditions.

5. Pest problems

Excess nitrogen fertilizers increase pest populations by increasing their reproduction rate.

6. Nutrient imbalance

Using more fertilizer than crops need creates nutrient pollution. A careful balance of soil nutrients and crop requirements must be maintained — excess in any nutrient causes pollution of some kind.
