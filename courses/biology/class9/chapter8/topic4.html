Balanced Diet & Malnutrition

Eating the right amount of the right nutrients is called a balanced diet. When the body gets too little, too much, or the wrong nutrients, malnutrition results — and it causes serious health problems around the world.

4.1 Balanced Diet — Related to Age, Sex & Activity

A balanced diet provides all nutrients in the right proportions for the body's needs. These needs change throughout life and depend on age, sex, and level of physical activity.

Childhood
Children grow rapidly and become more active, so their energy requirements increase fast. However, children have small stomachs — so they need small, frequent meals (not one large meal) to get enough energy for their age.
Adolescence
Adolescence is the period of rapid growth and development when puberty occurs. The demand for energy and most nutrients is high. Boys need more protein and energy than girls because they develop more muscle mass and larger bodies.
Adulthood
Adults need a good supply of protein, calcium, iron, vitamin A and vitamin D. Calcium and vitamin D together help maintain strong bones and teeth throughout life.
Men vs Women

Men generally need more energy than women of the same age because men have more muscle tissue and a larger body size. Growing boys also need more body-building nutrients (proteins, calcium) than girls of the same age.

4.2 Malnutrition

Malnutrition
A condition that occurs when the body does not get enough nutrients, or gets them in the wrong amounts. It results from a poor diet or lack of food. Consuming less than 2,100 calories a day means a person is under-nourished.

There are two types of malnutrition:

Type Characteristic
Chronic malnutrition Delayed growth in children (the child is shorter than normal for their age)
Acute malnutrition Insufficient weight for the child's height (emaciation — the child looks very thin). Can be moderate or severe.
WHO fact

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), malnutrition is the gravest single threat to global public health. It contributes to 45% of all deaths in children under 5 years of age.

Three major nutrient deficiencies of global concern:

  • Vitamin A deficiency — the world's most common cause of preventable child blindness
  • Iron deficiency — causes decreased cognitive (thinking) ability and reduced resistance to disease
  • Iodine deficiency — the major preventable cause of mental retardation worldwide

4.3 Protein Energy Malnutrition (PEM)

PEM refers to inadequate availability or absorption of energy and proteins in the body. It is the leading cause of death in children in developing countries, including Pakistan.

Kwashiorkor

Kwashiorkor is caused by protein deficiency (not enough protein in the diet, though calories may be sufficient).

  • Protein deficiency causes an osmotic imbalance in the gastrointestinal system
  • This causes swelling (edema) — especially in the belly (the characteristic "swollen belly" of starving children)
Kwashiorkor features
Moon face (puffy, rounded face)
Misery (child is very unhappy, irritable)
Thin muscles but fat is still present
Edema (swelling, especially in belly and feet)
Underweight
Hair changes (thin, discoloured, brittle hair)

Marasmus

Marasmus is caused by severe energy deficiency — the child doesn't get enough food (both calories and protein are severely lacking).

  • Body weight is reduced to less than 62% of the normal expected weight for the child's age
  • The child looks extremely emaciated (like a skeleton with skin)
Marasmus features
Normal hair (hair changes less prominent than in kwashiorkor)
Old man's face (wrinkled, aged appearance)
No edema (no swelling)
Thin muscles AND thin fat (almost no fat deposits)
Very underweight (<62% of normal)

4.4 Mineral Deficiency Diseases

Goiter (Iodine Deficiency)

  • Cause: insufficient iodine in the diet
  • Iodine is needed by the thyroid gland to produce hormones that control growth and normal body functions
  • Without iodine, the thyroid gland enlarges and forms a visible swelling in the neck

Anemia (Iron Deficiency)

  • Cause: insufficient iron in the diet
  • Iron is needed to make haemoglobin — the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen
  • Without enough iron → not enough haemoglobin → not enough RBCs → oxygen shortage throughout the body
  • The term "anemia" literally means "a lack of blood"
  • Symptoms: weakness, fatigue, shortness of breath, pale skin

Over-intake of Nutrients

  • Too many carbohydrates and fats → obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease
  • Excess Vitamin A → loss of appetite, liver problems
  • Excess Vitamin D → abnormal calcium deposition in tissues

4.5 Effects of Malnutrition

Starvation
The most extreme form of malnutrition — severe deficiency in caloric energy intake. Prolonged starvation causes permanent organ damage and eventually death.
Heart Disease
An unbalanced diet high in fatty foods increases blood cholesterol. Cholesterol gets deposited in blood vessels, obstructing blood flow and leading to heart attacks and strokes.
Constipation
Irregular meals and lack of dietary fibre lead to hardened faeces that are difficult to eliminate.
Obesity
When excess body fat accumulates to a level that negatively affects health. Often called "mother disease" because it leads to heart problems, hypertension, diabetes, and more.

4.6 Social Problems Related to Malnutrition

Malnutrition isn't just a health issue — it has deep social causes:

  • Food insecurity: some countries cannot produce enough food for their populations, and cannot afford to import it either. Drought and floods make this worse.
  • Poverty: even when food is available, poor households cannot buy it. In developing countries, food access depends heavily on household income.
  • Inequality: in many developing countries, boys receive more food than girls, putting girls at higher risk of malnutrition from an early age.
  • Risk of infection: malnutrition weakens the immune system, making the body less able to fight off bacteria, viruses, and other invaders.
Pakistan context

Malnutrition is one of the most prevalent public health problems in Pakistan. Poverty, lack of education, poor environmental hygiene, and food fads are some of the reasons for its high prevalence.
