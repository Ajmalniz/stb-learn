Chapter 4 · Topic 5

Animal & Plant Tissues

Single cells are powerful — but in big animals and plants, cells of similar type team up into tissues. This topic covers the 4 animal tissues and the major plant tissues with their structures and jobs.

5.1 What is a Tissue?

Tissue
A group of similar cells that work together to perform a common function.

Cells in the small intestine that absorb nutrients look very different from cells that move your body — because they do different jobs. Cells specialise, and similar specialists join into tissues.

5.2 Animal Tissues — Four Main Types

Humans and other large multicellular animals are made of four basic tissues:

  1. Epithelial tissue
  2. Connective tissue
  3. Muscle tissue
  4. Nervous tissue

5.3 Epithelial Tissue

Epithelial tissue covers the body surface, lines the spaces inside the body, and forms glands. Examples: outer layer of your skin, lining of the small intestine.

Epithelial cells are polarised — they have a top side (apical) and a bottom side (basal).

Classification by Number of Layers

  • Simple epithelium — a single layer of cells
  • Stratified epithelium — two or more layers of cells (only the bottom layer touches the basement membrane)

5.4 Connective Tissue

Connective tissue connects or binds different types of cells and other tissues together. It holds structures in the body together (like tendons).

Types of Connective Tissue

Type Description Example
CartilageDense supporting tissue; semisolid to flexible matrix; limited ground substancePinna of ear, joints, nose
BoneHard supporting tissue; can be compact (dense) or spongy; contains osteoblasts & osteocytesSkeleton
AdiposeStores excess energy / fat; provides cushioningUnder skin, around organs
BloodFluid connective tissue; contains red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets, plasmaThroughout the body in blood vessels
Yes — blood is a tissue!

Blood may look liquid, but it is officially a connective tissue because it connects different parts of the body and shares the basic structure of cells suspended in a matrix (plasma).

5.5 Muscle Tissue

Muscle tissue contains cells responsible for the contraction of muscles. There are three types:

Type Striated? Control Where found
SkeletalYes — stripedVoluntaryAttached to bones by tendons (arms, legs)
CardiacYes — stripedInvoluntaryWalls of the heart only
SmoothNo (unstriped)InvoluntaryWalls of blood vessels, digestive tract, uterus, urinary bladder
Why involuntary muscle is great

You don't have to think about beating your heart or pushing food through your gut — cardiac and smooth muscle do it for you automatically.

5.6 Nervous Tissue

Nervous tissue is made of neurons — cells that transmit information using electrical nerve impulses.

Where found: brain, spinal cord, nerves.

Functions:

  • Coordinates and controls body activities
  • Stimulates muscle contraction
  • Creates awareness of the environment
  • Plays a major role in emotions, memory and reasoning

Types of neurons: sensory neuron, motor neuron, multi-polar neuron. Parts of a typical neuron: cell body, nucleus, dendrites, axon, nerve fibre (covered in a sheath), motor or sensory endings.

5.7 Plant Tissues — Two Main Categories

Plant cells are also grouped into tissues. There are two main categories:

  • Meristematic tissues — cells can divide.
  • Permanent tissues — cells cannot divide.

5.8 Meristematic Tissues

Cells of meristematic tissues have the ability to divide. They are:

  • Thin-walled
  • Have large nuclei
  • Contain many small vacuoles
  • Have no intercellular spaces — packed tightly

Two main types:

Type Position Growth type
Apical meristemApex (tips) of roots and stemsPrimary growth — increase in length
Lateral meristemSides of roots and shootsSecondary growth — increase in girth (thickness)

5.9 Permanent Tissues

Permanent tissues originate from meristematic tissues. Their cells cannot divide and may have intercellular spaces. There are two types:

  • Simple permanent tissue — made of only one cell type
  • Complex (compound) permanent tissue — made of more than one cell type

5.10 Simple Permanent Tissues

(i) Epidermal Tissues

  • Single layer of cells covering the plant body — acts as a barrier between environment and internal tissues.
  • In roots — they absorb water and minerals.
  • On stems and leaves — they secrete cutin (the coating = cuticle) which prevents water loss.
  • Specialised structures include root hairs and stomata (with guard cells).

(ii) Ground Tissues — Parenchyma

  • Made of parenchyma cells — most abundant cells in plants.
  • Generally spherical, flat at points of contact.
  • Thin primary cell walls; large vacuoles for storage of food.
  • In leaves they are called mesophyll — site of photosynthesis.
  • In other parts — site of respiration and protein synthesis.
  • Some parenchyma cells can still divide to repair injuries.

(iii) Supporting Tissues

Two types provide strength and flexibility:

Tissue Cell wall Living or dead? Location / role
CollenchymaUnevenly thickened primary wallLivingCortex of young stems, midribs of leaves, petals — flexible support
SclerenchymaRigid secondary wall hardened with ligninMostly deadHard support; cannot elongate. Lignin = main component of wood.

5.11 Complex (Compound) Tissues — Xylem & Phloem

Found only in vascular plants. Made of more than one cell type.

(i) Xylem

  • Transports water and dissolved minerals from roots to the aerial parts.
  • Thick, rigid secondary walls (because of lignin) → also provides support.
  • Two main cell types: vessels (thick walls, no end walls, join into long tubes) and tracheids (slender cells with overlapping ends).

(ii) Phloem

  • Transports dissolved organic matter (food) between different parts of the plant.
  • Main cell types: sieve tube cells (long, with small pores in end walls — join into long sieve tubes) and companion cells (narrow, elongated parenchymatous cells next to sieve tubes that help conduction and make proteins for them).
Easy memory

Xylem = X-tra strong, takes water up. Phloem = Food (organic matter), flows in both directions.

5.12 Summary — Plant Tissue Tree

  • Meristematic (can divide)
    • Apical
    • Lateral
  • Permanent (cannot divide)
    • Simple: Epidermis, Parenchyma (incl. Chlorenchyma), Collenchyma, Sclerenchyma
    • Complex: Xylem (vessels, tracheids), Phloem (sieve tubes, companion cells)
