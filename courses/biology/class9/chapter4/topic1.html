Microscope & Cell Theory

Every living thing — from a blade of grass to your own skin — is built from cells. But cells are tiny, so we cannot see them with the naked eye. The microscope is the instrument that opened this hidden world and gave us the cell theory.

1.1 What is a Microscope?

Microscope
An instrument designed to produce magnified visual or photographic images of objects that are too small to be seen with the naked eye.

Two parameters are especially important in microscopy:

Magnification
The enlargement of an image. A microscope combines lenses to produce very high magnification values.
Resolution
The smallest distance between two points on a specimen that can still be distinguished as two separate objects. It measures the clarity of the image.
Why both matter

If a microscope has high magnification but low resolution, you only get a bigger blurry image. Both magnification and resolution are needed for a clear picture of something smaller than 0.1 mm.

1.2 Brief History of the Microscope

  • Zacharias Janssen (1590) — generally believed to be the first to invent the compound microscope. It was a tube with lenses at each end and magnified 3× to 9×.
  • Robert Hooke — improved the compound microscope and used it to observe organisms.
  • Anton van Leeuwenhoek — Dutch biologist who made many discoveries with his microscope, including the first protozoa and bacteria.

1.3 Light Microscope (LM)

In a light microscope, visible light passes through the specimen (the biological sample). A photograph taken through a microscope is called a micrograph.

The magnification of a light microscope is found by multiplying the powers of two lenses:

Total magnification = power of objective lens × power of eyepiece

Example: A 10× objective × 10× eyepiece = 100×.

Common objective lenses are 4×, 10×, 40×. With a 10× eyepiece they give magnifications of 40×, 100×, and 400×.

1.4 Electron Microscope (EM)

Electron microscopes use a beam of electrons instead of visible light. Because electrons have a much shorter wavelength than visible light, electron microscopes give much higher resolution than light microscopes.

  • Resolution: as small as 0.2 nm
  • Magnification: up to 250,000×
  • Limitation: a live cell cannot be imaged by an electron microscope

Two Major Types of EM

Type How it works Used for
SEM (Scanning)Electron beam moves back and forth across the surface of a specimenDetailed image of the 3D surface
TEM (Transmission)Sample cut into extremely thin slices; electrons pass through themDetailed images of internal structures of cells

1.6 History of Cell Theory

Before microscopes, no one knew cells existed. Discoveries built up step by step over 200 years:

Year Scientist Discovery
1665Robert HookeSaw honeycomb-like structures in a cork slice (dead tissue — only cell walls). Coined the term "cell".
1670Anton van LeeuwenhoekFirst saw living cells in pond water.
1683Anton van LeeuwenhoekDiscovered the first protozoa and bacteria.
1833Robert BrownDiscovered the nucleus in plant cells.
1839Schleiden & SchwannProposed the original Cell Theory: all plants and animals are made of cells.
1840Albrecht von RoellikerShowed that sperm and eggs are also cells.
1845Carl Heinrich BraunReworked the cell theory, calling cells the basic unit of life.
1855Rudolf VirchowAdded the third part of cell theory — all cells arise from pre-existing cells (not de novo).
1862Louis PasteurProvided experimental proof that cells arise only from existing cells.

1.7 The Cell Theory — Postulates

The modern Cell Theory includes the following points:

  1. All living organisms are made of one or more cells.
  2. The cell is the fundamental unit of structure and function in all living organisms.
  3. New cells are derived from pre-existing cells by cell division.
  4. The cell contains the hereditary material (DNA) which is passed from generation to generation.
Key idea

Schleiden + Schwann gave the first two points. Rudolf Virchow added the third point in 1855. The fourth point (about hereditary material) was added much later as scientists discovered DNA.

1.8 What about Viruses, Prions and Viroids?

According to the first principle, all organisms are made of cells. But viruses, prions and viroids are not composed of cells. They are called sub-cellular or acellular particles.

  • They do not run any metabolic activity on their own.
  • But they do show some features of life — they can increase in number (inside a host) and pass their traits to the next generation.

So they sit at the edge between living and non-living, and are excluded from the cell theory and the five-kingdom system.
