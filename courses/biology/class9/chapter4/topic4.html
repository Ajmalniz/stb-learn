Cell Size & Transport across Membranes

Why are cells small? Why don't they grow as big as a football? The answer is the surface area to volume ratio. This topic explains why cells stay small, and how substances move in and out of them.

4.1 How Big Are Cells?

  • Smallest cells: Mycoplasma bacteria — about 0.1 to 1.0 μm
  • Largest cells (by volume): bird eggs — packed with nutrients for the developing chick
  • Longest cells: some muscle cells and nerve cells — can stretch a long way to pull body parts or carry signals
  • Tiny but important: human red blood cells are only 8 μm across — small enough to squeeze through capillaries

Cell size and shape are closely related to function: bird eggs are bulky for nutrients, nerve cells are long to send messages, RBCs are small to fit through tiny vessels.

4.2 Why Cells Cannot Be Too Large — The Surface Area : Volume Ratio

A cell takes in nutrients and gets rid of waste through its surface (cell membrane). The need for nutrients and waste removal is directly related to its volume (the amount of cell inside).

As a cell grows bigger:

  • Its volume increases very fast (in cubed units — μm³)
  • Its surface area increases more slowly (in squared units — μm²)
  • So surface area : volume ratio falls
  • The cell membrane can no longer keep up with the cell's needs → cell dies

Worked Example — 1 Big Cube vs 27 Small Cubes

Both cases below have the same total volume, but very different total surface areas:

Case Volume Surface area
1 large cube (side 30 μm) 30 × 30 × 30 = 27,000 μm³ 6 × (30 × 30) = 5,400 μm²
27 small cubes (side 10 μm each) 27 × (10 × 10 × 10) = 27,000 μm³ 27 × 600 = 16,200 μm²

Same volume, but the small cubes together have 3× more surface area. Small cells can serve their volume far more easily than large cells.

Big idea

In biology, more surface area = more function. That's why villi line your small intestine, why lungs have alveoli, and why cells stay small.

4.3 Movement Across Membranes — Overview

Cells must constantly exchange substances with their environment — taking in oxygen, water, glucose and ions, and removing waste like CO₂. Four main ways this happens:

4.4 Diffusion

Diffusion
The movement of substances from a region of high concentration to a region of low concentration, down the concentration gradient.
  • A passive process — no energy input needed.
  • Can happen across a living or non-living membrane.
  • Can occur in a liquid or gas.
  • Examples: diffusion of O₂, CO₂, water and other small molecules that can dissolve through the lipid bilayer.
Real life example

Drop a sugar cube in a glass of water — the sugar spreads out by diffusion until it is evenly distributed.

4.5 Osmosis

Osmosis
The movement of water through a selectively permeable membrane from a dilute solution to a concentrated solution.
  • A passive process — no energy needed.
  • Cell membranes let water pass but not most dissolved substances (e.g. salt, sugar).

Effect of Solutions on Cells

Solution Concentration What happens to the cell
HypotonicLower than inside the cellWater moves into the cell → cell swells. RBC may burst (haemolysis).
IsotonicSame as inside the cellNo net movement → cell stays the same.
HypertonicHigher than inside the cellWater moves out of the cell → cell shrinks. In plant cells, this is called plasmolysis.
Plasmolysis
In hypertonic conditions, a plant cell loses water and the cytoplasm shrinks away from the cell wall.

Where osmosis matters:

  • Plants: absorb water from the soil and transport it up to leaves.
  • Kidneys: keep the body's water and salt levels balanced.

Potato Osmoscope Activity

Place a hollowed potato containing concentrated sugar solution in a beaker of water. After 15–20 minutes, the level of the sugar solution rises inside the potato.

Conclusion: water moved from the beaker (dilute) through the potato cells (selectively permeable membranes) into the sugar solution (concentrated) — proving osmosis.

4.6 Facilitated Diffusion

Facilitated Diffusion
A special form of diffusion that allows the rapid exchange of specific substances through carrier proteins in the membrane.
  • Still passive — no energy needed.
  • Still moves from high → low concentration.
  • Uses carrier proteins that change shape to release the particle on the other side, or ion channels.
  • Used for: molecules too large or too polar to cross the lipid bilayer directly (e.g. glucose, ions).

4.7 Active Transport

Active Transport
The movement of substances against a concentration gradient (low → high) using an input of energy in the form of ATP.

Classic example: the sodium-potassium pump (Na⁺/K⁺ pump) — pumps Na⁺ out and K⁺ into the cell against their gradients using ATP. Essential for nerve impulses.

Key difference

Diffusion = downhill (high → low), no energy. Active transport = uphill (low → high), needs ATP.

4.8 Summary — All Four at a Glance

  • Diffusion — any molecule, downhill, no energy.
  • Osmosis — water only, downhill (in terms of water), no energy.
  • Facilitated diffusion — specific molecule via carrier, downhill, no energy.
  • Active transport — specific molecule via pump, uphill, needs ATP.
