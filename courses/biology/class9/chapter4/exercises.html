Section 1 — Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

Encircle the correct answer. Correct option is marked in green.

(i) What is responsible for the high resolution of the electron microscope?
A. High magnification
B. Short wavelength of the electron beam
C. Use of heavy metal stains
D. Very thin section
Electrons have a much shorter wavelength than visible light. Shorter wavelength = better ability to separate two close points, so the electron microscope reaches a resolution as small as 0.2 nm.
(ii) What is a function of the rough endoplasmic reticulum?
A. Aerobic respiration
B. Intracellular digestion
C. Synthesis of steroids
D. Synthesis of protein
The rough ER has ribosomes attached to its surface, and ribosomes are the sites of protein synthesis. Aerobic respiration is in mitochondria; intracellular digestion is by lysosomes; steroids are made on the smooth ER.
(iii) Which statement about the fluid mosaic model of membrane structure is correct?
A. The less unsaturated the fatty acid, the more fluid nature.
B. The more unsaturated the fatty acid, the more fluid nature.
C. Higher the temperature, less fluid nature.
D. The lower the temperature, more fluid nature.
Unsaturated fatty acids have "kinks" that prevent tight packing, making the membrane more fluid. Saturated fatty acids pack tightly and make the membrane more rigid. Higher temperature also increases fluidity.
(iv) Which process allows movement in and out of cell: (I) Osmosis, (II) Diffusion, (III) Active transport?
A. I only
B. I and II only
C. II and III only
D. I, II and III
All three — osmosis (water), diffusion (small molecules), and active transport (ions, glucose against gradient) — move substances in and out of cells.
(v) All are postulates of cell theory except:
A. New cell is derived from pre-existing cells.
B. Cell does not contain the hereditary material.
C. All living organisms are made up of one or more cells.
D. Cell is the fundamental unit of life.
One of the postulates clearly states that cells DO contain hereditary material (DNA), which is passed from generation to generation. Option B is the opposite of the truth.
(vi) Secondary wall is made up of:
A. Pectin and cellulose
B. Cellulose and protein
C. Cellulose and lignin
D. Lignin and pectin
The secondary cell wall is made of cellulose held together by the hard, water-proof substance lignin. Pectin is in the middle lamella, not the secondary wall.
(vii) Select the odd one:
A. Active transport
B. Diffusion
C. Facilitated diffusion
D. Osmosis
Diffusion, facilitated diffusion, and osmosis are all passive processes (no energy needed). Active transport is the odd one — it needs ATP energy and moves substances against their gradient.
(viii) Trace the correct pathway of protein produced from protein factories:
A. RER → Ribosome → Golgi body → Lysosome
B. Ribosomes → RER → Golgi body → Lysosome
C. Golgi body → RER → Ribosome → Lysosome
D. RER → Ribosome → Lysosome → Golgi body
Proteins are made in ribosomes (the "factories"), passed into the RER for folding, then to the Golgi body for modification and packaging, and finally delivered to lysosomes or other destinations.
(ix) Cell organelle found in animal cell and helps intracellular digestion:
A. Lysosome
B. Ribosomes
C. Mitochondria
D. Golgi apparatus
Lysosomes contain powerful digestive enzymes and are abundant in animal cells. They digest food molecules, cellular waste, and even the cell itself when it dies.
(x) Select the mismatched:
A. Plastids → Storage of chemicals
B. Centriole → Help in cell division
C. Ribosomes → Synthesis of steroids
D. Mitochondria → Synthesis of ATP
Ribosomes synthesise proteins, NOT steroids. Steroids are made by the smooth endoplasmic reticulum. All the other matches are correct.

Section 2 — Fill in the Blanks

(i) Microscopes are instruments designed to produce __________ visual image.

Magnified. Microscopes enlarge objects that are too small to be seen with the naked eye.

(ii) Resolution of a microscope is defined as the smallest distance between __________ points.

Two. Resolution is the smallest distance between two points that can still be distinguished as separate objects — it measures the clarity of the image.

(iii) Magnification of a light microscope is formed by using a mixture of the power of the eyepiece and the __________ lens.

Objective. Total magnification = power of objective × power of eyepiece. A 10× objective with a 10× eyepiece gives 100×.

(iv) Electrons have a much shorter wavelength than visible light, and this allows electron microscopes to produce __________ images.

Higher resolution. Shorter wavelength = ability to distinguish very close points → higher resolution images (up to 0.2 nm).

(v) In plants, the cell wall is composed mainly of strong fibres of __________.

Cellulose. The primary cell wall of plants is mainly cellulose. Bacteria have peptidoglycan; fungi have chitin.

(vi) Cell membrane is composed of __________ layer.

Bilayer (phospholipid bilayer). The membrane is made of two layers of phospholipids, with hydrophilic heads facing out and hydrophobic tails facing in.

(vii) Diffusion is a __________ process, which does not require energy input.

Passive. Diffusion happens spontaneously down a concentration gradient and does not require ATP.

(viii) Plant cell loses water and cytoplasm shrinks in a process called __________.

Plasmolysis. This occurs when a plant cell is placed in a hypertonic solution — water leaves the cell and the cytoplasm pulls away from the cell wall.

(ix) Special type of movement of specific substances through carrier protein is __________.

Facilitated diffusion. It is still passive (no energy needed) but uses specific carrier proteins or ion channels to move particles across the membrane.

(x) The microtubules arranged in a very particular pattern to form centriole are __________ in number.

27 (twenty-seven). A centriole has 9 triplets of microtubules (9 × 3 = 27) arranged in rows.

Section 3 — Define the Following Terms

(i) Exocytosis

Exocytosis is the process by which a cell expels substances out of the cell. A vesicle inside the cell fuses with the cell membrane and releases its contents into the extracellular space. It is used for secretion of enzymes, hormones, and waste.

(ii) Vesicles

Vesicles are small, membrane-bound spherical sacs that help in the metabolism, transport, and storage of molecules. Many are made by the Golgi body and ER. Transport vesicles carry molecules around the cell.

(iii) Cartilage

Cartilage is a type of dense supporting connective tissue. It has limited ground substance and a matrix that can range from semisolid to flexible. Examples: pinna of the ear, nose, joints.

(iv) Nucleoplasm

Nucleoplasm is the granular fluid present inside the nuclear envelope of the nucleus. It surrounds the chromatin and nucleolus and supports the activities going on inside the nucleus.

(v) Cyclosis

Cyclosis is the streaming movement of the cytoplasm within a cell. It helps in the distribution of nutrients, oxygen, and other materials around the cell, especially in large plant cells.

(vi) Plasmolysis

Plasmolysis is the process in which a plant cell loses water and its cytoplasm shrinks away from the cell wall when placed in a hypertonic solution. It is the result of osmosis going outward.

(vii) Resolution

Resolution is the smallest distance between two points on a specimen that can still be distinguished as two separate objects. It measures the clarity of a microscope's image.

(viii) Tissue

A tissue is a group of similar cells that work together to perform a common function. Examples: epithelial, muscle, nervous, and connective tissue in animals; xylem and phloem in plants.

(ix) Magnification

Magnification is the enlargement of an image produced by a microscope. In a light microscope, total magnification = power of objective lens × power of eyepiece.

(x) Cisternae

Cisternae are the flattened, hollow, membrane-bound sacs that make up the endoplasmic reticulum and the Golgi body. They contain enzymes and serve as channels for the transport, modification, and packaging of cell products.

Section 5 — Short Answer Questions

(i) Why is mitochondria also called the power house of cell?

Mitochondria are called the power house of the cell because:

  • They are the site of aerobic respiration.
  • They break down glucose (and other foods) to release energy.
  • This energy is stored in molecules of ATP (adenosine triphosphate).
  • ATP is the universal energy currency that powers nearly every activity in the cell — muscle contraction, active transport, protein synthesis, cell division, and much more.
  • Without mitochondria, eukaryotic cells could not produce enough energy and would die.
(ii) Why is iodine used to stain the onion peel?

Iodine is used to stain the onion peel because:

  • Onion peel cells are colourless and transparent when fresh — they are hard to see under the microscope.
  • Iodine solution gives the cells a yellow-brown colour, making the cell wall, cytoplasm, and especially the nucleus clearly visible.
  • Iodine reacts with starch and proteins, helping different parts of the cell stand out against each other.
  • It is a safe, cheap, and easily available stain for school laboratories.

(iv) Why is cell membrane semipermeable in nature?

The cell membrane is selectively (semi-) permeable because:

  • It is made of a phospholipid bilayer with hydrophobic tails inside — this naturally blocks most polar molecules.
  • Small non-polar molecules (O₂, CO₂) can pass freely through the lipid bilayer.
  • Water can move through by osmosis (and special channels called aquaporins).
  • Larger or charged molecules (ions, glucose, amino acids) can only cross through specific channel or carrier proteins.
  • This way the cell controls what enters and leaves — keeping nutrients in, letting waste out.

(vi) Why is cell considered as the structural and functional unit of living things?

The cell is the structural and functional unit of living things because:

  • Structural unit: all bodies of living organisms — from a tiny bacterium to a huge tree — are built from cells. Tissues, organs, and organ systems are all made of cells.
  • Functional unit: every life process (respiration, digestion, growth, reproduction, response) ultimately happens inside cells.
  • A cell can carry out all the activities needed for life on its own (as a single-celled organism shows).
  • If a cell stops functioning, the tissue (and eventually the body) made of it stops functioning too.
  • The cell theory states this clearly: "The cell is the fundamental unit of structure and function in all living organisms."

Section 6 — Long Answer Questions

(i) Describe the structure and function of nucleus with the help of a diagram.

The nucleus is the largest organelle in a eukaryotic cell. It controls all cell activities and holds the hereditary information of the cell.

Structure (parts of the nucleus):

  • Nuclear envelope — a double membrane of two phospholipid bilayers that separates the nucleus from the cytoplasm.
  • Nuclear pores — tiny openings in the envelope that allow the regulated exchange of materials (especially RNA and proteins) between the nucleus and cytoplasm.
  • Nucleoplasm — the granular fluid present inside the envelope.
  • Nucleolus — a dense region rich in RNA; it makes ribosome subunits.
  • Chromatin network — in non-dividing cells the DNA exists as a network of fine threads; during division it condenses into chromosomes.

Functions of the nucleus:

  • Stores the cell's genetic information in DNA.
  • Controls all cellular activities by directing the synthesis of proteins (through RNA).
  • Carries out DNA replication before cell division.
  • Makes ribosomes in the nucleolus.
  • Passes the hereditary material to daughter cells during division.
(ii) What is microscope? Describe types of microscopes.

A microscope is an instrument designed to produce magnified visual or photographic images of objects too small to be seen with the naked eye. Two parameters are most important in microscopy:

  • Magnification — the enlargement of an image.
  • Resolution — the smallest distance between two points that can still be seen as separate; it measures clarity.

There are two main types of microscopes:

1. Light Microscope (LM)

  • Uses a beam of visible light that passes through the specimen.
  • A photograph taken through it is called a micrograph.
  • Total magnification = power of objective × power of eyepiece (e.g. 40×, 100×, 400×).
  • Can be used to view live cells.
  • Resolution is low compared to EM.
  • Inventors: Zacharias Janssen (1590) — first compound microscope; Robert Hooke improved it.

2. Electron Microscope (EM)

  • Uses a beam of electrons instead of light.
  • Electrons have a much shorter wavelength → resolution as small as 0.2 nm.
  • Magnification up to 250,000×.
  • Cannot view live cells.
  • Can study sub-cellular structures.
  • Two types:
    • SEM (Scanning EM) — beam moves over the surface; gives detailed 3D images of the surface.
    • TEM (Transmission EM) — beam passes through ultra-thin slices; gives detailed images of internal structures.
(iii) Describe the fluid mosaic model of cell membrane and also draw the diagram.

The Fluid Mosaic Model was proposed in 1972 by S.J. Singer and G.L. Nicolson. It is the modern accepted explanation of the structure of the cell membrane.

Main points of the model:

  • The membrane is made of a phospholipid bilayer — two layers of phospholipid molecules.
  • Each phospholipid has a hydrophilic head (faces water) and two hydrophobic tails (face inside, away from water).
  • The bilayer acts as a fluid matrix — phospholipid molecules can move sideways.
  • Various proteins are embedded in or attached to the bilayer:
    • Integral proteins — go all the way through the bilayer (some are channel proteins).
    • Peripheral proteins — attached to the surface only.
  • Glycoproteins (protein + carbohydrate) and glycolipids (lipid + carbohydrate) sit on the outside — they help in cell recognition.
  • Cholesterol molecules between phospholipids keep the membrane stable and fluid at different temperatures.
  • The membrane is selectively permeable — it controls what enters and leaves the cell.

Why it is called "fluid mosaic":

  • Fluid → the phospholipids and many proteins can move sideways through the bilayer.
  • Mosaic → the many proteins scattered in the bilayer look like a mosaic of pieces.

This model explains how the membrane carries out diffusion, osmosis, facilitated diffusion, active transport, endocytosis, and exocytosis — all the ways materials move in and out of the cell.
