Chapter 4 · Topic 2

Prokaryotes vs Eukaryotes & Plant vs Animal Cells

All cells share some basic features, but inside they fall into two very different types — prokaryotic and eukaryotic. And within eukaryotes, plant cells and animal cells have their own differences. This topic gives you the full comparison.

2.1 What is a Cell?

Cells are the basic units of organisms. All tissues and organs are made of cells. Some cell facts to remember:

  • Your body has several billion cells organised into over 200 major types.
  • Each cell type has hundreds of cell-specific functions.
  • Some functions are vital to all cells (e.g. cellular respiration); others are highly specialised (e.g. photosynthesis).
  • Energy flow in cells happens through the breakdown of carbohydrates by respiration.
  • DNA, the hereditary information, is passed from a parent cell to daughter cells during cell division.
  • Plant cells are generally cubical in shape; animal cells are usually spherical.

2.2 Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

The difference is in their name:

  • Eukaryote = "eu" (true) + "karyon" (kernel/nucleus) → cells with a true nucleus.
  • Prokaryote = "pro" (before) + "karyon" → cells before a true nucleus existed.
Eukaryotic cell
Cell whose nucleus is surrounded by a nuclear membrane and which has membrane-bound organelles. Found in animals, plants, fungi and protists.
Prokaryotic cell
Cell whose nucleus is not surrounded by a membrane. Found in bacteria and cyanobacteria.

Quick memory hook

Prokaryote = Prior to nucleus (simple, small, no organelles, 70S ribosomes). Eukaryote = Everything proper (true nucleus, membrane-bound organelles, 80S ribosomes).

2.3 Structure of a Bacterial Cell

A bacterium (Monera) is the most common example of a prokaryotic cell. Its main parts:

  • DNA — free in the cytoplasm (no nuclear membrane)
  • Ribosomes (70S) — for protein synthesis
  • Cytoplasm — jelly-like fluid inside
  • Plasma membrane — surrounds the cytoplasm
  • Cell wall — outside the plasma membrane (peptidoglycan)
  • Capsule — extra outer protective layer
  • Bacterial flagellum — whip-like for movement
  • Pili — short hair-like structures for attachment

2.4 Plant Cell vs Animal Cell

Both are eukaryotic cells, but they have important differences:

Feature Animal cell Plant cell
Cell wallAbsentPresent — made of cellulose
PlastidsAbsentPresent (chloroplasts, chromoplasts, leucoplasts)
Plasmodesmata / pitsAbsentPresent
VacuolesFew (if any)One large central vacuole filled with cell sap
Position of nucleusGenerally at the centre of cytoplasmNear the edge / periphery in mature cells
LysosomesPresent — digest macromoleculesRarely present — vacuole does this job
CentriolesPresent — organise microtubules for cell divisionTypically absent
ShapeUsually spherical (irregular)Generally cubical / rectangular
Why the difference?

Plants need a rigid cell wall for support (they can't move) and chloroplasts to make food from sunlight. Animals can move and eat their food, so they need flexibility instead of a wall, and lysosomes to digest food inside cells.
