Chapter 4 · Topic 3

Cell Structures & Organelles

A cell is like a tiny factory. Each organelle is a worker with a special job — wall, membrane, nucleus, ER, mitochondria, Golgi, ribosomes, plastids — they all work together to keep the cell alive. This is the longest part of the chapter, so we'll go organelle by organelle.

3.1 Cell Wall

Cell Wall
A tough, rigid, non-living, permeable protective layer found in plant cells, fungi, algae and bacteria — placed just outside the cell membrane.

Composition (varies by organism):

  • Plants: strong fibres of cellulose
  • Bacteria: sugar + amino acid = peptidoglycan
  • Fungi: mainly chitin, glucans, proteins

Layers of a Plant Cell Wall

Layer Made of Role
Middle lamellaPectin + cellulose (sticky)Outermost; separates one cell from another
Primary cell wallMainly celluloseLies inside the middle lamella
Secondary cell wallCellulose + ligninBeside the cell membrane; only in support cells (xylem tracheids & vessels)

Plasmodesmata are tiny openings in the cell wall containing strands of cytoplasm that connect adjacent plant cells. They allow molecules to travel between plant cells.

Functions of the cell wall:

  • Protects inner parts of the cell
  • Gives plant cells a regular, uniform shape
  • Provides support for the whole plant body
  • Is fully permeable to water and mineral salts → helps distribute nutrients

3.2 Cell Membrane (Plasma Membrane)

Cell Membrane
The outermost living boundary of all cells. It separates the inside of the cell (intracellular) from the outside (extracellular). It is selectively permeable.

The cell membrane is made of a double layer (bilayer) of phospholipids. Each phospholipid has:

  • A hydrophilic head ("water-loving") — pointing outward
  • A hydrophobic tail ("water-hating") — pointing inward

The Fluid Mosaic Model

In 1972, S.J. Singer and G.L. Nicolson proposed the Fluid Mosaic Model:

  • The membrane is a fluid phospholipid matrix.
  • Various proteins, carbohydrates and glycoproteins float freely in this fluid.
  • The membrane is selectively permeable — it lets ions (H⁺, Na⁺), small molecules (O₂, CO₂), and some larger molecules (glucose, amino acids) cross in a controlled way.

Components of the cell membrane:

  • Phospholipid bilayer — the basic matrix
  • Integral membrane proteins — embedded in the membrane
  • Peripheral membrane proteins — on the surface
  • Channel proteins — let specific molecules in / out
  • Glycoproteins (protein + carbohydrate) and glycolipids (lipid + carbohydrate) — for cell recognition
  • Cholesterol — keeps the membrane stable and fluid

Functions: osmosis, diffusion, transport of nutrients, ingestion, secretion, and controlling all exchanges between the cell and its environment.

3.3 Cytoplasm

The cytoplasm is the jelly-like substance that fills the cell. It is up to 90% water, with dissolved nutrients and waste products.

Functions:

  • Holds together the organelles
  • Nourishes the cell with salts and sugars
  • Provides a medium where metabolic reactions take place

3.4 Cytoskeleton

A microscopic network of protein consisting of microtubules and various filaments spread through the cytoplasm.

  • Microtubules — made of tubulin
  • Filaments — made of actin protein
  • Role: structural support + means of transport inside the cell

3.5 Nucleus

The largest organelle in the cell. It contains the entire cell's genetic information in the form of DNA. The presence of a nucleus is the main difference between eukaryotes and prokaryotes.

Parts of the nucleus:

  • Nuclear envelope — two phospholipid membranes separating the nucleus from the cytoplasm
  • Nuclear pores — openings that regulate exchange of RNA and proteins between nucleus and cytoplasm
  • Nucleoplasm — granular fluid inside the nuclear envelope
  • Nucleolus — aggregation of RNA inside the nucleus
  • Chromatin network — in non-dividing cells, the genetic material exists as a network of fibres (becomes chromosomes during division)
Function summary

The nucleus controls all activities of the cell and stores the hereditary information (DNA) that passes from parent to daughter cells during division.

3.6 Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

The ER is found only in eukaryotic cells. It is a double-membrane network of hollow tubes, flattened sheets, and round sacs called cisternae. It is connected to the nuclear envelope.

There are two types:

Feature Smooth ER (SER) Rough ER (RER)
Ribosomes attached?NoYes — gives "rough" look
FunctionSynthesis of lipids (oils, phospholipids, steroids); carbohydrate metabolism; calcium regulation; detoxificationSynthesis of proteins; membrane production

The folds of RER increase the surface area, allowing many more ribosomes to attach → more protein production.

3.7 Mitochondria — The Powerhouse

Mitochondria (singular: mitochondrion) are membrane-bound organelles found in eukaryotic cells.

Structure:

  • Two phospholipid bilayers — an outer membrane and an inner membrane
  • The inner membrane has folds called cristae with proteins that make ATP
  • Inside the inner membrane is a jelly-like matrix containing ribosomes, granules, and the mitochondrion's own DNA

Function: mitochondria are the site of aerobic respiration. Energy is produced in the form of ATP. That is why they are called the "power house of the cell".

Did you know?

ATP (adenosine triphosphate) and ADP are the molecules that move energy within cells. ATP is "charged"; ADP is "discharged".

3.8 Ribosomes

Ribosomes are made of RNA + protein and are the sites of protein synthesis ("protein factories"). They can be found:

  • Free in the cytoplasm — singly or in groups
  • Attached to the rough endoplasmic reticulum (RER)

Ribosomes have two subunits — a small subunit and a large subunit. Sizes:

  • Prokaryotes — 70S
  • Eukaryotes — 80S

3.9 Golgi Body (Golgi Apparatus)

Discovered by the Italian physician Camillo Golgi. It was one of the first organelles to be described in detail because its large size made it easy to observe.

Structure: a stack of flat membrane-bound sacs called cisternae, with enzymes inside that modify the cell's products.

Function — the "post office" of the cell:

  • Receives proteins from the rough ER
  • Modifies these proteins (adds sugars, etc.)
  • Packages them into vesicles
  • Sorts and ships the vesicles to where they are needed in the cell
Important pathway

Protein production route: Ribosomes (on RER) → RER → Golgi body → Vesicles → Lysosomes / cell membrane.

3.10 Vesicles and Lysosomes

Vesicles

Small, membrane-bound spherical sacs that help in metabolism, transport, and storage of molecules. Many vesicles are made by the Golgi body or the ER, or even from parts of the cell membrane. Transport vesicles move molecules around inside the cell.

Lysosomes

Lysosomes are formed by the Golgi body and contain powerful digestive enzymes. They can digest:

  • Cell structures (when a cell dies)
  • Food molecules — carbohydrates, proteins

Lysosomes are abundant in animal cells that ingest food through food vacuoles. When a cell dies, its lysosomes release the enzymes and digest the cell. For this reason lysosomes are sometimes called "suicide bags".

3.11 Vacuoles

Vacuoles are fluid-filled spaces in the cytoplasm.

  • In plant cells: usually one large central vacuole takes up most of the volume in mature cells.
  • In animal cells: very small or completely absent.
  • The vacuole is surrounded by a selectively permeable membrane called the tonoplast.
  • It contains cell sap — water + mineral salts + sugars + amino acids.

Functions: hydrolysis, excretion of cellular waste, storage of water and organic / inorganic substances.

3.12 Centrioles

Animal cells contain a special organelle called the centriole. Plant cells typically do not have centrioles.

  • Cylindrical, tube-like structure
  • Composed of 27 microtubules arranged in triplets in rows (9 triplets × 3 microtubules)
  • Two centrioles arranged at right angles to each other form a centrosome
  • Function: organise microtubules that position chromosomes correctly during cell division

3.13 Plastids

Plastids are large cytoplasmic organelles found in cells of plants and algae. They are sites for the manufacture and storage of important chemical compounds.

Plastid Colour / pigment Where found
ChloroplastsGreenPlants, algae — for photosynthesis
ChromoplastsRed, orange, yellowRipening fruits, flowers, autumn leaves
LeucoplastsColourlessStorage of starch / oil / protein

Chloroplast Structure

The chloroplast is a double-membraned organelle. Inside:

  • Stroma — gel-like substance containing enzymes for photosynthesis
  • Grana (singular: granum) — stacks of disc-shaped thylakoids
  • Chlorophyll — green pigment on the surface of thylakoid discs; absorbs sunlight for photosynthesis
  • Lamellae — connect different grana
