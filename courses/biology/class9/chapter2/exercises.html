Section 1 — Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

Encircle the correct answer. Correct option is marked in green.

(i) Select the correct sequence of biological method.
A. Law → Theory → Reasoning → Hypothesis
B. Hypothesis → Theory → Law → Reasoning
C. Hypothesis → Reasoning → Theory → Law
D. Law → Hypothesis → Reasoning → Theory
The correct order of the later stages of the biological method is Hypothesis → Reasoning → Experiment → Result → Conclusion → Theory → Law. Option C correctly shows Hypothesis first, then Reasoning, then Theory, then Law.
(ii) Select the odd one out.
A. Theory
B. Law
C. Hypothesis
D. Ratio
Theory, Law, and Hypothesis are all scientific concepts used in the biological method. Ratio is a mathematical concept (comparison of two values) — it is the odd one out as it does not belong to the same category.
(iii) Field of research that examines mathematical representations of biological systems is called:
A. Ratio
B. Mathematical biology
C. Proportion
D. Law
Mathematical biology is the field that uses equations and mathematical models to represent and understand biological systems such as population growth, disease spread, and protein structure.
(iv) Biological method involves all of the following except:
A. Data collection
B. Observation
C. Experiment
D. Proportion
Data collection, observation, and experiment are all steps within the biological method. Proportion is a mathematical concept used in data analysis — it is not a step of the biological method itself.
(v) Scientific reasoning that moves from specific to general is:
A. Inductive
B. Deductive
C. Observation
D. Both A and B
Inductive reasoning moves from specific observations to a general conclusion. Example: observing that many malaria patients have Plasmodium → concluding that Plasmodium causes malaria. Deductive reasoning goes the opposite direction (general → specific).
(vi) Quantitative observation is the use of:
A. Senses
B. Equipment
C. Guess
D. Ratio
Quantitative observation uses scientific equipment (like a microscope, thermometer, or ruler) to get measurable, numerical data. Qualitative observation uses the five senses only.
(vii) An equation stating that two ratios are equal is called:
A. Ratio
B. Proportion
C. Guess
D. Senses
A proportion is an equation stating that two ratios are equal. Example: 4:12 :: 1:3 (both ratios equal ¼, so they form a proportion). A ratio simply compares two values.
(viii) The comparison of two values is called:
A. Ratio
B. Proportion
C. Graph
D. Table
A ratio is the comparison of two values expressed as a quotient (first ÷ second). Example: 4 sepals and 12 petals → ratio = 4:12 = 1:3.
(ix) What is a hypothesis?
A. The same thing as an unproven theory.
B. A tentative explanation that can be tested and is falsified.
C. A verifiable observation.
D. A fact based on quantitative data that is falsified.
A hypothesis is a tentative (temporary) explanation — an intelligent guess — that can be tested through experimentation and can be falsified (proven wrong) if the evidence does not support it. It is not the same as a theory, which is well-tested and reliable.
(x) In data organisation, which method is mostly useful?
A. Table
B. Graph
C. Ratio
D. Both A and B
Both tables and graphs are used for data organisation. Tables allow easy comparison of exact values; graphs (bar charts, pie charts, line graphs) show trends and patterns visually. Both are useful and both are mentioned in your textbook.

Section 2 — Fill in the Blanks

(i) Problem-solving approach of biology and other sciences called __________.

Scientific method (Biological method). The biological method is the specific version of the scientific method used in biology. It involves observation, hypothesis, experiment, and conclusion to solve biological problems.

(ii) Solution of biological problem starts with __________.

Observation. The very first step of the biological method is observation — using your senses or scientific equipment to notice and record facts about the problem.

(iii) The key component of the scientific process is __________.

Hypothesis. The hypothesis is the key component because it is the testable statement that drives the entire experiment. Without a hypothesis, you have no clear direction for your investigation.

(iv) Scientific reasoning based on "if-then" statement called __________.

Deductive reasoning. Deductive reasoning moves from general to specific and uses "if-then" logic. Example: "If Plasmodium causes malaria, then all malaria patients must have Plasmodium in their blood."

(v) Final step of the scientific method is developing __________.

Conclusion. The conclusion is the final step where all results from the experiment are analysed and a determination is reached about whether the hypothesis is supported or rejected.

(vi) The uniform or constant fact of nature, virtually an irrefutable theory is __________.

Scientific Law (Law). A scientific law is a statement of a constant, unchanging fact of nature. It is so well supported that it is virtually impossible to disprove. Biology has fewer laws than physics because living things are more complex and variable.

(vii) Once you have the data, you may need to perform __________ analysis.

Mathematical (Statistical). After collecting data, mathematical analysis — using tools like ratio, proportion, averages, and percentages — is performed to support or refute the hypothesis.

(viii) The equation stating that two ratios are equal is called __________.

Proportion. A proportion states that two ratios are equal. Example: 4:12 :: 1:3. Both sides are equal, making this a proportion (4 ÷ 12 = 1 ÷ 3 = 0.33…).

(ix) Ratio is comparison of __________ values.

Two. A ratio always compares exactly two values. Example: sepals (4) to petals (12) — written as 4:12 or 1:3.

(x) The cause of malaria is __________.

Plasmodium. Plasmodium is a tiny parasite (protozoan) found in the blood of malaria patients. It is transmitted by the bite of a female Anopheles mosquito. This was proven by Laveran's experiment (1880) using the biological method.

Section 3 — Define the Following Terms

(i) Ratio

Ratio is the comparison of two values expressed as a quotient (1st ÷ 2nd). It shows how many times one value contains or is contained in another.
Example: A flower has 4 sepals and 12 petals. Ratio of sepals to petals = 4:12 = 1:3.

(ii) Biological Method

The biological method is the system of advancing knowledge about living things by formulating a question, collecting data through observation and experiment, and testing a hypothetical answer. It is the problem-solving approach used specifically in biology. Steps: Observation → Hypothesis → Reasoning → Experiment → Result → Conclusion.

(iii) Graph

A graph is a visual/pictorial representation of data that shows trends, patterns, and relationships between variables. Common types include bar charts (for comparing groups), pie charts (for showing percentages), and line graphs (for showing changes over time).

(iv) Hypothesis

A hypothesis is an intelligent guess made by a scientist in the form of a testable statement. It is suggested as an explanation for a natural event or observation. A hypothesis must be testable — it should be either supported or falsified by experimentation.
Example: "Plasmodium is the cause of malaria."

(v) Law

A scientific law is a uniform or constant fact of nature — a statement that is always true under the same conditions. It is virtually an irrefutable theory. A law describes what happens rather than explaining why it happens. Biology has fewer laws than physics due to the complex and variable nature of living things.

(vi) Inductive Reasoning

Inductive reasoning is the process of drawing a general conclusion from specific observations. It moves from specific → general.
Example: A shark is a fish. All the fish I have seen have scales. Therefore, sharks have scales.
You gather specific observations and then form a general rule.

(vii) Conclusion

A conclusion is the final step of the scientific/biological method. It is where all the results from the experiment are analysed and a determination is reached about the hypothesis. If the hypothesis is supported, it is accepted. If not, the experiment is repeated or redesigned.
Example: "Plasmodium is the cause of malaria."

(viii) Proportion

A proportion is an equation stating that two ratios are equal. It shows that two different comparisons give the same result.
Example: 4:12 :: 1:3 (read as "4 is to 12 as 1 is to 3"). Both ratios simplify to 1/3, confirming they are proportional.

(ix) Observation

An observation is a statement of knowledge gained through the senses (qualitative) or through the use of scientific equipment (quantitative). It is the first step of the biological method.
Qualitative: using sight, smell, taste, touch, hearing (no numbers).
Quantitative: using instruments to get numerical data.
Example: Laveran observed Plasmodium in blood samples under a microscope (quantitative).

(x) Mathematical Models

Mathematical models are equations or formulas that predict or describe natural occurrences in biology. They are used to represent biological systems mathematically. Examples include models for bacterial growth, population changes over time, protein structure, and spread of disease. They allow biologists to make predictions without having to run experiments every time.

Section 5 — Short Answer Questions

(i) Theory is a highly reliable scientific explanation — why?

A theory is considered highly reliable for the following reasons:

  • It is based on many repeated observations and a large amount of data.
  • It has been tested many times by many different scientists under different conditions.
  • It consistently produces the same results — it does not fail.
  • It unifies many different pieces of evidence into one explanation.
  • It can predict new phenomena that were not originally observed.

For these reasons, a scientific theory is far more reliable than an ordinary guess or even a hypothesis.

(ii) Why do biological sciences need mathematical models?

Biological sciences need mathematical models because:

  • Biological systems are very complex — it is impossible to study every individual organism.
  • Models allow biologists to measure, predict, and understand natural phenomena without running an experiment every time.
  • They describe patterns like population changes, bacterial growth, and disease spread using equations.
  • They help predict the structure of proteins and other molecules.
  • They allow estimation of endangered species populations from small sample counts.

In short, mathematics gives biologists the tools to quantify and predict what they observe in nature.

(iii) Draw a chart showing the steps involved in the biological method.

The steps of the biological method in sequence:

(iv) Why are tables or graphs necessary for data organisation?

Tables and graphs are necessary for data organisation because:

  • Raw data from an experiment is large and confusing — a table arranges it neatly in rows and columns for easy reading.
  • Graphs show trends and patterns that are not visible in a table (e.g. rise or fall of malaria cases over months).
  • They make it easy to compare the experimental group and control group at a glance.
  • They allow other scientists to quickly understand the data without reading long descriptions.
  • They are essential for mathematical analysis (e.g. calculating ratios and proportions from organised data).
(v) Why is an experiment necessary for theory?

An experiment is necessary for theory because:

  • A theory cannot be formed from a hypothesis alone — the hypothesis must be tested first.
  • Experiments provide real evidence that either supports or rejects the hypothesis.
  • A theory is only accepted after the hypothesis has been tested many times by many scientists — all through experiments.
  • Without experiments, science would be based on guessing, not evidence.
  • A theory that is not based on experimental evidence is not a scientific theory — it remains just an idea.

In short: Hypothesis → Experiment → Results → Conclusion → (repeated many times) → Theory. No experiment means no theory.
