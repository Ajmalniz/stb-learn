Chapter 2 · Topic 3

Reasoning, Experiment & Conclusion

After forming a hypothesis, scientists use reasoning to design an experiment. The experiment gives a result, which leads to a conclusion. This topic covers all three steps using the malaria case study.

3.1 Reasoning

Biologists use a reasoning process to connect their observations and hypotheses. There are two types:

Inductive Reasoning
Moves from specific observations → general conclusion.
Example: A shark is a fish. All the fish I have seen have scales. Therefore, sharks also have scales.
You observe specific cases first, then draw a general rule.
Deductive Reasoning
Moves from general rule → specific prediction. Uses "if…then" logic.
Example: If Plasmodium causes malaria, then every malaria patient must have Plasmodium in their blood.
This prediction can then be tested by experiment.

Exam tip

Deductive reasoning is the one based on "if-then" statement and moves from general to specific. This is the most asked in MCQs.

3.2 Experiment

Once a hypothesis is made and reasoning is applied, the next step is to design an experiment.

Experiment
A practical performance by a scientist to identify the real cause of a problem, based on inductive or deductive reasoning. A key rule: the experiment must be repeatable by other scientists.

Control Group vs Experimental Group

Every good experiment has two groups:

Experimental Group
The group that is exposed to the factor being tested.
Malaria: 100 malaria patients — their blood is examined for Plasmodium.
Control Group
The group that is not exposed — used as a baseline for comparison.
Malaria: 100 healthy persons — their blood is also examined to compare.
Why do we need a control group?

Without a control group, you cannot prove your hypothesis. If all malaria patients have Plasmodium and some healthy people also have it, then Plasmodium is not the cause. The control group rules out other explanations.

3.3 Result

Result is where you report exactly what happened in the experiment — all observations and data.

Malaria result:

  • Experimental group: All 100 malaria patients had Plasmodium in their blood.
  • Control group: All 100 healthy persons had no Plasmodium in their blood.

The result supports the hypothesis — Plasmodium was present only in malaria patients, not in healthy persons.

3.4 Conclusion

Conclusion is the final step of the biological method. Here all results are analysed and a decision is made about the hypothesis:

  • If the result supports the hypothesis → hypothesis is accepted.
  • If the result does not support the hypothesis → hypothesis is rejected and the process may be repeated or redesigned.

Malaria conclusion: "Plasmodium is the cause of malaria."

Exam tip

Conclusion is the last step of the scientific/biological method. It either accepts or rejects the hypothesis based on results. If rejected, you go back and redesign the experiment.
