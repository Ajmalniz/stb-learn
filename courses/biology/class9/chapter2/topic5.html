Data Organisation & Maths in Biology

After collecting data from an experiment, you need to organise it clearly and analyse it mathematically. This topic covers tables, graphs, ratio, proportion, and the role of mathematics in biology.

5.1 Data Organisation

After an experiment, you organise your data so it is easy to read and understand. The two main tools are:

Table
Data arranged in rows and columns. Makes it easy to compare values side by side.
Example: A table showing number of malaria patients per month across different districts.
Graph
A visual/pictorial representation of data. Shows trends and patterns that are hard to see in a table.
Types: Bar chart, pie chart, line graph.
Example: A bar chart showing malaria cases in Sindh from Jan–Dec 2017.
Important rule

Do not throw out data that seems wrong or does not support your prediction. Some of the most incredible discoveries in science were made because the data looked wrong! Always record everything honestly.

5.2 Data Analysis — Ratio and Proportion

Once data is organised, you analyse it using statistical methods. The two key concepts your textbook covers are ratio and proportion.

Ratio
A comparison of two values expressed as a quotient (first ÷ second).
Example: A flower has 4 sepals and 12 petals. The ratio of sepals to petals is 4:12, which simplifies to 1:3.
Proportion
An equation stating that two ratios are equal.
Example: 4:12 :: 1:3 (read as "4 is to 12 as 1 is to 3"). Both ratios are equal, so this is a proportion.
Easy trick

Ratio = comparison of TWO values (4:12).
Proportion = TWO ratios that are EQUAL (4:12 :: 1:3).

Worked example from textbook:

Concept Expression Meaning
Ratio of sepals to petals4 : 12For every 4 sepals there are 12 petals
Simplified ratio1 : 3For every 1 sepal there are 3 petals
Proportion4:12 :: 1:3Both ratios are equal (proportion)

5.3 Mathematics as an Integral Part of the Scientific Process

Mathematics is not just for maths class — it is a critical tool in biology at every step.

Mathematical Biology
A field of research that examines mathematical representations of biological systems. It uses equations and models to understand life.
Mathematical Models
Equations or formulas that predict or describe natural occurrences. They help biologists understand patterns without having to run experiments every single time.

Where maths is used in biology:

  • Population studies — counting insects in a field, estimating endangered species.
  • Population changes over time — modelling how a bacteria colony grows.
  • Protein structure — using geometry and equations to predict how proteins fold.
  • Height and growth — tracking growth patterns of organisms.
  • Disease spread — predicting how malaria or other diseases will spread.
  • Data analysis — comparing ratios of patients, survival rates, etc.
Think about it

Imagine you are a biologist studying insect populations. You go to the field and count insects in a small area. Then you use ratio and proportion to estimate the total population of a much larger region. Without maths, this would be impossible.

5.4 Chapter Summary (Quick Revision)

  • Science = systematic study of nature; biological method = problem-solving for living things.
  • Steps: Biological problem → Observation → Hypothesis → Reasoning → Experiment → Result → Conclusion → Theory/Law.
  • Observation: qualitative (5 senses) or quantitative (equipment/numbers).
  • Hypothesis = intelligent, testable guess; deductive reasoning uses "if-then"; inductive goes specific → general.
  • Experiment has control group (no treatment) and experimental group (treated).
  • Theory = well-tested explanation; Law = constant, virtually irrefutable fact of nature.
  • Data organised in tables and graphs; analysed using ratio (comparison of two values) and proportion (two equal ratios).
  • Mathematical biology uses models/equations to describe and predict biological phenomena.
