Chapter 2 · Topic 2

Observation & Hypothesis

Every biological investigation starts with observation — noticing something and asking why. Then comes the hypothesis — an intelligent, testable guess. This topic explains both, using the real malaria story.

2.1 Biological Problem

A biological problem is a set of questions to be solved about the natural world. These problems can be:

  • Environmental — e.g. why are fish dying in this river?
  • Ecological — e.g. why is a bird species disappearing?
  • Health-related — e.g. what causes malaria?

No matter what type of problem is studied, scientists use the same biological method to find answers that are logical and supported by evidence.

The malaria problem

For centuries, people believed malaria was caused by "bad air" (Latin: mala aria). This wrong idea persisted because nobody had properly investigated the problem using the biological method.

2.2 Observation

The first step of the biological method is observation. The solution to any biological problem starts here.

Observation
A statement of knowledge gained either through the five senses (qualitative) or through scientific equipment (quantitative).

There are two types of observation:

Qualitative Observation
Uses the five senses — sight, smell, hearing, touch, and taste.
Example: "The patient looks pale and is shivering" — observed by sight and touch.
Quantitative Observation
Uses scientific instruments/equipment to get measurable data with numbers.
Example: "280 million people suffer from malaria in over 100 countries" — measured using records and microscopes.
Easy way to remember

Qualitative = Quality (describes — uses senses).
Quantitative = Quantity (measures — uses equipment & numbers).

Malaria — the key observation

In 1880, a French physician named Laveran studied the blood sample of a malaria patient under a microscope (quantitative tool) and observed tiny creatures inside the blood cells. He named them Plasmodium.

So the observation was: "Plasmodium is present in the blood of malaria patients."

2.3 Hypothesis

After observation, a scientist makes a hypothesis.

Hypothesis
An intelligent guess made by a scientist in the form of a statement. It must be testable — you should be able to prove it right or wrong through experimentation.

Key rules about a hypothesis:

  • It must be a statement, not just a question.
  • It must be testable — you can design an experiment to check it.
  • It must be either supported or falsified by the experiment.
  • It is NOT a random guess — it is based on observation and prior knowledge.
Malaria hypothesis

After Laveran's observation, the hypothesis was made: "Plasmodium is the cause of malaria." — This is a testable statement. We can check it by looking at whether all malaria patients have Plasmodium and healthy persons do not.

Exam tip

A hypothesis is called an "intelligent guess" in your textbook. It is different from a theory — a hypothesis is just a starting guess; a theory is a well-tested explanation. Do NOT confuse the two.
