Section 1 — Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

Encircle the correct answer. Correct option is marked in green.

(i) Which of the following is the correct way of writing a scientific name of an organism?
A. Houbara bustard
B. E. coli
C. Alium Cepa
D. canis lupis
A scientific name must have the genus capitalised and the species in lowercase, and should be italicised or underlined. Houbara bustard follows this rule. E. coli is correct only after the full name has been written once. Alium Cepa wrongly capitalises the species, and canis lupis wrongly starts genus with a small letter.
(ii) Select the mismatched.
A. Plantae → Pteridophyta
B. Fungi → Mucor
C. Protista → Paramecium
D. Animalia → Amoeba
Amoeba is a protist, not an animal. It is a single-celled eukaryote and belongs to Kingdom Protista, not Animalia. The other pairs are correct: Pteridophytes are plants, Mucor is a fungus, and Paramecium is a protist.
(iii) Identify the correct sequence of classifying an organism.
A. Species → Genus → Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family
B. Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species
C. Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Family → Order → Genus → Species
D. Species → Genus → Class → Phylum → Order → Kingdom → Family
The taxonomic hierarchy from largest to smallest: Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species. Remember the mnemonic: "King Philip Came Over For Good Soup".
(iv) All are involved in classification except:
A. Analogous
B. Homologous
C. Cytology
D. Genetics
Homologous structures, cytology and genetics are all used in modern classification. Analogous structures (different in structure but same in function — e.g. bird wing and butterfly wing) are not used to classify organisms, because organisms with analogous structures are often unrelated.
(v) In the taxonomic hierarchy choose the term which encompasses all others in the list: (I) Genus, (II) Species, (III) Order, (IV) Class.
A. I and II
B. II
C. II and III
D. IV
In the hierarchy Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species, the Class is the highest of the four options listed and contains all the others under it.
(vi) In four-kingdom classification, kingdom Metaphyta includes all of the following except:
A. Algae
B. Angiosperm
C. Gymnosperm
D. Bryophyta
Metaphyta (Copeland, 1959) includes bryophytes, pteridophytes, gymnosperms and angiosperms. Algae were placed in Protista in the four-kingdom system, not in Metaphyta.
(vii) In five-kingdom system virus is placed in:
A. Monera
B. Protista
C. Plantae
D. None of these
Viruses are non-cellular — they have no cell, only nucleic acid wrapped in a protein coat (capsid). They cannot fit any of the five kingdoms (all of which contain cellular organisms), so they are placed in none.
(viii) Biological name of cat is:
A. Felis catus
B. Azadirachta indica
C. Allium cepa
D. Canis lupis
The scientific name of the cat is Felis catus. Azadirachta indica is neem, Allium cepa is onion, and Canis refers to dog/wolf.
(ix) Members of which kingdom have a cell wall and are all heterotrophic?
A. Monera
B. Protista
C. Plantae
D. Fungi
Fungi have a cell wall made of chitin and are absorptive heterotrophs (they absorb their food). Plants have a cell wall but are autotrophic. Animals are heterotrophic but have no cell wall. Monera have a cell wall but include both autotrophs and heterotrophs.
(x) Biodiversity is affected by: (I) Pollution (II) Deforestation (III) Over-hunting
A. I only
B. II only
C. I and II
D. I, II and III
All three — pollution, deforestation and over-hunting — are major human activities damaging biodiversity. Pollution destroys plant life, deforestation removes habitat, and over-hunting kills wildlife directly.

Section 2 — Fill in the Blanks

(i) Degree of variation within or among the species existing on different regions of the earth is called __________.

Biodiversity (biological diversity). It includes all bacteria, protozoans, algae, fungi, plants and animals on Earth.

(ii) __________ species are given scientific names.

1.5 million (about one and a half million) species have so far been described and given scientific names. Many more are believed to be undiscovered.

(iii) __________ structures have different functions (physiology) but have similar internal architecture.

Homologous structures. Example: human arm, bat wing and whale flipper — same bone pattern, different functions.

(iv) Science of classification is called __________.

Taxonomy (Tazm = group, Nomy = naming). A person who studies it is called a taxonomist.

(v) Most protists are __________.

Aquatic. Most members of Kingdom Protista live in water — examples include Paramecium and unicellular algae.

(vi) __________ are achlorophyllous and absorptive living organisms.

Fungi. They have no chlorophyll (achlorophyllous) and obtain food by absorption — they are absorptive heterotrophs.

(vii) __________ is a non-cellular obligate parasite.

Virus. A virus has no cell and can only reproduce inside a host cell — that's why it is called an obligate (= forced) endoparasite.

(viii) Scientific name of any organism should be __________ when printed.

Italicised when printed (e.g. Homo sapiens), and underlined when handwritten.

(ix) Cutting down of trees is called __________.

Deforestation. It is the conversion of forest land into non-forest land — a major cause of biodiversity loss.

(x) Animals which are at the risk of extinction in the future are called __________.

Endangered species. Examples from Pakistan: snow leopard, Indus river dolphin, Markhor, Marco Polo sheep.

Section 3 — Define the Following Terms

(i) Analogous

Analogous structures are organs that are different in structure but perform the same function.
Example: The wing of a bird and the wing of a butterfly — both are used for flying, but they are built very differently.

(ii) Classification

Classification is the arrangement of organisms into groups and sub-groups on the basis of their similar and dissimilar characteristics. It makes the study of millions of species easier and helps reveal evolutionary relationships.

(iii) Species

A species is the smallest and most basic unit of classification. It is a group of individual organisms with fundamental similarities that can naturally interbreed to produce a fertile offspring.
Example: All humans belong to the species Homo sapiens.

(iv) Family

A family is a taxonomic group made up of similar genera. It is one step above genus and one step below order in the taxonomic hierarchy.
Example: Humans belong to the family Hominidae.

(v) Metazoa

Metazoa is one of the four kingdoms in Copeland's 1959 classification. It includes all multicellular eukaryotic animals — both invertebrates and vertebrates.

(vi) Mycelium

Mycelium is the body of a fungus. It is made up of a network of thread-like structures called hyphae. The mycelium grows through the food source and absorbs nutrients.

(vii) Hyphae

Hyphae (singular: hypha) are the thread-like structures that make up the body (mycelium) of a fungus. They branch out, absorb food, and form the visible parts of fungi like mushrooms.

(viii) Genus

A genus (plural: genera) is a taxonomic group formed by combining closely related species. It is one step above species and one step below family.
Example: Homo is the genus of humans (Homo sapiens).

(ix) Endangered species

Endangered species are species that are at the risk of extinction in the future due to small population size or threats like habitat loss and over-hunting.
Example: Snow leopard, Indus river dolphin, Markhor.

(x) Kingdom

A kingdom is the highest taxon in classification. All organisms are first sorted into kingdoms before being divided into smaller taxa (phylum, class, etc.). The modern five kingdoms are Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae and Animalia.

Section 5 — Short Answer Questions

(i) Why is scientific naming necessary?

Scientific naming is necessary for the following reasons:

  • Common names change from region to region — onion is Piyaz in Urdu, ganda or basal in other areas, but its scientific name Allium cepa is the same everywhere.
  • Each scientific name applies to only one species — so no confusion.
  • Each species has only one universal name.
  • Scientific names are accepted by speakers of all languages, making research and communication clear worldwide.
  • They show the genus and species together — giving information about relationships between organisms.
(ii) How are living organisms classified into two-kingdom classification?

In the two-kingdom classification, organisms were divided based on the presence or absence of a cell wall and a few related features:

  • Organisms with a cell wall → Plant Kingdom (bacteria, algae, fungi, bryophytes, pteridophytes, gymnosperms, angiosperms)
  • Organisms without a cell wall → Animal Kingdom (invertebrates including protozoa, and vertebrates)

Other features used:

  • Plants — slow response, no locomotion, do not eat, no conductile/contractile system.
  • Animals — fast response, can move, eat their food, have nerves and muscles.
(iii) Why is virus not placed in any kingdom?

Viruses are not placed in any of the five kingdoms because:

  • They are non-cellular — they have no cell, while all five kingdoms contain cellular organisms.
  • They have no cellular organisation — just nucleic acid (DNA or RNA) inside a protein coat called capsid.
  • They are obligate endoparasites — they cannot reproduce on their own, only inside a host cell.
  • Outside a host, they show no signs of life — they behave almost like non-living particles.

Because of these unique features, viruses are excluded from the five-kingdom system. The same is true for prions and viroids.

(v) Why is Amoeba not placed in animal kingdom?

Amoeba is not placed in Animalia because:

  • It is unicellular, while all members of Animalia are multicellular.
  • It is a simple eukaryotic organism without tissue or organ-level organisation.
  • It belongs to a group of single-celled eukaryotes called protozoa, which fit the definition of Kingdom Protista.
  • In the five-kingdom system, Animalia excludes protozoa — they sit in Protista along with other unicellular eukaryotes.
(vi) Why are cyanobacteria placed in Monera?

Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) are placed in Kingdom Monera, not Plantae, because:

  • They are prokaryotic — their cells have no nuclear membrane and no membrane-bound organelles.
  • Their DNA lies free in the cytoplasm (no true nucleus).
  • Even though they can do photosynthesis like plants, their cell structure is bacterial, not plant-like.
  • Kingdom Monera is defined as the kingdom for all prokaryotes — so cyanobacteria fit there together with true bacteria.

Section 6 — Long Answer Questions

(i) Describe in detail the five-kingdom classification.

Robert Whittaker, in 1969, classified all living organisms into five kingdoms. His system was based on:

  • Cellular structure and body organisation — prokaryotic vs eukaryotic; unicellular vs multicellular.
  • Mode of nutrition — autotroph, ingestive heterotroph, or absorptive heterotroph.

1. Kingdom Monera

  • All prokaryotes — bacteria and cyanobacteria (blue-green algae).
  • Unicellular; no nuclear membrane; cell wall present (polysaccharide + amino acid).
  • Nutrition: both autotrophic (chemo/photosynthetic) and heterotrophic.

2. Kingdom Protista

  • Eukaryotic unicellular organisms (except yeast). Some show mixed plant + animal features.
  • Most are aquatic.
  • Includes protozoa (Paramecium, Amoeba) and unicellular algae.

3. Kingdom Fungi

  • Multicellular eukaryotes; achlorophyllous; absorptive heterotrophs.
  • Cell wall made of chitin.
  • Body is a mycelium, made of thread-like hyphae.
  • Examples: Mushroom, Mucor, yeast.

4. Kingdom Plantae

  • Multicellular eukaryotes; autotrophic (photosynthetic).
  • Cell wall made of cellulose.
  • Includes multicellular algae, bryophytes, pteridophytes, gymnosperms and angiosperms.

5. Kingdom Animalia

  • Multicellular eukaryotes; ingestive heterotrophs; no cell wall.
  • Body shows tissue / organ / organ-system level organisation.
  • Includes all invertebrates and vertebrates, except protozoa.

Note about viruses: Viruses, prions and viroids are non-cellular and do not fit any of the five kingdoms.

(ii) What is taxonomic hierarchy? Explain the aims of classification.

Taxonomic Hierarchy

The groups into which organisms are classified are called taxa (singular: taxon). When these taxa are arranged in ascending order, they form a ladder called the taxonomic hierarchy.

The order from biggest to smallest:

Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

  • Kingdom — the highest taxon; all organisms are first sorted into five kingdoms.
  • Phylum (Division in plants) — large groups within a kingdom.
  • Class — groups of related orders.
  • Order — groups of related families.
  • Family — groups of related genera.
  • Genus — groups of closely related species.
  • Species — the smallest and most basic unit; members can naturally interbreed to produce a fertile offspring.

Aims of Classification

  • To determine the similarities and dissimilarities among organisms, so they can be studied easily.
  • To find the evolutionary relationships between different organisms — discovering which species are descended from which ancestors.
  • To organise the huge biodiversity of around 1.5 million known species into manageable groups.
  • To make it possible to identify any new organism using its features and the established hierarchy.
(iii) Describe the effect of deforestation on biodiversity.

Deforestation is the cutting down of trees for the conversion of forest land into non-forest land. It is one of the biggest threats to biodiversity. Its main effects are:

  • Increase in greenhouse gases — Without trees, carbon dioxide builds up. Methane, water vapour and nitrous oxide also rise. This causes global warming.
  • Glacier melting — Rising temperatures cause glaciers to melt, which raises the sea level and increases the risk of floods in low areas.
  • Loss of habitat — Forests are home to billions of organisms. Cutting trees destroys their habitat, pushing many species toward becoming endangered or extinct.
  • Soil erosion — Without tree roots, soil washes away with rain and wind. Land becomes infertile.
  • Low rainfall — Trees release water vapour through transpiration. With fewer trees, there is less transpiration, less cloud formation, and therefore less rainfall.
  • Loss of forest products — Deforestation reduces food, fresh air, clothing, medicines and shelter that forests provide.
  • Climate change — All the above effects together change weather patterns, hurting agriculture and human life.

To protect biodiversity, deforestation must be controlled by laws, awareness, replanting trees (afforestation), and reducing demand for paper, timber and land for grazing.
