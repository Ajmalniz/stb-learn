History of Classification

Classification did not start with five kingdoms. It began over 2000 years ago with just two groups — plants and animals — and grew step by step as our knowledge improved. This topic walks through that journey.

3.1 The Early Scientists

Scientist Contribution
Aristotle (384–322 BC) Greek philosopher — the "father of science". First to introduce classification by type and binomial definition. Wrote Historia Animalium grouping animals into "with blood / without blood" and "water / land" animals.
Abu Usman Umer Aljahiz The first eminent Arab zoologist of the Muslim world. Studied animal anatomy and embryos. Wrote the encyclopedic Kitab al-Haywan (Book of Animals) in 7 volumes — famous work on zoology covering animal behaviour, diseases and treatments.
Carolus Linnaeus Swedish naturalist — the father of Taxonomy. First to frame principles for defining natural genera and species, and to create a uniform naming system (binomial nomenclature — covered in Topic 5).
Remember

Aristotle = father of science. Linnaeus = father of taxonomy. Aljahiz = first Muslim zoologist. These three names are common MCQ targets.

3.2 Two-Kingdom Classification

In the earliest system, all living things were divided into just two kingdoms:

Members includedBacteria, algae, fungi, bryophytes, pteridophytes, gymnosperms, angiospermsInvertebrates (including protozoa) and vertebrates
Problem with this system

Bacteria and fungi were forced into the plant kingdom even though they are very different. Things like Euglena (which acts both like a plant and like an animal) didn't fit anywhere properly.

3.3 Three-Kingdom Classification (Ernst Haeckel, 1866)

Ernst Haeckel in 1866 added a third kingdom called Protista to hold organisms that did not fit in either plants or animals — like Euglena and bacteria.

In the 1930s, electron microscopy revealed two patterns inside the kingdom Protista:

  • Lower protists — prokaryotic, unicellular (bacteria, cyanobacteria)
  • Higher protists — eukaryotic, unicellular or multicellular (fungi, algae — except blue-green algae)

In 1937, Édouard Chatton clarified the concepts of prokaryote and eukaryote to describe the cellular differences between organisms.

3.4 Four-Kingdom Classification (Copeland, 1959)

In 1959, Copeland noticed that prokaryotes (cells without a true nucleus) were so different from eukaryotes that they deserved a kingdom of their own. He created the kingdom Monera.

His four kingdoms were:

Kingdom Members
MoneraBacteria and blue-green algae (prokaryotic)
ProtistaAlgae and fungi (eukaryotic protists)
MetaphytaBryophytes, pteridophytes, gymnosperms, angiosperms
MetazoaInvertebrates and vertebrates
Watch out

In the four-kingdom system, algae and fungi were placed in Protista — not in Plantae. Metaphyta does not include algae.

3.5 Five-Kingdom Classification (Robert Whittaker, 1969)

Robert Whittaker in 1969 separated fungi into their own kingdom and gave us the modern five-kingdom system still used today.

His classification was based on two main features:

  • Cellular structure & body organisation: unicellular prokaryote, unicellular eukaryote, multicellular eukaryote.
  • Mode of nutrition:
    • Autotrophs (make their own food) → Plants
    • Ingestive heterotrophs (eat their food) → Animals
    • Absorptive heterotrophs (absorb food) → Fungi
