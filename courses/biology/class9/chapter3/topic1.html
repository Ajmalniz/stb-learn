What is Biodiversity?

Earth is bursting with life — from tiny bacteria to giant whales, from desert cactuses to tropical orchids. This variety of life is called biodiversity. This topic explains what biodiversity is, why it matters, and the kinds of organisms that make it up.

1.1 The Meaning of Biodiversity

The word biodiversity is made of two parts:

  • Bio means life
  • Diversity means variation (variety)
Biodiversity
The degree of variation within or among the species that exist in different regions of the Earth.

In simple words: biodiversity is the total variety of all living things on Earth — every plant, every animal, every microorganism in every habitat.

Key idea

Biodiversity includes bacteria, protozoans, algae, fungi, plants and animals — all six big groups of life found in different regions of Earth.

1.2 Why is Biodiversity Important?

Biodiversity does much more than make Earth beautiful. It is essential for human life and the health of the planet:

Benefit What it gives us
Useful productsFibre, oil, dyes, rubber, water, timber, paper, food
Ecosystem balanceRecycles nutrients and keeps the ecosystem stable
Reduces pollutionForests absorb carbon dioxide and clean the air
MedicinesAround 80% of the world's population uses medicines from nature
Tourism & beautyBeautiful animals and trees attract tourists and add value to a region
Exam tip

Remember the 80% figure — about 80% of the world's people rely on natural medicines. This number appears often in MCQs and short answers.

1.3 The Variety of Life on Earth

Biodiversity is huge. Currently around 1.5 million species have been described and given scientific names — and many more are still being discovered. Some examples from different habitats:

Group Examples
PlantsAngiosperms (flowering plants), Gymnosperms (cone-bearing plants), Mosses, Liverworts, Hornworts
Cold-region animalsPolar bear
Desert animalsDesert hopping mouse
BirdsBlue bird
ReptilesCobra
Did you know?

The number of species we know is only a small part of the total. Scientists believe millions more species are still undiscovered, especially in deep oceans and dense rainforests.

1.4 Why Do We Need to Classify Such a Huge Variety?

With around 1.5 million known species, it is impossible to study every organism one by one. So biologists do what humans naturally do with anything large — they arrange it into groups.

This grouping of organisms into smaller, related sets is called biological classification. It is based on the similar and dissimilar characteristics that organisms share.
