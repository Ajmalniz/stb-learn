Chapter 3 · Topic 4

The Five Kingdoms

All known life is sorted into five big boxes: Monera, Protista, Fungi, Plantae, Animalia. This topic explains each kingdom in simple words — what cells they have, what they eat, and what examples to remember.

4.1 Kingdom Monera

Kingdom Monera includes all the prokaryotes — the simplest cells, without a true nucleus.

  • Cell type: Prokaryotic
  • Cell wall: Present (made of polysaccharide + amino acids, not cellulose)
  • Body organisation: Cellular, without membrane-bound organelles
  • Nutrition: Autotrophic (chemo- or photosynthetic) and heterotrophic (saprophytic / parasitic)
  • Examples: Bacteria and Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae)

Structure of a Bacterium (key parts)

Common parts found in bacteria and cyanobacteria:

  • Chromosomal DNA — the main genetic material, loose in the cytoplasm
  • Plasmid DNA — small extra rings of DNA
  • Ribosomes — for making proteins
  • Cell wall + Capsule — outer protection
  • Flagellum — whip-like structure for movement
  • Pilus — hair-like structures for attachment / exchange
  • Heterocyst, Akinetes, Vegetative cell, Cytoplasmic inclusion — special structures in cyanobacteria
Did you know?

Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae) are placed in Monera, not in Plantae, because they are prokaryotic — even though they can do photosynthesis like plants.

4.2 Kingdom Protista

Kingdom Protista is the home for eukaryotic unicellular organisms (with the exception of yeast). Many show features of both plants and animals.

  • Cell type: Eukaryotic
  • Body: Mostly unicellular (some multicellular)
  • Cell wall: Present in some, absent in others
  • Nutrition: Autotrophic (photosynthetic) and heterotrophic
  • Habitat: Most protists are aquatic
  • Examples: Protozoa (Paramecium, Amoeba), unicellular algae
Why isn't Amoeba in Animalia?

Amoeba is a single-celled eukaryote — too simple to be a true animal. So it sits in Protista, not Animalia.

4.3 Kingdom Fungi

Fungi are multicellular eukaryotic organisms that do not have chlorophyll (so cannot photosynthesise).

  • Cell type: Eukaryotic, multicellular
  • Cell wall: Present — mainly made of chitin (not cellulose)
  • Nutrition: Absorptive heterotrophs — they absorb their food (saprophytic or parasitic)
  • Special feature: Achlorophyllous (no chlorophyll)
  • Body: Called a mycelium, made of thread-like structures called hyphae
  • Examples: Mushroom, Mucor, yeast
Mycelium
The body of a fungus, made of a network of thread-like hyphae.
Hyphae
Thread-like structures that build up the mycelium of a fungus.

4.4 Kingdom Plantae

Plantae includes all the multicellular, eukaryotic, photosynthetic organisms.

  • Cell type: Eukaryotic, multicellular
  • Cell wall: Present — mainly made of cellulose
  • Nutrition: Autotrophic (photosynthetic) — they make their own food using sunlight
  • Body organisation: Tissue / organ / organ-system level
  • Examples: Multicellular algae (e.g. Ulva lactuca), bryophytes, pteridophytes, gymnosperms, angiosperms
Key parts of a plant body (like Ulva)

Thallus — flat plant body; Undulate margin — wavy edge; Holdfast — root-like part that anchors it.

4.5 Kingdom Animalia

All multicellular eukaryotic ingestive heterotrophs without a cell wall.

  • Cell type: Eukaryotic, multicellular
  • Cell wall: Absent
  • Nutrition: Heterotrophic — they eat (ingest) food (holozoic / saprophytic)
  • Body organisation: Tissue / organ / organ-system level
  • Includes: All vertebrates and invertebrates except protozoa (which sit in Protista)
  • Examples: Kangaroo, starfish, humans, cobra, frog

4.7 Where Do Viruses Fit?

Viruses are a strange case. They are placed in none of the five kingdoms — because they don't quite fit anywhere.

Virus
A non-cellular obligate endoparasite — it lives only inside a host cell. It has no cellular organisation, but does have nuclear material (either DNA or RNA) inside a protein coat called a capsid.

Why viruses don't fit in any kingdom:

  • They have no cell — so they cannot be Monera/Protista/Fungi/Plantae/Animalia.
  • They reproduce only inside a host cell — outside, they act like a non-living particle.
  • Their entire body is just nucleic acid + protein coat.

Diseases caused by viruses:

  • In plants: Tobacco mosaic disease
  • In animals/humans: Cold, flu, dengue, polio, hepatitis, AIDS
Also non-cellular

Prions and viroids are also non-cellular particles, so they too cannot be placed in any of the five kingdoms.
