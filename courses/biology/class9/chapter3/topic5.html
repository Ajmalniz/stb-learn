Binomial Nomenclature & Conservation

In this final topic we cover two big ideas: how scientists give every species a unique two-part name, and why we must conserve the biodiversity of Pakistan from threats like deforestation and over-hunting.

5.1 Why Do We Need Scientific Names?

Common names of organisms change from language to language and region to region. This causes confusion:

Example

In Urdu, the onion is called 'Piyaz'; in other regions of Pakistan it is also called 'ganda' or 'basal'. But in scientific language it has just one name: Allium cepa.

To remove this confusion, Carolus Linnaeus introduced a uniform naming system called binomial nomenclature.

Advantages of scientific names:

  • Accepted by speakers of all languages.
  • Each name applies to only one species.
  • Each species has only one name.

5.2 What is Binomial Nomenclature?

Binomial Nomenclature
A system in which every species of animal, plant or other organism receives a name of two parts: the first identifies the genus, and the second is the species name.

"Bi" = two, "nomial" = name → "two names".

5.3 Rules for Writing Scientific Names

These rules are universal — used by all biologists in the world:

  • When printed, the scientific name is written in italics. When handwritten, it is underlined.
  • The first word (genus) always starts with a capital letter.
  • The second word (species) is never capitalised.
  • The first time you write a scientific name, write it in full (e.g. Rosa indica). Later, it may be abbreviated as R. indica.
  • If an author's name appears after the species, it means the species was first described by that scientist. Example: Mangifera indica L. means it was first described by Linnaeus.
Quick check — which is correct?

✔ Homo sapiens
✔ Allium cepa
✘ Homo Sapiens (species capitalised — wrong)
✘ homo sapiens (genus not capitalised — wrong)
✘ E.coli when written for the first time (must be full first)

5.5 Conservation of Biodiversity

Pakistan has every kind of geological structure — plains, deserts, forests, hills, plateaus, coastal areas of the Arabian Sea and the high Karakoram mountains in the north. This gives it a very rich biodiversity of fauna (animals) and flora (plants).

But in the last 20–30 years, many species have become threatened or endangered due to over-exploitation and habitat loss. Major threats include deforestation, overgrazing, soil erosion, salinity, and waterlogging.

Conservation
The way of caring for and saving the species inhabiting Earth from dangers.

Why conserve biodiversity?

  • Many biological resources and services are essential for human life (food, oxygen, medicines).
  • It boosts ecosystem productivity — every species has a role in its habitat. If biodiversity drops, food chains and ecosystems collapse.
  • More plants and animals = stronger soil — less erosion, less drought, less flooding.
  • Biologists warn the global ecosystem will collapse if biodiversity keeps dropping at the current rate.

5.6 Problems in Conserving Biodiversity in Pakistan

A 2009 article, "Biodiversity in Pakistan: Key Issues", listed the main problems in carrying out the Biodiversity Action Plan (BAP):

  • Lack of awareness of environmental issues among decision-makers and the public.
  • Weak governance — slow decision-making, weak policy, and confusion between public and private interests.
  • Weak capacity of government departments — and lack of incentives for performance.
  • Lack of funding for conservation work.

5.7 Human Activities That Harm Biodiversity

The IUCN (International Union for Conservation of Nature) reports:

  • 75% of genetic diversity of agricultural crops has been lost.
  • 75% of the world's fisheries are overexploited.
  • 1/3 of coral reefs are threatened with extinction.

Main human activities damaging biodiversity:

Human activity Effect
Urbanisation / industrialisationOverpopulation needs more buildings → habitat lost
DeforestationSoil erosion, low rainfall, climate change
PollutionDestroys plant life, causes global warming
Over-huntingDirect killing of wild animals

Other pressures include habitat loss and degradation, invasive species, and climate change.

5.8 Deforestation — Causes & Effects

Forests cover about 31% of land area on Earth. They produce oxygen, provide homes for wildlife, and give food, fresh air, clothing, medicine and shelter. Forests also act as a carbon sink — absorbing CO₂ that would otherwise warm the planet.

Deforestation
The cutting down of trees for the conversion of forest into non-forest land.

Causes of Deforestation

  • Mining
  • Paper making
  • Urbanisation
  • Timber harvesting
  • Building roads
  • Agriculture expansion & livestock breeding

Effects of Deforestation

  • Increase in greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane, water vapour, nitrous oxide) → global warming
  • Higher temperatures → glaciers melt → sea levels rise → floods
  • Habitat loss for wildlife
  • Soil erosion (no roots to hold soil)
  • Low rainfall due to reduced transpiration

5.9 Endangered & Extinct Species

Endangered Species
Species that are at risk of extinction in the future.
Extinct Species
Species for which there is certainty that the last individual has died — none can be found in the ecosystem any more.

Endangered species of Pakistan (must-know list):

  • Snow leopard
  • Long-billed vulture
  • Green sea turtle
  • Marco Polo sheep
  • European otter
  • Baluchistan forest dormouse
  • Indus river dolphin
  • Asian black bear
  • Sindh Ibex (Markhor)
Exam tip

The Indus river dolphin and Markhor are favourite MCQ targets — they live only in Pakistan and are heavily endangered.
