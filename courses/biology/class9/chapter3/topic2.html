Aims & Principles of Classification

With millions of species on Earth, biologists need a system to organise them. This topic explains why we classify, how we classify, and the famous taxonomic hierarchy — the ladder of seven levels from kingdom down to species.

2.1 What is Classification?

Organisms share some similar fundamental characteristics with each other and differ in others. Grouping them on the basis of these shared features is called classification.

Classification
The arrangement of organisms into groups and sub-groups on the basis of their similar and dissimilar characteristics.
Taxonomy
The science of classification (Tazm = group, Nomy = naming). A person who studies taxonomy is called a taxonomist.

2.2 Aims of Classification

Biologists classify organisms with two main aims:

  • To determine the similarities and dissimilarities among organisms so they can be studied easily.
  • To find the evolutionary relationships among organisms — who is related to whom in nature.

2.3 Principles of Classification

Classification cannot always be done on the basis of one feature alone. Scientists use several different principles:

Principle What is studied
MorphologyExternal features of the organism (shape, size, parts)
CytologyCellular structure of organisms
GeneticsGenetic constitution & development pattern
BiochemistryComparing the chemical substances inside organisms

Homologous vs Analogous Structures

Within morphology, biologists look at two special kinds of structure:

Feature Homologous Analogous
StructureSimilar in structureDifferent in structure
FunctionDifferent functionsSame function
ExampleArm of a human, wing of a bat, flipper of a whale — all have similar bonesWing of a bird and wing of a butterfly — both fly but built very differently
Easy way to remember

Homologous = similar look, different job (like our arm and a bat's wing). Analogous = different look, same job (a bird wing and a butterfly wing — both for flying).

2.4 Taxonomic Hierarchy

The groups into which organisms are classified are called taxa (singular: taxon). These taxa are arranged in ascending order to form a ladder — the taxonomic hierarchy.

From biggest group to smallest group:

Kingdom (biggest)
↓
Phylum (or Division in plants)
↓
Class
↓
Order
↓
Family
↓
Genus
↓
Species (smallest)

All organisms are first classified into five kingdoms, so kingdom is the highest taxon. Each kingdom is then divided into smaller taxa step by step.

Exam tip

Memorise the order: Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species. A common mnemonic is "King Philip Came Over For Good Soup".

2.5 Units of Classification

The smallest and most basic unit of classification is the species.

Species
A group of individual organisms with fundamental similarities that can naturally interbreed to produce a fertile offspring.

Higher units are formed by grouping species together:

  • Closely related species → grouped into a genus (plural: genera)
  • Similar genera → grouped into a family
  • Families → grouped into orders
  • Orders → grouped into classes
  • Classes → grouped into phyla (or divisions for plants)
  • Phyla / divisions → grouped into kingdoms
