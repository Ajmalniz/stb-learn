Chapter 6 · Topic 4

Mechanism of Enzyme Action

How does an enzyme actually do its job? The substrate joins the enzyme at the active site, the reaction takes place, and the products leave — freeing the enzyme to work again. Two famous models explain how the fit happens: Lock and Key and Induced Fit.

4.1 The General Action of an Enzyme

Every enzyme-catalysed reaction follows the same simple sequence:

1. Enzyme exposes its active site
↓
2. Specific substrate enters the active site
↓
3. Enzyme-Substrate Complex (ESC) forms
↓
4. Reaction occurs → Enzyme-Products complex
↓
5. Products leave; enzyme is free to use again

To explain step 2 and step 3, scientists came up with two models.

4.2 Model 1 — Lock and Key Model

Lock and Key Model
A model proposed by Emil Fischer in 1894. It explains that an enzyme and its substrate have specific complementary geometric shapes that fit exactly into one another, just like a key fits into a lock.

In this model:

  • The enzyme is like a lock.
  • The active site is like the key hole.
  • The substrate is like the key — only the correct size and shape fits.
  • Example: lipase fits with lipids to break them down — but it cannot fit with proteins.

This model explains the high specificity of enzymes very nicely.

Limitation

The lock-and-key model does NOT explain how the enzyme stabilises the transition state of the reaction. The active site is shown as a rigid shape — but in reality it can flex. So a second model was proposed.

4.3 Model 2 — Induced Fit Model

Induced Fit Model
A model proposed by Daniel Koshland in 1958. It explains that the active site is flexible — it continuously changes shape until the substrate binds to it, moulding around the substrate for a perfect fit.

Key idea: the active site is not rigid. When the substrate approaches, the enzyme's active site adjusts its shape to grip the substrate tightly — like a glove adjusting to a hand.

This explains things the lock-and-key model could not — including how the enzyme helps stabilise the transition state of the reaction.
