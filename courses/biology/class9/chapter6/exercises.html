Section 1 — Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

Encircle the correct answer. Correct option is marked in green.

(i) All are characters of enzymes except:
A. Enzymes speed up a biochemical reaction.
B. Enzymes are sensitive to minor changes in pH.
C. Enzyme activity is enhanced by inhibitors.
D. The portion of the enzyme where substrate attaches is called the active site.
Inhibitors decrease enzyme activity, not enhance it. It is activators that enhance enzyme activity. All the other options are correct properties of enzymes.
(ii) Enzymes are:
A. Steroid in nature
B. Protein in nature
C. Lipid in nature
D. Carbohydrate in nature
Enzymes are mostly proteins — three-dimensionally folded chains of amino acids with a specific shape. This protein nature is why they are so sensitive to changes in temperature and pH.
(iii) Metabolic reactions are: (I) Constructive (II) Destructive (III) Inhibiting
A. I only
B. I and II only
C. I, II and III
D. II and III only
Metabolic reactions are of two types: constructive (anabolism) and destructive (catabolism). "Inhibiting" is not a category of metabolism — inhibitors affect enzyme activity but are not a type of reaction.
(iv) The point where the enzyme is most active is known as:
A. Neutral pH
B. Acidic pH
C. Basic pH
D. Optimum pH
Optimum pH is the specific pH at which an enzyme is most active. It varies by enzyme — pepsin has an acidic optimum (~ 2), trypsin has a basic optimum, most others are at pH 7.4.
(v) "Active site continuously changes its shape until the substrate binds to it." This is the statement of:
A. Induced fit model
B. Lock and key model
C. Fluid mosaic model
D. Both A and B
The induced fit model (Daniel Koshland, 1958) says the active site is flexible and adjusts its shape to fit the substrate. The lock-and-key model treats the active site as rigid.
(vi) Select the mismatched:
A. Proteases → Carbohydrate
B. Lipases → Lipids
C. Trypsin → Protein
D. All are correctly matched
Proteases act on proteins, not carbohydrates. Carbohydrates are broken down by carbohydrases (e.g. amylase). Lipase → lipid and trypsin → protein are correct matches.
(vii) Chemical reactions require particular conditions to carry down at a proper rate, especially:
A. Temperature and Nature
B. Nature and Pressure
C. Nature and Structure
D. Temperature and Pressure
Chemical reactions need particular temperature and pressure conditions. In a living body, these are usually too gentle for the reactions to proceed without enzymes.
(viii) All are factors affecting enzyme activity except:
A. pH
B. Substrate concentration
C. Organic solvent
D. Temperature
The three main factors that affect enzyme activity (per the textbook) are substrate concentration, temperature, and pH. Organic solvent is not listed as one of the main factors.
(ix) Rate of reaction will increase when temperature:
A. Increases
B. Decreases
C. Below 10 °C
D. Both A and C
As temperature increases, the rate of reaction initially increases because of more kinetic energy and more collisions between enzymes and substrates. Beyond the optimum the enzyme denatures, but increase is the initial effect.
(x) Choose the correct statement regarding lock and key model.
A. Enzyme and substrate possess specific complementary geometric shapes.
B. Active site of enzyme is flexible.
C. Active site continuously changes.
D. All above statements are correct.
The lock-and-key model says enzyme and substrate have specific complementary geometric shapes that fit exactly into one another. A flexible/changing active site is the idea of the induced fit model, not lock-and-key.

Section 2 — Fill in the Blanks

(i) There are __________ types of metabolic reactions.

Two. The two types are anabolic (constructive) and catabolic (destructive) reactions.

(ii) Enzymes catalyse chemical reactions by __________ the activation energy.

Lowering (reducing). Enzymes work by lowering the activation energy required to start a reaction, allowing the reaction to happen at body temperature and pressure.

(iii) Presence of an enzyme does not affect the nature or properties of __________.

End products. Enzymes only speed up the reaction — they do not change the chemical nature of the final products.

(iv) In a constructive reaction, __________ molecules are formed.

Large. In anabolic (constructive) reactions, small molecules are joined together to form large molecules used to build cell or body structures.

(v) Activity of enzymes can be enhanced by __________.

Activators. Activators are molecules that increase the activity of an enzyme. The opposite is inhibitors, which decrease it.

(vi) Small portion of an enzyme where substrate attaches with enzyme is called __________.

Active site. The active site has a shape that is complementary to the substrate, which is why enzymes are so specific.

(vii) Enzyme activity is decreased by __________.

Inhibitors. Inhibitors bind to the enzyme and slow it down or stop it. Many medicines are inhibitors of enzymes in pathogens.

(viii) As temperature increases, initially the rate of reaction will __________.

Increase. Higher temperature gives molecules more kinetic energy → more collisions between enzyme and substrate → faster reaction. This only continues until the optimum temperature, after which the enzyme denatures.

(ix) Extreme changes in pH can cause enzymes to __________.

Denature. Extreme pH breaks the bonds holding the enzyme's 3D shape together. The active site is destroyed and the enzyme permanently loses its function.

(x) In the human body there are more than __________ known enzymes.

1000. Scientists have identified over a thousand different enzymes in the human body, each with its own specific job.

Section 3 — Define the Following Terms

(i) Substrate

A substrate is the reactant of an enzyme-catalysed reaction — the molecule that the enzyme acts on. It binds to the active site of the enzyme and is converted into the product. Example: for lipase, the substrate is lipid (fat).

(ii) Active site

The active site is the small region of an enzyme where the substrate attaches. Its shape is complementary to the shape of the substrate (lock-and-key) or it can adjust to fit (induced fit). The active site is the source of the enzyme's specificity.

(iii) Inhibitor

An inhibitor is a molecule that binds to an enzyme and decreases its activity. Many drugs are inhibitors — they block enzymes inside a pathogen, helping kill it.

(iv) Activator

An activator is a molecule that increases the activity of an enzyme. Activators help an enzyme work faster or make a low-activity enzyme functional.

(v) Anabolism

Anabolism is the type of metabolism in which large molecules are formed from smaller molecules. These constructive reactions are used to build the structure of cells and the body. Example: photosynthesis, protein synthesis.

(vi) Catabolism

Catabolism is the type of metabolism in which large molecules are broken down into smaller molecules. These destructive reactions release energy or generate building blocks for reuse. Example: cellular respiration, digestion.

(vii) Activation energy

Activation energy is the minimum amount of energy required to initiate (start) a chemical reaction. Higher activation energy means the reaction is harder to start. Enzymes lower the activation energy.

(viii) Cofactor

A cofactor is a non-protein substance required by some enzymes for their functioning. It can be inorganic (Zn²⁺, Mg²⁺, Mn²⁺, Fe²⁺, Cu²⁺, K⁺, Na⁺) or organic (NAD, NADP, FAD).

(ix) Prosthetic group

A prosthetic group is an organic cofactor that is tightly (permanently) bound to its enzyme. It is needed for the enzyme to function and stays attached throughout the reaction.

(x) Coenzymes

Coenzymes are organic cofactors that are loosely attached to their enzyme. They can come and go during reactions. Examples: NAD, NADP, FAD.

Section 5 — Short Answer Questions

(i) Why are enzymes specific in nature?

Enzymes are specific because of their active site:

  • The active site has a specific geometric shape that fits only the matching substrate (like a key fits a lock).
  • The amino acids around the active site carry different charges — acidic, basic, hydrophilic — which only attract the right kind of substrate.
  • Enzymes can also be specific to the type of bond, the functional group or the shape of the substrate.
  • Because of this, a single enzyme catalyses only one reaction (or a small group of related reactions).
  • Example: Lipase only acts on lipids; protease only acts on proteins.
(ii) How does an enzyme reduce the amount of activation energy?

An enzyme reduces activation energy by:

  • Bringing the substrate molecules close together in the correct orientation at the active site, so they can react easily.
  • Holding the substrate in a position that makes it easier to break or form bonds.
  • Using its amino acid side groups to stabilise the transition state of the reaction.
  • Forming an enzyme-substrate complex that requires less energy to convert into products than the substrate would alone.

By doing this, the reaction can take place at body temperature and pressure, instead of needing very high temperatures.

(iii) Why does the presence of enzymes not affect the nature and properties of the end product?

The enzyme does not affect the end product because:

  • An enzyme is a catalyst — it only speeds up the reaction, it does not take part in it as a reactant.
  • It just lowers the activation energy and brings reactants together.
  • After the reaction, the enzyme detaches from the products in the same shape and chemical form as before.
  • So the final product has the same nature whether the enzyme was present or not — the enzyme just makes it appear much faster.
  • This also means a single enzyme can be reused many times.
(iv) How does substrate concentration affect enzyme activity?

If the enzyme amount is kept constant and the substrate concentration is gradually increased:

  • At first, the rate of reaction increases — more substrate gives more chances for collisions with enzyme molecules.
  • This continues until a maximum rate is reached — the point of saturation.
  • After saturation, adding more substrate has no significant effect on the rate.
  • This is because every enzyme molecule is already occupied with a substrate. Excess substrate must wait until the bound substrate has been released as product.
(v) How are enzymes used in industries?

Many enzymes are used commercially in different industries:

  • Paper industry: to extract cellulose from raw plant material for paper making.
  • Food industry: in making bakery products and pizza dough (e.g. amylase, protease).
  • Brewing industry: enzymes from yeast convert sugar into alcohol by fermentation.
  • Bio-detergents: enzymes are added to washing powders to remove different stains — protease for protein stains (like blood), lipase for fat stains, amylase for starch stains. They work even at low water temperatures.

Section 6 — Long Answer Questions

(i) What are enzymes? Describe characteristics of enzymes.

Enzymes are biocatalysts (mostly made of protein) that facilitate biochemical reactions by lowering the activation energy required to start them. They allow biological reactions to occur quickly at the relatively low temperature and pressure inside a living organism (e.g. ~ 37 °C in humans).

The word "enzyme" means "in yeast" — first coined when scientists saw yeast convert fruit sap into alcohol.

Characteristics of enzymes:

  • Protein nature — 3D folded chains of amino acids with a specific shape held by bonds like hydrogen bonds.
  • Biocatalysts — they speed up reactions by lowering activation energy.
  • Not used up — a single enzyme molecule can drive many reaction cycles.
  • Do not change the product — only speed it up.
  • The reactant they act on is called the substrate.
  • Highly specific — each enzyme acts on only one substrate (or a small group of related ones).
  • Have an active site — a small region complementary in shape to the substrate.
  • Sensitive to conditions — small changes in pH, temperature or substrate concentration can affect activity.
  • Many need a cofactor (inorganic ion or organic molecule like NAD, NADP, FAD). Organic cofactors that are tightly bound are prosthetic groups; loosely bound ones are coenzymes.
  • Their activity can be enhanced by activators and decreased by inhibitors.
  • Many enzymes work together in metabolic pathways, with the product of one being the substrate of the next.
  • Used widely in industries — paper, food, brewing, bio-detergents.
(ii) Describe factors affecting enzyme activity.

The three main factors that affect enzyme activity are substrate concentration, temperature and pH.

1. Substrate Concentration

  • If enzyme amount is constant and substrate is increased, the rate of reaction increases.
  • This continues until the point of saturation — when all enzyme molecules are busy.
  • After saturation, more substrate does not increase the rate, because excess substrate has nowhere to bind.

2. Temperature

  • Because enzymes are proteins, they are very sensitive to temperature.
  • As temperature rises, kinetic energy increases → more collisions → rate increases.
  • At the optimum temperature (~ 37 °C for humans), the rate is at a maximum.
  • Above the optimum, increased vibrations break bonds in the enzyme's structure → the enzyme is denatured.
  • A denatured enzyme loses its active-site shape and the rate falls rapidly, sometimes to zero.

3. pH

  • Enzymes have a narrow range of pH where they work best — the optimum pH.
  • Examples: pepsin works at very low (acidic) pH in the stomach; trypsin works at high (basic) pH in the small intestine; most other enzymes work at neutral pH ~ 7.4.
  • Small changes in pH cause only temporary changes — bonds can reform.
  • Extreme changes in pH cause permanent denaturation, and the enzyme loses its function.

By controlling these factors, organisms keep their enzymes working at their best rate.
