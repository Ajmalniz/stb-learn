Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

Enzymes only work well under the right conditions. The three big factors are substrate concentration, temperature and pH. We'll also cover the main industrial uses of enzymes.

3.1 Why These Factors Matter

Organisms adjust the conditions around their enzymes to keep an optimum rate of reaction. Some organisms even have special enzymes adapted for extreme environments (very hot springs, very acidic stomachs).

3.2 Factor 1 — Substrate Concentration

If the enzyme amount is kept constant and the substrate concentration is gradually increased:

  • Reaction velocity increases at first.
  • It reaches a maximum (the point of saturation).
  • After that, increasing substrate further produces no significant change in the reaction rate.

Why? At saturation, every enzyme molecule is busy. Extra substrate molecules cannot react until the substrate already bound to the enzyme has been released.

3.3 Factor 2 — Temperature

Because enzymes are proteins, they are extremely sensitive to thermal changes. Enzyme activity occurs over a narrow temperature range compared to ordinary chemical reactions.

As temperature rises:

  • At first, the reaction rate increases — more kinetic energy means more collisions between enzymes and substrate molecules.
  • At the optimum temperature, the rate is the highest. For human enzymes this is close to 37 °C.
  • Beyond that, vibrations break the bonds holding the enzyme shape — the enzyme is denatured.
  • A denatured enzyme loses its active site shape and the rate falls rapidly — it may even stop completely.
Optimum Temperature
The temperature at which an enzyme works at its maximum rate. For human enzymes, ~ 37 °C.
Denaturation
The loss of the enzyme's 3D shape (and therefore its active site) due to extreme temperature or pH. A denatured enzyme cannot work.

3.4 Factor 3 — pH

Because enzymes are proteins, they are also very sensitive to pH. Each enzyme has a narrow pH range where it works best — its optimum pH.

Enzyme Optimum pH Where it works
PepsinVery low (~ 2) — highly acidicStomach
TrypsinHigh — basicSmall intestine
Most other enzymes~ 7.4 (neutral)Blood, most cells
  • Small changes in pH (above or below the optimum) cause only a temporary change — bonds can reform when pH is restored.
  • Extreme pH changes cause denaturation — the enzyme loses its function permanently.

3.5 Industrial Uses of Enzymes

Enzymes are not just found in living cells — they are used in many industries:

Industry What enzymes do
Paper industryBreak down material to extract cellulose for paper making
Food industryUsed in making bakery products and pizza dough
Brewing industryConvert sugar into alcohol (fermentation)
Bio-detergentsRemove different types of stains (protein, fat, starch) from clothes at lower temperatures
