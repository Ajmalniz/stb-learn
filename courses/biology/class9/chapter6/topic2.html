Chapter 6 · Topic 2

Characteristics of Enzymes

All enzymes share a set of common properties. This topic lists them — the active site, cofactors, activators, inhibitors and metabolic pathways — and gives you the key vocabulary for the rest of the chapter.

2.1 The Key Characteristics

  1. Biocatalysts made of protein. Enzymes are 3D folded chains of amino acids with a very specific shape, held together by bonds such as hydrogen bonds. The shape depends on the sequence of amino acids.
  2. They lower activation energy. They bring reactants together and speed up reactions without being used up themselves.
  3. Not used up. A single enzyme molecule can catalyse many reaction cycles — even a tiny amount of enzyme can drive a reaction very fast.
  4. Don't change the end product. Their presence does not affect the nature or properties of the final product.
  5. Reactants are called substrates. The substance that an enzyme acts on is called its substrate.
  6. Highly specific action. A single enzyme catalyses only a single reaction (or a small group of related reactions).
  7. Active site. A small portion of the enzyme where the substrate attaches is called the active site — its shape is complementary to the shape of the substrate.
  8. Sensitive to conditions. Enzymes are very sensitive to even minor changes in pH, temperature and substrate concentration.
  9. Often need cofactors. Some enzymes only work when a non-protein helper molecule is attached (see 2.3 below).
  10. Can be enhanced or blocked. Activators speed enzymes up; inhibitors slow them down or stop them.
  11. Work in metabolic pathways. Many enzymes work in sequence, with the product of one becoming the substrate of the next.

2.2 The Active Site & the Substrate

Substrate
The reactant that an enzyme acts on.
Active Site
A small portion of the enzyme where the substrate attaches. Its shape is complementary to the shape of the substrate (like a key fitting a lock).

The active site is what gives an enzyme its specificity — only the right-shaped substrate will fit. Different amino acids in the active site carry different charges (acidic, basic, hydrophilic), making the active site highly selective.

2.3 Cofactors — The Enzyme's Helpers

Cofactor
A non-protein substance required by some enzymes for their functioning. Can be organic or inorganic.

Examples of cofactors:

  • Inorganic ions: Zn²⁺, Mg²⁺, Mn²⁺, Fe²⁺, Cu²⁺, K⁺, Na⁺
  • Organic cofactors: NADP, NAD, FAD

Two Categories of Organic Cofactors

Type How it is attached
Prosthetic groupTightly bound to the enzyme
CoenzymeLoosely attached to the enzyme
Memory hook

Prosthetic = stuck on permanently (think of a prosthetic limb). Coenzyme = comes and goes.

2.4 Activators & Inhibitors

Activator
A molecule that increases the activity of an enzyme.
Inhibitor
A molecule that binds to an enzyme and decreases its activity (or stops it altogether).

Inhibitors are very important in medicine — many drugs are enzyme inhibitors that block the enzymes of a pathogen, helping to kill it.

2.5 Metabolic Pathways

Many enzymes work together in a sequence. The product of the first enzyme becomes the substrate of the next, and so on.

Metabolic Pathway
A sequence of enzyme-catalysed reactions in which many enzymes work in order to produce a specific product.

Example: cellular respiration is a long metabolic pathway with many enzymes working step by step to break down glucose and produce ATP.
