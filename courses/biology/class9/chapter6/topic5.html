Chapter 6 · Topic 5

Specificity of Enzymes

There are more than 1000 known enzymes in the human body — and each one works only on its own special substrate. This is called enzyme specificity. We'll also classify enzymes by where they work — inside or outside the cell.

5.1 Why Are Enzymes So Specific?

Specificity comes from the active site of the enzyme. Two reasons make it picky:

  • The active site has a specific geometric shape — only a substrate of the right shape fits.
  • The amino acids around the active site carry different charges and properties (acidic, basic, hydrophilic etc.) — so only the right kind of substrate is attracted.

This means a single enzyme will only catalyse one reaction (or a small group of related reactions).

5.2 What Enzymes Recognise

Different enzymes can be specific to different features of their substrate:

  • The bond formed between molecules.
  • The functional group present in the molecules.
  • The geometric shape of the molecules.

Examples

Enzyme Substrate Action
Proteases (e.g. pepsin, trypsin)ProteinsBreak down peptide bonds in proteins
LipasesLipids / fatsBreak down the ester bond in lipids

So enzymes are bond specific. Lipase, for example, acts on the ester bond in lipids — it won't act on a peptide bond in a protein.

5.3 Intracellular vs Extracellular Enzymes

Enzymes can also be classified by where they do their job:

Type Where they work Examples
IntracellularInside the cellATPase, cytochrome C reductase
ExtracellularOutside the cells (released by the cells, e.g. into the gut)Pepsin, lipase
More than 1000!

In your body alone, scientists have identified more than 1000 enzymes. Each one has its own substrate and its own special job — together they keep you alive every second.
