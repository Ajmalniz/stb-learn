Transpiration — Stomata, Significance & Factors Affecting Rate

Plants absorb far more water from the soil than they use in photosynthesis. Most of it escapes as water vapour from the leaves. This process — transpiration — is both essential and potentially dangerous for plants. This topic explains what it is, how stomata control it, why it matters, and what factors speed it up or slow it down.

2.1 What is Transpiration?

Transpiration
The loss of internal water of a plant in the form of water vapour from the aerial (above-ground) parts of the plant. Transpiration mainly takes place through tiny pores called stomata, which are guarded by specialised guard cells.

Evidence of Transpiration (Bell Jar Experiment)

  1. Take a potted plant and wrap a polythene bag tightly around the pot (not the plant) to prevent water vapour coming from the soil.
  2. Place it on a glass plate and cover with a dry bell jar. Set up a second bell jar without a plant as a control.
  3. Place both in sunlight for two hours.
  4. Observation: Water droplets appear on the bell jar with the plant. The control jar stays dry.
  5. Conclusion: The water vapour came from the plant — this is transpiration.

2.2 Transpiration and Leaf Surface

Plants have different arrangements of stomata on their leaves. This affects how much they transpire.

Leaf Type Stomata Location Example
BifacialLower epidermis onlyMango
Monofacial (Isobilateral)Both upper and lower epidermisMaize
Water plantsUpper epidermis onlyWater lily

Larger leaves have more stomata → higher transpiration rate. Desert plants have smaller leaves or spine-like leaves to reduce the number of stomata and conserve water.

Experiment: Proving stomata control transpiration

Take three equal-sized mango leaves. Apply petroleum jelly to: (1) upper surface, (2) lower surface, (3) both surfaces. Weigh before and after hanging in sunlight for a few hours. The leaf with jelly on the upper surface only loses the most weight — because mango stomata are on the lower surface, and upper surface transpiration was not blocked. This proves water vapour is lost from the surface where stomata are present.

2.3 Structure of Stomata & Opening/Closing Mechanism

Stomata are pores found in the leaf epidermis, each surrounded by two kidney-shaped guard cells.

  • Guard cells contain chloroplasts (other epidermal cells do not).
  • Inner wall of guard cells = thick and inelastic.
  • Outer wall = thin, elastic, and permeable.
  • The turgidity (water content) of the guard cells controls whether stomata open or close.
Condition Guard Cell State Stoma
Sunny day — photosynthesis produces sugar → guard cells gain solutes → water enters by osmosis Turgid (swollen) Open
Night — no photosynthesis → solutes decrease → water leaves guard cells Flaccid (limp) Closed
Why does the stoma open when guard cells are turgid?

The inner wall of the guard cell is thick and inelastic, while the outer wall is thin and elastic. When the guard cell fills with water and swells, the thin outer wall stretches more than the inner wall. This causes each guard cell to curve outward — pulling the inner walls apart and opening the pore between them.

2.4 Significance of Transpiration

Transpiration Pull
As leaf cells lose water by transpiration, their solute potential rises. They attract water from xylem. This creates a continuous pull (tension) that draws water up the xylem — the transpiration stream — helping the ascent of sap.
Faster Absorption
Loss of water at the top increases demand at the bottom. So transpiration speeds up absorption of water and minerals from the soil through the roots.
Excess Water Removal
Plants absorb far more water than they use in photosynthesis. Transpiration removes the surplus water.
Temperature Regulation
Evaporation of water from leaf surfaces cools the plant (like sweating in humans) — keeping the temperature suitable for metabolism.
Indirect control of photosynthesis
Opening and closing of stomata (regulated by transpiration) also controls CO₂ entry into leaves, indirectly influencing photosynthesis and respiration.
Transpiration — a "necessary evil"

Despite its benefits, transpiration is also called an "evil" for plants. Every year, millions of plants die on hot summer days because excessive transpiration causes wilting (loss of turgidity) and dehydration. If water loss is faster than absorption, the plant collapses.

2.5 Factors Affecting Rate of Transpiration

Temperature
Higher temperature increases the rate of evaporation of water from cell surfaces. More water turns to vapour and escapes through stomata → transpiration rate increases.
Humidity
Transpiration occurs only when water vapour concentration is lower outside the leaf than inside. High humidity (air already full of water vapour) reduces the concentration gradient → transpiration rate decreases.
Wind
Higher wind velocity blows away water vapour from around the leaf, keeping the air dry. This maintains a high concentration gradient → increases transpiration rate.
Atmospheric Pressure
Low atmospheric pressure reduces the density of air, making it easier for water vapour molecules to escape → increases transpiration rate.
