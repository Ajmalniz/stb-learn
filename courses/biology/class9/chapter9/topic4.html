Transport in Animals — Blood: Composition & Disorders

In large multicellular animals, simple diffusion is not enough to supply every cell with oxygen and nutrients. The solution is a circulatory system — a network of tubes and a pump that moves blood to every cell. This topic covers the types of circulatory systems and the detailed composition of blood, including its cells and disorders.

4.1 Why Multicellular Animals Need a Circulatory System

In unicellular organisms (e.g. Amoeba), the cytoplasm is close to the cell surface — so O₂ can diffuse directly in and CO₂ can diffuse directly out. Simple diffusion is sufficient.

In multicellular organisms like humans, most cells are far from the body surface and cannot exchange gases directly with the environment. They need a dedicated circulatory system to:

  • Carry O₂ from lungs to every cell
  • Carry CO₂ from every cell back to lungs
  • Transport nutrients, hormones, excretory products and defence proteins

4.2 Blood — Overview

Blood
A special type of fluid connective tissue that circulates in the body, transporting substances. It has two main components: (a) Plasma (the liquid part) and (b) Corpuscles (the cellular part: RBCs, WBCs, and platelets).

4.3 Plasma

Plasma makes up about 55% of blood volume. It is a pale yellowish liquid in which about 90% is water. The remaining 10% is a complex mixture of dissolved substances:

Mineral salts
Bicarbonates, sulphates, chlorides, phosphates of sodium (Na) and potassium (K) — present as ions. Calcium salts are also present for blood clotting.
Plasma proteins
Serum albumin, serum globulin, fibrinogen and prothrombin. Fibrinogen and prothrombin are vital for blood clotting.
Antibodies
Proteins that fight disease-causing organisms (pathogens).
Digested food
Glucose, amino acids, fatty acids and vitamins absorbed from the intestine.
Excretory products
Urea, uric acid and creatinine (waste from protein metabolism). Also CO₂ (carried as bicarbonate ions).
Hormones
Chemical messengers (e.g. insulin, adrenaline) secreted by glands and transported to target organs.

4.4 Erythrocytes (Red Blood Cells — RBCs)

Quantity5,000,000 per mm³ of blood
Size0.007–0.008 mm in diameter
ShapeBi-concave circular disc
CompositionNo nucleus; contain red pigment haemoglobin (a protein containing iron)
Produced inBone marrow
LifespanAbout 120 days
Destroyed inSpleen and liver
FunctionsTransport O₂ from lungs to body cells; transport CO₂ from body cells to lungs. When broken down: iron is stored in liver; bile pigments are excreted in gut.

4.5 Leucocytes (White Blood Cells — WBCs)

WBCs are colourless, irregular in shape, nucleated and larger than RBCs. There are about 7,500 per mm³. They are called the "police of the body" because they protect us from infections.

4.6 Platelets

Platelets are fragments of large cells formed in the bone marrow. They are not complete cells — they have no nucleus. There are about 250,000–400,000 per mm³.

Function — blood clotting:

  • On injury, exposure to air triggers platelets to produce an enzyme.
  • This enzyme converts soluble fibrinogen (a plasma protein) into insoluble fibrin.
  • Fibrin forms a network of fibres across the wound — trapping blood cells and forming a clot.
  • The clot stops bleeding and prevents pathogens from entering the wound.

4.7 Blood Disorders

Leukemia (Blood Cancer)
A type of cancer affecting the blood, bone marrow and lymphatic system. The number of WBCs increases abnormally while RBCs decrease.

Symptoms: Fever/chills, persistent fatigue, frequent infections, unexplained weight loss, swollen lymph nodes, enlarged liver or spleen, easy bleeding or bruising, recurrent nosebleeds, tiny red spots on skin (petechiae), night sweats, bone pain.

Causes: Mutations in DNA of blood cells; genetic disorder; exposure to certain chemicals; smoking; family history. Abnormal cells grow rapidly and crowd out healthy cells.
Thalassemia (Inherited Blood Disorder)
An inherited condition in which the body produces little or no normal haemoglobin. Red blood cells cannot carry enough oxygen.

Symptoms: Pale and restless appearance, poor appetite, slowed growth and delayed puberty, dark urine, enlarged spleen/liver/heart, jaundice.

Thalassemia Major: Child inherits two mutated genes (one from each parent). Develops severe anaemia within the first year of life. Cannot produce normal haemoglobin; suffers chronic fatigue.

Thalassemia Minor: Child inherits one mutated gene. Has mild anaemia and slightly low haemoglobin. Resembles mild iron-deficiency anaemia. Usually no obvious symptoms.
