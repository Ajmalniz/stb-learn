Water & Food Transport in Stem — Xylem and Phloem

Flowering plants have two dedicated "pipe systems" running through every root, stem and leaf. Xylem carries water and minerals upward. Phloem carries manufactured food (sugar) from the leaves to every other part. This topic explains the structure of each and how they work.

3.1 Two Types of Vascular Tissue

Flowering plants have a system of vessels for transport of water, minerals and food. These are called vascular (transport) tissues. There are two types:

🪵
XYLEM (Wood)
Transports water and dissolved minerals from roots up to leaves
🍃
PHLOEM (Bast)
Transports manufactured food (sucrose) from leaves to all other parts

3.2 Xylem — Structure and Function

Structure of Xylem

  • In flowering plants, xylem is made up of four types of tissues. The main and most important are the xylem vessels.
  • A xylem vessel is a long, hollow, tubular structure running from root to leaf.
  • It is made of many dead cells arranged vertically. The cells have no living contents (no cytoplasm, no nucleus) and their end walls have broken down, creating one continuous hollow tube.
  • The walls of xylem vessels are thickened by lignin — a tough chemical that makes them rigid and waterproof.
Why are xylem cells dead?

Dead xylem cells have no cytoplasm and no end walls — this creates a wide, hollow lumen with minimal resistance to water flow. The thick lignified walls also give the plant mechanical support (like a skeleton). Living cells with their contents would block the flow of water.

How Water and Minerals Move Through Xylem

  • The hollow lumen of xylem vessels reduces resistance to water flow, allowing rapid passage of the sap (water + dissolved minerals).
  • The transpiration pull (created by water evaporating from leaves) draws water up the xylem in a continuous column — this works because water molecules are attracted to each other (cohesion).
  • The thick, lignified walls provide mechanical support, preventing the tubes from collapsing under the tension.

3.3 Phloem — Structure and Function

Structure of Phloem

  • Like xylem, phloem is made of four types of tissues. The main ones are sieve tubes and companion cells.
  • Sieve tubes (sieve tube elements): columns of elongated, thin-walled living cells. The transverse walls between cells have many minute pores — these porous walls are called sieve plates because they look like a sieve. Food flows through these pores from cell to cell.
  • Companion cells: narrow, thin-walled cells sitting beside each sieve tube cell. They have many mitochondria, cytoplasm and a nucleus. Each sieve tube cell has lost its own nucleus and organelles, so it relies on its companion cell to carry out metabolic processes and keep it alive.
Loading sugar into phloem

Companion cells use active transport (energy from their many mitochondria) to load sugar (sucrose) from mesophyll cells in the leaf into the sieve tube cells. Once inside, the sugar flows through the sieve plates down to the rest of the plant.

How Food Moves Through Phloem (Munch / Bulk-Flow Hypothesis)

The most widely accepted explanation is the bulk-flow (Munch) hypothesis:

SOURCE (Leaves)
Photosynthesis makes large amounts of sucrose → mesophyll cells have high solute concentration → they draw water from xylem → their turgor pressure rises
↓ Turgor pressure gradient pushes sucrose through sieve tubes
SINK (Roots, Stem, Fruits, Growing Buds)
Lower turgor pressure. Solutes consumed or stored as insoluble starch. Excess water released back into xylem.

This process — movement of food from source to sink through phloem — is called translocation.
