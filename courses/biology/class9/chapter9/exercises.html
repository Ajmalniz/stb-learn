Section 1 — Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

Encircle the correct answer. Correct option is marked in green.

(i) The movement of organic materials (food) takes place through:
A. Xylem
B. Vessels
C. Tracheid
D. Phloem
Phloem transports manufactured food (sucrose) from leaves (source) to all other parts of the plant (sink) — a process called translocation. Xylem transports water and minerals only.
(ii) What regulates the flow of water through the xylem?
A. Passive transport by the endodermis
B. The number of companion cells in the phloem
C. The evaporation of water from the leaves
D. Active transport by sieve-tube membrane
Transpiration (evaporation of water from leaves) creates a transpiration pull — a tension that draws water up through the xylem from roots to leaves. The cohesion of water molecules maintains the continuous column.
(iii) Phloem transport of sucrose goes from "source to sink." Which of the following would NOT normally function as a sink?
A. Mature leaf
B. Storage organ
C. Growing root
D. Both B and C
A mature leaf is a source — it produces sugar by photosynthesis and loads it into the phloem. A sink is a region that uses or stores sugar: roots, fruits, seeds and storage organs are all sinks.
(iv) Human plasma proteins include: (I) Fibrinogen, (II) Haemoglobin, (III) Albumin. Which are plasma proteins?
A. I only
B. II only
C. I and II
D. I and III
Fibrinogen (for blood clotting) and Albumin (for osmotic balance and transport) are both dissolved in plasma. Haemoglobin is found inside red blood cells — it is not a plasma protein.
(v) Which of these is involved in the clotting of blood?
A. Platelets
B. Haemoglobin
C. Albumin
D. Globulin
Platelets are cell fragments that initiate clotting. On injury, they produce an enzyme that converts soluble fibrinogen into insoluble fibrin, which forms a network trapping blood cells and sealing the wound.
(vi) In the human heart, blood returning from the lungs first drains into the:
A. Left atrium
B. Left ventricle
C. Right atrium
D. Right ventricle
Oxygenated blood from the lungs returns via the pulmonary veins directly into the left atrium — the first chamber it enters on the left side of the heart. From there it goes to the left ventricle, then to the body.
(vii) Root hairs are most important to a plant because they:
A. Store starch
B. Contain xylem tissue
C. Provide a habitat for nitrogen-fixing bacteria
D. Increase the surface area for absorption
Root hairs are fine tubular outgrowths that enormously increase the surface area of the root. A larger surface area means faster uptake of water and dissolved minerals from the soil by osmosis and diffusion.
(viii) A type of circulatory system in which blood flows through the spaces among tissues is called:
A. Open type circulatory system
B. Close type circulatory system
C. Pulmonary circulatory system
D. Both II and III
In an open circulatory system, blood flows through open spaces called sinuses among tissues, directly contacting them. Found in arthropods and molluscs. In a closed system (like humans), blood stays inside blood vessels.
(ix) The part of the root between the epidermis and the endodermis is called:
A. Xylem
B. Root hair
C. Phloem
D. Cortex
The cortex is the region of the root that lies between the outer epidermis and the inner endodermis. It consists of multiple layers of cells through which water and minerals pass on their way to the xylem.
(x) Movement of water molecules from the area of higher water potential to the area of lower water potential is called:
A. Diffusion
B. Osmosis
C. Active transport
D. Water potential
Osmosis is the specific process of water molecules moving from a region of higher water potential (more water, fewer solutes) to a region of lower water potential (less water, more solutes) through a semi-permeable membrane.

Section 2 — Fill in the Blanks

(i) Loss of internal water of plant in the form of vapours from aerial parts of plants is called __________.

Transpiration. It mainly occurs through stomata in the leaves. Transpiration is driven by evaporation of water from the leaf surface, creating a pull that draws water up from the roots through the xylem.

(ii) In flowering plants xylem is made up of __________ types of tissues.

Four (4). Xylem consists of four types of cells: xylem vessels, tracheids, xylem fibres and xylem parenchyma. The main and most important conducting elements are the xylem vessels.

(iii) Root hair is a long, thin and tubular structure. It increases surface area which increases the __________.

Rate of absorption of water and minerals. The increased surface area of root hairs maximises contact with the soil solution, allowing more water and dissolved minerals to be absorbed in the same time period.

(iv) Opening and closing of stomata is also regulated by __________.

Transpiration. Transpiration affects the turgidity of guard cells — when transpiration rate is high, guard cells can lose water and close. The opening/closing of stomata in turn regulates the rate of transpiration, creating a feedback relationship.

(v) A type of cancer that affects the blood, bone marrow and lymphatic system is called __________.

Leukemia. In leukaemia, abnormal WBCs multiply rapidly and crowd out healthy RBCs, WBCs and platelets in the bone marrow. This weakens the immune system and reduces oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood.

(vi) Pumping of blood by the rhythmic pulsation of heart throughout the body is called __________.

Heartbeat. It is a two-phase action: diastole (relaxation — chambers fill with blood) and systole (contraction — blood is pumped out). The average heart beats about 72 times per minute.

(vii) Relaxation of heart muscles and allowing the chambers to fill with blood is called __________.

Diastole. During diastole, the heart muscle relaxes and the chambers expand, allowing blood to flow in from the veins (atria fill) and from the atria (ventricles fill). The opposite phase, systole, is when the heart contracts and pumps blood out.

(viii) Dead cells of xylem vessels arranged vertically have empty space inside called __________.

Lumen. The lumen is the hollow interior of the xylem vessel. Because the dead cells have no cytoplasm or end walls, the lumen forms a wide, continuous, unobstructed channel that greatly reduces resistance to water flow — allowing rapid movement of water and minerals up the plant.

(ix) Colourless, irregular in shape, nucleated and larger sized cells than RBCs are called __________.

Leucocytes (White Blood Cells / WBCs). There are about 7,500 WBCs per mm³ of blood. They protect the body by engulfing pathogens (phagocytosis) or producing antibodies. They are the "police" of the blood.

(x) An inherited condition that affects the blood haemoglobin is called __________.

Thalassemia. In thalassemia, the genes for making haemoglobin are mutated, so the body produces little or no functional haemoglobin. This results in anaemia, fatigue, and in severe cases (thalassemia major) requires regular blood transfusions to survive.

Section 3 — Define the Following Terms

(i) Blood

Blood is a special type of fluid connective tissue that circulates throughout the body in blood vessels. It consists of a liquid portion (plasma) and cellular components (RBCs, WBCs and platelets). It transports oxygen, carbon dioxide, nutrients, hormones, excretory products and defence proteins.

(ii) Water potential

Water potential is a measure of the tendency of water to move from one area to another. Pure water has the highest water potential. Adding solutes lowers the water potential. Water always moves by osmosis from an area of higher water potential to an area of lower water potential through a semi-permeable membrane.

(iii) Diffusion

Diffusion is the movement of molecules (or ions) from a region of higher concentration to a region of lower concentration — down the concentration gradient. It is a passive process that requires no energy. It continues until the concentration is equal on both sides (equilibrium).

(iv) Stomata

Stomata (singular: stoma) are tiny pores found in the epidermis of leaves, each surrounded by two kidney-shaped guard cells. They allow exchange of gases (CO₂ in, O₂ out) for photosynthesis and control the rate of transpiration by opening and closing in response to changes in guard cell turgidity.

(v) Bifacial leaves

Bifacial leaves are leaves that have stomata on only one surface — specifically the lower epidermis (underside). The mango leaf is a classic example. By keeping stomata on the cooler, shaded lower surface, the plant reduces water loss through transpiration.

(vi) Humidity

Humidity is the amount of water vapour present in the air. High humidity means the air is already saturated with water vapour, which reduces the concentration gradient between the inside of the leaf and the outside air — thereby decreasing the rate of transpiration.

(vii) Sieve plates

Sieve plates are the perforated transverse walls between adjacent sieve tube cells in phloem. They have many small pores that allow the cytoplasm of neighbouring cells to be connected, enabling a continuous flow of dissolved food (sucrose and other organic molecules) from one sieve tube cell to the next during translocation.

(viii) Sink

A sink is any region of the plant that receives and uses or stores the food (sucrose) transported by phloem. Examples include roots, growing buds, fruits, seeds and storage organs. The opposite of a sink is a source (usually the mature leaves that produce sugar by photosynthesis).

(ix) Granulocytes

Granulocytes are a type of white blood cell (WBC) that contain visible granules in their cytoplasm when stained and viewed under a microscope. There are three types: neutrophils (62%, destroy bacteria by phagocytosis), eosinophils (2%, fight parasites) and basophils (<1%, release heparin and histamine).

(x) Heartbeat

Heartbeat is the rhythmic pumping of blood by the heart throughout the body. It is a two-phase cycle: diastole (heart relaxes, chambers fill with blood) followed by systole (heart contracts, blood is pumped into arteries). An electrical signal coordinates each beat. The normal heart beats 60–100 times per minute.

Section 5 — Short Answer Questions

(i) Why are capillaries made up of a single layer of endothelium?

Capillaries are made of just a single layer of flat endothelial cells for the following reasons:

  • This makes the capillary wall extremely thin, so substances can pass through it easily by diffusion.
  • Oxygen, glucose and other nutrients pass from the blood into the surrounding tissue cells.
  • Carbon dioxide and waste products pass from tissue cells back into the blood.
  • A thick wall would slow down or prevent this essential exchange.

The single-layer design maximises exchange efficiency while still holding the blood inside the vessel.

(ii) Why is transpiration necessary for plants?

Transpiration is necessary for plants because:

  • Transpiration pull: It creates a tension in the xylem that draws water upward from the roots to the leaves — the ascent of sap. Without transpiration, water could not reach the top of tall trees.
  • Faster mineral absorption: As water is lost at the top, demand at the bottom increases, so roots absorb more water and minerals from the soil.
  • Temperature control: Evaporation of water from leaves cools the plant, preventing overheating during hot weather.
  • Excess water removal: Plants absorb far more water than they use — transpiration removes the surplus.
(iii) How does water flow through xylem?

Water flows through xylem due to the transpiration pull, explained as follows:

  • Leaves lose water by transpiration (evaporation through stomata).
  • This increases the solute concentration in leaf cells (lowers their water potential).
  • Leaf cells draw water from the xylem vessels in the leaf.
  • This creates a deficit (tension/pull) in the xylem, which is transmitted down the xylem all the way to the roots.
  • Because water molecules are attracted to each other (cohesion), they form a continuous column that is pulled upward as one unit.
  • At the root end, this pull draws more water in from the soil by osmosis.

The lumen of xylem vessels (hollow, no cytoplasm) minimises resistance, allowing rapid flow.

(iv) Why do veins contain semi-lunar valves?

Veins contain semi-lunar (pocket) valves because:

  • Blood pressure in veins is very low — unlike arteries, veins have no direct force from the heart to keep blood moving forward.
  • Veins often carry blood upward against gravity (e.g., from the legs to the heart).
  • Without valves, blood would flow backwards and pool in the lower limbs due to gravity.
  • The semi-lunar valves are like one-way doors — they open to let blood flow toward the heart, then snap shut to prevent backward flow.
  • Skeletal muscle contractions also help squeeze blood in veins forward; the valves then close to prevent it from going back.
(v) Why does atherosclerosis cause myocardial infarction and stroke?

Atherosclerosis causes these serious conditions through the following chain of events:

  • In atherosclerosis, bad fats (LDL cholesterol) are deposited in the inner layer of artery walls, forming plaques.
  • These plaques gradually narrow the lumen (inner space) of the artery.
  • A narrowed artery reduces blood flow to the organ it supplies.
  • Myocardial infarction (heart attack): If the coronary artery (supplying the heart muscle) is blocked, the heart muscle is starved of oxygen → heart muscle cells die → heart attack.
  • Stroke: If arteries supplying the brain are blocked (or burst due to high pressure), brain cells are deprived of oxygen → they die rapidly → paralysis, loss of speech, or death.
  • A blood clot can also form on top of the plaque and suddenly block the artery completely.

Section 6 — Long Answer Questions

(i) Describe the structure of the human heart with a suitable diagram.

The human heart is a muscular, hollow organ located in the thorax slightly to the left. It is the central pump of the closed circulatory system.

External features:

  • Enclosed in a tough fibrous bag: the pericardium.
  • Between the pericardium and the heart wall is the pericardial cavity, filled with lubricating pericardial fluid.
  • Conical shape externally.

Internal structure — 4 chambers:

  • Right atrium (RA) — receives deoxygenated blood from superior and inferior vena cava.
  • Right ventricle (RV) — pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs via the pulmonary artery.
  • Left atrium (LA) — receives oxygenated blood from the lungs via pulmonary veins.
  • Left ventricle (LV) — pumps oxygenated blood to the whole body via the systemic aorta. Has the thickest wall.

Valves (prevent backflow):

  • Tricuspid valve: between RA and RV.
  • Bicuspid (Mitral) valve: between LA and LV.
  • Semi-lunar valves: at the entrance of pulmonary arch and systemic aorta.

Flow diagram:

Body (deoxygenated blood) → Vena Cava → Right Atrium
↓ Tricuspid Valve
Right Ventricle → Pulmonary Artery → Lungs (oxygenation)
↓ Pulmonary Veins
Left Atrium → Bicuspid Valve → Left Ventricle
↓ Systemic Aorta
All Body Organs (O₂ delivered)
(ii) What is blood? Explain the composition of blood and the function of corpuscles.

Blood is a special fluid connective tissue circulating in the closed blood vessels. It acts as the body's delivery and waste-removal system, transporting oxygen, nutrients, hormones, CO₂ and waste products.

Composition of blood:

Blood has two main parts: Plasma (55%) and Corpuscles (45%).

Plasma: Pale yellowish liquid, ~90% water. Contains mineral salts (Na, K, Ca ions), plasma proteins (albumin, globulin, fibrinogen, prothrombin), antibodies, glucose, amino acids, fatty acids, vitamins, hormones, urea, uric acid, creatinine, and CO₂ (as bicarbonate).

Corpuscles:

  • RBCs (Erythrocytes): Bi-concave, non-nucleated, contain haemoglobin. ~5 million/mm³. Made in bone marrow; live ~120 days. Function: transport O₂ (from lungs to tissues) and CO₂ (from tissues to lungs).
  • WBCs (Leucocytes): Nucleated, colourless, irregular. ~7,500/mm³. Five types: neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils, monocytes and lymphocytes. Function: destroy pathogens by phagocytosis (neutrophils, monocytes) or by producing antibodies (lymphocytes). Called "the police of the body."
  • Platelets: Tiny cell fragments, no nucleus. ~250,000–400,000/mm³. Function: initiate blood clotting. On injury, they produce an enzyme that converts fibrinogen → fibrin, sealing the wound and preventing blood loss and infection.
(iii) What is transpiration? Explain the mechanism of transpiration and factors affecting it.

Transpiration is the loss of water as water vapour from the aerial parts of a plant, mainly through the stomata in the leaves.

Mechanism of transpiration:

  1. During the day, photosynthesis produces sugar → solute concentration inside guard cells increases → water enters guard cells by osmosis → guard cells swell (become turgid) → stomata open.
  2. Open stomata allow water vapour from the moist leaf interior to diffuse out into the drier air — this is transpiration.
  3. As leaf mesophyll cells lose water, their solute concentration rises (water potential falls).
  4. These cells draw water from the xylem vessels in the leaf.
  5. This creates a transpiration pull (tension) that draws water up the xylem continuously from the roots — the transpiration stream.
  6. The cohesion of water molecules maintains the continuous water column in the xylem.
  7. At night, photosynthesis stops, guard cells lose solutes, become flaccid, stomata close, and transpiration stops.

Factors affecting transpiration rate:

  • Temperature: Higher → faster evaporation → more transpiration.
  • Humidity: Higher → smaller concentration gradient for water vapour → less transpiration.
  • Wind: Stronger → removes water vapour from leaf surface → increases concentration gradient → more transpiration.
  • Atmospheric pressure: Lower → less dense air → easier for vapour to escape → more transpiration.
  • Leaf surface area and stomatal density: More stomata / larger leaves → more transpiration.
