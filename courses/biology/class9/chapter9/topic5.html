Human Heart, Blood Vessels & Cardiovascular Disorders

The heart is the pump at the centre of the circulatory system — beating over 100,000 times a day to keep blood flowing to every cell. This topic explains the heart's structure, how it pumps blood through two circuits, the three types of blood vessels, and what can go wrong in cardiovascular disease.

5.1 Structure of the Human Heart

  • The heart is the major organ of the circulatory system — a muscular pump that keeps blood circulating throughout the body.
  • Located in the thorax (chest cavity), slightly to the left side.
  • Enclosed in a tough fibrous bag called the pericardium. Between the pericardium and the heart is the pericardial cavity, filled with pericardial fluid. This fluid reduces friction and the pericardium protects the heart and prevents over-extension.
  • The heart is conical in shape externally.

Internal Chambers and Valves

Internally, the heart has four chambers:

  • Two atria (upper, thin-walled) — right atrium and left atrium. Separated by the inter-atrial septum. Function: stretch to receive returning blood, then contract to push blood down into the ventricles.
  • Two ventricles (lower, thick-walled) — right ventricle and left ventricle. Separated by the inter-ventricular septum. Function: pump blood out of the heart into arteries with great force.

Why is the left ventricle wall thicker than the right?

The right ventricle pumps blood only to the nearby lungs — a short distance, low pressure. The left ventricle must pump oxygenated blood to the entire body — a much longer route with much higher resistance. So the left ventricle needs a much thicker, more powerful muscle wall. This is why the left side of the heart is thicker and narrower.

5.2 Double Circulation — Two Circuits

In humans, blood passes through the heart twice per complete circulation — that is why it is called a double circuit system. The two circuits are:

① PULMONARY CIRCUIT — Heart ↔ Lungs
Right ventricle → Pulmonary artery (deoxygenated blood) → Lungs (CO₂ out, O₂ in) → Pulmonary veins (oxygenated blood) → Left atrium
② SYSTEMIC CIRCUIT — Heart ↔ Body
Left ventricle → Systemic aorta (oxygenated blood) → All body organs (hepatic artery → liver; renal artery → kidney; coronary artery → heart) → Superior & Inferior Vena Cava (deoxygenated blood) → Right atrium

5.3 Heartbeat, Heart Rate & Pulse

Heartbeat
The rhythmic pumping of blood by the heart. It is a two-phase action: (1) Diastole — heart muscle relaxes, chambers fill with blood. (2) Systole — heart muscle contracts, blood is pumped out into arteries. An electrical signal starts in the right atrium and coordinates both phases.
Normal Heart Rate
60–100 beats per minute (average 72). Must be kept in this range for healthy function.
Bradycardia
Heart rate below 60 beats/min (too slow). Reduces oxygen supply to organs → shortness of breath, low blood pressure, extreme fatigue.
Tachycardia
Heart rate above 100 beats/min (too fast). Heart does not have time to fill properly → not enough blood pumped forward → chest pain, dizziness, fainting. Caused by fever, dehydration, caffeine, medication, cardiac arrest, lung disease.
Pulse Rate
Exactly equal to heart rate. Taking a pulse (feeling the beat in an artery) is a direct measure of heartbeat.

Major Arteries and Veins

  • Arteries: Pulmonary artery (right ventricle → lungs), Aorta (left ventricle → body). From aorta: coronary artery (heart), hepatic artery (liver), renal artery (kidney), femoral artery (lower limbs).
  • Veins: Pulmonary veins (lungs → left atrium). Superior vena cava (head/neck/arms → right atrium). Inferior vena cava (lower body → right atrium): includes renal vein, hepatic vein, femoral vein.
Historical discoveries

Ibn-al-Nafees — the first Arab physician to describe pulmonary (heart-to-lung) circulation. William Harvey — the English physician who described systemic (whole-body) circulation in detail.

5.5 Cardiovascular Disorders (CVD)

CVD refers to diseases of the heart and blood vessels. They are the leading cause of death worldwide.

Atherosclerosis (ATH)
The most common cardiovascular disease. Bad fats (LDL cholesterol) gradually get deposited in the inner layer of blood vessel walls. This narrows the lumen → reduces blood supply to organs → can cause myocardial infarction (heart attack) and stroke.
Arteriosclerosis
Arteries lose their elasticity due to ATH or simply aging → high blood pressure → risk of vascular haemorrhage (rupture of blood vessels).
Myocardial Infarction (Heart Attack) — Risk Factors

Non-modifiable (cannot change): Sex (more common in males), Age (more common in old age), Race (more common in Black populations), Family history.

Modifiable (can change): Stationary lifestyle (no exercise), Smoking, Stress, Heavy alcohol consumption, High-fat diet.
Vascular Surgery
Surgery on blood vessels: bypass surgery (creating a new route around a blocked artery), angioplasty (widening a blocked artery with a balloon and stent), and fistula formation (for patients with kidney failure).
CVD in Pakistan

Since 2018, cardiovascular disorders and stroke have become the major cause of death in Pakistan. Contributing factors include stationary lifestyles, poor socioeconomic conditions, lack of health and dietary awareness, and limited access to specialist medical facilities in rural areas.
