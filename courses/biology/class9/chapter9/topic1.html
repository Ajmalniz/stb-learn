Transport in Plants — Root Structure & Water/Ion Uptake

Every organism needs to move substances around its body. Plants absorb water and minerals from the soil and distribute them to every leaf and stem. This topic explains why transport is needed, how the root is built for absorption, and the two processes — osmosis and active transport — that get water and minerals into the plant.

1.1 Why Do Organisms Need a Transport System?

Every organism requires substances (raw materials and metabolites) to survive. If the distance between the source of a substance and the organ that needs it is small enough, simple diffusion is enough. But when the distance is too large, a dedicated transport system is necessary.

A transport system must do two things:

  • (i) Transport raw materials from the environment to the organ where they are needed for metabolism.
  • (ii) Transport metabolites from cells to organs where they are required.
Plants vs Animals

Plants (autotrophs) take up inorganic molecules from the environment (water, CO₂, minerals), convert them into organic biomolecules (glucose, proteins, etc.) by photosynthesis, and then transport these biomolecules internally to wherever they are needed.

Animals (heterotrophs) take in organic food, digest it in the digestive tract, absorb digested molecules into the blood, and blood then carries them to every organ in the body.

1.2 Structure of the Root

Water and mineral salts enter the plant through the root. To understand how, we need to look at the root from the outside and inside.

External Structure

  • At the very tip is the root tip — the growing part, protected by a root cap.
  • The rest of the root is highly branched; each branch is densely covered by root hairs.
  • A root hair is a fine, tubular outgrowth of an epidermal cell. It grows between soil particles and remains in close contact with the soil solution that surrounds those particles.
Why root hairs?

A single root hair is tiny, but a plant has millions of them. Together they create an enormous surface area — massively increasing the rate at which water and minerals can be absorbed from the soil.

Internal Structure (Transverse Section of Root)

If you cut across a root and look at it under a microscope (transverse section, T.S.), you can see:

  • Epidermis (Epiblema) — the outermost layer of cells. Some of these cells have root hairs projecting outward.
  • Cortex — the region between the epidermis and the inner endodermis. It consists of several layers of cells that carry water inward.
  • Endodermis — a ring of cells that controls what passes from the cortex into the vascular core.
  • Xylem and Phloem — vascular tissues at the centre for transport of water and food.

1.3 Two Processes of Water and Ion Uptake

Passive Transport
The movement of water and minerals from high concentration to low concentration (down the concentration gradient) by osmosis and diffusion — without using ATP energy. The plant does not have to spend energy; the concentration difference does the work.
Active Transport
The movement of substances from low concentration to high concentration (against the concentration gradient) using energy from ATP. Carrier molecules (proteins) in the cell membrane attach to the substance and carry it across, powered by ATP.
Osmosis
The movement of water molecules from a solution of higher water potential (more water, fewer solutes) to a solution of lower water potential (less water, more solutes) through a semi-permeable membrane.

1.4 How Water Enters the Root (Osmosis)

Here is the step-by-step journey of water from the soil into the xylem:

Soil water (higher water potential)
↓ Osmosis
Root hair cell (lower water potential — cell sap is rich in solutes)
↓ Root hair becomes turgid → cell-to-cell osmosis
Cortex cells → Endodermis
↓
Xylem → Ascent of Sap (upward to leaves)

The cell sap inside root hair cells is a mixture of sugars, salts and amino acids. This gives the cell sap a lower water potential than the surrounding soil solution. So water moves from the soil (higher water potential) into the root hair (lower water potential) by osmosis.

Once in the root hair, the cell becomes turgid and its sap is now diluted — so it has a higher water potential than the next cortex cell. Water passes cell to cell until it reaches the xylem and rises up as ascent of sap.

Critical condition for absorption

For water to enter the root, the soil solution must have lower solute concentration (higher water potential) than the root hair cell sap. If the soil becomes too salty (e.g., due to excess fertiliser), the direction of water movement reverses and the plant loses water to the soil — causing wilting and possibly death.

1.5 How Minerals Enter the Root

Plants need minerals such as nitrates, sulphates and phosphates. These are taken up by root hairs in two ways, depending on their concentration in the soil:

  • By diffusion (passive transport) — when the concentration of certain ions in the soil is higher than inside the root hair. The ions simply diffuse down the concentration gradient, no energy needed.
  • By active transport — when the plant needs a mineral even though it is present in the soil in low concentration. The root uses ATP energy and carrier proteins to absorb the ions against the concentration gradient — from low concentration in soil to high concentration inside the root cell.
Real-life example

In poor soils, nitrogen concentration may be very low. Yet plants still absorb nitrates for making amino acids — using active transport. This is why actively growing plants need more energy (and a healthy supply of oxygen for respiration to make ATP).
