Introduction to Physics

Physics is defined as the branch of science which observes nature, represents it mathematically and concludes with experiments. It deals with matter, energy, and their interactions.

1.1 What is Physics?

Physics comes from the Greek word physicos, meaning "knowledge of nature."

Textbook Definition

"Physics is the branch of science which observes the nature, represents it mathematically and concludes with the experiment."

A broader scientific definition is:

Scientific Definition

"Physics is the branch of natural science that studies matter, its motion, its behavior through space and time and the related entities of energy and force."

Physics is often called the mother of all sciences because its laws and methods provide the foundation for chemistry, biology, engineering, and medicine.

Types of Physicists

Theoretical Physicist
Observes natural phenomena and forms mathematical theories to explain them. Example: Einstein developed the theory of relativity through thought experiments and mathematics.
Experimental Physicist
Tests theories through carefully designed experiments to confirm or challenge them. Example: scientists at CERN test particle physics theories using the Large Hadron Collider.
Did You Know?

Ancient Greek philosophers who studied natural phenomena were the first physicists. The word physics itself is Greek — Aristotle used it to describe the study of the natural world more than 2,300 years ago.

1.2 Branches of Physics

Physics covers a vast range of natural phenomena. Over centuries it has been divided into the following major branches:

1. Mechanics
Study of the laws of motion and gravitation. Explains how and why objects move — from a rolling ball to orbiting planets.
2. Thermodynamics
Study of heat and temperature and their relation to energy and work. Explains engines, refrigerators, and weather systems.
3. Electricity
Study of the properties of electric charges at rest and in motion. The basis of all electrical technology from light bulbs to computers.
4. Magnetism
Study of the magnetic properties of materials and the forces between magnets and current-carrying conductors.
5. Atomic Physics
Study of the composition, structure and properties of the atom — protons, neutrons, electrons, and their arrangement inside the atom.
6. Optics
Study of the physical aspects of light and its properties with the help of optical instruments such as lenses, mirrors, and telescopes.
7. Sound
Study of the production, properties and applications of sound waves — hearing, music, ultrasound diagnosis, and sonar navigation.
8. Nuclear Physics
Study of the constituents, structure, behavior and interactions of atomic nuclei. Foundation of nuclear power generation and medical radiation therapy.
9. Particle Physics
Study of the elementary constituents of matter and radiation and the interactions between them. Explores quarks, leptons, and the forces that hold particles together.
10. Astrophysics
Study of celestial objects such as stars, galaxies, and black holes using the laws of physics. Also includes cosmology — the origin and fate of the universe.
11. Plasma Physics
Study of the ionized state of matter and its properties. Plasma is the fourth state of matter, found in the sun, lightning, and fusion reactors.
12. Geophysics
Study of the internal structure of the Earth using physical methods — includes earthquakes (seismology), Earth's magnetic field, volcanoes, and plate tectonics.
Exam Tip

A common exam question is: "Name four branches of physics and briefly describe each." Learn the branch name and its one-line definition for each of the twelve branches above.

1.3 Importance of Physics in Science, Technology and Society

Society's dependence on technology shows how deeply physics affects daily life. Every modern device and industry is built on physical principles.

  • Television, computers and smartphones — based on discoveries in magnetism, electricity, and semiconductor physics (conductors and insulators).
  • Medical instruments — X-ray machines, MRI scanners, and ultrasound equipment all use principles of physics to diagnose and treat illness.
  • Modern transport — aircraft rely on fluid mechanics and thermodynamics; cars use combustion engines explained by thermodynamics and mechanics.
  • Telecommunications — mobile phone networks, satellites, and the internet rely on electromagnetism and signal processing.
  • Energy production — nuclear power plants, solar panels, and wind turbines all operate on physical laws.

Physics is also the foundation for all other natural sciences:

Chemistry
Uses atomic physics and quantum mechanics to explain chemical bonding and reactions at the molecular level.
Biology
Uses optics (microscopes), electricity (nerve impulses), and thermodynamics (body temperature regulation) to study living organisms.
Engineering
All branches of engineering — civil, mechanical, electrical, chemical — are applied physics put into practical use.
Medicine
Medical imaging, radiation therapy, pacemakers, and biomedical devices are all rooted in physical principles.
Real-Life Connection

Every time you use a smartphone, you are using the combined discoveries of dozens of branches of physics — from transistors in the chip (quantum mechanics) to the GPS signal (Einstein's theory of relativity) to the touchscreen (electromagnetism) to the battery (electrochemistry).

Key Point

Physics is the mother of all sciences — it provides the universal laws that apply everywhere in the universe, from the smallest particle to the largest galaxy.
