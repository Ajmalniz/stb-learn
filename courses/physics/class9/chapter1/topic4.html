Prefixes & Scientific Notation

Physical quantities can be extremely large or extremely small. Prefixes and scientific notation provide compact, standard ways to express these values without writing long strings of zeros.

4.1 SI Prefixes

Definition

"A unit prefix is a specifier. It indicates multiples or fractions of the units." Prefixes of the metric system represent multiplication by powers of ten.

Instead of writing 1,000,000 metres we write 1 Mm (megametre). Instead of 0.001 metres we write 1 mm (millimetre). The prefix carries the power of ten.

4.2 Converting with Prefixes

When converting between units with prefixes:

  • Smaller unit → Larger unit: divide (the number gets smaller)
  • Larger unit → Smaller unit: multiply (the number gets bigger)

Common Conversion Examples

1 km = 1000 m
kilo means 10³, so 1 km = 1 × 10³ m = 1000 m
1 mm = 0.001 m
milli means 10⁻³, so 1 mm = 1 × 10⁻³ m = 0.001 m
1 μg = 10⁻⁶ g
micro means 10⁻⁶, so 1 μg = 0.000001 g
1 ns = 10⁻⁹ s
nano means 10⁻⁹, so 1 ns = 0.000000001 s
1000 g = 1 kg
Divide by 1000 to convert from g to kg
100 cm = 1 m
centi means 10⁻², so 100 cm = 1 m
1 hour = 3600 s
1 hour × 60 min/hr × 60 s/min = 3600 s

4.3 Scientific Notation

Definition

"Scientific notation is a simple method of writing very large numbers or very small numbers as powers of ten."

Every number in scientific notation has three parts:

Coefficient (M)
A number that is greater than or equal to 1 and less than 10 (i.e., 1 ≤ M < 10). It is never zero.
Base
Always 10.
Exponent (n)
A whole number that can be positive (for large numbers) or negative (for small numbers).
Format

M × 10ⁿ

where 1 ≤ M < 10 and n is an integer (positive or negative).

4.4 Worked Examples

Example 1 — Mass of the Sun

Mass of Sun = 2,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000,000 kg

Step 1: The decimal point lies at the far right end of the number.

Step 2: Move the decimal point to the left until only one non-zero digit remains before it. Count the number of places moved.

Moving decimal 30 places to the left:

= 2.00 × 10³⁰ kg

Since we moved left, the exponent is positive.

Example 2 — Mass of an Electron

Mass of electron = 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg (convert from scientific notation to ordinary form)

Step 1: The exponent is −31, so move the decimal point 31 places to the left.

Step 2: Insert leading zeros as needed.

= 0.000 000 000 000 000 000 000 000 000 000 911 kg

(31 decimal places, with 9 in the 31st place)

More Conversion Examples

5,600 m
= 5.6 × 10³ m (move decimal 3 places left → positive exponent)
0.0042 kg
= 4.2 × 10⁻³ kg (move decimal 3 places right → negative exponent)
0.53 Å (radius of hydrogen atom first orbit)
1 Å = 10⁻¹⁰ m, so 0.53 Å = 0.53 × 10⁻¹⁰ m = 5.3 × 10⁻¹¹ m
300,000,000 m/s (speed of light)
= 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s (move decimal 8 places left)
2,628,000,000,000 m
= 2.628 × 10¹² m (move decimal 12 places left)
Why Scientific Notation Matters

The mass of the sun is 2 × 10³⁰ kg. The mass of an electron is 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg. The ratio between them is about 10⁶¹ — that is 1 followed by 61 zeros. Scientific notation makes comparing such extreme values manageable.

Key Rules Summary
  • Move decimal left → exponent is positive (large number)
  • Move decimal right → exponent is negative (small number)
  • The coefficient must always satisfy: 1 ≤ M < 10
  • The base is always 10 — never 2, never 100
