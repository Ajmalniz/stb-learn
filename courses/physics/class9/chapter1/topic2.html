Physical Quantities & Units

Physics describes nature mathematically using physical quantities. Understanding what they are, how they are classified, and how they are measured forms the backbone of all physics.

2.1 Physical Quantities

Definition

"A physical quantity is a physical property of a phenomenon, body, or substance that can be quantified by measurement."

Every physical quantity has two parts:

Numerical Value (Magnitude)
The number that tells how much. Example: the 5 in "5 metres."
Unit
The standard of measurement. Example: the metres in "5 metres." Without a unit, a number is meaningless in physics.

Physical quantities are divided into two categories: fundamental and derived.

2.2 Fundamental (Base) Quantities

Definition

"Physical quantities which cannot be explained by other physical quantities are called fundamental physical quantities."

There are seven fundamental quantities in the International System (SI):

# Fundamental Quantity SI Unit Symbol
1Lengthmetrem
2Masskilogramkg
3Timeseconds
4Electric CurrentAmpereA
5TemperatureKelvinK
6Amount of Substancemolemol
7Luminous Intensitycandelacd
Exam Tip

Memorise all seven fundamental quantities with their SI units and symbols. A very common question is: "Name any four fundamental quantities with their SI units."

2.3 Derived Quantities

Definition

"Physical quantities which are explained on the basis of fundamental physical quantities are called derived physical quantities."

Derived quantities are formed by combining fundamental quantities through multiplication or division. Examples:

Derived Quantity SI Unit Symbol
Volumecubic metrem³
Velocitymetre per secondm/s
ForceNewtonN
Densitykilogram per cubic metrekg/m³
Accelerationmetre per second squaredm/s²

2.4 Standard of Length

The SI unit of length is the metre (m).

Modern Definition of the Metre

"The metre is the length of the path travelled by light in vacuum during the time interval of 1/299,792,458 of a second."

In everyday terms, length is the minimum distance between two points on the same plane. Common instruments for measuring length:

  • Metre rule / metre rod — measures lengths up to 1 m with 1 mm precision
  • Inch tape — flexible tape for longer objects or curved surfaces
  • Vernier caliper — for precise measurements (covered in Topic 3)
  • Micrometer screw gauge — for very small dimensions (covered in Topic 3)

2.5 Standard of Mass

The SI unit of mass is the kilogram (kg).

The standard kilogram is a cylinder of a special metal alloy (platinum-iridium) approximately 39 mm wide and 39 mm tall. The National Prototype Kilogram is kept at the International Bureau of Weights and Measures in France.

Physical Balance

The physical balance measures mass using the principle of moments. When the beam is balanced, the mass on each pan is equal.

Parts of a physical balance:

  • Light rigid brass beam
  • Metallic pillar (support)
  • Wooden base
  • Two pans (left for unknown mass, right for standard masses)
  • Metallic pointer and ivory scale (to check balance)
  • Plumb line and leveling screws (to ensure balance is level)
  • Arrestment knob (to lock the beam when not in use)
  • Glass box enclosure (to protect from air currents)

Electronic Balance

Works on the principle of balancing forces. To use: turn on → set to zero (tare) → place object → read the digital display.

Did You Know?

The physical balance is kept inside a glass box to protect it from wind and dust. Even a tiny air current can push the pans and give a wrong reading.

2.6 Standard of Time

The SI unit of time is the second (s).

Before 1960, the second was defined based on the mean solar day. In 1967, it was redefined using an atomic clock.

Modern Definition of the Second

"One second is equal to 9,192,631,770 times the period of vibration of radiation from the cesium-133 atom."

Instruments for Measuring Time

Mechanical Stopwatch
Measures time up to 0.1 second precision (least count = 0.1 s). A knob winds the spring. First press = start, second press = stop, third press = reset. Used for timing pendulum swings and sports events.
Digital Stopwatch
Measures time up to 0.01 second precision (least count = 0.01 s). Has start/stop button and a reset button. Mobile phones have a built-in digital stopwatch.
Human Reaction Time
The delay between seeing an event and pressing start/stop. Human reaction time is 0.3 to 0.5 seconds — this introduces a random error in time measurements made by hand.
Light Gates
Electronic sensors that start and stop a timer automatically when a beam of light is broken by a moving object. Eliminates human reaction time error, giving much more accurate results.
Key Point — Reducing Time Measurement Error

To reduce reaction time error when timing repeated events (like a pendulum), record the time for 10 oscillations and divide by 10. This reduces the percentage error in the measurement significantly.
