Section A — Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

Q1. A student reads the vernier caliper shown in the figure. What is the correct reading?
a) 6.5 cm
b) 6.55 cm
c) 6.05 cm
d) 6.50 cm
Answer: (b) 6.55 cm
Main scale reading = 6.5 cm. The 5th vernier division coincides with a main scale line → vernier reading = 5 × 0.01 = 0.05 cm.
Total = 6.5 + 0.05 = 6.55 cm.
Q2. Ten steel balls each of mass 27 g are placed in a measuring cylinder. The water level rises from 20 cm³ to 50 cm³. What is the density of steel?
a) 3.0 g/cm³
b) 6.75 g/cm³
c) 9.0 g/cm³
d) 13.5 g/cm³
Answer: (c) 9.0 g/cm³
Total mass = 10 × 27 g = 270 g.
Volume of balls = 50 − 20 = 30 cm³.
ρ = m/V = 270/30 = 9.0 g/cm³.
Q3. An object of mass 100 g is placed in a measuring cylinder. The water level reads 40 cm³ (from the figure). What is the density of the object?
a) 0.4 g/cm³
b) 1.0 g/cm³
c) 4.0 g/cm³
d) 2.5 g/cm³
Answer: (d) 2.5 g/cm³
Volume = 40 cm³, mass = 100 g.
ρ = m/V = 100/40 = 2.5 g/cm³.
Q4. A student reads the micrometer screw gauge shown in the figure. What is the correct reading?
a) 6.13 mm
b) 6.63 mm
c) 6.53 mm
d) 7.13 mm
Answer: (b) 6.63 mm
Main scale (sleeve) reading = 6.5 mm (6 mm + 0.5 mm mark visible).
Thimble reading: 13th division aligns with datum line → 13 × 0.01 = 0.13 mm.
Total = 6.5 + 0.13 = 6.63 mm.
Q5. A field measures 4.5 m × 5.9 m. What is the area to the correct number of significant figures?
a) 26 cm²
b) 27 cm²
c) 26.5 cm²
d) 26.55 cm²
Answer: (b) 27 cm²
Area = 4.5 × 5.9 = 26.55 cm².
Both measurements have 2 significant figures, so the answer must also have 2 sig figs.
26.55 rounded to 2 sig figs = 27 cm².
Q6. The SI is an abbreviation for:
a) Standard International
b) International System of Units
c) Scientific Instruments
d) System International
Answer: (b) International System of Units
SI stands for Système International d'Unités (French), meaning the International System of Units — the modern form of the metric system adopted worldwide.
Q7. All accurately known digits plus the first doubtful digit in a measurement are called:
a) Derived figures
b) Significant figures
c) Base figures
d) Estimated figures
Answer: (b) Significant figures
Significant figures include all the digits that are reliably known plus the first doubtful digit (the estimated last digit in a measurement).
Q8. When the jaws of a vernier caliper are fully closed, the zero line of the vernier scale coincides exactly with the zero of the main scale. The zero error is:
a) Positive
b) Zero (no error)
c) Negative
d) Cannot be determined
Answer: (b) Zero (no error)
When both zero marks align exactly with the jaws fully closed, there is no zero error. The instrument reads correctly without any correction needed.
Q9. Zero error is a type of:
a) Systematic error
b) Random error
c) Human error
d) Gross error
Answer: (a) Systematic error
A systematic error is one that affects all readings in the same way — always too high or always too low. Zero error shifts every reading by the same fixed amount, so it is a systematic error. It can be corrected by subtracting the zero error from every reading.
Q10. Length, mass, electric current, and time are examples of:
a) Base quantities
b) Derived quantities
c) Vector quantities
d) Scalar quantities
Answer: (a) Base quantities
Length, mass, time, electric current, temperature, amount of substance, and luminous intensity are the seven fundamental (base) quantities. All other quantities are derived from these.

a) 230 cm = ? m
100 cm = 1 m, so divide by 100:
230 ÷ 100 = 2.3 m

b) 250 g = ? kg
1000 g = 1 kg, so divide by 1000:
250 ÷ 1000 = 0.25 kg

c) 0.5 s = ? ms

0.5 × 1000 = 500 ms

d) 0.8 m = ? mm
1 m = 1000 mm, so multiply by 1000:

Q4. A student needs to measure the width of an aluminium sheet. She has a metre rule, a vernier caliper, and a micrometer screw gauge available. (a) What is the approximate width she should measure? (b) Which instrument gives the most precise measurement and why?

(a) The width of an aluminium sheet is approximately 3.0 cm (a typical thin sheet). This is measurable with all three instruments.

(b) The vernier caliper gives the most precise measurement for this task.

  • Metre rule: least count = 1 mm — not precise enough for a thin sheet measurement.
  • Vernier caliper: least count = 0.1 mm — ideal for dimensions in the cm range with high precision.
  • Micrometer screw gauge: least count = 0.01 mm — extremely precise, but its range is only 0–25 mm, making it awkward for larger widths and designed for very small dimensions like wire diameter.

Therefore the vernier caliper is the best choice for measuring the width of an aluminium sheet — it provides high precision (0.1 mm) within a practical measurement range.

Q5. A student wants to measure the time period of a pendulum. (i) What is the best method to measure the time period accurately? (ii) How can the measurement be made more accurate using electronic equipment?

(i) Best method:

Option (b): Record the time for 10 complete oscillations and divide by 10.

This is better than timing a single oscillation because:

  • Human reaction time is 0.3–0.5 s. When measuring one oscillation (lasting about 1–2 s), the reaction time error is a large percentage of the total time.
  • By timing 10 oscillations, the total time is much longer (10–20 s), so the same reaction time error becomes a much smaller percentage.
  • Dividing by 10 at the end further reduces the error in each individual period.

(ii) Using light gates:

Light gates connected to a digital timer start and stop the timer automatically when the pendulum passes through the sensor beam. This eliminates human reaction time completely, giving a much more accurate measurement of the time period.

Q6. Express the following using SI prefixes.

a) 75,000 m = ? hm (hectometres)
1 hm = 10² m = 100 m
75,000 ÷ 100 = 750 hm

b) 2/1000 s = ? ms (milliseconds)
2/1000 s = 0.002 s
1 ms = 10⁻³ s = 0.001 s
0.002 ÷ 0.001 = 2 ms

c) 1/1,000,000 g = ? (using prefix)
1/1,000,000 g = 10⁻⁶ g
micro (μ) = 10⁻⁶
= 1 μg (1 microgram)

d) 1,000,000,000 m = ? (using prefix)
1,000,000,000 m = 10⁹ m
giga (G) = 10⁹
= 1 Gm (1 gigametre)

Q7. Write the following in scientific notation (standard form).

a) 0.53 Å (radius of hydrogen atom first orbit)
1 Å (Angstrom) = 10⁻¹⁰ m
So 0.53 Å = 0.53 × 10⁻¹⁰ m
Converting coefficient to standard form: 0.53 = 5.3 × 10⁻¹
= 5.3 × 10⁻¹ × 10⁻¹⁰ = 5.3 × 10⁻¹¹ m

b) 2,628,000,000,000 m
Move decimal 12 places to the left (the number has 13 digits, so exponent = 12):
= 2.628 × 10¹² m

c) 2.7 × 10⁻⁴ torr
This is already in correct scientific notation. The coefficient 2.7 satisfies 1 ≤ 2.7 < 10, and the base is 10 with exponent −4.
= 2.7 × 10⁻⁴ torr ✓

Q8. Calculate the volume of each shape using l = r = 2 m (length, radius, and height all equal to 2 m where applicable). Use π = 3.14.

a) Volume of a Sphere (r = 2 m)
V = (4/3) × π × r³
V = (4/3) × 3.14 × (2)³
V = 1.333 × 3.14 × 8
V = 1.333 × 25.12
V ≈ 33.5 m³

b) Volume of a Cube (l = 2 m)
V = l³ = (2)³
V = 8 m³

c) Volume of a Cylinder (r = 2 m, h = 2 m)
V = π × r² × h
V = 3.14 × (2)² × 2
V = 3.14 × 4 × 2
V = 25.1 m³

d) Volume of a Square Pyramid (base l = 2 m, h = 2 m)
V = (1/3) × base area × height
V = (1/3) × l² × h
V = (1/3) × (2)² × 2
V = (1/3) × 4 × 2
V ≈ 2.67 m³

Q9. A 1 kg block of wood is reshaped into (a) a sphere and (b) a cube using the dimensions from Q8. Find the density in each case. What do you notice?

(a) Density if shaped as a sphere (V = 33.5 m³)
ρ = m/V = 1 kg / 33.5 m³ ≈ 0.030 kg/m³

(b) Density if shaped as a cube (V = 8 m³)
ρ = m/V = 1 kg / 8 m³ = 0.125 kg/m³

Observation and Explanation:

The two calculated values are different because we used the same parameter value (2 m) for both shapes — but equal parameter values give very different volumes for different shapes. The same 1 kg of wood occupies very different mathematical volumes depending on the shape formula used with r = l = 2 m.

Important Physical Point

In reality, density does NOT change when you reshape a material. If you take a 1 kg block of wood and carve it into a sphere, the density stays exactly the same — because both the mass and the actual physical volume of the wood are unchanged. Density is an intrinsic (characteristic) property of the material, not its shape. The different results in this exercise arise only from applying the same r = 2 m to different volume formulae — not from a real change in density.

Q10. A measuring cylinder contains 500 cc of water. A stone of mass 20 g is placed inside. The water level rises to 800 cc. What can you conclude?
a) The mass of the stone equals the volume of water displaced
b) The difference between the two readings gives the volume of the stone
c) The stone's density equals 20 g/cc
d) The water level rises by the mass of the stone

Answer: (b) The difference between the two readings gives the volume of the stone.

Volume of stone = V₂ − V₁ = 800 − 500 = 300 cc = 300 cm³

We can now calculate density:
ρ = m/V = 20 g / 300 cm³ ≈ 0.067 g/cm³

Note: This density is less than water (1 g/cm³), meaning the stone would actually float — the question uses idealized numbers for practice. In a real experiment, a stone that floats would not stay submerged, and the displacement method would not give the full volume.

Q11. State the number of significant figures in each of the following:

a) 980
The trailing zero has no decimal point shown, so it is NOT significant.
Significant digits: 9 and 8
Answer: 2 significant figures

b) 91.60
The trailing zero after the decimal point IS significant (Rule 3 — explicit decimal present).
Significant digits: 9, 1, 6, 0
Answer: 4 significant figures

c) 10010.100
All digits are significant here:
— The two zeros between 1-0-0-1-0 are trapped zeros (significant by Rule 4).
— The decimal point is explicit, so trailing zeros (.100) are significant (Rule 3).
Significant digits: 1, 0, 0, 1, 0, 1, 0, 0
Answer: 8 significant figures

d) 0.0086
The leading zeros before 8 are NOT significant (Rule 2).
Significant digits: 8 and 6
Answer: 2 significant figures
