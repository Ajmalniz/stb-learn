Measuring Instruments

The choice of instrument depends on the quantity being measured and the precision needed. Each instrument has a least count — the smallest reading it can make. A more precise instrument has a smaller least count.

3.1 Metre Rule

The metre rule is used to measure the length of objects. It is 1 m (100 cm) long. Each centimetre is divided into 10 millimetres.

Least Count
1 mm (0.1 cm) — the smallest division on the scale.
Material
Made of wood or steel.
How to Use
Align the zero end of the rule with one end of the object. Read where the other end of the object meets the scale. Keep eyes directly above the scale to avoid parallax error.

3.2 Vernier Caliper

The vernier caliper is a precision instrument that measures internal and external distances more accurately than a metre rule. It has both metric and imperial scales.

Main Parts

  • Main jaws (lower) — for measuring external diameter or width
  • Smaller jaws (upper) — for measuring internal diameter (e.g., inside a pipe)
  • Depth gauge — for measuring depth of a hole
  • Main scale — fixed, graduated in mm
  • Vernier scale — slides along to open/close the jaws, has 10 divisions
Least Count
0.1 mm (or 0.01 cm) — one-tenth of a millimetre.

How to Read a Vernier Caliper (4 Steps)

  1. Step 1: Place the object firmly between the jaws and close them.
  2. Step 2: Note the main scale reading — the last whole-mm mark on the main scale before the zero line of the vernier scale.
  3. Step 3: Find the vernier scale reading — count which vernier division line coincides exactly with a main scale line. Multiply that number by 0.1 mm.
  4. Step 4: Add both readings: Total = Main scale reading + Vernier reading.
Worked Example

Main scale reading = 2.8 mm
Vernier scale: 6th division coincides → 6 × 0.1 = 0.6 mm
Total = 2.8 + 0.6 = 3.4 mm

Zero Error of the Vernier Caliper

Before measuring, check for zero error — a fault where the jaws are fully closed but the scales do not read exactly zero.

Checking for Zero Error Observed Reading Corrected Reading
Two zero marks coincide exactly — No zero error 3.14 cm 3.14 cm
Vernier zero is slightly to the right of main scale zero — Positive zero error (+0.03 cm) 3.17 cm 3.17 − 0.03 = 3.14 cm
Vernier zero is slightly to the left of main scale zero — Negative zero error (−0.07 cm) 3.11 cm 3.11 − (−0.07) = 3.18 cm
Zero Error Rule

Corrected reading = Observed reading − Zero error
A positive error is subtracted; a negative error is subtracted (which means it gets added).

3.3 Micrometer Screw Gauge

The micrometer screw gauge (or simply "micrometer") is used to measure extremely small dimensions — smaller than what a vernier caliper can measure accurately.

It works on the principle of a screw: converting a small linear distance into a larger rotational movement that is easy to read.

Main Parts

Anvil
The fixed surface against which the object is placed.
Spindle
The moving surface that advances toward the anvil to grip the object.
Sleeve (Barrel)
The fixed outer tube. Bears the main scale graduated in 0.5 mm divisions.
Thimble
Rotates over the sleeve. Has 50 divisions around its circumference. One full turn advances the spindle 0.5 mm, so each division = 0.01 mm.
Ratchet
A friction mechanism at the end of the thimble that slips when the object is gripped with enough force, preventing over-tightening.
Frame (C-shaped)
The strong metal frame that holds all parts and maintains precision.
Least Count
0.01 mm — one-hundredth of a millimetre.

How to Read a Micrometer (4 Steps)

  1. Step 1: Turn the thimble until the anvil and spindle grip the object, then turn the ratchet until it clicks (ensures consistent gripping force).
  2. Step 2: Take the main scale (sleeve) reading — read the last mm or 0.5 mm mark visible at the edge of the thimble.
  3. Step 3: Take the thimble scale reading — read the thimble division that aligns with the datum line on the sleeve, and multiply by 0.01 mm.
  4. Step 4: Add both readings: Total = Sleeve reading + Thimble reading.
Worked Example

Sleeve reading = 4.5 mm
Thimble: 12th division aligns with datum line → 12 × 0.01 = 0.12 mm
Total = 4.5 + 0.12 = 4.62 mm

Zero Error of the Micrometer

Checking for Zero Error Observed Reading Corrected Reading
Zero mark of thimble coincides with datum line, main scale at zero — No zero error 2.0 + 0.25 = 2.25 mm 2.25 mm
Zero mark is above datum line when jaws closed — Positive zero error (+0.07 mm) 2.0 + 0.32 = 2.32 mm 2.32 − 0.07 = 2.25 mm
Zero mark is below (not visible above) datum line when jaws closed — Negative zero error (−0.02 mm) 2.0 + 0.23 = 2.23 mm 2.23 − (−0.02) = 2.25 mm

3.4 Measuring Cylinder

A measuring cylinder is a glass or plastic cylinder graduated (marked) in cm³ or ml. It is used to measure the volume of liquids and, through the displacement method, the volume of irregular solids.

Reading the Measuring Cylinder

  • Water curves up at the edges, forming a curved surface called a meniscus.
  • Always read from the bottom of the meniscus.
  • Keep your eye level with the liquid surface to avoid parallax error.

Volume of an Irregular Solid — Displacement Method

For objects with an irregular shape (like a stone or a metal bolt), you cannot use a formula. Instead, use the displacement method:

  1. Pour a known volume of water into the measuring cylinder. Record this as V₁.
  2. Carefully lower the solid object into the water using a thread (so there is no splash).
  3. Record the new water level as V₂.
  4. Volume of object = V₂ − V₁
Example

Water level before stone: V₁ = 50 ml
Water level after adding stone: V₂ = 73 ml
Volume of stone = 73 − 50 = 23 ml = 23 cm³

Archimedes Connection

The displacement method was famously discovered by the ancient Greek scientist Archimedes, who used it to check whether a crown was made of pure gold. He noticed the water level rise when he stepped into his bath — and ran through the streets shouting "Eureka!" (meaning "I found it!").

Important Note

The object must be fully submerged for the displacement method to work correctly. The object must also not absorb water and must not float.
