Density, Volume & Significant Figures

Density explains why some objects float and others sink. Significant figures tell us how precisely a measurement was made. Both are essential tools in scientific measurement.

5.1 States of Matter and Volume

Matter exists in three common states, each with different volume behaviour:

Solid
Has a fixed shape and fixed size (volume). Particles are tightly packed and cannot move past each other.
Liquid
Has no fixed shape — takes the shape of its container — but has a fixed volume. Nearly incompressible. Particles are close but can slide past each other.
Gas
Has neither fixed shape nor fixed volume. Expands to fill any container. Particles move freely and are far apart.
Common Misconception

People say "iron is heavier than wood" — but this is not always true! A large piece of wood can be heavier than a small piece of iron. The correct statement is: "Iron is denser than wood." Density compares equal volumes, not equal masses.

5.2 Measuring Volume

Volume of a Liquid

Use a measuring cylinder. Read at the bottom of the meniscus with eyes level with the liquid surface.

Volume of a Regular Solid

Use the appropriate geometric formula:

Rectangular Block
V = length × width × height
Cylinder
V = π × r² × h (r = radius, h = height)
Sphere
V = (4/3) × π × r³ (r = radius)
Pyramid
V = (1/3) × base area × height

Volume of an Irregular Solid (Displacement Method)

Lower the object into a partially filled measuring cylinder:

  1. Record initial water level: V₁
  2. Submerge the solid object completely
  3. Record new water level: V₂
  4. Vobject = V₂ − V₁
Example

V₁ = 50 ml, V₂ = 73 ml
Volume of object = 73 − 50 = 23 ml = 23 cm³

5.3 Density

Definition

"The density of a substance is defined as mass of substance (m) per unit volume (V)."

Density uses the symbol ρ (Greek letter rho). The formula triangle:

Density formula
ρ = m / V
Rearranged for mass
m = ρ × V
Rearranged for volume
V = m / ρ

SI unit of density: kg/m³ (also written kg m⁻³)

Density is also commonly expressed in g/cm³. For example, density of aluminium = 2.70 g/cm³ = 2700 kg/m³.

Density is a characteristic property of a pure substance — it does not depend on how much of the substance you have. A small piece of iron and a large piece of iron have the same density.

5.4 Relative Density (Specific Gravity)

Definition

Relative density = density of substance / density of water

  • Relative density has no unit (it is dimensionless — the units cancel).
  • Its numerical value is the same as the density expressed in g/cm³.
  • The reference is the density of water at 4°C = 1000 kg/m³ = 1 g/cm³.
  • If relative density > 1, the substance sinks in water. If < 1, it floats.
Real-Life Example — Dead Sea

The Dead Sea in the Middle East is so salty that its water has a density much higher than ordinary water. Because the water's density exceeds that of the human body, people float effortlessly — it is almost impossible to sink!

5.5 Worked Example — Density Calculation

Problem

Find the mass of a solid iron ball of radius r = 18 cm if the density of iron is ρ = 7.8 g/cm³.

Step 1: Write the known quantities

  • ρ = 7.8 g/cm³ = 7800 kg/m³
  • r = 18 cm = 0.18 m

Step 2: Find the volume of the sphere

V = (4/3) × π × r³
V = (4/3) × 3.14 × (0.18)³
V = 1.333 × 3.14 × 0.005832
V = 0.024 m³

Step 3: Calculate the mass

m = ρ × V
m = 7800 kg/m³ × 0.024 m³
m = 187.2 kg

5.6 Significant Figures

Definition

"The numbers of reliably known digits in a value are known as significant figures."

In physics, we do not just need numbers — we need accurate numbers. The speed of light, the mass of an electron, the charge on an electron — each is known to a specific number of reliable digits. Significant figures communicate how precisely a value was measured.

The 4 Rules for Counting Significant Figures

Rule 1 — Non-zero digits
All non-zero digits are significant.
Example: 225 has 3 significant figures (2, 2, and 5).
Rule 2 — Leading zeros
Leading zeros (zeros before the first non-zero digit) are NOT significant.
Example: 0.0000034 has only 2 significant figures (3 and 4).
Example: 200 has only 1 significant figure (the 2).
Rule 3 — Trailing zeros
Trailing zeros (zeros after the last non-zero digit) are significant only if an explicit decimal point is present.
Example: 200 = 1 sig fig; 200. = 3 sig figs; 2.00 = 3 sig figs.
Rule 4 — Trapped (sandwiched) zeros
Zeros between non-zero digits are always significant.
Example: 0.00509 has 3 significant figures (5, 0, 9).
Example: 2045 has 4 significant figures (2, 0, 4, 5).

5.7 Worked Example — Significant Figures

Problem

A cylinder has a diameter of d = 5 cm. How many significant figures are in the area of its circular cross-section?

Step 1: Convert and find radius

d = 5 cm = 0.05 m
r = d/2 = 0.025 m

Step 2: Calculate area

A = π × r²
A = 3.14 × (0.025)²
A = 3.14 × 0.000625
A = 0.0019625 m²

Step 3: Round to correct significant figures

A ≈ 0.0019 m²

In 0.0019: the leading zeros are NOT significant (Rule 2). Only 1 and 9 are significant.

Answer: 2 significant figures

Expressed in mm²: 0.0019 m² = 1.9 mm² (clearly showing 2 sig figs)

Key Takeaway

In 0.0019, all the zeros before the "1" are leading zeros and are not significant. The significant digits are only 1 and 9. Therefore the answer has 2 significant figures, matching the precision of the original measurement (d = 5 cm has 1 sig fig, but our calculation yields 2 sig figs in the result).
