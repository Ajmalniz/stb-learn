Bohr's Theory & Modern Atomic Models

Rutherford's model left a critical question unanswered: why don't electrons crash into the nucleus? In 1913, Niels Bohr solved this problem with a revolutionary new idea — electrons can only exist in specific allowed orbits. Later, De Broglie and Schrödinger pushed our understanding even further.

3.1 Bohr's Atomic Theory (1913)

Niels Bohr proposed four key postulates to fix the problems with Rutherford's model:

Postulate 1 — Fixed Orbits (Shells)
Electrons revolve around the nucleus only in certain fixed circular orbits called shells or energy levels, numbered n = 1, 2, 3, 4... (also named K, L, M, N). Each shell is at a specific distance from the nucleus.
Postulate 2 — No Energy Loss in Orbit
An electron in a permitted orbit does NOT radiate energy. These orbits are called stationary states. This directly fixes Rutherford's stability problem.
Postulate 3 — Energy Jumps
An electron can jump from one orbit to another:
  • Absorbs energy (photon) → jumps to a higher energy level (farther from nucleus)
  • Emits energy (photon) → falls to a lower energy level (closer to nucleus)
This explains why atoms emit specific colours of light (discrete spectral lines).
Postulate 4 — Quantized Energy
The energy of each orbit is fixed (quantized). The lowest energy state is orbit n = 1 (K shell, closest to nucleus). Energy increases with n. The formula: E ∝ 1/n².

How Bohr fixed Rutherford's problems

1. Electrons in fixed orbits don't radiate energy → atoms are stable ✓
2. Electrons emit light only when jumping between levels → explains discrete spectral lines ✓

Limitations of Bohr's Model:

  • Works well for hydrogen but fails for multi-electron atoms.
  • Cannot explain the fine structure of spectral lines (closely spaced lines within a series).
  • Treats the electron purely as a particle — ignores its wave nature.

3.2 De Broglie Hypothesis (1924)

French physicist Louis de Broglie asked a bold question: if light (a wave) can behave like a particle (photon), can a particle like an electron behave like a wave?

De Broglie's Hypothesis
All moving particles have an associated wavelength. For an electron:
λ = h / mv
where λ = wavelength, h = Planck's constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s), m = mass, v = velocity.

This was confirmed experimentally — electrons produce diffraction patterns (a wave behaviour) when passing through a crystal.

Wave-particle duality

This idea — that matter has both particle and wave properties — is called wave-particle duality. It applies to all matter, but the wave nature is only noticeable for very tiny particles like electrons. A cricket ball also has a wavelength, but it's so unimaginably small it has no practical effect!

3.3 Schrödinger's Atomic Model (Quantum Mechanical Model)

In 1926, Erwin Schrödinger used De Broglie's idea to develop the wave mechanical model of the atom. He wrote a mathematical equation (the Schrödinger wave equation, Ψ) to describe the electron as a wave.

Key ideas:

  • Electrons do not travel in fixed circular paths (Bohr was wrong about the exact path).
  • Instead, we can only talk about the probability of finding an electron at any point in space.
  • The region of space where there is a high probability of finding an electron is called an orbital.
  • Orbitals are 3D probability clouds, not flat circles.
