Electronic Configuration

Electronic configuration tells us how electrons are arranged in an atom — which shells and sub-shells they occupy. This arrangement determines almost everything about how an element behaves chemically.

4.1 Shells (Energy Levels)

Electrons are arranged in shells (also called energy levels) at increasing distances from the nucleus.

4.2 Sub-shells (s and p)

Each shell is divided into sub-shells. Class 9 focuses on the s and p sub-shells.

s Sub-shell
Contains 1 orbital → maximum 2 electrons.
Shape: spherical.
Notation: 1s (in shell 1), 2s (in shell 2), 3s (in shell 3)...
p Sub-shell
Contains 3 orbitals (px, py, pz) → maximum 6 electrons.
Shape: dumbbell (figure-8) — each orbital points in a different direction (x, y, z axes).
Notation: 2p (in shell 2), 3p (in shell 3)...
Note: There is no 1p sub-shell.
Reading the Notation

3p⁴ means: shell 3, p sub-shell, 4 electrons in it.
The number before the letter = shell number.
The letter = sub-shell type (s, p, d, f).
The superscript = number of electrons in that sub-shell.

4.3 Rules for Filling Electrons

Aufbau Principle
Electrons fill the lowest available energy sub-shell first. Order: 1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d...
Pauli Exclusion Principle
Each orbital can hold a maximum of 2 electrons, and they must have opposite spins (↑↓).
Hund's Rule
When filling orbitals of equal energy (e.g., the three p orbitals), electrons go into each orbital singly first (one per orbital) before any orbital gets a second electron. All singly-filled orbitals have the same spin direction.

Valence Electrons

The electrons in the outermost shell are called valence electrons. They determine an element's chemical properties. For example, Na has 1 valence electron (3s¹) and Cl has 7 valence electrons (3s² 3p⁵). That's why Na readily loses 1 electron and Cl readily gains 1 electron to form NaCl (table salt).
