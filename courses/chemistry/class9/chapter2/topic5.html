Chapter 2 · Topic 5

Isotopes

Not all atoms of the same element are identical. Some have more neutrons than others. These variations of the same element are called isotopes — and they have some remarkably useful applications, from dating ancient fossils to treating cancer.

5.1 Definition

Isotopes
Atoms of the same element that have the same atomic number (same number of protons) but different mass numbers (different number of neutrons).
  • Same element: same number of protons → same atomic number → same position in periodic table
  • Same chemical properties: same number of valence electrons → react the same way chemically
  • Different physical properties: different masses → different density, melting point, boiling point
  • Different neutrons: some isotopes are radioactive (unstable nucleus); others are stable

Carbon (Z = 6):

  • ¹²C — 6 protons, 6 neutrons. Most abundant (98.9%). Stable.
  • ¹⁴C — 6 protons, 8 neutrons. Radioactive. Used in carbon dating.

Chlorine (Z = 17):

  • ³⁵Cl — 75% abundance
  • ³⁷Cl — 25% abundance
  • Average atomic mass = (35 × 0.75) + (37 × 0.25) = 35.5

Uranium (Z = 92):

  • ²³⁵U — fissile (can undergo nuclear fission), used in nuclear reactors and atomic bombs. Rare (~0.7%).
  • ²³⁸U — most abundant (99.3%), not directly fissile.

5.3 Uses of Isotopes

¹⁴C — Carbon Dating (Archaeology)
Half-life = 5,730 years. Living organisms constantly absorb ¹⁴C from the atmosphere. After death, ¹⁴C decays at a known rate. By measuring how much ¹⁴C remains, scientists can calculate how long ago the organism died. Used to date ancient wood, bone, and cloth — up to ~50,000 years old.
²³⁵U — Nuclear Energy
Used as fuel in nuclear reactors to generate electricity (nuclear fission). Also used in atomic bombs. Uranium must be "enriched" to increase the proportion of ²³⁵U.
²H (Deuterium) — Nuclear Reactors
Used in the form of heavy water (D₂O) as a moderator in nuclear reactors — it slows down fast neutrons to the right speed for sustaining the chain reaction.
¹³¹I (Iodine-131) — Medicine
Used for diagnosis and treatment of thyroid cancer. The thyroid gland absorbs iodine naturally — radioactive iodine concentrates in the thyroid and destroys cancer cells.
⁹⁹ᵐTc (Technetium-99m) — Medical Imaging
The most widely used radioisotope in medicine. Used in diagnostic scans (SPECT imaging) to detect tumours, heart disease, bone disorders.
⁶⁰Co (Cobalt-60) — Cancer Treatment
Emits gamma rays used in radiation therapy to kill cancer cells. Also used for food irradiation — gamma rays kill bacteria and pathogens in food, extending shelf life without making the food radioactive.
Why do isotopes have the same chemical properties?

Chemical reactions involve electrons — specifically valence electrons. Since isotopes of the same element have the same number of protons and electrons, their electronic configurations are identical. The extra neutrons sit quietly in the nucleus and play no role in chemical bonding.
