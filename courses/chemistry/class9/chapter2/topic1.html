Discovery of Sub-atomic Particles

For centuries, scientists thought atoms were the smallest particles — indivisible and solid. Then came a series of brilliant experiments that revealed atoms are themselves made of even smaller particles: electrons, protons, and neutrons. This topic covers how each was discovered.

1.1 The Electron — J.J. Thomson (1897)

The electron was the first sub-atomic particle to be discovered. British physicist J.J. Thomson found it while experimenting with cathode ray tubes.

The Cathode Ray Tube Experiment

A glass tube was sealed, air pumped out, and high voltage applied between two metal plates (cathode = negative, anode = positive). A glowing beam called cathode rays was produced, traveling from the cathode to the anode.

Key observations:

  • The beam traveled in a straight line from cathode (−) to anode (+).
  • When an electric field was applied, the beam bent toward the positive plate → the particles carry negative charge.
  • When a magnetic field was applied, the beam also deflected.
  • The beam was the same regardless of which metal was used for the cathode → electrons are present in ALL atoms.
  • The beam caused a ZnS (zinc sulphide) screen to fluoresce (glow).

Properties of cathode rays (electrons):

  • Travel in straight lines
  • Carry negative charge
  • Deflect in both electric and magnetic fields
  • Have mass (but very small)
  • Same from all cathode materials
  • Cause fluorescence in ZnS
Electron Properties
Charge = −1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C (relative charge = −1)
Mass = 9.1 × 10⁻³¹ kg (relative mass = 1/1836 ≈ negligible)
Charge/mass ratio = 1.76 × 10⁸ C/g
Did you know?

The relative mass of an electron is so tiny (1/1836 of a proton) that it contributes almost nothing to the total mass of an atom. All the mass is in the nucleus!

1.2 The Proton — Goldstein (1886)

Eugen Goldstein discovered positive rays (also called canal rays) in 1886 — before Thomson's electron discovery, though the proton was fully characterized later.

When a perforated cathode was used in a discharge tube, rays passed backward through the holes in the cathode. These were deflected toward the negative plate → they are positively charged.

The lightest positive ray came from hydrogen → this particle is the proton.

Proton Properties
Charge = +1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C (relative charge = +1)
Mass = 1.673 × 10⁻²⁷ kg (relative mass = 1)
Mass of proton ≈ 1836 × mass of electron
Located in the nucleus

1.3 The Neutron — Chadwick (1932)

James Chadwick discovered the neutron in 1932. He bombarded beryllium with alpha particles (helium nuclei) and detected a new type of radiation that:

  • Had approximately the same mass as a proton
  • Carried no electric charge (neutral)
  • Was not deflected by electric or magnetic fields
Neutron Properties
Charge = 0 (neutral)
Mass ≈ proton mass (1.675 × 10⁻²⁷ kg, relative mass = 1)
Located in the nucleus along with protons

1.5 Atomic Number and Mass Number

Atomic Number (Z)
The number of protons in the nucleus. In a neutral atom, it also equals the number of electrons. Each element has a unique atomic number. Example: Carbon always has Z = 6.
Mass Number (A)
The total number of protons + neutrons in the nucleus. Example: Carbon-12 has 6 protons + 6 neutrons = A = 12.
Number of Neutrons = Mass Number (A) − Atomic Number (Z)

Example: Sodium (Na) — Atomic number = 11, Mass number = 23
→ Protons = 11, Electrons = 11, Neutrons = 23 − 11 = 12
