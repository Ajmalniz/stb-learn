Rutherford's Atomic Model

Before Rutherford, scientists thought the atom was a solid ball or a "plum pudding" (Thomson's model — electrons embedded in a positive sphere). In 1911, Ernest Rutherford performed one of the most famous experiments in science and completely changed our understanding of the atom.

2.1 The Gold Foil Experiment (1911)

Experimental Setup

Source: A radioactive source emitting alpha particles (helium nuclei, charge 2+, mass 4 u).
Target: An extremely thin sheet of gold foil (only a few atoms thick).
Detector: A circular screen coated with ZnS (zinc sulphide) that glows when hit by alpha particles.

What they observed:

Observation Conclusion
Most alpha particles passed straight throughAtom is mostly empty space
A small fraction deflected at small anglesPositive charge is somewhat spread near the centre
Very few (1 in ~20,000) bounced straight backA tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus exists at the centre
Rutherford's own words

"It was as if you fired artillery shells at tissue paper and they came back and hit you. It was the most incredible thing that ever happened to me in my life."

2.2 Rutherford's Atomic Model — Postulates

  1. The atom consists of a tiny, dense nucleus at the centre containing all positive charge and almost all the mass.
  2. The atom is mostly empty space — the nucleus is extremely small compared to the atom.
  3. Electrons revolve around the nucleus in circular orbits, like planets around the Sun.
  4. Electrons are held in orbit by the electrostatic attraction between negative electrons and the positive nucleus.
  5. The number of electrons equals the number of protons → the atom is electrically neutral.
Nucleus (tiny, dense, +ve) ← surrounded by → Electrons orbiting in empty space

2.3 Defects of Rutherford's Model

Rutherford's model was a massive breakthrough, but it had two serious problems that classical physics could not solve:

Problem 1 — Unstable Atoms
An electron moving in a circular orbit is constantly accelerating (changing direction). According to classical physics, an accelerating charged particle must continuously emit electromagnetic radiation and lose energy. This means the electron should spiral inward and crash into the nucleus in about 10⁻⁸ seconds. But atoms are stable — this never happens!
Problem 2 — Cannot Explain Atomic Spectra
When elements are heated, they emit light of specific colours (discrete lines), not a continuous spectrum. Rutherford's model predicted electrons should emit all frequencies as they spiral in — a continuous spectrum. It could not explain why atoms only emit specific wavelengths.
Key Takeaway

Rutherford proved the nuclear atom — the idea of a tiny dense nucleus surrounded by empty space. This was revolutionary. But it couldn't explain WHY electrons don't fall into the nucleus. That question was answered by Niels Bohr.
