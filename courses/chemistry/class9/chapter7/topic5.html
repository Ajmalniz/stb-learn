Chapter 7 · Topic 5

Corrosion, Electroplating & Alloy Formation

Corrosion costs the world economy trillions of dollars every year. Understanding the electrochemistry behind rusting allows us to prevent it through smart techniques including electroplating. Alloys take this further — by mixing metals, we create materials with superior properties for construction, industry, and technology.

5.1 Corrosion and Rusting of Iron

Corrosion
The gradual destruction of a metal by chemical reaction with its environment, especially oxygen and moisture. It is essentially an electrochemical oxidation process.
Rusting
The specific corrosion of iron (Fe) to form iron(III) oxide hydrate (Fe₂O₃·xH₂O) — the reddish-brown substance known as rust.

Conditions required for rusting:

  • Presence of water (moisture)
  • Presence of oxygen
  • Both are required — iron does not rust in dry air or in deoxygenated water alone
  • Rusting is accelerated by salt (seawater), acid rain, and electrical contact with less active metals

Electrochemical mechanism of rusting:

  • Anode (iron surface): Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻ (oxidation)
  • Cathode (nearby region with O₂): O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ → 4OH⁻ (reduction)
  • Fe²⁺ + 2OH⁻ → Fe(OH)₂ → further oxidized to Fe₂O₃·xH₂O (rust)

Prevention of rusting:

Method How it works Examples
PaintingBarrier — keeps out O₂ and H₂OBridges, cars, buildings
GalvanisingCoating with zinc — Zn is a sacrificial anode (corrodes first, protecting Fe)Corrugated iron roofs, pipes
ElectroplatingCoating with corrosion-resistant metal (Cr, Ni, Sn)Taps, car parts, cans
AlloyingMixing metals to increase corrosion resistanceStainless steel (Fe + Cr + Ni)
Cathodic protectionMake the iron the cathode using a sacrificial Mg/Zn anode or external currentUnderground pipes, ship hulls

5.2 Electroplating

Electroplating
The process of depositing a thin layer of one metal onto another using electrolysis. The object to be plated is made the cathode; the plating metal is the anode; the electrolyte contains ions of the plating metal.

General setup for electroplating with metal X:

  • Cathode: object to be plated
  • Anode: pure metal X
  • Electrolyte: solution of X salt (e.g., CuSO₄ for copper plating)
  • At cathode: X²⁺ + 2e⁻ → X (metal deposits)
  • At anode: X → X²⁺ + 2e⁻ (metal dissolves to replenish solution)
Tin Plating (Tinplate)
Steel coated with tin. Used for food cans — tin resists corrosion and is non-toxic. Electrolyte: SnSO₄ solution. However, if the tin layer is scratched, the iron underneath rusts faster (tin is cathodic to iron).
Zinc Plating (Galvanising)
Steel coated with zinc. Zinc is anodic to iron, so even if scratched, the zinc corrodes sacrificially, protecting the iron beneath. Used for pipes, roofing.
Silver Plating
Used in jewellery, cutlery, electrical contacts. Electrolyte: silver nitrate (AgNO₃) solution. Produces a shiny, corrosion-resistant layer.
Chromium Plating
Used on car parts, taps, and tools. Chromium is very hard and corrosion-resistant. Electrolyte: chromic acid (H₂CrO₄) solution. Gives a shiny mirror-like finish.

5.3 Alloy Formation

Alloy
A mixture of two or more metals (or a metal and a non-metal) that has properties superior to its individual components. Alloys are not pure compounds — components do not combine chemically, they mix at the atomic level.

Why alloys? Pure metals are often too soft, too corrosive, or too heavy for practical use. Alloying improves hardness, strength, corrosion resistance, appearance, or electrical properties.

Alloy Components Properties & Uses
BrassCu + Zn (70:30)Hard, corrosion-resistant, musical instruments, plumbing fittings
BronzeCu + Sn (90:10)Hard, strong, statues, medals, bearings, bells
Stainless SteelFe + Cr (18%) + Ni (8%)Corrosion-resistant, strong, cutlery, surgical tools, kitchen equipment
German SilverCu + Zn + NiSilver-coloured, used in jewellery, coins, utensils (no actual silver)
DuraluminAl + Cu + Mg + MnLight and strong, aircraft frames, spacecraft
SolderPb + Sn (37:63)Low melting point (~183°C), used to join electrical components
AmalgamHg + Ag + Sn + CuDental fillings (being replaced by composites)
Did you know?

The Statue of Liberty is made of copper but appears green — because over decades, the copper reacted with oxygen, water, and CO₂ to form a green layer of copper carbonate (Cu₂(CO₃)(OH)₂), called patina. This layer actually protects the copper underneath!
