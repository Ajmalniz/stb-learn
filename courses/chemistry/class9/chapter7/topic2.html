Electrolytes & Electrolysis

Some substances conduct electricity when dissolved in water or melted — these are called electrolytes. When electricity is passed through an electrolyte, it drives a chemical reaction — this is called electrolysis. It is used to extract metals, purify copper, make chlorine, and plate metals with thin coatings.

2.1 Electrolytes

Electrolyte
A substance that conducts electricity when dissolved in water (aqueous solution) or in the molten state. Conduction happens because the electrolyte breaks into ions (charged particles) that can move.
Non-electrolyte
A substance that does not conduct electricity in solution because it does not form ions. Examples: sugar (glucose), ethanol, urea.

Electrolytes are divided into three types based on how completely they dissociate (break into ions):

Type Dissociation Conductivity Examples
Strong electrolyteComplete (100%)Very goodHCl, H₂SO₄, NaOH, NaCl, KNO₃
Weak electrolytePartialPoorCH₃COOH (acetic acid), NH₃, HF, H₂CO₃
Non-electrolyteNoneNoneSugar (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁), ethanol, urea
Why do ionic compounds conduct electricity?

In the solid state, ions are fixed in a lattice and cannot move — so solid NaCl does NOT conduct. When dissolved or melted, the ions become free to move toward the electrodes and carry charge — so molten or dissolved NaCl DOES conduct.

2.2 Electrolysis — The Basic Setup

Electrolysis
The decomposition of a substance by passing an electric current through it in the molten or dissolved state. Electrical energy is converted into chemical energy.
Electrodes
The conductors dipped into the electrolyte to pass current through it. Usually made of platinum or graphite (carbon) — inert materials that don't react with the electrolyte.
Cathode
The negative electrode. Cations (positive ions) migrate toward it. Reduction occurs at the cathode. Memory: C–C–C (Cathode, Cations, reduction = Gain)
Anode
The positive electrode. Anions (negative ions) migrate toward it. Oxidation occurs at the anode. Memory: A–A–A (Anode, Anions, Oxidation)
Trick to remember cathode vs anode

At the cathode there is reduction — both words start with a consonant. At the anode there is oxidation — both words start with a vowel. Or use: RED CAT (REDuction at CAThode) and AN OX (ANode OXidation).

2.3 Electrolysis of Water

Pure water is a very poor conductor. A small amount of dilute H₂SO₄ (or NaOH) is added to make it conduct without adding ions that would themselves be discharged.

At the cathode (−):

2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂↑   (reduction)

At the anode (+):

2H₂O → O₂↑ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻   (oxidation)
Volume ratio of gases

Hydrogen gas is produced at twice the volume of oxygen gas. The ratio H₂ : O₂ = 2 : 1 by volume. This matches the formula of water, H₂O.

2.4 Electrolysis of Brine (NaCl Solution)

Brine is a concentrated solution of sodium chloride (NaCl) in water. Electrolysis of brine is one of the most important industrial processes — the chlor-alkali process.

At the cathode (−):

2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂↑   (hydrogen gas)

At the anode (+):

2Cl⁻ → Cl₂↑ + 2e⁻   (chlorine gas)

Left in solution: Na⁺ and OH⁻ → NaOH (sodium hydroxide)

Product Where produced Industrial use
Hydrogen (H₂)CathodeFuel, margarine production, ammonia synthesis
Chlorine (Cl₂)AnodeWater purification, PVC, bleach, solvents
Sodium hydroxide (NaOH)Remains in solutionSoap, paper, textiles, drain cleaners

2.5 Electrolysis of Copper Sulphate Solution (CuSO₄)

This is an important example because the result depends on the electrode material used.

With inert (platinum or graphite) electrodes:

  • Cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu(s) — copper metal is deposited
  • Anode: 2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ — oxygen gas is released
  • Solution becomes more acidic as Cu²⁺ ions are removed

With copper electrodes (used in copper refining):

  • Cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu(s) — pure copper deposits on cathode
  • Anode: Cu(s) → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ — copper anode dissolves
  • The concentration of CuSO₄ stays constant — anode dissolves as fast as cathode deposits
  • Impurities from impure copper anode fall as anode sludge (may contain gold, silver)
Did you know?

The copper refinery at a large plant uses enormous electrolytic cells with impure copper anodes and pure copper cathodes. The precious metals (gold, silver) that fall from the impure anode as sludge are recovered and can offset part of the cost of the refining process!
