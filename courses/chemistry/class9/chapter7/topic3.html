Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

Michael Faraday's two laws of electrolysis are the quantitative foundation of electrochemistry. They tell us exactly how much substance is deposited or released during electrolysis — and allow precise calculations for industrial electroplating, metal refining, and electrochemical production.

3.1 Faraday's First Law of Electrolysis

Faraday's First Law
The mass of a substance deposited or liberated at an electrode during electrolysis is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity (charge) passed through the electrolyte.

m ∝ Q  →  m = ZQ

where m = mass (grams), Q = charge (coulombs), Z = electrochemical equivalent
m = ZQ     where Q = I × t    (I = current in amperes, t = time in seconds)
Electrochemical Equivalent (Z)
The mass of substance deposited by 1 coulomb of charge.
Z = M / (n × F)
where M = molar mass (g/mol), n = number of electrons transferred, F = Faraday constant (96,500 C/mol)
Faraday Constant (F)
The charge carried by one mole of electrons.
F = 96,500 C/mol ≈ 96,500 coulombs per mole of electrons

3.2 Faraday's Second Law of Electrolysis

Faraday's Second Law
When the same quantity of electricity is passed through different electrolytes connected in series, the masses of substances deposited are proportional to their equivalent weights (molar mass ÷ valency).

m₁/m₂ = E₁/E₂    where E = equivalent weight = M/n

In other words: one mole of electrons (1 Faraday = 96,500 C) always deposits one equivalent weight of any substance — whether it's silver, copper, or hydrogen.

Substance Ion Electrons (n) Molar Mass (M) Equiv. Weight (M/n)
SilverAg⁺1108 g/mol108 g
CopperCu²⁺263.5 g/mol31.75 g
AluminiumAl³⁺327 g/mol9 g
HydrogenH⁺11 g/mol1 g

3.3 Worked Examples

Example 1 — Using m = ZQ

How much copper is deposited when a current of 2 A flows for 30 minutes through CuSO₄ solution? (M of Cu = 63.5 g/mol, n = 2)

Step 1: Q = I × t = 2 A × (30 × 60 s) = 2 × 1800 = 3600 C

Step 2: Z = M/(n × F) = 63.5 / (2 × 96500) = 63.5 / 193000 = 3.29 × 10⁻⁴ g/C

Step 3: m = Z × Q = 3.29 × 10⁻⁴ × 3600 = 1.18 g

So 1.18 g of copper is deposited. ✓

Example 2 — Finding time

How long must a current of 3 A flow to deposit 5.4 g of silver from AgNO₃? (M of Ag = 108 g/mol, n = 1)

Step 1: Z = M/(n × F) = 108/(1 × 96500) = 1.119 × 10⁻³ g/C

Step 2: Q = m/Z = 5.4 / (1.119 × 10⁻³) = 4826 C

Step 3: t = Q/I = 4826/3 = 1609 s ≈ 26.8 min

Example 3 — Using the second law

The same current passes through AgNO₃ and CuSO₄ solutions in series. If 5.4 g of Ag is deposited, how much Cu is deposited?

Using: m₁/m₂ = E₁/E₂
E(Ag) = 108/1 = 108 · E(Cu) = 63.5/2 = 31.75
5.4/m(Cu) = 108/31.75
m(Cu) = 5.4 × 31.75/108 = 1.59 g

Did you know?

Michael Faraday discovered his laws in 1833 without any knowledge of electrons or atomic structure — these were discovered decades later. He deduced the laws purely from careful experimental measurements of mass and current!
