Galvanic Cells & Batteries

A galvanic cell (also called a voltaic cell) converts chemical energy into electrical energy through spontaneous redox reactions. Understanding galvanic cells explains how all batteries — from the AA battery in your remote to the lithium-ion pack in your phone — work.

4.1 The Daniel Cell

The Daniel cell is the classic example of a galvanic cell. It uses a zinc anode in ZnSO₄ solution and a copper cathode in CuSO₄ solution, connected by a salt bridge.

Anode (−)
The negative electrode in a galvanic cell. Oxidation occurs here. Zinc loses electrons:
Zn(s) → Zn²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻    (oxidation)
Cathode (+)
The positive electrode in a galvanic cell. Reduction occurs here. Copper ions gain electrons:
Cu²⁺(aq) + 2e⁻ → Cu(s)    (reduction)
Salt Bridge
A U-shaped tube filled with an electrolyte solution (e.g., KCl in agar gel). It completes the circuit by allowing ions to flow between the two half-cells, maintaining electrical neutrality — without it, the cell quickly stops working.

Overall Daniel cell reaction:

Zn(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Zn²⁺(aq) + Cu(s)     E°cell = +1.10 V

The zinc electrode gradually dissolves (oxidized); copper metal deposits on the copper electrode. Electrons flow through the external circuit from the anode (Zn) to the cathode (Cu).

Memory aid — Galvanic cells

AN OX — ANode is OXidation
RED CAT — REDuction at the CAThode
In galvanic cells: anode is negative (−), cathode is positive (+). (Opposite to electrolytic cells!)

4.2 Standard Electrode Potential

Standard Electrode Potential (E°)
The voltage of a half-cell measured against the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) at 25°C, 1 atm, and 1 mol/L concentration. A more positive E° means a stronger tendency to be reduced (accept electrons).
Half-reaction E° (V)
Li⁺ + e⁻ → Li−3.04
Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Zn−0.76
2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂0.00 (reference)
Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu+0.34
Ag⁺ + e⁻ → Ag+0.80
F₂ + 2e⁻ → 2F⁻+2.87

E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode    → Daniel cell: 0.34 − (−0.76) = +1.10 V

A positive E°cell means the reaction is spontaneous (galvanic cell can do work).

4.3 Types of Batteries

Dry Cell (Leclanché Cell)
The common household battery (AA, AAA). Anode: zinc can; Cathode: carbon rod surrounded by MnO₂ paste; Electrolyte: paste of NH₄Cl and ZnCl₂. Voltage: ~1.5 V. Non-rechargeable — used in remote controls, torches.
Lead-Acid Battery
The car battery. Anode: lead (Pb); Cathode: lead dioxide (PbO₂); Electrolyte: dilute H₂SO₄. Each cell: ~2 V; a 12V car battery has 6 cells. Rechargeable. Used in cars, motorcycles, UPS systems.
Lithium-Ion Battery
Used in smartphones, laptops, and electric vehicles. High energy density, lightweight, rechargeable. Lithium ions move between electrodes during charge/discharge. Voltage: ~3.6 V per cell.
Fuel Cell
Converts chemical energy of a fuel (H₂) directly to electricity. Hydrogen is supplied to the anode, oxygen to the cathode; they combine to form water. No recharging needed as long as fuel is supplied. Zero carbon emissions. Used in experimental cars and spacecraft.

Did you know?

Alessandro Volta invented the first battery — the "voltaic pile" — in 1800, using alternating discs of zinc and silver separated by salt-soaked cloth. This directly inspired Michael Faraday's later work on electrolysis.
