The Liquid State

Liquids are the intermediate state of matter — particles close together but not locked in place. This gives liquids their unique combination of properties: a definite volume but no fixed shape, the ability to flow, and fascinating phenomena like evaporation, vapour pressure, and surface tension.

3.1 General Properties of Liquids

  • Definite volume but no fixed shape — take the shape of their container
  • Particles are close together but can move past each other (short-range order)
  • Much less compressible than gases
  • Higher density than gases (but usually less than solids)
  • Can diffuse but much slower than gases
  • Have intermolecular forces (van der Waals, hydrogen bonds, dipole-dipole)

3.2 Evaporation

Evaporation
The escape of high-energy molecules from the surface of a liquid into the gas phase, at temperatures below the boiling point. It is a surface phenomenon and causes cooling of the remaining liquid.

Factors that increase the rate of evaporation:

  • Higher temperature — more molecules have enough energy to escape
  • Larger surface area — more molecules at the surface can escape
  • Lower humidity / air flow — removal of vapour above the liquid keeps concentration gradient high
  • Weaker intermolecular forces — easier for molecules to break free (e.g., petrol evaporates faster than water)
Did you know?

Sweating cools you down by evaporation. When high-energy water molecules evaporate from your skin, they carry energy away — lowering the temperature of the remaining liquid (your body).

3.3 Vapour Pressure

Vapour Pressure
The pressure exerted by the vapour of a liquid in equilibrium with the liquid at a given temperature. It measures the tendency of a liquid to evaporate.

Vapour pressure increases with temperature because more molecules have enough energy to escape into the vapour phase.

Liquids with weak intermolecular forces have high vapour pressure (volatile liquids, e.g., acetone, ether).

Liquids with strong intermolecular forces have low vapour pressure (e.g., water, glycerol).

3.4 Boiling Point and Freezing Point

Boiling Point
The temperature at which the vapour pressure of a liquid equals the external atmospheric pressure. At this point, bubbles form throughout the liquid (not just at the surface). Normal boiling point is defined at 1 atm.
Freezing Point
The temperature at which a liquid changes to a solid at a given pressure. For water, this is 0°C at 1 atm.

Factors affecting boiling point:

  • External pressure — at higher altitude (lower pressure), water boils below 100°C; in a pressure cooker (higher pressure), water boils above 100°C
  • Intermolecular forces — stronger forces → higher boiling point (water, 100°C, has hydrogen bonding; hexane, 69°C, only has van der Waals forces)
  • Dissolved solutes — dissolving a solute raises the boiling point (boiling point elevation)

Factors affecting freezing point:

  • Pressure — increased pressure slightly lowers the freezing point of water
  • Dissolved solutes — lowers the freezing point (freezing point depression — e.g., salt on icy roads)

3.5 Viscosity and Density

Viscosity
The resistance of a liquid to flow. A high-viscosity liquid (honey, glycerol) flows slowly; a low-viscosity liquid (water, acetone) flows easily. Viscosity decreases as temperature increases.
Density of Liquids
Liquids are much denser than gases but usually slightly less dense than their solid form. Water is a famous exception — ice is less dense than liquid water, so ice floats. The density of most liquids decreases with temperature.
