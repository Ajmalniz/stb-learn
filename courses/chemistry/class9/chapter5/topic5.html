Allotropy, Plasma & Bose-Einstein Condensate

Beyond the three familiar states of matter lie two exotic states — plasma and the Bose-Einstein condensate. We also explore allotropy: how the same element can exist in completely different structural forms with totally different properties.

5.1 Allotropy

Allotropy
The existence of an element in two or more different physical forms (allotropes) in the same physical state. The allotropes have the same chemical formula but different structures and therefore different physical properties.

Allotropes of Carbon

Diamond
Each carbon atom is bonded to 4 others in a giant 3D tetrahedral network. This makes diamond the hardest natural substance, an electrical insulator, and transparent. Used in cutting tools, drill bits, and jewellery.
Graphite
Carbon atoms are bonded in flat hexagonal layers. Each atom bonds to 3 others; the 4th electron is delocalised between layers. Layers are held by weak van der Waals forces and slide easily → lubricant, pencil "lead". Graphite conducts electricity (delocalised electrons).
Fullerene (C₆₀ — Buckminsterfullerene)
60 carbon atoms arranged in a hollow sphere shaped like a football (20 hexagons + 12 pentagons). Discovered in 1985. Used in drug delivery, nanotechnology, and superconductors.

Allotropes of Sulfur

Rhombic Sulfur (α-sulfur)
The stable form of sulfur below 96°C. Yellow crystals with S₈ ring molecules arranged in a rhombic (orthorhombic) crystal structure. Melting point: 113°C.
Monoclinic Sulfur (β-sulfur)
Stable between 96°C and 119°C. Pale yellow needle-like crystals. Also composed of S₈ rings but packed differently. Above 96°C, rhombic sulfur slowly converts to monoclinic sulfur.

5.2 Plasma — The Fourth State of Matter

Plasma
A state of matter consisting of ionized gas — a mixture of free electrons and positively charged ions. At extremely high temperatures, electrons are stripped from atoms, creating this electrically conductive "soup".

Properties of plasma:

  • Extremely high temperature (thousands to millions of degrees)
  • Electrically conductive (free electrons)
  • Responds strongly to electromagnetic fields
  • Does not have a fixed shape or volume

Where plasma is found:

  • The Sun and stars — the entire interior of the Sun is plasma
  • Lightning bolts — the channel of ionized air is plasma
  • Neon and fluorescent lights — plasma glows when electrons recombine with ions
  • Plasma TVs — tiny cells of xenon/neon plasma emit UV light
  • Fusion reactors (e.g., ITER) — plasma is heated to enable nuclear fusion
Plasma is the most common state of matter in the universe

About 99% of all visible matter in the universe is in the plasma state — stars, nebulae, and the solar wind. The three common states (solid, liquid, gas) are actually quite rare on a cosmic scale!

5.3 Bose-Einstein Condensate — The Fifth State of Matter

Bose-Einstein Condensate (BEC)
A state of matter formed when a group of atoms (bosons) is cooled to extremely low temperatures — near absolute zero (−273.15°C or 0 K). At this point, atoms lose their individual identity and behave as a single quantum entity.

Key features of BEC:

  • Formed at temperatures just billionths of a degree above absolute zero
  • All atoms occupy the same quantum state — they behave as one "super-atom"
  • Exhibits quantum effects on a macroscopic scale (superfluidity, superconductivity)
  • First created experimentally in 1995 by Eric Cornell and Carl Wieman (using rubidium atoms) — they won the Nobel Prize in 2001

BEC is purely a laboratory phenomenon on Earth — nothing in nature is cold enough for BEC to form naturally.

Did you know?

In a Bose-Einstein condensate, light can be slowed to a near-complete stop. In 1999, physicist Lene Hau slowed light from 300,000 km/s to just 17 m/s (about 61 km/h) using a BEC — slower than a car on a highway!
