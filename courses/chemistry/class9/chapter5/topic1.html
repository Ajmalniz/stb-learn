The Gaseous State

Gases are everywhere — the air we breathe, the fuel that burns, the steam from boiling water. The kinetic molecular theory explains all gas behavior from first principles. In this topic, we explore the key properties of gases and why they behave the way they do.

1.1 Kinetic Molecular Theory of Gases

The kinetic molecular theory (KMT) describes ideal gases. Its five main postulates are:

Postulate 1 — Tiny Particles
Gas molecules are very small compared to the distances between them. The volume of the molecules themselves is negligible compared to the total volume of the gas.
Postulate 2 — Random Motion
Gas molecules move in rapid, random, straight-line motion in all directions. They collide with each other and with the walls of their container.
Postulate 3 — Elastic Collisions
Collisions between gas molecules (and with container walls) are perfectly elastic — no kinetic energy is lost in collisions.
Postulate 4 — No Intermolecular Forces
Gas molecules have no attractive or repulsive forces between them (except during brief collisions). They are completely independent.
Postulate 5 — Temperature and Kinetic Energy
The average kinetic energy of gas molecules is directly proportional to the absolute temperature (Kelvin) of the gas. Higher temperature = faster molecules.

1.2 Properties of Gases

Diffusion
The spontaneous spreading of gas molecules from a region of higher concentration to lower concentration until evenly distributed. Example: the smell of perfume spreading across a room.
Effusion
The escape of gas molecules through a tiny hole into a vacuum. Lighter molecules effuse faster (Graham's Law: rate ∝ 1/√molar mass).
Pressure
Gas pressure is caused by molecules colliding with the walls of the container. Measured in atm, kPa, or mmHg. Standard atmospheric pressure = 1 atm = 101.3 kPa = 760 mmHg.
Compressibility
Gases are highly compressible because there is a lot of empty space between molecules. Squeezing the container reduces volume and increases pressure.
Mobility
Gas molecules move freely and rapidly in all directions. This high mobility explains both diffusion and the ability of gases to fill any container completely.
Density
Gases have very low densities (about 1000× less than liquids or solids) because the molecules are so far apart. Density increases with pressure and decreases with temperature.

1.3 Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP)

STP: Temperature = 0°C (273 K)  ·  Pressure = 1 atm (101.3 kPa)

At STP, 1 mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.4 litres (the molar volume). STP is used as a standard reference point for comparing gas properties.

Converting Temperature to Kelvin

Gas laws always use Kelvin (K), never Celsius.
K = °C + 273
Examples: 0°C = 273 K · 25°C = 298 K · 100°C = 373 K · −73°C = 200 K

1.4 Real vs Ideal Gases

An ideal gas perfectly obeys KMT — no intermolecular forces and negligible molecular volume. Real gases deviate from ideal behavior at:

  • High pressure — molecules are forced close together; their volume matters
  • Low temperature — molecules slow down enough for intermolecular attractions to matter

Under normal conditions (moderate pressure, room temperature), most gases behave nearly ideally.

Did you know?

Hydrogen gas diffuses about 4 times faster than oxygen gas at the same temperature, because it is 16× lighter (Graham's Law). This is why hydrogen escapes from balloons so quickly!
