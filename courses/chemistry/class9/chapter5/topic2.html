Gas Laws — Boyle's Law & Charles's Law

Two fundamental gas laws — Boyle's Law and Charles's Law — describe exactly how pressure, volume, and temperature of a gas are related. Once you understand these laws, you can solve quantitative problems and explain everyday phenomena like how a syringe works or why hot air balloons rise.

2.1 Boyle's Law — Pressure and Volume

Boyle's Law
At constant temperature, the volume of a fixed mass of gas is inversely proportional to its pressure.

P ∝ 1/V (at constant T)  →  P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

This means: double the pressure → half the volume (temperature stays the same).

P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

Real-life applications:

  • A syringe: pulling the plunger back increases volume, decreasing pressure inside and drawing in liquid
  • Bicycle pump: compressing the cylinder increases pressure to inflate the tyre
  • Deep-sea diving: compressed air tanks use Boyle's law — at depth, the pressure increases and gas volume is managed carefully

Worked Examples — Boyle's Law

Example 1

A gas occupies 4.0 L at a pressure of 2.0 atm. What volume will it occupy at 4.0 atm (temperature constant)?

Solution: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
(2.0)(4.0) = (4.0)(V₂)
V₂ = 8.0 / 4.0 = 2.0 L

When pressure doubles, volume halves — consistent with Boyle's Law. ✓

Example 2

500 mL of a gas is at 760 mmHg. The volume is compressed to 200 mL at constant temperature. What is the new pressure?

Solution: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
(760)(500) = (P₂)(200)
P₂ = 380 000 / 200 = 1900 mmHg

Example 3

A gas occupies 3.5 L at 1.5 atm. Find the pressure needed to compress it to 1.0 L.

Solution: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
(1.5)(3.5) = (P₂)(1.0)
P₂ = 5.25 / 1.0 = 5.25 atm

2.2 Charles's Law — Volume and Temperature

Charles's Law
At constant pressure, the volume of a fixed mass of gas is directly proportional to its absolute temperature (Kelvin).

V ∝ T (at constant P)  →  V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂

This means: double the absolute temperature → double the volume (pressure stays the same).

V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂    (T must be in Kelvin)
Always convert to Kelvin first!

K = °C + 273. Charles's Law only works with Kelvin because the relationship is based on absolute temperature. Using °C gives wrong answers.

Real-life applications:

  • Hot air balloon: heating the air inside increases its volume, making it less dense than surrounding air — the balloon rises
  • Car tyres in summer: hot roads heat the air in the tyre, increasing its pressure (and slightly volume)
  • Bread rising in oven: CO₂ gas inside the dough expands as temperature increases

Worked Examples — Charles's Law

Example 1

A gas occupies 2.0 L at 27°C. What volume will it occupy at 127°C at constant pressure?

Solution: Convert to Kelvin: T₁ = 27 + 273 = 300 K · T₂ = 127 + 273 = 400 K
V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
2.0/300 = V₂/400
V₂ = (2.0 × 400) / 300 = 2.67 L

Example 2

A gas has a volume of 500 mL at 0°C. At what temperature (°C) will its volume be 750 mL?

Solution: T₁ = 0 + 273 = 273 K
V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ → 500/273 = 750/T₂
T₂ = (750 × 273) / 500 = 409.5 K
T₂ in °C = 409.5 − 273 = 136.5°C

Example 3

A balloon contains 1.5 L of gas at 20°C. It is cooled to −30°C. What is the new volume?

Solution: T₁ = 20 + 273 = 293 K · T₂ = −30 + 273 = 243 K
V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
1.5/293 = V₂/243
V₂ = (1.5 × 243) / 293 = 1.24 L

Cooling reduces volume — consistent with Charles's Law. ✓

2.3 Comparison of Boyle's and Charles's Laws

Boyle's Law Charles's Law
Proposed byRobert Boyle (1662)Jacques Charles (1787)
Constant factorTemperaturePressure
RelationshipP & V inversely proportionalV & T directly proportional
FormulaP₁V₁ = P₂V₂V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
Graph shapeHyperbola (P vs V)Straight line through origin (V vs T in K)
Did you know?

If you combine Boyle's and Charles's laws together with Avogadro's Law, you get the Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT, where n = moles of gas and R = 8.314 J/(mol·K). This single equation unifies all gas behavior!
