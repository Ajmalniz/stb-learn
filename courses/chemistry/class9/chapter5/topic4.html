Chapter 5 · Topic 4

The Solid State

Solids are the most ordered state of matter. Their particles are held in fixed positions by strong forces, giving solids their characteristic rigidity, high density, and definite shape. Understanding solids means understanding the two very different ways atoms can arrange themselves — crystalline and amorphous.

4.1 General Properties of Solids

Rigidity
Solids have a fixed shape and volume. Particles vibrate in place but cannot move past each other. They resist any change in shape — this is rigidity.
Melting Point
The temperature at which a solid changes to liquid. At this point, the kinetic energy of particles overcomes the intermolecular forces holding them in place. Higher intermolecular forces → higher melting point.
Density
Solids have the highest densities of all states of matter (except water, where ice is less dense than liquid water). Particles are packed closely together with minimum empty space.

Other solid properties:

  • Virtually incompressible — no empty space between particles
  • Very low diffusion rate — particles cannot move freely
  • Can be crystalline or amorphous in structure

4.2 Crystalline Solids

Crystalline Solid
A solid in which particles are arranged in a highly ordered, repeating three-dimensional pattern called a crystal lattice. They have a sharp, definite melting point.

Crystalline solids are further classified by the type of particle and bonding in the lattice:

4.3 Amorphous Solids

Amorphous Solid
A solid in which particles have no long-range ordered arrangement. They are sometimes described as "supercooled liquids." Unlike crystalline solids, they have no sharp melting point — they soften gradually over a range of temperatures.

Examples of amorphous solids: glass, rubber, plastic, candle wax, tar, coal

Why glass "flows" very slowly

Glass is an amorphous solid. Old window panes are sometimes slightly thicker at the bottom — glass flows extremely slowly at room temperature because it lacks the rigid crystal structure that locks particles in place.

4.4 Crystalline vs Amorphous Solids

Feature Crystalline Solids Amorphous Solids
Particle arrangementOrdered, repeating (lattice)Random, no pattern
Melting pointSharp, definiteGradual (range of temperatures)
Cleavage planesYes — break along definite planesNo — break irregularly
IsotropyAnisotropic (properties vary by direction)Isotropic (same in all directions)
ExamplesNaCl, quartz, diamond, iceGlass, rubber, plastic, wax
Did you know?

Diamond is the hardest natural substance on Earth — a crystalline solid with every carbon atom bonded to four others in a rigid 3D network. Graphite, made of the same carbon atoms, is soft and slippery because its layers can slide over each other.
