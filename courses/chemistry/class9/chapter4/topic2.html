Ionic Bonds

Ionic bonds form when a metal gives electrons to a non-metal. The result is two oppositely charged ions that attract each other strongly — creating stable, crystalline compounds like table salt.

2.1 What Is an Ionic Bond?

Ionic Bond
A chemical bond formed by the complete transfer of one or more electrons from a metal atom to a non-metal atom, resulting in oppositely charged ions that attract each other electrostatically.
Cation
A positive ion — formed when a metal atom loses electrons. Example: Na loses 1e⁻ → Na⁺.
Anion
A negative ion — formed when a non-metal atom gains electrons. Example: Cl gains 1e⁻ → Cl⁻.
Key rule

Metals lose electrons → form cations (+). Non-metals gain electrons → form anions (−). Opposite charges attract → ionic bond forms.

2.2 Formation of Ionic Bonds — Worked Examples

Example 1 — Sodium Chloride (NaCl) — Table Salt

Sodium (Na): Electronic config = 2, 8, 1 → has 1 valence electron (1 too many → unstable)

Chlorine (Cl): Electronic config = 2, 8, 7 → has 7 valence electrons (needs 1 more → unstable)

What happens:

Na loses its 1 valence electron → becomes Na⁺ (config: 2, 8 — same as Ne ✓)

Cl gains that 1 electron → becomes Cl⁻ (config: 2, 8, 8 — same as Ar ✓)

Result:

Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → NaCl (ionic bond — strong electrostatic attraction)

Example 2 — Magnesium Oxide (MgO)

Magnesium (Mg): Config = 2, 8, 2 → has 2 valence electrons → loses both → Mg²⁺ (config: 2, 8)

Oxygen (O): Config = 2, 6 → needs 2 more electrons → gains 2 → O²⁻ (config: 2, 8)

Mg²⁺ + O²⁻ → MgO

Example 3 — Magnesium Chloride (MgCl₂)

Magnesium (Mg): Loses 2 electrons → Mg²⁺

Two Chlorine atoms: Each gains 1 electron → 2 × Cl⁻

One Mg²⁺ balances two Cl⁻ (charges cancel: 2+ and 2×1− = neutral)

Mg²⁺ + 2Cl⁻ → MgCl₂

2.3 Properties of Ionic Compounds

Ionic compounds have a characteristic set of properties because of their strong electrostatic forces and crystal lattice structure:

Property Explanation
High melting & boiling pointVery strong electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions requires a lot of energy to break
Solid at room temperatureIons are locked in a fixed, ordered 3D crystal lattice
Hard but brittleHard because ions are tightly packed. Brittle because hitting the crystal shifts layers — like charges come face-to-face → repel → crystal shatters
Conduct electricity when dissolved or moltenIn solution or liquid state, ions are free to move and carry charge. As a solid, ions are fixed → cannot conduct
Soluble in polar solvents (water)Water molecules surround and pull apart the ions (hydration). Ionic compounds do NOT dissolve in non-polar solvents like oil
Why does salt conduct in water but not as a solid?

In solid NaCl, the Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions are locked in place in the crystal — they cannot move. When dissolved in water or melted, the ions break free and can carry electrical charge. This is why electricity only flows when NaCl is in solution or liquid form.
