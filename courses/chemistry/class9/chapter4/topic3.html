Covalent Bonds, Dative Bonds & Metallic Bonds

When two non-metals meet, they don't steal electrons — they share them. This topic covers the three remaining bond types: covalent (sharing), dative (one-sided sharing), and metallic (sea of electrons).

3.1 Covalent Bonds — Sharing Electrons

Covalent Bond
A chemical bond formed by the sharing of one or more pairs of electrons between two non-metal atoms. Each atom contributes one electron to each shared pair. Both atoms count the shared electrons toward their octet.

Types of Covalent Bonds

Bond Type Shared Pairs Strength / Length Examples
Single bond (—)1 pair (2 electrons)Weakest, longestH₂, Cl₂, HCl, H₂O
Double bond (=)2 pairs (4 electrons)Stronger, shorterO₂, CO₂, C₂H₄
Triple bond (≡)3 pairs (6 electrons)Strongest, shortestN₂, C₂H₂

Lewis Structures of Common Molecules

H — H
H₂ — single bond
1 shared pair, both H get duplet
Cl — Cl
Cl₂ — single bond
each Cl has 3 lone pairs + 1 shared pair = 8 electrons
O = O
O₂ — double bond
2 shared pairs
N ≡ N
N₂ — triple bond
3 shared pairs (very stable)

3.2 Dative (Coordinate) Covalent Bonds

Dative Bond (Coordinate Covalent Bond)
A covalent bond in which both electrons of the shared pair come from the same atom. The atom donating the pair is called the donor (Lewis base); the atom accepting is the acceptor (Lewis acid). The bond is represented with an arrow: donor → acceptor.
Important

Once a dative bond is formed, it is identical to an ordinary covalent bond — you cannot tell them apart. The distinction is only in how the bond formed, not in what it looks like.

Example 1 — Ammonium Ion (NH₄⁺)

Ammonia (NH₃) has a lone pair on the nitrogen atom. When NH₃ meets a H⁺ ion (which has an empty orbital), nitrogen donates its lone pair to H⁺:

NH₃ + H⁺ → NH₄⁺

The resulting NH₄⁺ has 4 identical N−H bonds. All four bonds — including the dative one — are now equivalent.

Example 2 — Hydronium Ion (H₃O⁺)

Water (H₂O) has lone pairs on oxygen. When water meets H⁺:

H₂O + H⁺ → H₃O⁺

Oxygen donates one lone pair to H⁺ → forms the hydronium ion (important in acid-base chemistry).

3.3 Metallic Bonds — The Sea of Electrons

Metallic Bond
The electrostatic attraction between positive metal cations and a surrounding "sea" of delocalized (free-moving) electrons. Metal atoms release their valence electrons into a shared pool that belongs to the whole metal.

Metal atom model

Metal atoms → lose valence electrons → become positive cations (Na⁺, Cu²⁺, Fe²⁺, etc.)

Released electrons → flow freely around the cations → "electron sea"

Electron sea holds cations together → metallic bond

Why This Explains Metal Properties

Metal Property Explanation from Metallic Bonding
Good conductors of electricityDelocalized electrons move freely → carry charge
Malleable & ductileLayers of cations can slide over each other — the electron sea adjusts and holds them together (bond doesn't break)
Lustrous (shiny)Free electrons absorb and re-emit light → shiny surface
High melting & boiling pointsStrong attraction between many cations and the electron sea — requires much energy to break
Good conductors of heatFree electrons transfer kinetic energy rapidly through the metal
Why can you bend a metal but not salt?

In ionic crystals (like NaCl), shifting layers brings like charges face-to-face → repulsion → the crystal shatters. In metals, the electron sea surrounds and adjusts to any position the cations take — so the metal bends without breaking. That's why you can hammer gold into thin sheets but NaCl crumbles.
