Chapter 4 · Topic 5

Intermolecular Forces

Chemical bonds hold atoms together within a molecule. But what holds one molecule attracted to another? These intermolecular forces — though weaker than bonds — control boiling points, melting points, viscosity, and even why life is possible on Earth.

5.1 What Are Intermolecular Forces?

Intermolecular Forces (IMFs)
Forces of attraction between molecules (not within a molecule). They are much weaker than covalent or ionic bonds. They determine a substance's physical properties: boiling point, melting point, viscosity, surface tension.
Intramolecular vs Intermolecular

Intramolecular = forces within a molecule (chemical bonds — very strong). Intermolecular = forces between molecules (IMFs — much weaker). When you boil water, you are NOT breaking O−H bonds — you are breaking the hydrogen bonds between water molecules.

5.2 Three Types of Intermolecular Forces

① London Dispersion Forces (Induced Dipole-Dipole)

  • Present in ALL molecules — polar or non-polar, even noble gas atoms.
  • Caused by temporary (instantaneous) dipoles: electrons are always moving. At any instant, electrons may bunch up on one side of a molecule → creating a brief δ− on that side and δ+ on the other. This temporary dipole induces a dipole in a neighbouring molecule → weak attraction.
  • Strength increases with molar mass (more electrons = more polarizable = stronger London forces).
  • Example trend — boiling points of halogens (all non-polar): F₂ (−188°C) < Cl₂ (−34°C) < Br₂ (59°C) < I₂ (184°C). As molar mass increases, London forces increase → higher BP.
  • Also called van der Waals forces.

② Dipole-Dipole Interactions (Permanent)

  • Occur between polar molecules that have a permanent dipole.
  • The δ+ end of one molecule is attracted to the δ− end of the next molecule.
  • Stronger than London forces for molecules of similar size.
  • Example: HCl molecules — the δ+ H of one HCl attracts the δ− Cl of another HCl.
  • Example: SO₂, H₂S — both polar molecules, exhibit dipole-dipole interactions.

③ Hydrogen Bonding (Strongest IMF)

  • A special, extra-strong type of dipole-dipole interaction.
  • Occurs when H is covalently bonded to F, O, or N — the three most electronegative small atoms.
  • These atoms pull electrons so strongly that H becomes very δ+. This δ+ H is attracted to a lone pair on the F, O, or N of a neighbouring molecule.
  • The hydrogen bond is represented as: X−H ··· Y (where X and Y are F, O, or N; ··· = H-bond)
  • Examples: H₂O (O−H···O), HF (F−H···F), NH₃ (N−H···N).
  • Explains water's anomalously high boiling point (100°C vs H₂S = −60°C), ice floating (H-bonds in ice form an open hexagonal lattice, less dense than liquid water), DNA double helix, protein shapes.

Water — the molecule that makes life possible

Without hydrogen bonding, water would boil at about −80°C — it would be a gas at room temperature. Earth would have no oceans, no rain, no liquid water anywhere. All known life depends on liquid water. Hydrogen bonding is literally one of the reasons life exists.
