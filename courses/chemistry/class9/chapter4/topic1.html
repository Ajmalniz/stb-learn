Why Atoms Form Chemical Bonds

Most atoms in nature are unstable on their own — they are constantly trying to reach a more stable arrangement. Understanding why atoms bond together is the foundation of all chemistry.

1.1 The Goal: Noble Gas Stability

Look at the noble gases (Group 18 of the periodic table): Helium (He), Neon (Ne), Argon (Ar). These elements almost never react with other atoms — they are perfectly stable on their own. Why? Because their outermost electron shell is completely full.

Noble Gas Electron Configuration Outer Shell Electrons
Helium (He)22 (full duplet)
Neon (Ne)2, 88 (full octet)
Argon (Ar)2, 8, 88 (full octet)

All other atoms want to reach this same stable arrangement. This is the driving force behind all chemical bonding.

1.2 The Octet Rule (and Duplet Rule)

Octet Rule
Atoms tend to react in ways that give them 8 electrons in their outermost (valence) shell, achieving the stable configuration of a noble gas.
Duplet Rule
Hydrogen (H) and Helium (He) are stable with only 2 electrons in their single shell — they follow the duplet rule, not the octet rule.
Valence Electrons
The electrons in the outermost shell of an atom. These are the only electrons involved in chemical bonding.
Quick rule for valence electrons

The group number of an element (for Groups 1–8) equals its number of valence electrons. Sodium (Group 1) has 1 valence electron. Oxygen (Group 6) has 6. Chlorine (Group 7) has 7.

1.3 Three Ways to Achieve Stability

Atoms achieve the noble gas configuration through three main mechanisms:

① Transfer of electrons → Ionic Bond

A metal gives its valence electrons to a non-metal. Both become ions with full outer shells. The opposite charges attract. Example: Na gives 1e⁻ to Cl → NaCl.

② Sharing of electrons → Covalent Bond

Two non-metal atoms each contribute electrons to a shared pair. Both atoms count the shared electrons as their own. Example: H + H → H₂ (both H count the shared pair → duplet satisfied).

③ Donation of an electron pair → Dative (Coordinate) Bond

One atom donates both electrons of a shared pair to another atom that has an empty orbital. Example: N in NH₃ donates a lone pair to H⁺ → forms NH₄⁺.

1.4 Lewis Dot Structures

A Lewis dot structure is a simple diagram that shows the valence electrons of an atom as dots around its chemical symbol. It helps us visualise how many electrons are available for bonding.

Think of it like a handshake

When two atoms bond, it is like two people shaking hands — they share something. In covalent bonding, the "handshake" is a pair of electrons shared between the two atoms. Both atoms count those electrons as belonging to them, which helps both reach their target of 8.

1.5 Chemical Bond — Definition

Chemical Bond
A force of attraction that holds two or more atoms together in a compound. It forms when atoms interact to achieve a lower, more stable energy state.

Bond formation always releases energy (the system becomes more stable). Bond breaking always requires energy input.
