Polar & Non-polar Bonds — Properties of Compounds

Not all covalent bonds share electrons equally. When one atom pulls harder on the shared electrons, the bond becomes polar — one end is slightly negative, the other slightly positive. This affects everything from boiling points to solubility.

4.1 Electronegativity — Who Pulls Harder?

Electronegativity
The ability of an atom to attract shared electrons toward itself in a covalent bond. Higher electronegativity = stronger pull on shared electrons.

The most electronegative elements are in the top right of the periodic table (excluding noble gases): F > O > N > Cl > Br > C > H

Element Electronegativity (Pauling scale)
F (Fluorine)4.0 (highest)
O (Oxygen)3.5
N (Nitrogen)3.0
Cl (Chlorine)3.0
H (Hydrogen)2.1
Na (Sodium)0.9 (low — metals are low)

4.2 Polar Covalent Bonds

Polar Covalent Bond
A covalent bond in which electrons are shared unequally because the two atoms have different electronegativities. The more electronegative atom gets a partial negative charge (δ−); the less electronegative atom gets a partial positive charge (δ+).

Example — H−Cl (Hydrogen Chloride)

Cl electronegativity = 3.0  |  H electronegativity = 2.1  |  ΔEN = 0.9

Hδ+ — Clδ−

Cl pulls the shared electrons closer → Cl becomes δ− (slightly negative). H becomes δ+ (slightly positive). This is a bond dipole.

Example — H₂O (Water)

Oxygen (EN=3.5) is much more electronegative than H (EN=2.1). Both O−H bonds are polar. The water molecule has a bent shape, so the bond dipoles do NOT cancel — water is a polar molecule overall.

This makes water: a great solvent, have a high boiling point, have surface tension, and be able to dissolve ionic compounds.

4.3 Non-polar Covalent Bonds

Non-polar Covalent Bond
A covalent bond where electrons are shared equally — either between identical atoms, or atoms with very similar electronegativities (ΔEN < 0.4).
  • H−H (H₂): ΔEN = 0 — perfectly equal sharing. Pure non-polar.
  • Cl−Cl (Cl₂): ΔEN = 0 — non-polar.
  • C−H (in methane CH₄): ΔEN = 0.4 — considered non-polar.
  • CO₂: Each C=O bond is polar, but the molecule is linear → the dipoles point in opposite directions and exactly cancel → non-polar molecule overall.
  • CH₄: Each C−H is slightly polar, but the tetrahedral symmetry means all dipoles cancel → non-polar molecule.

"Like dissolves like"

Polar solvents (like water) dissolve polar and ionic compounds. Non-polar solvents (like petrol, benzene) dissolve non-polar compounds. This is why oil does not mix with water — oil is non-polar, water is polar.
