Types of Solutions by Physical State

Solutions can form between any two states of matter — gas, liquid, or solid. That gives us 9 possible combinations. Each type has important real-world examples, from the air we breathe to the metal alloys in our buildings.

2.1 The 9 Types of Solutions

A solution is formed when a solute dissolves in a solvent. Both can be in any physical state. Here are all nine combinations:

Solute State Solvent State Example
GasGasAir (N₂, O₂, CO₂ mixed)
GasLiquidCO₂ in water (soda), O₂ in water (fish breathe)
GasSolidH₂ absorbed in palladium metal
LiquidGasWater vapour in air (humidity)
LiquidLiquidAlcohol in water; oil and water (immiscible)
LiquidSolidMercury in silver/gold (amalgam)
SolidGasIodine vapour; smoke particles in air
SolidLiquidNaCl in water, sugar in water (most common)
SolidSolidAlloys: brass, bronze, stainless steel

2.2 Gas-in-Gas Solutions

All gases mix completely and uniformly with each other — they are all miscible. Air is the most important gas-in-gas solution:

  • Nitrogen (N₂) — ~78% by volume
  • Oxygen (O₂) — ~21% by volume
  • Argon (Ar) — ~0.93%
  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂) — ~0.04%

2.3 Gas-in-Liquid Solutions

Gas molecules dissolve in liquids when they have some attraction to the liquid molecules.

  • CO₂ in water: Carbonated drinks (soda, cola, sparkling water). CO₂ dissolves under high pressure; when you open the bottle, pressure drops and CO₂ escapes as bubbles.
  • O₂ in water: Essential for aquatic life — fish extract dissolved oxygen through their gills.
  • NH₃ in water: Household ammonia cleaning solution.
Key fact

Gas solubility in liquids DECREASES with increasing temperature. Warm soda goes flat faster than cold soda. In hot summers, rivers lose dissolved oxygen → aquatic animals suffer.

2.4 Liquid-in-Liquid Solutions

Two liquids may be miscible (mix completely) or immiscible (do not mix).

  • Miscible: Ethanol (alcohol) in water — they mix in all proportions. Glycerol in water.
  • Immiscible: Oil and water do not mix because oil is non-polar and water is polar. They form two separate layers.
Why oil and water don't mix

Water molecules are polar — they attract each other strongly. Oil molecules are non-polar — they attract each other but not water. The water-water and oil-oil attractions are both stronger than any water-oil attraction, so they stay separate.

2.5 Liquid-in-Solid: Amalgams

An amalgam is a solution of mercury (liquid metal) in another metal (solid).

  • Hg-Ag amalgam: Used historically in dental fillings (now being phased out due to mercury toxicity).
  • Hg-Au amalgam: Used historically to extract gold from ore — mercury dissolves gold, then mercury is evaporated off.

2.6 Solid-in-Liquid Solutions

This is the most common type we work with in chemistry labs and in daily life.

  • NaCl in water → clear salt solution (Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions surrounded by water molecules)
  • Sugar (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁) in water → sugar solution (molecules dispersed)
  • CuSO₄ in water → blue solution (Cu²⁺ ions give the blue colour)
  • KMnO₄ in water → deep purple solution

2.7 Solid-in-Solid Solutions: Alloys

An alloy is a solid solution of one metal in another. They are formed by melting the metals together, mixing them as liquids, then cooling.

Alloy Components Properties & Uses
BrassCu + ZnHarder than Cu; musical instruments, plumbing fittings
BronzeCu + SnVery hard; historical weapons, coins, statues, bells
Stainless SteelFe + Cr + NiCorrosion-resistant; cutlery, surgical tools, buildings
SteelFe + CStronger than iron; construction, machinery
SolderPb + SnLow melting point; joining electrical components
