Chapter 6 · Topic 4

Solubility

Why does salt dissolve in water but not in oil? Why does more sugar dissolve in hot tea than in cold tea? Why does soda go flat when you heat it? All of these questions are answered by understanding solubility and the "like dissolves like" principle.

4.1 What is Solubility?

Solubility
The maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in a given amount of solvent at a specific temperature and pressure. Usually expressed as grams of solute per 100 g (or 100 mL) of solvent.

Example: The solubility of NaCl in water at 20°C is 36 g per 100 mL. You cannot dissolve more than 36 g — it just stays at the bottom. At 100°C, the solubility increases to about 39 g per 100 mL.

4.2 "Like Dissolves Like" — Solute-Solvent Interaction

The most important rule in solubility is: polar solvents dissolve polar/ionic solutes, and non-polar solvents dissolve non-polar solutes.

Solvent Type Dissolves Example
Polar (water, alcohol)Polar and ionic solutesNaCl, sugar, HCl dissolve in water
Non-polar (hexane, oil)Non-polar solutesIodine, fats, waxes dissolve in hexane/oil

Why does this happen?

Dissolving occurs when the attractions between solute and solvent are strong enough to pull apart the solute particles. For a polar solute (like NaCl) in water: water molecules are polar and attract the Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions strongly (ion-dipole forces). This attraction is strong enough to break the ionic bonds in the crystal → the salt dissolves.

For a non-polar solute (like iodine, I₂) in water: water molecules attract each other much more strongly than they attract iodine molecules (water prefers water). Iodine molecules prefer other non-polar molecules → so iodine does not dissolve in water.

Practical Example

A grease stain does not come off with water — because grease is non-polar and water is polar. Dry cleaning uses non-polar solvents (like perchloroethylene) that dissolve the non-polar grease. Oil-based paints are cleaned with turpentine (non-polar); water-based paints can be cleaned with water.

4.3 Effect of Temperature on Solubility

Solids Dissolved in Liquids

For most solid solutes, solubility INCREASES with increasing temperature.

  • More kinetic energy at higher temperatures → solvent molecules move faster → break down solute particles more effectively.
  • Example: More sugar dissolves in hot tea than in cold tea.
  • KNO₃: solubility jumps from 13 g/100g at 0°C to 246 g/100g at 100°C — a huge increase!
  • NaCl: solubility is nearly flat — barely changes with temperature (from 35.7 g at 0°C to 39.2 g at 100°C).

Gases Dissolved in Liquids

For gas solutes, solubility DECREASES with increasing temperature.

  • As temperature rises, gas molecules gain kinetic energy and escape from the liquid more easily.
  • Warm soda goes flat much faster than cold soda — CO₂ escapes when heated.
  • In hot summer months, the oxygen dissolved in rivers and lakes decreases → fish and aquatic animals may suffocate.
Summary

Temperature ↑ → Solid solubility ↑ (usually) | Gas solubility ↓

4.4 Effect of Pressure on Solubility

Pressure has very little effect on the solubility of solids and liquids, but it greatly affects gases in liquids.

Henry's Law: The solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of that gas above the liquid.

  • Higher pressure → gas molecules are forced into the liquid more → more gas dissolves.
  • Lower pressure → gas escapes more easily → less dissolved.
Soda Bottles — Henry's Law in Action

Soda is bottled with CO₂ under high pressure (~3–4 atmospheres). This forces large amounts of CO₂ to dissolve. When you open the bottle, pressure drops to atmospheric pressure → CO₂ becomes "oversaturated" → it escapes as bubbles (the fizz). A warm bottle fizzes much more violently because temperature also reduces gas solubility.
