Concentration Units

Concentration tells us how strong or weak a solution is — how much solute is packed into a given amount of solution. There are three important ways to express concentration: percentage by mass, percentage by volume, and molarity.

3.1 What is Concentration?

Concentration
The amount of solute dissolved per unit volume (or mass) of solution. A high concentration = strong solution (lots of solute). A low concentration = weak/dilute solution (little solute).

Think of it like tea: two tea bags in one cup = strong (high concentration). One tea bag in a large pot = weak (low concentration).

3.2 Percentage by Mass (% w/w)

Expresses concentration as the mass of solute per 100 g of solution.

% mass = (mass of solute / mass of solution) × 100

Remember: Mass of solution = mass of solute + mass of solvent.

Example 1: 5 g of NaCl is dissolved in 95 g of water. What is the % mass?

  • Mass of solution = 5 + 95 = 100 g
  • % mass = (5/100) × 100 = 5%

Example 2: 10 g of sugar is dissolved in 90 g of water. What is the % mass?

  • Mass of solution = 10 + 90 = 100 g
  • % mass = (10/100) × 100 = 10%

Example 3: Seawater is approximately 3.5% NaCl by mass — that means 3.5 g of salt in every 100 g of seawater.

Everyday Example

When a food label says "contains 5 g of sugar per 100 g" — that is 5% sugar by mass. Chemists use the same idea!

3.3 Percentage by Volume (% v/v)

Used for liquid-in-liquid solutions. Expresses how many mL of solute are in 100 mL of solution.

% volume = (volume of solute / volume of solution) × 100

Example: Vodka is labelled "40% alcohol (v/v)" — this means 40 mL of pure alcohol in every 100 mL of the drink.

Example: Surgical spirit is "70% ethanol (v/v)" — 70 mL ethanol per 100 mL solution. This concentration is most effective at killing bacteria.

3.4 Molarity (M)

Molarity is the most important concentration unit in chemistry. It counts the number of moles of solute per litre of solution.

Molarity (M) = moles of solute (n) / volume of solution in litres (V)
Unit: mol/L or M (molar)

To find moles from mass: moles (n) = mass (g) / molar mass (g/mol)

Molar mass = sum of atomic masses of all atoms in the formula.

Critical Reminder

Always convert volume from mL to L before using the molarity formula! 500 mL = 0.5 L, 250 mL = 0.25 L.

Worked Examples

Example 1: 58.5 g of NaCl is dissolved in water to make 1 litre of solution. What is the molarity?

  • Molar mass of NaCl = 23 (Na) + 35.5 (Cl) = 58.5 g/mol
  • n = 58.5 / 58.5 = 1 mol
  • M = 1 / 1 = 1 M

Example 2: 9.8 g of H₂SO₄ is dissolved in water to make 500 mL of solution. What is the molarity?

  • Molar mass of H₂SO₄ = 2(1) + 32 + 4(16) = 2 + 32 + 64 = 98 g/mol
  • n = 9.8 / 98 = 0.1 mol
  • V = 500 mL = 0.5 L
  • M = 0.1 / 0.5 = 0.2 M

Example 3: How many grams of NaOH are needed to make 250 mL of a 2 M solution?

  • Molar mass of NaOH = 23 + 16 + 1 = 40 g/mol
  • V = 250 mL = 0.25 L
  • n = M × V = 2 × 0.25 = 0.5 mol
  • mass = n × molar mass = 0.5 × 40 = 20 g
Why use molarity?

Chemical reactions happen between moles of particles, not between grams. Using molarity means we know exactly how many molecules are in our solution — which is what matters for reactions. If we mix equal volumes of 1 M HCl and 1 M NaOH, we know they will exactly neutralise each other.
