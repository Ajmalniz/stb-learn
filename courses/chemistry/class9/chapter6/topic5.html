Chapter 6 · Topic 5

Solutions, Colloids and Suspensions

Not all mixtures are the same. Salt water, milk, and muddy water all look different — and they behave differently too. The key difference is the size of the particles being mixed. This gives us three distinct categories of mixtures.

5.1 The Three Types of Mixtures

All mixtures can be classified into three groups based on their particle size:

  • True Solutions — particle size < 1 nm
  • Colloids — particle size 1–100 nm
  • Suspensions — particle size > 100 nm (often visible)

5.2 True Solutions

True Solution
A homogeneous mixture where the solute particles are individual ions or molecules (size <1 nm). They are too small to see or filter out.
  • Appearance: Transparent and clear — no cloudiness.
  • Settling: Does NOT settle on standing.
  • Filtration: Cannot be separated by ordinary filtration (particles pass through filter paper).
  • Tyndall effect: NO — a beam of light passes through without scattering (the particles are too small to scatter light).
  • Examples: Salt water, sugar water, copper sulfate solution, dilute HCl.

5.3 Colloids

Colloid
A mixture where one substance is dispersed as particles of size 1–100 nm in another substance. The particles are too small to settle but large enough to scatter light.
  • Appearance: Cloudy, turbid, or translucent.
  • Settling: Does NOT settle on standing (particles too small for gravity to pull down).
  • Filtration: Cannot be separated by ordinary filtration. Needs ultracentrifuge or dialysis.
  • Tyndall effect: YES — light beam is scattered and visible when passing through.
  • Examples: Milk (fat droplets in water), fog/clouds (water droplets in air), smoke (carbon particles in air), jelly/gelatin, blood, mayonnaise, butter, aerosol sprays, shaving cream.

5.4 Suspensions

Suspension (Turbid Mixture)
A heterogeneous mixture where particles are large (>100 nm — often visible to the naked eye) and dispersed in a medium. The particles will settle out over time.
  • Appearance: Cloudy or opaque.
  • Settling: SETTLES on standing — particles are large enough for gravity to pull them down.
  • Filtration: Can be separated by ordinary filtration (particles are too large to pass through filter paper).
  • Examples: Muddy water, chalk in water, sand in water, oil and water (shaken), blood after standing (red cells settle).

5.5 The Tyndall Effect

Tyndall Effect
The scattering of a beam of light by colloidal particles, making the beam visible from the side. Named after John Tyndall (1869).

When light hits a colloidal particle (1–100 nm), it is scattered in all directions. This makes the beam of light visible when viewed from the side.

  • True solution: Light passes straight through — beam NOT visible from the side.
  • Colloid: Light is scattered — beam IS visible from the side (Tyndall effect present).
Tyndall Effect in Real Life

When sunlight shines through a dusty room or through fog, you can see the beam of light — that's the Tyndall effect. The dust particles or water droplets scatter the light. Also: why the sky is blue! Tiny gas molecules in the atmosphere scatter blue light more than red — Tyndall-type scattering makes the sky appear blue.
