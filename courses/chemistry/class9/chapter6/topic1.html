Introduction to Solutions

Look around you — the water you drink, the air you breathe, the blood in your veins — they're all solutions. This topic explains exactly what a solution is, the difference between solute and solvent, and how we classify solutions by how much solute they contain.

1.1 What is a Solution?

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more substances. "Homogeneous" means it is uniform throughout — every part of it looks and feels the same. The particles in a solution are individual atoms, ions, or small molecules — so small that they cannot be seen even under a microscope (size < 1 nm).

Solution
A homogeneous mixture where one substance is dissolved in another. Particles are <1 nm — invisible, cannot be filtered out.
Solvent
The substance present in the larger amount — the one doing the dissolving. Example: water in salt water.
Solute
The substance present in the smaller amount — the one being dissolved. Example: salt in salt water.
Quick Rule

The solvent is always more than the solute. In a cup of tea: water = solvent, tea leaves extract + sugar = solutes.

1.2 Aqueous Solutions

An aqueous solution is any solution where water is the solvent. This is the most common and important type of solution in chemistry, biology, and everyday life.

  • Salt water (NaCl dissolved in H₂O) → aqueous solution
  • Sugar water → aqueous solution
  • Copper sulfate solution (CuSO₄ in H₂O) → aqueous solution (blue colour)
  • Hydrochloric acid (HCl gas dissolved in H₂O) → aqueous solution
Did You Know?

Ocean water is a solution (~3.5% NaCl). Coffee is a solution. The air you breathe is a solution of gases (N₂, O₂, CO₂, Ar, etc.). Even our blood is a complex aqueous solution!

1.3 Types of Solutions by Amount of Solute

How much solute is dissolved in the solvent tells us whether the solution is unsaturated, saturated, or supersaturated.

Unsaturated Solution
A solution that can dissolve more solute at the given temperature. It is "not full" yet. Example: 5 g of NaCl dissolved in 100 mL water at 20°C (maximum is ~36 g — so this can take much more).
Saturated Solution
A solution that contains the maximum dissolved solute possible at a given temperature. If you add more solute, it just sinks to the bottom undissolved. Example: 36 g of NaCl in 100 mL water at 20°C.
Supersaturated Solution
A solution that temporarily holds more dissolved solute than the maximum. It is unstable — any tiny disturbance (a crystal seed, a scratch on the glass, a vibration) causes rapid crystallization (all the extra solute suddenly falls out as crystals).

How to make a supersaturated solution:

  1. Heat the solvent (hot water dissolves more solute).
  2. Dissolve a large amount of solute in the hot solvent.
  3. Cool the solution very slowly and gently, without disturbing it.
  4. The solution is now supersaturated — more solute than it should hold at room temperature.
Real-life Application

Hand warmers use supersaturated sodium acetate (hot ice). When you flex the metal disc inside, it triggers crystallization → the excess solute suddenly crystallizes → releases heat. That's why the pack gets warm!

1.4 Dilution of a Solution

Dilution means adding more solvent to a solution to decrease its concentration. The amount of solute stays the same — only the volume increases.

C₁V₁ = C₂V₂

Where C₁ = initial concentration, V₁ = initial volume, C₂ = final concentration, V₂ = final volume.

Worked Example: You have 2 M HCl. How much of it do you need to make 1000 mL of 0.5 M HCl?

  • C₁ = 2 M, V₁ = ?, C₂ = 0.5 M, V₂ = 1000 mL
  • 2 × V₁ = 0.5 × 1000
  • V₁ = 500 / 2 = 250 mL
  • Take 250 mL of the 2 M HCl and add water until the total volume reaches 1000 mL.
Safety Note

When diluting concentrated acids, always add acid to water (never water to acid). Adding water to concentrated acid can cause a violent exothermic reaction and splashing.
