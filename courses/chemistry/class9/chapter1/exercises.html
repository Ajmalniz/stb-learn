Section A — Multiple Choice Questions

1. The branch of Chemistry which deals with hydrocarbons:
A. Industrial chemistry
B. Inorganic chemistry
C. Organic chemistry
D. Physical chemistry
Organic chemistry deals with carbon-containing compounds, especially hydrocarbons (compounds of carbon and hydrogen) such as methane, ethane, and benzene.
2. The atomic mass of an element expressed in gram is:
A. Gram molecular mass
B. Gram atomic mass
C. Gram formula mass
D. Mole
Gram atomic mass is the atomic mass of an element expressed in grams. It equals the mass of one mole of atoms. E.g., gram atomic mass of Na = 23 g/mol.
3. Which of the following can be separated by physical means?
A. Mixture
B. Element
C. Compound
D. Substance
A mixture can be separated by physical methods like filtration, distillation, or evaporation because components are not chemically bonded. Compounds require chemical means to separate.
4. The molar mass of H₂SO₄ is:
A. 98 a.m.u
B. 9.8 gm
C. 98 gm
D. 9.8 a.m.u
Molar mass of H₂SO₄ = 2(1) + 32 + 4(16) = 2 + 32 + 64 = 98 g/mol. Molar mass is expressed in grams (g/mol), not a.m.u.
5. The molecule that consists of two atoms is:
A. Monoatomic molecule
B. Polyatomic molecule
C. Hetero atomic molecule
D. Diatomic molecule
A diatomic molecule contains exactly two atoms. Examples: H₂, O₂, N₂, Cl₂ (homoatomic diatomic) and HCl, CO (heteroatomic diatomic).
6. A formula that indicates the actual number and type of atoms in a molecule is called:
A. Chemical formula
B. Empirical formula
C. Molecular formula
D. Formula mass
The molecular formula shows the actual (exact) number and type of each atom in one molecule. E.g., glucose = C₆H₁₂O₆. The empirical formula only shows the simplest ratio.
7. Ethyl alcohol was prepared by:
A. Ibne-Sina
B. Al-Razi
C. Al-Beruni
D. Jabir bin-Hayan
Al-Razi (Rhazes, 854–925 AD) was the first to prepare pure ethyl alcohol (ethanol) through fermentation and distillation. He also discovered sulphuric acid and hydrochloric acid.
8. Which of the following is a homoatomic molecule?
A. H₂
B. NH₃
C. H₂O
D. CO₂
H₂ is homoatomic — it contains only one type of atom (hydrogen). NH₃, H₂O, and CO₂ are heteroatomic because they contain two different types of atoms.
9. The empirical formula of hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) is:
A. H₂O₂
B. HO
C. OH₂
D. O₂H₂
H₂O₂ has H : O = 2 : 2 = 1 : 1. The simplest whole-number ratio is 1:1, so empirical formula = HO. The molecular formula H₂O₂ = 2 × (HO).
10. A piece of matter in pure form is termed as:
A. Radical
B. Mixture
C. Compound
D. Substance
A substance is matter in pure form — it has a definite, uniform composition throughout. Elements and compounds are both pure substances. Mixtures are not pure substances.

Q2. Write down the classification of molecules.

Molecules are classified in two ways:

A. Based on number of atoms:

  • Monoatomic: 1 atom — e.g., He, Ne, Ar (noble gases)
  • Diatomic: 2 atoms — e.g., H₂, O₂, N₂, HCl
  • Triatomic: 3 atoms — e.g., H₂O, CO₂, SO₂
  • Polyatomic: 4 or more atoms — e.g., NH₃ (4 atoms), C₆H₁₂O₆ (24 atoms)

B. Based on type of atoms:

  • Homoatomic (Homogeneous): All atoms are of the same element — e.g., H₂, O₂, O₃, N₂
  • Heteroatomic (Heterogeneous): Atoms of two or more different elements — e.g., H₂O, CO₂, NH₃, HCl

Q4. Define the following terms: (a) Gram atomic mass   (b) Gram molecular mass   (c) Gram formula mass

(a) Gram Atomic Mass:
The atomic mass of an element expressed in grams. It is equal to the mass of one mole of atoms of that element. Example: Gram atomic mass of Na = 23 g (because atomic mass of Na = 23 amu; one mole of Na atoms weighs 23 g).

(b) Gram Molecular Mass:
The molecular mass of a covalent substance expressed in grams. It is the mass of one mole of molecules. Example: Gram molecular mass of H₂O = 18 g (molecular mass = 18 amu; one mole of H₂O weighs 18 g).

(c) Gram Formula Mass:
The formula mass of an ionic compound expressed in grams. Used for ionic substances (which do not form individual molecules). It is the mass of one mole of formula units. Example: Gram formula mass of NaCl = 58.5 g (formula mass = 23 + 35.5 = 58.5 amu).

Q6. What is a Free Radical?

A free radical is an atom, molecule, or ion that has an unpaired electron in its outer shell. This unpaired electron makes it extremely reactive — it immediately seeks to pair its electron by reacting with another atom or molecule.

Representation: A dot (·) is written next to the symbol to show the unpaired electron. Example: Cl· (chlorine radical), ·OH (hydroxyl radical), CH₃· (methyl radical).

Formation: Free radicals form when a covalent bond breaks and one electron goes to each atom (homolytic cleavage). They are produced by heat, light, or radiation.

Importance: Free radicals initiate chain reactions in combustion and polymerisation. In the body, free radicals cause cell damage and are linked to ageing and disease — antioxidants neutralise them.

Q7. Describe the relationship between empirical and molecular formula. Explain with examples.

The empirical formula shows the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound. The molecular formula shows the actual number of each type of atom in one molecule.

Relationship: Molecular formula = n × Empirical formula

where n is a positive whole number (1, 2, 3, …) found by:

n = Molecular mass ÷ Empirical formula mass

Examples:

  • Glucose: Empirical = CH₂O (mass = 30), Molecular = C₆H₁₂O₆ (mass = 180). n = 180 ÷ 30 = 6. ✓ C₆H₁₂O₆ = 6 × CH₂O
  • Benzene: Empirical = CH (mass = 13), Molecular = C₆H₆ (mass = 78). n = 78 ÷ 13 = 6. ✓ C₆H₆ = 6 × CH
  • Water: Empirical = H₂O (mass = 18), Molecular = H₂O (mass = 18). n = 1. They are the same.
  • Hydrogen peroxide: Empirical = HO (mass = 17), Molecular = H₂O₂ (mass = 34). n = 2. ✓ H₂O₂ = 2 × HO
Q8. Explain why hydrogen and oxygen are considered elements whereas water is a compound.

Hydrogen (H₂) is an element because:

  • It contains only one type of atom — hydrogen atoms only.
  • It cannot be broken down into simpler substances by ordinary chemical means.
  • It has a unique set of properties: colourless, odourless, flammable gas; lightest known substance.

Oxygen (O₂) is an element because:

  • It contains only one type of atom — oxygen atoms only.
  • It cannot be split into simpler substances by ordinary chemical means.
  • It has its own properties: colourless, odourless, supports combustion and respiration.

Water (H₂O) is a compound because:

  • It contains two different types of atoms — hydrogen AND oxygen — chemically bonded together.
  • Hydrogen and oxygen combine in a fixed ratio (H : O = 2 : 1 by atoms, 1 : 8 by mass).
  • Water has completely different properties from hydrogen and oxygen: liquid at room temperature, does not burn, does not support combustion, and is essential for life.
  • Water can only be decomposed by a chemical reaction (electrolysis: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂).

In short: H₂ and O₂ each have one kind of atom = elements. H₂O has two different elements chemically combined in a fixed ratio with new properties = compound.

Section C — Detailed Questions

Q1. What do you mean by chemical species? Explain ion, molecular ion, and free radical.

A chemical species is any atom, molecule, ion, or radical that takes part in chemical reactions. It refers to any identifiable particle involved in chemistry.

Ion:
An ion is an electrically charged atom or group of atoms formed by gaining or losing electrons.

  • Cation (positive ion): Formed when an atom loses electrons. Number of protons > electrons. Example: Na⁺ (sodium loses 1 electron), Ca²⁺ (calcium loses 2 electrons), Al³⁺.
  • Anion (negative ion): Formed when an atom gains electrons. Number of electrons > protons. Example: Cl⁻ (chlorine gains 1 electron), O²⁻, S²⁻.

Molecular Ion (Polyatomic Ion):
A molecular ion is a charged species made up of two or more atoms covalently bonded together, carrying a net positive or negative charge. These groups act as a single unit in chemical reactions.

  • Positive molecular ions: NH₄⁺ (ammonium), H₃O⁺ (hydroxonium/hydronium)
  • Negative molecular ions: SO₄²⁻ (sulphate), NO₃⁻ (nitrate), OH⁻ (hydroxide), CO₃²⁻ (carbonate)

Free Radical:
A free radical is an atom, molecule, or ion with one or more unpaired electrons in its outer shell. The unpaired electron makes it highly reactive and short-lived. Free radicals are formed during bond breaking (homolytic cleavage) by heat, light, or radiation. They are represented with a dot: Cl·, ·OH, CH₃·. They play important roles in chain reactions, combustion, polymerisation, and biological processes (ageing, disease).

Q2. Write down the applications of chemistry in daily life.

Chemistry touches almost every aspect of our daily lives. Major applications include:

1. Food and Agriculture: Fertilisers (urea, ammonium nitrate) increase crop yield. Pesticides protect crops. Food preservatives extend shelf life. The chemistry of cooking (baking, fermentation) relies on chemical reactions.

2. Medicine and Health: All medicines — antibiotics, painkillers, vaccines, vitamins — are chemical compounds. Chemistry is used to develop new drugs, analyse blood and urine, and sterilise equipment. Anaesthetics used in surgery are chemicals.

3. Materials and Industry: Metals, plastics, rubber, glass, ceramics, concrete, and textiles are all produced through chemical processes. Alloys (steel, brass) are made by chemistry. Synthetic fibres (nylon, polyester) and paints are chemical products.

4. Fuels and Energy: Petroleum, natural gas, coal, and LPG are chemical fuels. Combustion of these fuels (a chemical reaction) generates electricity and heat. Batteries and fuel cells use chemical reactions to produce electrical energy.

5. Cleaning and Hygiene: Soaps, detergents, shampoos, toothpaste, and disinfectants are all chemical products. They work through chemical reactions (saponification, emulsification) to clean and kill germs.

6. Environment: Chemistry helps in treating water (water purification, chlorination), managing air pollution, treating sewage, and developing biodegradable materials to reduce pollution.

7. Technology: Semiconductors, computer chips, solar cells, and lithium-ion batteries all depend on advanced chemistry. Photography, printing, and LCD screens are based on chemical principles.

In conclusion, chemistry is essential to modern civilisation. It improves food security, health, materials, energy, and environmental protection.

Q3. Explain in detail empirical and molecular formula.

Empirical Formula:

The empirical formula of a compound shows the simplest whole-number ratio of the atoms of each element present in the compound. It does NOT necessarily tell us how many atoms are actually in one molecule — it only shows the ratio.

How to find: Find the mole ratio of atoms → divide all by the smallest → write as subscripts.

Examples:

  • H₂O₂: H : O = 2 : 2 = 1 : 1 → Empirical formula = HO
  • C₆H₁₂O₆: C : H : O = 6 : 12 : 6 = 1 : 2 : 1 → Empirical formula = CH₂O
  • C₆H₆: C : H = 6 : 6 = 1 : 1 → Empirical formula = CH

Molecular Formula:

The molecular formula shows the actual (true) number of atoms of each element in one molecule of the compound. It is a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula.

Formula: Molecular formula = n × Empirical formula, where n = Molecular mass ÷ Empirical formula mass

Examples:

  • Glucose: Empirical = CH₂O, Molecular = C₆H₁₂O₆ (n = 6)
  • Benzene: Empirical = CH, Molecular = C₆H₆ (n = 6)
  • Water: Empirical = H₂O, Molecular = H₂O (n = 1)

Key difference: Empirical formula is the simplest ratio; molecular formula is the actual count. Two different compounds can share the same empirical formula — e.g., glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) and acetic acid (CH₃COOH = C₂H₄O₂) both have empirical formula CH₂O.

Q4. Explain the steps for balancing a chemical equation.

A balanced chemical equation has equal numbers of each type of atom on both sides (Law of Conservation of Mass). The steps for balancing by the inspection (hit and trial) method are:

Step 1 — Write the unbalanced (skeleton) equation:
Write the correct formulas for all reactants and products. Never change the formulas of the substances.
Example: H₂ + O₂ → H₂O (unbalanced)

Step 2 — Count atoms of each element on both sides:
Left side: 2 H, 2 O  |  Right side: 2 H, 1 O. Oxygen is not balanced.

Step 3 — Balance one element at a time — start with the most complex molecule:
Start with the element that appears in fewest places. Place coefficients (whole numbers) in front of formulas — never change subscripts.
Add coefficient 2 before H₂O: H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
Now: Left: 2H, 2O  |  Right: 4H, 2O. Hydrogen is unbalanced.

Step 4 — Adjust other coefficients and recount:
Add coefficient 2 before H₂: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
Left: 4H, 2O  |  Right: 4H, 2O. ✓ Balanced!

Step 5 — Verify by counting all atoms on both sides:
Confirm every element has equal atoms on both sides. Check that coefficients are in the lowest whole-number ratio.

Step 6 — Write state symbols (optional):
Add (s), (l), (g), or (aq) after each formula if required.

Important rule: Only change coefficients (numbers in front). Never change subscripts inside a chemical formula.

Q5. Name the branches of chemistry and discuss any five branches.

Main Branches of Chemistry:
Organic, Inorganic, Physical, Analytical, Biochemistry, Industrial, Nuclear, Environmental, Medicinal, Electrochemistry, Geochemistry, Thermochemistry.

Five Branches in Detail:

1. Organic Chemistry:
Deals with the study of compounds containing carbon (C), particularly hydrocarbons and their derivatives. Most drugs, fuels, plastics, dyes, and food molecules are organic. Examples of organic compounds: methane (CH₄), ethanol (C₂H₅OH), glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆), benzene (C₆H₆). Exceptions: CO, CO₂, and carbonates are inorganic despite having carbon.

2. Inorganic Chemistry:
Studies all compounds that do not primarily contain carbon — metals, minerals, salts, acids, bases, and gases. Examples: NaCl (table salt), HCl (hydrochloric acid), NaOH (caustic soda), Fe₂O₃ (rust), NH₃ (ammonia). Inorganic chemistry is important in ceramics, semiconductors, catalysis, and materials science.

3. Physical Chemistry:
Applies the principles of physics (energy, thermodynamics, quantum mechanics) to chemistry. It studies how and why chemical reactions occur — energy changes, reaction rates (kinetics), equilibrium, bonding, and spectroscopy. Useful in designing industrial processes and understanding atomic structure.

4. Analytical Chemistry:
Concerned with identifying substances (qualitative analysis) and measuring their amounts (quantitative analysis). Techniques include titration, chromatography, spectroscopy, and mass spectrometry. Applications: drug testing, environmental monitoring, quality control in food and industry, forensic science.

5. Biochemistry:
Studies the chemistry of living organisms — the structure and function of biological molecules such as proteins, carbohydrates, fats, DNA, RNA, enzymes, and vitamins. It explains how food is digested, how genes work, how muscles contract, and how medicines act. It bridges chemistry and biology.

Q4. How many moles are present in 40 gm of H₂SO₄?

Given: Mass (m) = 40 g, Formula = H₂SO₄

Step 1 — Find molar mass of H₂SO₄:
M = 2(1) + 32 + 4(16) = 2 + 32 + 64 = 98 g/mol

Step 2 — Calculate moles:
n = m ÷ M = 40 ÷ 98

n = 0.408 mol ≈ 0.41 mol

Q5. Calculate the number of moles and number of molecules present in: (a) 16 g of H₂CO₃   (b) 20 g of C₆H₁₂O₆

Avogadro's number Nₐ = 6.022 × 10²³

(a) 16 g of H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid):

Molar mass of H₂CO₃ = 2(1) + 12 + 3(16) = 2 + 12 + 48 = 62 g/mol

Number of moles: n = m ÷ M = 16 ÷ 62 = 0.258 mol

Number of molecules: N = n × Nₐ = 0.258 × 6.022 × 10²³ = 1.55 × 10²³ molecules

(b) 20 g of C₆H₁₂O₆ (glucose):

Molar mass of C₆H₁₂O₆ = 6(12) + 12(1) + 6(16) = 72 + 12 + 96 = 180 g/mol

Number of moles: n = m ÷ M = 20 ÷ 180 = 0.111 mol

Number of molecules: N = n × Nₐ = 0.111 × 6.022 × 10²³ = 6.69 × 10²² molecules
