What is Chemistry?

Chemistry is the science of matter — everything that has mass and takes up space. It is one of the most important and practical sciences, directly connected to medicine, food, materials, energy, and the environment.

1.1 Definition of Chemistry

The word Chemistry is believed to have originated from the Arabic word al-kimiya (الكيمياء), which itself may have come from the ancient Egyptian word kmt meaning "black earth" — referring to the fertile soil of the Nile valley, where early chemical arts were practised.

Chemistry
The branch of science that deals with the composition, structure, properties, and changes of matter. It studies what substances are made of, how they interact with each other, and what happens during chemical reactions.

Chemistry investigates matter at the level of atoms and molecules. Every substance you encounter — water, salt, plastic, medicine, food — is made of chemical substances that chemists study and work with.

Chemistry is Central

Chemistry is often called the central science because it connects and overlaps with physics (on one side) and biology (on the other). Understanding chemistry is essential for medicine, engineering, materials science, and environmental science.

1.2 Brief History of Chemistry

Chemistry has a long history, evolving from ancient practices to a modern science:

Ancient Times (before 500 BC)
Humans used chemistry practically — making fire, smelting metals (bronze, iron), making pottery, fermenting food and drink, and extracting dyes and medicines from plants.
Alchemy (500 BC – 1600 AD)
Alchemists tried to convert common metals into gold and find the "elixir of life." Though their goals were mistaken, they developed many laboratory techniques and discovered new substances. Muslim scholars like Jabir ibn Hayyan (Geber) greatly advanced chemical knowledge.
Jabir ibn Hayyan (721–815 AD)
Known as the Father of Chemistry. He introduced experimental methods into chemistry, discovered many acids (hydrochloric acid, sulphuric acid, nitric acid), and described distillation, crystallisation, and calcination.
Antoine Lavoisier (1743–1794)
Known as the Father of Modern Chemistry. He established the law of conservation of mass, discovered that water is made of hydrogen and oxygen, and helped create the modern system of naming chemical compounds.
John Dalton (1766–1844)
Proposed the first modern atomic theory — that all matter is made of tiny, indivisible particles called atoms, and each element has atoms of a specific mass.
Dmitri Mendeleev (1834–1907)
Created the first periodic table of elements (1869), arranging elements by increasing atomic mass and grouping them by similar properties.
Did You Know?

Jabir ibn Hayyan, writing in Arabic in 8th-century Baghdad, described over 700 chemical processes and invented laboratory apparatus including the alembic (used for distillation) that are still referenced today.

1.3 Importance of Chemistry

Chemistry is not just a school subject — it affects nearly every aspect of modern life:

Medicine and Health

  • All medicines (tablets, injections, syrups) are chemical compounds — aspirin, antibiotics, paracetamol
  • Vaccines are developed using chemical and biochemical knowledge
  • Medical tests (blood, urine) are chemical analyses
  • Anaesthetics used in surgery are chemical substances

Food and Agriculture

  • Fertilisers (urea, ammonium nitrate) are chemical compounds that increase crop yield
  • Pesticides and herbicides protect crops from pests and weeds
  • Food preservation uses chemical additives and processes (salting, pickling, canning)
  • Understanding nutrition requires knowledge of biochemistry

Industry and Materials

  • Plastics, rubber, glass, ceramics — all manufactured using chemical processes
  • Fuels (petrol, diesel, natural gas) are chemical substances refined from crude oil
  • Metals are extracted from ores using chemical reduction reactions
  • Synthetic fibres (nylon, polyester) are produced by chemical reactions

Environment

  • Understanding acid rain, ozone depletion, and climate change requires chemistry
  • Water treatment (purification, chlorination) is a chemical process
  • Developing biodegradable plastics and clean energy sources relies on chemistry

1.4 Branches of Chemistry

Because chemistry covers such a vast range of substances and reactions, it is divided into specialised branches:

Organic Chemistry
The study of compounds containing carbon (except simple ones like CO₂ and carbonates). Organic compounds include fuels, plastics, medicines, proteins, fats, and sugars. The word "organic" originally meant "from living things."
Inorganic Chemistry
The study of all compounds that do not mainly contain carbon. Covers metals, minerals, salts, acids, bases, and compounds like water (H₂O), ammonia (NH₃), and sodium chloride (NaCl).
Physical Chemistry
Studies the physical principles behind chemical reactions — energy changes, reaction rates, equilibrium, thermodynamics, and quantum mechanics. It applies physics to chemistry.
Analytical Chemistry
Deals with identifying and measuring the composition of substances — what is present (qualitative analysis) and how much is present (quantitative analysis). Used in quality control, forensics, and medicine.
Biochemistry
The study of chemical processes occurring in living organisms — metabolism, DNA replication, enzyme action, protein synthesis. The bridge between chemistry and biology.
Industrial Chemistry
The application of chemistry to the large-scale manufacture of chemical products — sulphuric acid, fertilisers, plastics, pharmaceuticals, and fuels.
Nuclear Chemistry
Studies changes in atomic nuclei — radioactivity, nuclear fission, nuclear fusion, and radioactive decay. Used in nuclear power and medical imaging (PET scans, radiotherapy).
Environmental Chemistry
Studies the chemical processes in the environment — air, water, and soil chemistry, pollution, and its effects on ecosystems and human health.
Most Important Branches to Know

For Class 9, focus on: Organic, Inorganic, Physical, Analytical, Biochemistry, Industrial, Nuclear, and Environmental Chemistry. These are the eight main branches mentioned in the STB textbook.
