Chemical Species

Chemistry studies matter at the level of its smallest particles. These particles — atoms, molecules, ions, and others — are collectively called chemical species. Understanding them is essential for all of chemistry.

3.1 Atom

Atom
The smallest particle of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element. An atom can take part in chemical reactions but normally cannot be broken down further by ordinary chemical means.

Key facts about atoms:

  • Atoms are extremely small — the diameter of a hydrogen atom is about 0.1 nanometres (10⁻¹⁰ m)
  • Atoms consist of a nucleus (containing protons and neutrons) surrounded by electrons in shells
  • Each element has a unique number of protons — called the atomic number
  • Some atoms exist as single particles (e.g., noble gases like He, Ne, Ar are monatomic)
  • Most atoms bond with other atoms to form molecules or ionic compounds

Examples: A carbon atom (C), a hydrogen atom (H), a sodium atom (Na)

Historical Note

John Dalton proposed in 1803 that atoms are indivisible. We now know they ARE divisible into protons, neutrons, and electrons — but in chemistry, we treat atoms as the fundamental building blocks because chemical reactions involve the rearrangement of atoms, not the splitting of nuclei.

3.2 Molecule

Molecule
The smallest particle of an element or compound that can exist independently and still retain all the chemical properties of that substance. A molecule is formed when two or more atoms are joined together by covalent bonds.

Types of Molecules

Homoatomic Molecules (Molecules of Elements)

Molecules made of atoms of the same element:

  • Diatomic: H₂ (hydrogen), O₂ (oxygen), N₂ (nitrogen), Cl₂ (chlorine), Br₂ (bromine), I₂ (iodine), F₂ (fluorine) — these 7 elements always exist as diatomic molecules in nature
  • Triatomic: O₃ (ozone) — 3 oxygen atoms
  • Polyatomic: P₄ (phosphorus), S₈ (sulfur)

Heteroatomic Molecules (Molecules of Compounds)

Molecules made of atoms of different elements:

  • H₂O (water) — 2 hydrogen + 1 oxygen
  • CO₂ (carbon dioxide) — 1 carbon + 2 oxygen
  • NH₃ (ammonia) — 1 nitrogen + 3 hydrogen
  • HCl (hydrogen chloride) — 1 hydrogen + 1 chlorine
  • CH₄ (methane) — 1 carbon + 4 hydrogen
The Seven Diatomic Elements

Seven elements always exist as diatomic (2-atom) molecules under normal conditions: H₂, N₂, O₂, F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂. A helpful memory trick: Have No Fear Of Ice Cold Beer (H, N, F, O, I, Cl, Br).

3.3 Ion

Ion
An atom or group of atoms that has gained or lost one or more electrons, and therefore carries an electric charge. Ions form when electrons are transferred between atoms during chemical reactions.

Types of Ions

Cation (positive ion)
Formed when an atom loses one or more electrons. It has MORE protons than electrons → positive charge. Metals typically form cations.
Examples: Na⁺ (sodium loses 1e⁻), Mg²⁺ (magnesium loses 2e⁻), Al³⁺ (aluminium loses 3e⁻), Ca²⁺, K⁺, Fe²⁺, Fe³⁺
Anion (negative ion)
Formed when an atom gains one or more electrons. It has MORE electrons than protons → negative charge. Non-metals typically form anions.
Examples: Cl⁻ (chlorine gains 1e⁻), O²⁻ (oxygen gains 2e⁻), N³⁻ (nitrogen gains 3e⁻), S²⁻, F⁻, Br⁻
Polyatomic Ion
A group of atoms bonded together that carries a net electric charge. They act as a single unit in ionic compounds.
Examples: SO₄²⁻ (sulphate), NO₃⁻ (nitrate), OH⁻ (hydroxide), CO₃²⁻ (carbonate), NH₄⁺ (ammonium), PO₄³⁻ (phosphate)
Important Rule

When a neutral atom loses electrons, the remaining particle has more protons than electrons → net positive charge → cation.
When a neutral atom gains electrons, it has more electrons than protons → net negative charge → anion.

3.4 Free Radical

Free Radical
An atom or molecule that has an unpaired electron in its outermost shell. This makes it extremely reactive because it tries to pair up the lone electron by reacting with other atoms or molecules.

Free radicals are represented with a dot (·) to show the unpaired electron:

  • Cl· (chlorine free radical) — formed when Cl₂ is split by UV light
  • OH· (hydroxyl free radical) — formed in the body and in atmospheric chemistry
  • CH₃· (methyl radical) — important in organic chemistry reactions
  • H· (hydrogen radical)

Why free radicals matter:

  • They start chain reactions in many important processes (combustion, polymerisation)
  • In the body, free radicals damage cells and DNA — this is why antioxidants (Vitamins C and E) are important in diet
  • They play a role in the breakdown of the ozone layer

3.5 Formula Unit

Formula Unit
The simplest ratio of ions in an ionic compound. Ionic compounds do not exist as individual molecules — instead, they form giant lattice structures. The "formula unit" represents the smallest repeating unit of that lattice.

Ionic compounds are made of cations and anions held together by electrostatic attraction (opposite charges attract). They do not form discrete molecules.

Examples of formula units:

  • NaCl — one Na⁺ and one Cl⁻ (sodium chloride / table salt)
  • MgCl₂ — one Mg²⁺ and two Cl⁻ (magnesium chloride)
  • CaCO₃ — one Ca²⁺ and one CO₃²⁻ (calcium carbonate)
  • Al₂O₃ — two Al³⁺ and three O²⁻ (aluminium oxide)
Molecule vs Formula Unit

We use the term molecule for covalent compounds (e.g., water, CO₂, glucose). We use the term formula unit for ionic compounds (e.g., NaCl, MgO) because ionic compounds exist as giant lattices of ions, not as individual molecules.

3.6 Summary of Chemical Species

Atom
Smallest particle of an element that retains its chemical properties. May or may not exist freely.
Molecule
Two or more atoms joined by covalent bonds. Smallest independently existing particle of a covalent element or compound.
Cation
Atom or group that has LOST electrons → positive charge. Example: Na⁺, Ca²⁺, NH₄⁺
Anion
Atom or group that has GAINED electrons → negative charge. Example: Cl⁻, O²⁻, SO₄²⁻
Free Radical
Atom or molecule with an unpaired electron → extremely reactive. Example: Cl·, OH·
Formula Unit
Simplest ratio of ions in an ionic compound. Not a molecule — represents the repeating unit of an ionic lattice. Example: NaCl, MgCl₂
