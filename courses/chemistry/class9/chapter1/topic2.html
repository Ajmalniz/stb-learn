Matter and Its Classification

Everything around us — air, water, rocks, living things — is made of matter. Understanding how matter is classified is the foundation of all chemistry.

2.1 What is Matter?

Matter
Anything that has mass and occupies space (volume). Everything you can touch, see, smell, or weigh is matter. Air, water, rock, wood, plastic — all are matter.

Matter is made up of tiny particles called atoms. The way these atoms are arranged and bonded together determines whether a substance is an element, a compound, or a mixture.

What is NOT Matter?

Light, heat, sound, and electricity are not matter — they have no mass and do not occupy space. They are forms of energy.

2.3 Elements

Element
A pure substance that is made of only one type of atom and cannot be broken down into a simpler substance by any ordinary chemical or physical means.

There are 118 elements known to science — 94 occur naturally and the rest are artificially made. Every element has a unique chemical symbol (one or two letters).

Examples of elements:

  • Iron (Fe) — a metal used in construction
  • Oxygen (O) — a non-metal gas essential for respiration
  • Gold (Au) — a precious metal
  • Carbon (C) — exists as diamond, graphite, and charcoal
  • Hydrogen (H) — the lightest element; component of water
  • Sulfur (S) — a yellow non-metal used in making sulphuric acid

Elements are organised in the Periodic Table, which you will study in detail in Chapter 3.

2.4 Compounds

Compound
A pure substance made of two or more different elements chemically combined together in a fixed ratio by mass. A compound has entirely different properties from the elements that form it and can only be separated by chemical means.

Examples of compounds:

  • Water (H₂O) — hydrogen and oxygen in ratio 1:8 by mass. Water is liquid; hydrogen and oxygen are gases.
  • Salt (NaCl) — sodium (a reactive metal) + chlorine (a poisonous gas) → safe table salt
  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂) — carbon + oxygen; produced in respiration and combustion
  • Ammonia (NH₃) — nitrogen + hydrogen; used to make fertilisers
  • Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) — carbon, hydrogen, oxygen; the main energy molecule in cells

Key Differences: Element vs Compound

Element
Made of ONE type of atom only. Cannot be broken down further by chemical means. Example: O₂ (oxygen gas = 2 oxygen atoms)
Compound
Made of TWO or MORE different types of atoms chemically bonded. CAN be broken down by chemical reactions. Example: H₂O (water = 2 hydrogen + 1 oxygen)

2.5 Mixtures

Mixture
A combination of two or more substances (elements or compounds) that are physically combined, NOT chemically bonded. The components retain their own properties and can be separated by physical methods.

There are two types of mixtures:

Homogeneous Mixture (Solution)

A mixture in which the components are uniformly distributed throughout — it has the same composition throughout. You cannot see the individual components.

  • Salt dissolved in water (saltwater)
  • Sugar dissolved in water (sugar solution)
  • Air (a mixture of N₂, O₂, CO₂, and other gases)
  • Alloys: brass (copper + zinc), steel (iron + carbon)

Heterogeneous Mixture

A mixture in which the components are not uniformly distributed — you can see distinct parts with different compositions in different regions.

  • Sand and water (sand settles at the bottom)
  • Oil and water (they form separate layers)
  • Salad (you can see individual vegetables)
  • Granite rock (you can see different mineral grains)

Compound vs Mixture — Key Differences

Compound
Components combined CHEMICALLY in a FIXED ratio. Has NEW properties different from its components. Can only be separated by CHEMICAL methods. Example: Water (H₂O).
Mixture
Components combined PHYSICALLY in ANY ratio. Components RETAIN their own properties. Can be separated by PHYSICAL methods (filtration, distillation, evaporation). Example: Saltwater.

2.6 Physical and Chemical Changes

When matter changes, it undergoes either a physical change or a chemical change. Understanding the difference is fundamental in chemistry.

Physical Change

Physical Change
A change in which the physical form or appearance of a substance changes, but its chemical composition remains the same. No new substance is formed. The change is usually reversible.

Examples of physical changes:

  • Melting of ice → water (H₂O remains H₂O — only state changes)
  • Boiling of water → steam (still H₂O)
  • Cutting paper (smaller pieces, but still paper)
  • Dissolving sugar in water (sugar can be recovered by evaporation)
  • Stretching a rubber band
  • Breaking glass (smaller pieces, but still glass)

Chemical Change

Chemical Change
A change in which new substances with new properties are formed due to the breaking and forming of chemical bonds. The change is usually irreversible.

Examples of chemical changes:

  • Burning wood → ash, CO₂, water vapour (wood is destroyed, new substances form)
  • Rusting of iron → iron oxide Fe₂O₃ (iron becomes rust — a completely different substance)
  • Cooking an egg → proteins are permanently changed (cannot be uncooked)
  • Digestion of food → complex molecules broken into simpler ones
  • Burning of magnesium → bright white flame, white ash (magnesium oxide)
  • Souring of milk → lactic acid is produced

Signs That a Chemical Change Has Occurred

  • Production of gas (bubbles)
  • Formation of a precipitate (solid in a liquid)
  • Change in colour
  • Energy change — heat, light, or sound produced or absorbed
  • Change in smell
  • The change is not easily reversible
Key Distinction

In a physical change, the substance is the same before and after — only its form changes. In a chemical change, entirely new substances with new properties are formed. The original substance no longer exists.
