Mole Concept

Atoms and molecules are far too small to count individually. Chemists use a special counting unit called the mole — just like a dozen means 12, a mole means 6.022 × 10²³. This concept connects the atomic scale to the laboratory scale.

5.1 Relative Atomic Mass

Relative Atomic Mass (Aᵣ)
The average mass of one atom of an element compared to 1/12 of the mass of one carbon-12 atom. It is a ratio, so it has no units. Also called atomic weight.

The formula for relative atomic mass:

Aᵣ = (Mass of one atom of element) / (1/12 × mass of one C-12 atom)

Carbon-12 was chosen as the standard reference because it is the most abundant isotope of carbon and can be measured precisely.

Relative atomic masses of common elements (from periodic table):

  • H = 1  |  He = 4  |  C = 12  |  N = 14  |  O = 16  |  Na = 23
  • Mg = 24  |  Al = 27  |  S = 32  |  Cl = 35.5  |  K = 39  |  Ca = 40
  • Fe = 56  |  Cu = 64  |  Zn = 65  |  Ag = 108  |  Pb = 207
Why not Hydrogen = 1 as the standard?

Historically, hydrogen (mass = 1) was the first standard. Carbon-12 replaced it because it gives more precise measurements and the atomic mass of C-12 is exactly 12 (not an approximation).

5.2 Avogadro's Number

Avogadro's Number (Nₐ)
The number of particles (atoms, molecules, or ions) in one mole of any substance. Its value is 6.022 × 10²³. Named after Italian scientist Amedeo Avogadro (1776–1856).

Nₐ = 6.022 × 10²³ particles/mol

This is an astronomically large number. To put it in perspective:

  • 6.022 × 10²³ grains of sand would cover the entire surface of Earth to a depth of several metres
  • If you counted at 1 billion per second, it would take 19 million years to count one mole
  • One mole of water molecules = 6.022 × 10²³ water molecules = 18 grams of water = about 18 mL
Avogadro's Number — Why This Value?

Avogadro's number is defined so that the mass of one mole of any element in grams equals its relative atomic mass. For example, the relative atomic mass of carbon is 12, so 1 mole of carbon atoms weighs exactly 12 grams and contains 6.022 × 10²³ atoms.

5.3 The Mole

Mole (mol)
The SI unit of amount of substance. One mole is the amount of a substance that contains 6.022 × 10²³ particles (atoms, molecules, ions, or formula units) — the same as Avogadro's number.

Think of the mole as a chemist's counting unit, just like:

  • A dozen = 12 items
  • A gross = 144 items
  • A mole = 6.022 × 10²³ items

Mole of Atoms

1 mole of carbon atoms = 6.022 × 10²³ atoms of carbon = 12 g
1 mole of hydrogen atoms = 6.022 × 10²³ atoms of hydrogen = 1 g
1 mole of oxygen atoms = 6.022 × 10²³ atoms of oxygen = 16 g

Mole of Molecules

1 mole of H₂O molecules = 6.022 × 10²³ molecules of water = 18 g
1 mole of CO₂ molecules = 6.022 × 10²³ molecules of CO₂ = 44 g
1 mole of O₂ molecules = 6.022 × 10²³ molecules of O₂ = 32 g

5.4 Molar Mass

Molar Mass (M)
The mass of one mole of a substance expressed in grams per mole (g/mol). Numerically equal to the relative atomic mass or molecular mass of the substance — but with the unit g/mol.

The molar mass is the bridge between the microscopic (atomic) scale and the macroscopic (laboratory) scale.

Examples of molar masses:

  • Molar mass of H = 1 g/mol
  • Molar mass of O = 16 g/mol
  • Molar mass of H₂O = 18 g/mol
  • Molar mass of NaCl = 58.5 g/mol
  • Molar mass of CO₂ = 44 g/mol
  • Molar mass of H₂SO₄ = 98 g/mol
  • Molar mass of CaCO₃ = 100 g/mol

5.5 Mole Calculations

The three key relationships in mole calculations:

Number of Moles (n)
↕
Mass (m)
n = m ÷ M
No. of Particles (N)
n = N ÷ Nₐ
Moles from mass
n = m ÷ M
n = moles, m = mass in grams, M = molar mass in g/mol
Mass from moles
m = n × M
Moles from number of particles
n = N ÷ Nₐ
N = actual number of particles, Nₐ = 6.022 × 10²³
Number of particles from moles
N = n × Nₐ

Worked Examples

Example 1: How many moles are in 36 g of water (H₂O)?

Molar mass of H₂O = (2 × 1) + 16 = 18 g/mol

n = m ÷ M = 36 ÷ 18 = 2 moles

Example 2: What is the mass of 3 moles of CO₂?

Molar mass of CO₂ = 12 + (2 × 16) = 44 g/mol

m = n × M = 3 × 44 = 132 g

Example 3: How many molecules are in 2 moles of H₂O?

N = n × Nₐ = 2 × 6.022 × 10²³ = 1.204 × 10²⁴ molecules

Example 4: How many moles is 3.011 × 10²³ atoms of carbon?

n = N ÷ Nₐ = (3.011 × 10²³) ÷ (6.022 × 10²³) = 0.5 mol

Example 5: What is the mass of 0.5 moles of NaCl?

Molar mass of NaCl = 23 + 35.5 = 58.5 g/mol

m = n × M = 0.5 × 58.5 = 29.25 g

5.6 Summary of Mole Concept

Relative Atomic Mass (Aᵣ)
Average mass of an atom relative to 1/12 of C-12 mass. No units. Found on the periodic table.
Avogadro's Number (Nₐ)
6.022 × 10²³ particles per mole. The number of particles in one mole of any substance.
Mole (mol)
Amount of substance containing 6.022 × 10²³ particles. The SI unit of amount.
Molar Mass (M)
Mass of one mole of substance in g/mol. Numerically equal to the relative atomic/molecular mass.
Key Formula
n = m/M    |    m = n × M    |    N = n × Nₐ    |    n = N/Nₐ
