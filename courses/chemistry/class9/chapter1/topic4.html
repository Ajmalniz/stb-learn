Chemical Formulas and Masses

A chemical formula is the language of chemistry — it tells us exactly which atoms are present in a substance and in what ratio. Understanding formulas and calculating masses from them are fundamental skills for all chemistry calculations.

4.1 Chemical Formula

Chemical Formula
A representation using chemical symbols and subscript numbers that shows which elements are present in a compound and the number of atoms of each element in one unit (molecule or formula unit) of that compound.

In a chemical formula:

  • Each element is represented by its chemical symbol (e.g., H, O, C, Na, Cl)
  • Subscript numbers (written below and to the right of the symbol) show how many atoms of that element are present
  • If there is only 1 atom of an element, no subscript is written

Reading chemical formulas:

  • H₂O — 2 hydrogen atoms + 1 oxygen atom
  • CO₂ — 1 carbon atom + 2 oxygen atoms
  • H₂SO₄ — 2 hydrogen + 1 sulfur + 4 oxygen atoms
  • Ca(OH)₂ — 1 calcium + 2 oxygen + 2 hydrogen atoms (brackets mean the group OH repeats twice)
  • Al₂(SO₄)₃ — 2 aluminium + 3 sulfur + 12 oxygen atoms

4.2 Empirical Formula

Empirical Formula
The simplest whole-number ratio of atoms of each element in a compound. It does not necessarily show the actual number of atoms in a molecule — only the smallest possible ratio.

The empirical formula is derived by finding the ratio of atoms and reducing it to the lowest whole-number ratio.

Examples

Glucose: C₆H₁₂O₆
Ratio of C:H:O = 6:12:6 → divide all by 6 → 1:2:1 → Empirical formula = CH₂O
Hydrogen peroxide: H₂O₂
Ratio of H:O = 2:2 → divide by 2 → 1:1 → Empirical formula = HO
Ethane: C₂H₆
Ratio of C:H = 2:6 → divide by 2 → 1:3 → Empirical formula = CH₃
Water: H₂O
Ratio of H:O = 2:1 → already in simplest form → Empirical formula = H₂O
Benzene: C₆H₆
Ratio of C:H = 6:6 → divide by 6 → 1:1 → Empirical formula = CH

4.3 Molecular Formula

Molecular Formula
The formula showing the actual number of each type of atom in one molecule of a covalent compound. It may be the same as the empirical formula, or a whole-number multiple of it.

The molecular formula = empirical formula × n, where n is a whole number (1, 2, 3...)

Comparison: Empirical vs Molecular Formula

Glucose
Empirical formula: CH₂O  |  Molecular formula: C₆H₁₂O₆  |  n = 6
Hydrogen peroxide
Empirical formula: HO  |  Molecular formula: H₂O₂  |  n = 2
Ethane
Empirical formula: CH₃  |  Molecular formula: C₂H₆  |  n = 2
Water
Empirical formula: H₂O  |  Molecular formula: H₂O  |  n = 1 (same)
Benzene
Empirical formula: CH  |  Molecular formula: C₆H₆  |  n = 6
Key Difference

The empirical formula shows the SIMPLEST RATIO. The molecular formula shows the ACTUAL NUMBER of atoms. The molecular formula is always a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula.

4.4 Atomic Mass

Atomic Mass
The mass of one atom of an element expressed in atomic mass units (amu). It is approximately equal to the number of protons + neutrons in the nucleus (the mass number). Listed on the periodic table.
Atomic Mass Unit (amu)
A very small unit of mass defined as 1/12 of the mass of one carbon-12 atom. 1 amu = 1.66 × 10⁻²⁷ kg. Used to express the masses of atoms and molecules.

Atomic masses of common elements:

  • H = 1 amu  |  C = 12 amu  |  N = 14 amu  |  O = 16 amu
  • Na = 23 amu  |  Mg = 24 amu  |  Al = 27 amu  |  S = 32 amu
  • Cl = 35.5 amu  |  Ca = 40 amu  |  Fe = 56 amu  |  Cu = 64 amu

4.5 Formula Mass

Formula Mass
The sum of the atomic masses of all atoms present in one formula unit of an ionic compound. Used for ionic compounds (which don't have molecules). Also called formula weight.

How to Calculate Formula Mass

Add up the atomic masses of all atoms in the formula, multiplying each by the number of times it appears.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Calculate the formula mass of NaCl (sodium chloride)

NaCl contains: 1 Na atom + 1 Cl atom

Atomic mass of Na = 23 amu
Atomic mass of Cl = 35.5 amu

Formula mass of NaCl = 23 + 35.5 = 58.5 amu

Example 2: Calculate the formula mass of CaCO₃ (calcium carbonate)

CaCO₃ contains: 1 Ca + 1 C + 3 O

Ca = 40, C = 12, O = 16

Formula mass = 40 + 12 + (3 × 16) = 40 + 12 + 48 = 100 amu

Example 3: Calculate the formula mass of Al₂(SO₄)₃ (aluminium sulphate)

Al₂(SO₄)₃ contains: 2 Al + 3 S + 12 O

Al = 27, S = 32, O = 16

Formula mass = (2 × 27) + (3 × 32) + (12 × 16)
= 54 + 96 + 192 = 342 amu

4.6 Molecular Mass

Molecular Mass
The sum of the atomic masses of all atoms in one molecule of a covalent compound. Used for molecular (covalent) substances. Also expressed in amu.

Molecular mass is calculated the same way as formula mass — just applied to covalent molecules.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Molecular mass of H₂O (water)

H₂O contains: 2 H + 1 O

H = 1, O = 16

Molecular mass = (2 × 1) + 16 = 2 + 16 = 18 amu

Example 2: Molecular mass of H₂SO₄ (sulphuric acid)

H₂SO₄ contains: 2 H + 1 S + 4 O

H = 1, S = 32, O = 16

Molecular mass = (2 × 1) + 32 + (4 × 16) = 2 + 32 + 64 = 98 amu

Example 3: Molecular mass of glucose C₆H₁₂O₆

C₆H₁₂O₆ contains: 6 C + 12 H + 6 O

C = 12, H = 1, O = 16

Molecular mass = (6 × 12) + (12 × 1) + (6 × 16) = 72 + 12 + 96 = 180 amu

Formula Mass vs Molecular Mass

Formula mass is used for ionic compounds (e.g., NaCl, CaCO₃) because they don't form discrete molecules.
Molecular mass is used for covalent (molecular) compounds (e.g., H₂O, CO₂, glucose).
The calculation method is the same for both.
