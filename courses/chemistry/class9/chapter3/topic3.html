Chapter 3 · Topic 3

Atomic Size

Atomic size is one of the most important periodic trends. It tells us how big or small an atom is — and understanding how it changes across periods and down groups helps explain much of chemistry, from bond lengths to reactivity.

3.1 What Is Atomic Radius?

Atomic Radius
The distance from the nucleus to the outermost electron shell. Since electron clouds have no definite boundary, atomic radius is typically defined as half the distance between two identical bonded atoms (covalent radius) or neighbouring atoms in a metal crystal (metallic radius). Measured in picometres (pm) where 1 pm = 10⁻¹² m.
Why Can't We Measure Atomic Radius Directly?

Electrons do not have a sharp outer boundary — they exist as a probability cloud. So we always measure radius indirectly by looking at the distance between two nuclei in a bond or crystal, then halving it.

3.2 Trend Across a Period (Left → Right)

Atomic radius decreases across a period from left to right.

Why? As you move across a period:

  • The number of protons increases (nuclear charge increases)
  • The electrons are added to the same shell (same energy level)
  • The increased nuclear pull pulls the electrons closer to the nucleus
  • Result: the atom gets smaller

Key Rule — Period Trend

Across a period (same number of shells): More protons → stronger pull → smaller atom.
Na (186 pm) → Cl (99 pm) — nearly half the size in just 6 steps!

3.3 Trend Down a Group (Top → Bottom)

Atomic radius increases down a group.

Why? As you move down a group:

  • Each new period adds an extra electron shell
  • Outermost electrons are farther from the nucleus
  • Inner shells shield the outer electrons from the nuclear pull
  • Result: the atom gets larger

3.4 Ionic Radius

Ionic Radius
The size of an ion — an atom that has gained or lost electrons to form a charged particle.

Cations (atoms that lose electrons) are SMALLER than the parent atom:

  • Na → Na⁺: loses 1 electron, 11 protons now pull on only 10 electrons → radius decreases (Na: 186 pm → Na⁺: 102 pm)
  • Mg → Mg²⁺: loses 2 electrons → much smaller

Anions (atoms that gain electrons) are LARGER than the parent atom:

  • Cl → Cl⁻: gains 1 electron, 17 protons now pull on 18 electrons → weaker pull per electron → radius increases (Cl: 99 pm → Cl⁻: 181 pm)
  • O → O²⁻: gains 2 electrons → much larger
Memory Trick

Cation = Cat — a cat is small and wiry (loses electrons, gets smaller)
Anion = Onion — an onion is fat and round (gains electrons, gets bigger)

3.5 Why Atomic Size Matters

  • Bond length: Larger atoms form longer bonds
  • Reactivity: Larger alkali metals (Cs > K > Na > Li) lose electrons more easily → more reactive
  • Ionization energy: Larger atoms have lower ionization energy (easier to remove outer electrons)
  • Electronegativity: Smaller atoms attract electrons more strongly
Did you know?

The largest neutral atom (by atomic radius) is caesium (Cs) at ~265 pm. The smallest is helium (He) at ~31 pm. Cs is over 8× bigger than He!
