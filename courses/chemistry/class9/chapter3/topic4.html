Chapter 3 · Topic 4

Ionization Energy, Electron Affinity & Shielding Effect

Three powerful periodic properties — ionization energy, electron affinity, and the shielding effect — explain why some atoms easily lose electrons, others easily gain them, and how inner electrons protect outer ones from the full nuclear charge.

4.1 Ionization Energy (IE)

Ionization Energy
The minimum energy required to remove the most loosely held electron from a neutral gaseous atom to form a positive ion (cation). Measured in kJ/mol.

X(g) → X⁺(g) + e⁻     Energy required = 1st Ionization Energy

Trend Across a Period (Left → Right)

Ionization energy increases across a period.

  • Nuclear charge increases → electrons held more tightly
  • More energy needed to remove an electron
  • Example: Na (IE = 496 kJ/mol) → Cl (IE = 1251 kJ/mol)

Trend Down a Group (Top → Bottom)

Ionization energy decreases down a group.

  • Atomic radius increases → outermost electrons are farther away
  • Shielding by inner electrons increases
  • Less energy needed to remove the outermost electron
  • Example: Li (IE = 520) → Na (IE = 496) → K (IE = 419 kJ/mol)
Element 1st IE (kJ/mol) Trend
Li520Low (1 valence e⁻, easy to remove)
Na496Lower (more shielding)
Mg738Higher (2+ nuclear charge in period 3)
Cl1251High (7 valence e⁻, nucleus pulls hard)
Ar1521Very high (full shell, very stable)
Successive Ionization Energies

Each successive electron removal requires more energy (IE₁ < IE₂ < IE₃…). There is a large jump in ionization energy when you start removing electrons from an inner shell — this jump reveals the group number of the element.

4.2 Electron Affinity (EA)

Electron Affinity
The energy released when a neutral gaseous atom gains one electron to form a negative ion (anion). A larger (more negative) value means the atom gains electrons more readily.

X(g) + e⁻ → X⁻(g) + Energy released = Electron Affinity

Trend Across a Period

Electron affinity generally increases (more negative) across a period.

  • Increased nuclear charge attracts the incoming electron more strongly
  • Halogens (Group 17) have the highest electron affinities — they need just one more electron to complete their shell
  • Noble gases have near-zero or positive EA — their shells are already full

Trend Down a Group

Electron affinity generally decreases (less negative) down a group.

  • Larger atoms: the incoming electron goes into a shell farther from the nucleus
  • Less attraction → less energy released
  • Exception: F has lower EA than Cl because F is so small that electron-electron repulsion in the tiny 2p orbital reduces the attraction

4.3 Shielding Effect (Screening Effect)

Shielding Effect
The reduction in effective nuclear charge experienced by outer electrons due to the repulsive force exerted by inner-shell electrons. Inner electrons act as a shield between the nucleus and the outermost electrons.
Effective Nuclear Charge (Zeff)
The net positive charge experienced by an outer electron after accounting for shielding by inner electrons.
Zeff = Actual nuclear charge (Z) − Shielding constant (σ)

How shielding works:

  • Inner electrons repel outer electrons → reduce the effective pull of the nucleus
  • More inner shells = more shielding = weaker hold on outer electrons
  • Going down a group: more shells added → shielding increases → outer electrons feel less pull → easier to remove → IE decreases, atomic size increases
  • Going across a period: electrons added to the same shell → no new shielding → Zeff increases → atoms shrink
Shielding vs. Nuclear Charge

These two forces compete. Going down a group, the increasing shielding effect wins — atoms get bigger and IE decreases. Going across a period, increased nuclear charge wins — atoms shrink and IE increases.

Did you know?

The shielding effect is why caesium (Cs) is so reactive — its outermost electron is in the 6th shell, heavily shielded by 54 inner electrons. The nuclear charge (55+) barely reaches it, so the electron is lost very easily.
