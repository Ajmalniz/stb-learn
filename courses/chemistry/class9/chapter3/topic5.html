Chapter 3 · Topic 5

Electronegativity

Electronegativity is one of the most useful concepts in all of chemistry. It tells us how strongly an atom attracts the shared electrons in a bond — and from that single number, we can predict whether a bond will be ionic, polar covalent, or non-polar covalent.

5.1 What Is Electronegativity?

Electronegativity
A measure of the tendency of an atom to attract the shared pair of electrons in a covalent bond toward itself. It is a relative, dimensionless quantity.

Electronegativity is not the same as electron affinity (which applies to isolated atoms). Electronegativity applies specifically to atoms within a bond.

Pauling Scale

The most widely used scale was developed by Linus Pauling. Fluorine — the most electronegative element — is assigned a value of 4.0. All other elements are scaled relative to fluorine. Francium (Fr) and caesium (Cs) have the lowest values (~0.7).

5.2 Electronegativity Trends

Across a Period (Left → Right)

Electronegativity increases across a period.

  • Nuclear charge increases → stronger pull on shared electrons
  • Atomic radius decreases → bonding electrons are held closer to the nucleus

Down a Group (Top → Bottom)

Electronegativity decreases down a group.

  • Atomic radius increases → bonding electrons are farther from the nucleus
  • Shielding effect increases → outer electrons feel less nuclear pull

5.3 Predicting Bond Type Using ΔEN

The difference in electronegativity (ΔEN) between two bonded atoms tells us the type of bond formed:

ΔEN Range Bond Type Example
0Non-polar covalentH₂, Cl₂, O₂
0.1 – 1.7Polar covalentHCl (ΔEN = 0.9), H₂O (ΔEN = 1.4)
> 1.7IonicNaCl (ΔEN = 2.1), NaF (ΔEN = 3.1)
How to calculate ΔEN

ΔEN = |EN of more electronegative atom − EN of less electronegative atom|
Example: NaCl → ΔEN = |3.0 − 0.9| = 2.1 → Ionic bond ✓

Did you know?

Fluorine (EN = 4.0) is so electronegative that it forms ionic bonds with almost every metal and even highly polar bonds with non-metals. The H–F bond in hydrofluoric acid is extremely polar, making HF a weak but very corrosive acid.

5.4 Importance of Electronegativity

  • Predicts bond polarity and bond type (ionic vs covalent)
  • Explains dipole moments in molecules (e.g., why water is polar)
  • Predicts hydrogen bonding — only N, O, and F are electronegative enough
  • Helps predict reactivity — highly electronegative elements are strong oxidizing agents
  • Explains acidic/basic character of oxides across a period
