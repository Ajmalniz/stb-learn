Chapter 8 · Topic 4

Non-metals & Halogens

While metals react by losing electrons, non-metals react by gaining electrons. The halogens (Group 17) are the most reactive non-metals — fluorine is so reactive it attacks almost everything it touches. This topic explains why, and how halogens compete with each other.

4.1 Electronegative Character of Non-metals

Electronegative Character
The tendency of an element to gain electrons and form negative ions (anions). Non-metals have high electronegative character — they attract electrons toward themselves.

The most electronegative element on the periodic table is Fluorine (F) — it is in the top-right corner of the periodic table (small atom, high nuclear charge, needs just one electron to complete its octet).

Trends in Electronegative Character

Across a period (left → right): nuclear charge increases, atomic size decreases → atoms pull electrons in more strongly → electronegative character increases.
Down a group: atomic size increases, outer shell is further away and more shielded → gained electron goes into a larger, less-attracted orbital → electronegative character decreases.

This is the opposite trend to metals: non-metal reactivity decreases going down a group (while metal reactivity increases going down).

4.2 The Halogens — Group 17

The halogens are: F (Fluorine), Cl (Chlorine), Br (Bromine), I (Iodine), At (Astatine). Each has 7 valence electrons — they need only one more to complete their octet, making them highly reactive.

Halogen Symbol State at room temp. Colour
FluorineF₂GasPale yellow
ChlorineCl₂GasGreenish-yellow
BromineBr₂LiquidReddish-brown
IodineI₂SolidGrey-black (violet vapour)

4.3 Reactivity Trend of Halogens

Reactivity of halogens decreases down the group: F > Cl > Br > I

Why does halogen reactivity decrease going down?

Going down the group, atomic size increases. The electron being gained goes into a larger, more shielded shell that is further from the nucleus → less energy is released when the electron is gained → the atom is less attracted to electrons → less reactive.

Fluorine is the most reactive non-metal — it reacts with almost every element, including some noble gases (Xe, Kr)!

4.4 Displacement Reactions of Halogens

Just like metals displace each other, a more reactive halogen can displace a less reactive halogen from a solution of its salt.

Cl₂ + 2KBr → 2KCl + Br₂   (Cl displaces Br — solution turns orange-brown)

Cl₂ + 2KI → 2KCl + I₂     (Cl displaces I — solution turns brown/violet)

Br₂ + 2KI → 2KBr + I₂     (Br displaces I)

Br₂ + 2KCl → No reaction    (Br CANNOT displace Cl — Cl is more reactive)

The rule is simple: a halogen higher in the group (more reactive) displaces one lower (less reactive). It cannot work the other way.

4.5 Reactions of Halogens with Hydrogen

All halogens react with hydrogen to form hydrogen halides (HF, HCl, HBr, HI) — but the vigour decreases down the group:

Reaction Conditions Vigour
H₂ + F₂ → 2HFEven in the dark, at –200°CExplosive
H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HClIn presence of lightVery fast
H₂ + Br₂ → 2HBrNeeds heat (~300°C)Moderate
H₂ + I₂ ⇌ 2HIHigh temp., catalyst, reversibleSlow, incomplete

4.6 Uses of Halogens

Chlorine (Cl₂)
Water purification (kills bacteria), bleach (NaOCl), manufacturing PVC plastic and pesticides
Bromine (Br)
Fire retardants in plastics, photographic film (AgBr sensitive to light)
Iodine (I₂)
Antiseptic solution (iodine tincture), iodized salt (prevents goitre — thyroid needs iodine)
Fluorine (F)
Toothpaste (sodium fluoride — hardens tooth enamel), Teflon (non-stick coating)
Chlorine saved millions of lives

Adding small amounts of chlorine to drinking water (chlorination) kills the bacteria that cause cholera, typhoid, and dysentery. Before water chlorination was introduced in the early 1900s, these diseases killed hundreds of thousands of people every year. This simple chemistry change is considered one of the greatest public health achievements in history.
