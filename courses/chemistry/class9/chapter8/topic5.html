Chapter 8 · Topic 5

Reactivity Compared & Applications

Now that you've studied metals and non-metals separately, let's see the big picture — how their reactivity trends compare, and how chemists and engineers use reactivity differences in the real world.

5.1 Comparing Reactivity Trends

Metals and non-metals follow opposite trends in reactivity:

Direction Metals Non-metals (Halogens)
Down a group ↓Reactivity increases (K > Na > Li)Reactivity decreases (F > Cl > Br > I)
Across a period →Reactivity decreases (Na > Mg > Al)Reactivity increases (C < N < O < F)
ReasonBigger atom = easier to lose electronSmaller atom = easier to gain electron
The key insight

Metals and non-metals react in opposite ways — metals give away electrons, non-metals take electrons. Whatever makes it easier to give electrons away increases metal reactivity; whatever makes it easier to grab electrons increases non-metal reactivity.

5.3 Practical Applications of Reactivity

1. The Thermite Reaction — Welding with Chemistry

Aluminium is more reactive than iron → Al can displace Fe from its oxide:

2Al + Fe₂O₃ → Al₂O₃ + 2Fe + enormous heat

This produces molten iron and so much heat that it is used to weld broken railway tracks in the field — no electricity or other heat source needed.

2. Metal Extraction from Ores

How a metal is extracted from its ore depends on where it sits in the reactivity series:

Reactivity Metals Extraction method
Very highK, Na, Mg, AlElectrolysis of molten ore (too reactive for chemical reduction)
ModerateZn, Fe, PbReduction with carbon (coke) in blast furnace
LowCu, AgHeating the ore alone or simple reduction
Very lowAu, PtFound free (native) in nature — no extraction needed

3. Sacrificial Protection (Cathodic Protection)

Attach a more reactive metal (Mg or Zn) to an iron structure. The more reactive metal acts as the anode and corrodes instead of the iron — protecting it. This is called sacrificial protection because the reactive metal "sacrifices" itself.

  • Ship hulls: blocks of Mg or Zn bolted to the steel hull.
  • Underground pipelines: Mg or Zn rods buried alongside iron pipes.
  • Galvanising: coating iron with a layer of zinc (Zn layer corrodes first, protecting the Fe underneath).

4. Reactivity and Safety in Daily Life

  • Alkali metals must be handled very carefully and stored in oil — water contact can cause explosions.
  • Fluorine is so reactive it must be handled with special equipment; it attacks glass, rubber and most metals.
  • Aluminium cans don't rust: Al reacts with air to form a thin, hard Al₂O₃ layer that seals the surface and prevents further corrosion.
  • Gold phones: the gold used in circuit board connectors never corrodes, ensuring electrical contact for decades.
  • Iron fences rust because Fe is high enough in the reactivity series to oxidise in moist air.
Reactivity shapes the world around us

Every bridge, every battery, every water pipe, every surgical instrument — the choice of material is driven by reactivity. Engineers pick metals that are reactive enough to form strong bonds in alloys, but not so reactive that they corrode too fast. Chemistry reactivity is one of the most practically important concepts in all of science.
