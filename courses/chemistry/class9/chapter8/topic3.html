Alkaline Earth Metals & Noble Metals

Group 2 metals (like calcium and magnesium) are reactive — but not as dramatic as Group 1. Then there are metals like gold and platinum that barely react with anything. This topic explores both extremes of the reactivity spectrum.

3.1 Alkaline Earth Metals — Group 2

Alkaline Earth Metals
Group 2 elements: Be, Mg, Ca, Sr, Ba, Ra. Each has two valence electrons (ns² configuration). Called "alkaline earth" because their oxides ("earths") are alkaline, and they were historically found in the Earth's crust.

Physical Properties

Compared to Group 1, alkaline earth metals are:

  • Harder and less malleable (two electrons forming stronger metallic bonds)
  • Denser and have higher melting points
  • Still good conductors of heat and electricity
  • Less reactive than alkali metals (need to lose 2 electrons instead of 1)
Reactivity Trend

Reactivity increases down Group 2 (Ba > Ca > Mg > Be) — same reason as Group 1: atomic size and shielding increase, making it easier to lose the two outer electrons.

3.2 Reactions of Group 2 Metals with Water

Metal Reaction with water Notes
BeNo reaction with water or steamLeast reactive in the group
MgMg + H₂O(steam) → MgO + H₂Very slow with cold water; vigorous with steam; burns with brilliant white flame in O₂
CaCa + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂Steady fizzing; solution turns milky (limewater)
BaBa + 2H₂O → Ba(OH)₂ + H₂More vigorous than Ca
Magnesium and the brilliant white flame

Magnesium burns in air with such an intensely bright white flame that it was used in old camera flashbulbs and is still used in fireworks and flares. It is so bright it can damage your eyes — never look directly at burning magnesium!

3.3 Important Compounds of Group 2 Metals

CaCO₃
Limestone, marble, chalk — used in cement production, building materials, making quicklime
CaO (Quicklime)
Made by heating CaCO₃. Used in cement, steelmaking, water treatment
Ca(OH)₂ (Slaked lime)
Made by adding water to CaO. Used to neutralise soil acidity (agriculture), whitewash walls, water purification
MgO (Magnesia)
Refractory material for furnace linings (very high melting point); also used as an antacid
Mg metal
Lightweight alloys for aircraft and car parts; flares; photographic flashbulbs
MgSO₄ (Epsom salt)
Used in medicine (laxative, bath salts), agriculture (Mg fertilizer)

3.4 Noble Metals — The Inert Ones

Noble Metals
Metals with very low electropositive character that are extremely resistant to corrosion and oxidation. The main noble metals are Gold (Au), Platinum (Pt), and Silver (Ag).

These metals sit at the very bottom of the reactivity series. Their outer electrons are held very tightly — they have almost no tendency to lose electrons.

Why Are They So Unreactive?

  • Small atomic size relative to nuclear charge → nucleus has a very strong grip on outer electrons
  • Completely or nearly filled d-orbitals provide extra stability
  • Very high ionization energies — huge energy cost to remove electrons

Key Properties

Metal Reactivity Uses
Gold (Au)Does not react with any single acid. Dissolves only in aqua regia (3:1 HCl:HNO₃). Does not tarnish ever.Jewelry, electronics (non-corroding connectors), dentistry
Platinum (Pt)Even more inert than gold. Extremely high melting point. Does not corrode even in hot exhaust gases.Catalytic converters in cars, lab equipment, jewelry
Silver (Ag)Slightly more reactive than Au/Pt. Tarnishes in air (reacts with H₂S → black Ag₂S coating).Photography (AgBr), mirrors, electrical contacts, antimicrobial applications
Why do we treasure gold?

The reason civilisations throughout history valued gold is precisely because it is so unreactive. A gold coin buried for 3,000 years still looks perfect — no rust, no tarnish, no corrosion. Iron or copper objects from the same era crumble. Gold's resistance to change made it the perfect store of value and the ideal metal for coins and jewellery.
