Metals & the Reactivity Series

Why does sodium explode in water while gold sits safely in acid? It all comes down to how easily an element loses or gains electrons. This topic introduces electropositive character and the reactivity series — the league table of how reactive metals are.

1.1 Electropositive Character

Metals tend to lose electrons when they react, forming positively charged ions called cations. The tendency of a metal to lose electrons is called its electropositive character.

Electropositive Character
The tendency of an element (especially a metal) to lose electrons and form positive ions. The greater this tendency, the more reactive the metal.

Three factors determine how easily a metal loses its outer electrons:

Factor Effect on electropositive character
Atomic sizeLarger atom → outer electrons farther from nucleus → easier to lose → more electropositive
Nuclear chargeFewer protons relative to electrons → weaker pull on outer e⁻ → more electropositive
Shielding effectMore inner electron shells → more shielding of outer e⁻ → weaker attraction to nucleus → more electropositive
Periodic Trends

Down a group: atomic size increases, shielding increases → electropositive character increases.
Across a period (left → right): nuclear charge increases → electropositive character decreases.

1.2 The Reactivity Series

The reactivity series (also called the activity series) is a list of metals ranked from most reactive to least reactive — based on how easily they lose electrons.

⚡ Reactivity Series (High → Low)
K Potassium — most reactive
Na Sodium
Ca Calcium
Mg Magnesium
Al Aluminium
Zn Zinc
Fe Iron
Pb Lead
H Hydrogen (reference point)
Cu Copper
Ag Silver
Au Gold — least reactive

What the Reactivity Series Predicts

1. Displacement reactions: A metal higher in the series can displace a metal lower in it from a solution of its salt.

Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu   (iron is above copper → displaces Cu)
Zn + FeSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Fe   (zinc is above iron → displaces Fe)

2. Reactions with dilute acids: Metals above hydrogen in the series react with dilute acids to release H₂ gas. Metals below hydrogen (Cu, Ag, Au) do NOT react with dilute acids.

Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂↑
Cu + H₂SO₄ → No reaction

3. Reactions with water: Very reactive metals (K, Na, Ca) react even with cold water.

1.3 Reactions of Metals with Water

Metal Reaction Observation
K2K + 2H₂O → 2KOH + H₂Violent — H₂ ignites with lilac flame
Na2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂Vigorous — floats, moves rapidly on water surface
CaCa + 2H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ + H₂Steady fizzing, solution becomes milky
MgMg + H₂O(steam) → MgO + H₂Only with steam, not cold water
Fe3Fe + 4H₂O(steam) → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂Only with steam, not with liquid water
Cu—No reaction with water at all
Why is sodium stored in kerosene?

Sodium metal is stored under kerosene oil because it reacts so violently with water and oxygen in the air that it would ignite and explode if left exposed. The kerosene forms a barrier, keeping air and moisture away.
