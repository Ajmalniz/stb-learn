Alkali Metals — Group 1

The alkali metals are the most reactive group of metals on the periodic table. They react so aggressively with water that they must be stored under oil — and they get more reactive as you go down the group. Meet Li, Na, K and their dramatic chemistry.

2.1 Who Are the Alkali Metals?

Group 1 of the periodic table contains: Li (Lithium), Na (Sodium), K (Potassium), Rb (Rubidium), Cs (Caesium), Fr (Francium).

Alkali Metals
Group 1 elements with one valence electron (ns¹ configuration). Called "alkali" metals because they react with water to form strong alkalis (NaOH, KOH, LiOH).

Having only one electron in the outermost shell means they very easily lose that single electron to achieve a stable noble gas configuration. This makes them highly electropositive and highly reactive.

2.2 Physical Properties

Property Description
HardnessSoft — can be cut with a knife. Freshly cut surface is shiny but tarnishes quickly in air.
DensityLow density — Li, Na and K are less dense than water and float on it.
Melting pointLow for metals. Decreases down the group (Cs melts near room temperature).
ColourSilvery-white metallic appearance when freshly cut.
ConductivityGood conductors of heat and electricity.

2.3 Reactivity Trend in Group 1

Reactivity increases down Group 1: K is more reactive than Na, which is more reactive than Li.

Why does reactivity increase going down?

Going down the group: atomic size increases, shielding increases. The single outer electron is farther from the nucleus and more shielded by inner shells → it is easier to lose → the metal is more reactive.

2.4 Reactions with Water

All alkali metals react with water to produce a metal hydroxide (a strong alkali) and hydrogen gas:

2Li + 2H₂O → 2LiOH + H₂↑   (mild reaction)

2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂↑  (vigorous — Na fizzes and moves on water)

2K + 2H₂O → 2KOH + H₂↑   (violent — K ignites H₂, burns with lilac flame)

The metal hydroxide formed (NaOH, KOH) is a strong alkali. Adding a drop of phenolphthalein indicator to the solution turns it pink, confirming its alkaline nature.

Flame colours of alkali metals

Each alkali metal produces a distinctive flame colour when burned: Li = red, Na = yellow/orange, K = lilac/violet, Rb = blue-red, Cs = blue. This is why sodium street lamps give off a characteristic yellow glow!

2.5 Reactions with Air (Oxygen)

All alkali metals react rapidly with oxygen in the air, forming metal oxides. This is why their surfaces tarnish quickly after being cut.

4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O   (sodium oxide)
Na also forms Na₂O₂ (sodium peroxide) with excess oxygen.

To prevent this, alkali metals are stored under kerosene oil or paraffin oil, which forms a protective barrier against air and moisture.

2.6 Reactions with Chlorine

Alkali metals react vigorously with chlorine gas to form ionic metal chlorides — white crystalline salts:

2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl   (common table salt)
2K + Cl₂ → 2KCl

2.7 Uses of Alkali Metals

Sodium (Na)
Street lamps (sodium vapour → yellow light); coolant in nuclear reactors; manufacture of NaOH, soap, paper
Lithium (Li)
Lithium-ion batteries (phones, laptops, electric cars); lightweight alloys for aerospace
Potassium (K)
KNO₃ (potassium nitrate) as fertilizer; KCl as a salt substitute; K⁺ ions essential for nerve function
Caesium (Cs)
Atomic clocks (most accurate timekeeping devices); photoelectric cells
